  1. HIGH-LEVEL EXPLANATION (Layman's Terms)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

So far, we've seen that Level 4 with MMSE combining is the best approach.
But MMSE is a LINEAR method — it uses a simple weighted sum of the received
signals. Could we do even better with FANCIER, non-linear methods?

Imagine a classroom where 40 students are speaking simultaneously, and a
teacher (the CPU) is trying to understand all of them:

  LINEAR approach (MMSE): The teacher listens to everyone simultaneously,
  using spatial hearing (beamforming) to focus on each student one at a
  time. This works great when students are well-separated.

  NON-LINEAR approach (SIC): The teacher first listens to the LOUDEST
  student, fully decodes their message, then MENTALLY SUBTRACTS that
  student's voice from the room noise. Now the room is quieter. The teacher
  then decodes the next loudest student, subtracts again, and so on.

  In theory, SIC can achieve the MAXIMUM possible total capacity (the
  "capacity region" of the multiple access channel).

THE PUNCHLINE: In cell-free mMIMO, SIC provides almost NO improvement
over linear MMSE — only about 1% higher sum SE.

Why? Because the "favorable propagation" property makes all students'
"voices" (channels) nearly orthogonal. When everyone is already well-
separated, there's very little interference left to cancel. MMSE already
captures almost all the available capacity.

This is wonderful news for practical systems: you don't need the
complexity of SIC. Simple linear MMSE is (almost) optimal.

2.1  BACKGROUND: SUCCESSIVE INTERFERENCE CANCELLATION (SIC)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SIC is a NON-LINEAR multi-user detection scheme. It works as follows:

  Step 1: Decode UE π(1) treating all other UEs as noise.
          Use MMSE combining optimized for UE π(1).

  Step 2: Reconstruct UE π(1)'s signal contribution: ĥ_π(1) × ŝ_π(1)

  Step 3: SUBTRACT this contribution from the received signal:
          y' = y - ĥ_π(1) × ŝ_π(1)

  Step 4: Decode UE π(2) from the "cleaned" signal y'.
          Now UE π(2) sees LESS interference.

  Step 5: Continue for UEs π(3), ..., π(K).

  Where π is the decoding ORDER (a permutation of {1, ..., K}).

WHY SIC IS CAPACITY-ACHIEVING:

  For the Gaussian Multiple Access Channel (MAC), MMSE-SIC achieves
  EVERY point on the boundary of the capacity region [52, Sec. 8.3.4].
  Different decoding orders achieve different points on the boundary,
  but the SUM RATE is the same for all orders.

  This is a fundamental result from information theory:

    • Cover & Thomas (1991): "Elements of Information Theory" — MAC
      capacity region and corner points
    • Tse & Viswanath (2005) [52]: "Fundamentals of Wireless
      Communications" — MMSE-SIC for MIMO MAC

WHY SIC IS NOT PERFECT WITH IMPERFECT CSI:

  When subtracting UE π(1)'s signal, we use the ESTIMATED channel:

    y' = y - ĥ_π(1) × ŝ_π(1)

  The estimation error h̃_π(1) means the subtraction is IMPERFECT:

    y' = y - (h_π(1) - h̃_π(1)) × ŝ_π(1) = y - h_π(1)×ŝ_π(1) + h̃_π(1)×ŝ_π(1)

  The residual h̃_π(1) × ŝ_π(1) remains as interference. This is why
  SIC doesn't achieve the perfect-CSI capacity, but it still provides
  gains over linear combining.


2.2  PROPOSITION 4: ACHIEVABLE SUM SE WITH MMSE-SIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  ►  Proposition 4. At Level 4, with MMSE estimation and MMSE-SIC
     decoding, for ANY decoding order, the achievable SUM SE is:

                     ⎛    τₚ ⎞
    SSE⁽ˢⁱᶜ⁾  = ⎜1 - ──⎟ 𝔼{log₂ det(Iₖ + P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹ Ĥ)}        ... (41)
                     ⎝    τc ⎠

  Where:
    • P = diag(p₁, ..., pₖ) ∈ ℝᴷˣᴷ  — diagonal power matrix
    • Ĥ = [ĥ₁, ..., ĥₖ] ∈ ℂᴸᴺˣᴷ    — matrix of all channel estimates
    • E = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ pᵢCᵢ + σ²Iₗₙ ∈ ℂᴸᴺˣᴸᴺ — estimation error + noise covariance
    • The expectation is over the channel estimates

IMPORTANT NOTES:

  (a) This gives the SUM SE, not individual per-user SEs. The per-user
      SE depends on the decoding order (later-decoded users benefit more).

  (b) The SUM SE is INDEPENDENT of the decoding order. This is because
      log₂ det(·) is the same regardless of which corner of the MAC
      capacity region we operate at.

  (c) For any decoding order, EACH UE gets at least as high SE with
      MMSE-SIC as with linear MMSE. So MMSE-SIC is strictly better
      (or equal) per-user.


2.3  PROOF OF PROPOSITION 4 (FROM APPENDIX C)
──────────────────────────────────────────────

Step 1: Rewrite the received signal using estimate + error decomposition:

             K
    y  =  ∑  ĥᵢ sᵢ  +  e                                        ... (49)
            i=1

  Where: e = n + ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ h̃ᵢ sᵢ  (noise + estimation errors)

  Properties of e:
    • 𝔼{e} = 0
    • 𝔼{eeᴴ} = E = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ pᵢCᵢ + σ²Iₗₙ
    • e is uncorrelated with ĥᵢsᵢ for all i  (MMSE orthogonality)

Step 2: Treat (49) as a MULTIPLE ACCESS CHANNEL (MAC):

    y = Ĥ s + e

  Where: s = [s₁, ..., sₖ]ᵀ ~ 𝒩ℂ(0, P) and e ~ (0, E)

  The "channels" are the KNOWN estimates {ĥᵢ}. The "noise" is e,
  which includes estimation errors — this is the "worst case" treatment.

Step 3: Apply pre-whitening:

  Multiply both sides by E⁻¹/²:

    E⁻¹/² y = E⁻¹/² Ĥ s + E⁻¹/² e
                ⏟              ⏟
               ≜ H̃ₑ           ≜ ñ ~ 𝒩ℂ(0, I)

  Now ñ is WHITE (identity covariance). This is a standard MIMO MAC.

Step 4: Apply the MIMO MAC sum-capacity formula:

  For a K-user MAC with channel matrix H̃ₑ and white noise:

    C_sum = log₂ det(Iₗₙ + H̃ₑ P H̃ₑᴴ)

          = log₂ det(Iₗₙ + E⁻¹/² Ĥ P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹/²)

Step 5: Apply the identity det(I + AB) = det(I + BA):

  Let A = E⁻¹/² Ĥ P¹/² (size LN × K)
      B = P¹/² Ĥᴴ E⁻¹/² (size K × LN)

  Then: det(Iₗₙ + ABᴴ) = det(Iₖ + BᴴA) ... wait, let me be precise.

  Actually: det(Iₗₙ + E⁻¹/² Ĥ P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹/²)

  Let C = E⁻¹/² Ĥ P¹/² (LN × K) and D = (E⁻¹/² Ĥ P¹/²)ᴴ = P¹/² Ĥᴴ E⁻¹/² (K × LN)

  det(Iₗₙ + CDᴴ) is not quite right. Let me use the identity directly:

    det(Iₗₙ + XY) = det(Iₖ + YX)

  Where X = E⁻¹/² Ĥ (LN × K) and Y = P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹/² (K × LN)

  Then: YX = P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹/² E⁻¹/² Ĥ = P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹ Ĥ  (K × K)

  So: det(Iₗₙ + E⁻¹/² Ĥ P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹/²) = det(Iₖ + P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹ Ĥ)

  This converts the determinant from LN × LN to K × K — much more
  efficient to compute since K = 40 << LN = 400.

Step 6: Add the pre-log factor and expectation:

    SSE⁽ˢⁱᶜ⁾ = (1 - τₚ/τc) × 𝔼{log₂ det(Iₖ + P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹ Ĥ)}    ✓

Step 7: Order independence:

  The sum capacity of a MAC is achieved at EVERY corner point of the
  capacity region. Different decoding orders access different corner
  points, but the sum ∑ₖ Rₖ is the same for all corners. This is a
  fundamental property of the MAC capacity region polytope.

KEY OBSERVATIONS:

  (a) MMSE-SIC improves sum SE by only ~1% over linear MMSE at Level 4.

      This is a NEGLIGIBLE gain considering the MASSIVE increase in
      complexity (SIC requires K sequential decoding steps instead of
      K parallel MMSE combinings).

  (b) The reason: FAVORABLE PROPAGATION.

      In cell-free mMIMO with L = 400 distributed APs, the collective
      channels of different UEs become nearly orthogonal:

        |ĥᵢᴴ ĥⱼ| / (‖ĥᵢ‖ ‖ĥⱼ‖) ≈ 0   for i ≠ j

      When channels are orthogonal, MMSE combining ALREADY cancels
      inter-user interference almost perfectly. There's nothing left
      for SIC to improve.

  (c) Level 1 and Level 3 provide roughly the SAME sum SE (~175-180),
      while Level 2 is far behind (~130).

      This is interesting: Level 3 (LSFD) doesn't provide much gain
      over Level 1 (small cells) in terms of total throughput. The
      cooperative processing mostly helps redistribute SE from strong
      to weak UEs (fairness), not increase the total.

  (d) The gap between Level 4 and Levels 1/3 (about 100 bit/s/Hz
      or ~55%) further reinforces that centralized processing is
      strongly preferred.


2.5  FAVORABLE PROPAGATION — THE PHYSICS BEHIND THE RESULT
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Favorable propagation means: ĥᵢᴴĥⱼ → 0 as LN → ∞ for i ≠ j.

WHY IT OCCURS IN CELL-FREE mMIMO:

  Each UE k has a unique "spatial signature" defined by its large-scale
  fading profile: [βₖ₁, βₖ₂, ..., βₖₗ].

  UE k is close to some APs (large βₖₗ) and far from others (small βₖₗ).
  Two different UEs k and j almost certainly have DIFFERENT sets of
  "close" APs (different spatial signatures).

  The inner product ĥₖᴴĥⱼ is approximately:

    ĥₖᴴĥⱼ ≈ ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ ĥₖₗ* ĥⱼₗ

  Each term ĥₖₗ*ĥⱼₗ is a product of independent random variables
  (for non-co-pilot UEs). By the law of large numbers, the sum converges
  to zero as L → ∞.

  For co-pilot UEs, the estimates are correlated, which prevents perfect
  orthogonality. But with good pilot assignment, the correlation is
  manageable.

WHY IT'S STRONGER IN CELL-FREE THAN CELLULAR:

  In cellular mMIMO (co-located array): favorable propagation comes from
  the large number of antenna elements M creating angular diversity.
  But all UEs in the same cell may arrive from similar directions.

  In cell-free mMIMO (distributed APs): favorable propagation comes from
  the GEOGRAPHIC diversity — UEs have different distances to different
  APs. This is a much more robust source of orthogonality.

4.1  THE det(I + AB) = det(I + BA) IDENTITY
────────────────────────────────────────────

This is Sylvester's determinant identity. For A ∈ ℂᵐˣⁿ, B ∈ ℂⁿˣᵐ:

    det(Iₘ + AB) = det(Iₙ + BA)

Proof sketch: Consider the block matrix:

    ⎡ Iₘ   A ⎤
    ⎣ B    Iₙ ⎦

Compute its determinant two ways using Schur complements:
  • Eliminate lower-left: det(Iₘ + AB) × det(Iₙ)
  • Eliminate upper-right: det(Iₘ) × det(Iₙ + BA)

Both give the same determinant → det(Iₘ + AB) = det(Iₙ + BA).  ✓

Application in Proposition 4:
  Converts det(Iₗₙ + E⁻¹/²Ĥ P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹/²) to det(Iₖ + P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹ Ĥ)
  This saves computation: K×K determinant instead of LN×LN.


4.2  CONNECTION BETWEEN LINEAR MMSE AND SIC
────────────────────────────────────────────

For linear MMSE: Sum SE = ∑ₖ₌₁ᴷ (1-τₚ/τc) 𝔼{log₂(1+SINR⁽⁴⁾ₖ)}

For MMSE-SIC: Sum SE = (1-τₚ/τc) 𝔼{log₂ det(Iₖ + P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹ Ĥ)}

The connection via the matrix determinant lemma:

  det(Iₖ + P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹ Ĥ) = ∏ₖ₌₁ᴷ (1 + SINR⁽ˢⁱᶜ⁾_π(k))

Where SINR⁽ˢⁱᶜ⁾_π(k) is the SINR of UE π(k) after canceling UEs
π(1), ..., π(k-1). We always have:

  SINR⁽ˢⁱᶜ⁾_π(k) ≥ SINR⁽⁴⁾_π(k)  (SIC removes some interference)

But when channels are nearly orthogonal (favorable propagation):

  SINR⁽ˢⁱᶜ⁾_π(k) ≈ SINR⁽⁴⁾_π(k)  (almost no interference to cancel)

Hence: Sum SE (SIC) ≈ Sum SE (MMSE) when favorable propagation holds.


4.3  NUMERICAL EXAMPLE: WHY SIC GAIN IS TINY
──────────────────────────────────────────────

Consider K = 2 UEs at Level 4 with LN = 400.

Suppose: ĥ₁ᴴĥ₂ / (‖ĥ₁‖‖ĥ₂‖) = 0.05  (nearly orthogonal)

  ‖ĥ₁‖² = 50, ‖ĥ₂‖² = 40, |ĥ₁ᴴĥ₂|² = 0.05² × 50 × 40 = 5

  With σ² = 0.1, p₁ = p₂ = 1 (simplified):

  SINR_MMSE for UE 1 ≈ 50 / (5/40 × 40 + noise) ≈ 50 / (5 + noise)
  (interference from UE 2 through the combiner is proportional to
   |ĥ₁ᴴĥ₂|²/‖ĥ₂‖² = 5/40 after normalization)

  After SIC cancels UE 2: interference term 5 drops to ~0 (residual
  from imperfect cancellation). So SINR improves from ~50/5 = 10 to
  ~50/0.1 = 500. But in log scale: log₂(11) vs log₂(501) isn't the
  right comparison because the original interference was already small.

  Actually with proper MMSE combining, the interference is already
  very well suppressed. The MMSE combiner for UE 1 "points away" from
  ĥ₂, making the effective interference much smaller than |ĥ₁ᴴĥ₂|².

  With favorable propagation (0.05 correlation), MMSE already achieves
  >99% of the SIC capacity.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The "fronthaul" is the data connection between each AP and the central
computer (CPU). Think of it as the "pipes" through which information flows.

A natural worry about Level 4 (centralized): "If every AP sends its raw
signals to the CPU, won't the pipes need to be HUGE?"

This section shows the counterintuitive answer: Level 4 actually needs
SMALLER pipes than Levels 2/3!

Here's the intuition:

  Level 4: Each AP sends its raw received signal. This is an N-dimensional
  vector (N = number of antennas at the AP). For a typical AP with 1-4
  antennas, that's 1-4 numbers per time slot.

  Levels 2/3: Each AP first processes the signal locally and sends K
  estimated data symbols (one per UE). For K = 40 UEs, that's 40 numbers
  per time slot.

  Since K >> N (many more users than antennas per AP), Levels 2/3 EXPAND
  the data! The AP takes a small signal (N dimensions) and blows it up
  into a big report (K dimensions). Level 4 just sends the small signal.

It's like the difference between:
  • Level 4: Sending the raw security camera footage (small file)
  • Level 2/3: Having each camera write a separate report about each of
    the 40 suspects (40 reports per camera — much more data!)

But there's a twist: if APs are connected in a CHAIN (serial fronthaul),
Levels 2/3 can aggregate results along the chain, reducing their fronthaul
dramatically. Level 4 cannot benefit from this.

  6. DETAILED TECHNICAL EXPLANATION (PhD Level) — SECTION VI
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

6.1  FRONTHAUL SIGNALING TABLE (TABLE I REVISITED)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────

From Table I in the paper:

  ┌─────────┬───────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────┐
  │ Level   │ Per Coherence Block       │ Statistical Parameters        │
  │         │ (complex scalars)         │ (complex scalars, one-time)   │
  ├─────────┼───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
  │ Level 4 │ τc N L                    │ KLN²/2                        │
  │ Level 3 │ (τc - τₚ) K L            │ KL + (L²K² + KL)/2           │
  │ Level 2 │ (τc - τₚ) K L            │ —                             │
  │ Level 1 │ —                         │ —                             │
  └─────────┴───────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┘

BREAKDOWN OF LEVEL 4 FRONTHAUL:

  Each AP l sends per coherence block:
    • τₚ × N complex scalars for pilot signals (z_tₗ for each pilot t)
    • (τc - τₚ) × N complex scalars for data signals (yₗ per data slot)
    • Total: τc × N per AP

  Across all L APs: τc × N × L total

  ADDITIONALLY, correlation matrices {Rₖₗ} must be shared:
    • Each Rₖₗ is N×N Hermitian: N²/2 complex scalars (exploiting symmetry)
    • K UEs × L APs: K × L × N²/2 total
    • But these change SLOWLY (only with UE mobility/statistics change)

BREAKDOWN OF LEVELS 2/3 FRONTHAUL:

  Each AP l sends per coherence block:
    • K complex scalars per data time slot (ŝₖₗ for each UE k)
    • Over (τc - τₚ) data time slots: (τc - τₚ) × K per AP

  No pilot signals are sent (estimation is done locally).

  Across all L APs: (τc - τₚ) × K × L total

  Level 3 ADDITIONALLY needs statistical parameters for LSFD:
    • 𝔼{gₖₖ}: KL complex scalars
    • 𝔼{gₖᵢgₖᵢᴴ}: K²L²/2 complex scalars (dominant term!)
    • Dₖ: KL/2 real scalars
    • Total: KL + (L²K² + KL)/2


6.2  THE KEY COMPARISON — EQUATION (42)
────────────────────────────────────────

Level 4 requires LESS fronthaul per coherence block than Levels 2/3 when:

    τc N L  <  (τc - τₚ) K L

  Dividing both sides by L:

    τc N  <  (τc - τₚ) K

  Rearranging:

        τc       N
    ─────────  ×  ─  <  1                                         ... (42)
     τc - τₚ     K

  Since τc/(τc - τₚ) is slightly > 1 (because τₚ is small relative to τc),
  the condition simplifies to approximately:

    N/K  <  1     (i.e., N < K)

  This is ALMOST ALWAYS TRUE in cell-free mMIMO:
    • N = 1, K = 40 → N/K = 0.025 << 1  ✓
    • N = 4, K = 40 → N/K = 0.10 << 1   ✓
    • Even N = 16, K = 40 → N/K = 0.40 < 1  ✓

  The ONLY exception would be if K < N (fewer users than antennas per AP),
  which never happens in cell-free mMIMO's operating regime.


6.3  FIGURE 6: FRONTHAUL PER CHANNEL USE vs τc
────────────────────────────────────────────────

Setup: L = 100, N = 4, K = 40, τₚ = 10

Per AP, per channel use:

  Level 4: τc × N / τc = N = 4 scalars/channel use

  Wait, the figure shows scalars per channel use, so:

    Level 4: N = 4 scalars per channel use (constant, independent of τc)

    Levels 2/3: (τc - τₚ)/τc × K = (1 - τₚ/τc) × K scalars/channel use

    As τc → ∞: Levels 2/3 → K = 40 scalars/channel use
    At τc = 200: Levels 2/3 = (190/200) × 40 = 38 scalars/channel use

  ┌──────────┬──────────┬──────────────┬───────────────────┐
  │ τc       │ Level 4  │ Level 2/3    │ Ratio (L2/3 : L4) │
  ├──────────┼──────────┼──────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ 11       │ 4        │ (1/11)×40=3.6│ 0.91 (L4 costs more)│
  │ 20       │ 4        │ (10/20)×40=20│ 5.0               │
  │ 100      │ 4        │ (90/100)×40=36│ 9.0              │
  │ 200      │ 4        │ 38           │ 9.5               │
  │ ∞        │ 4        │ 40           │ 10.0 = K/N        │
  └──────────┴──────────┴──────────────┴───────────────────┘

  Level 4 requires MORE fronthaul ONLY when τc ≤ 11.
  For any practical τc (≥100), Level 4 requires ~10× LESS fronthaul!

  As τc → ∞, the ratio approaches K/N = 40/4 = 10.

  PHYSICAL EXPLANATION: The fronthaul is dominated by DATA signals
  (τc - τₚ data symbols >> τₚ pilot symbols). Level 4 sends N scalars
  per data symbol while Levels 2/3 send K scalars. Since K >> N, Level 4
  wins for any reasonably long coherence block.


6.4  WHY LEVELS 2/3 EXPAND THE DATA
─────────────────────────────────────

At each AP l, the received signal per data time slot is:

    yₗ ∈ ℂᴺ   (N-dimensional)

Level 4 sends yₗ as-is: N complex scalars.

Levels 2/3 process yₗ into {ŝ₁ₗ, ŝ₂ₗ, ..., ŝₖₗ}: K complex scalars.

The processing EXPANDS the data from N to K dimensions!

  N-dim input ──→ [AP local processing] ──→ K-dim output

  This expansion ratio K/N is the fundamental reason Level 4 is
  fronthaul-efficient. The AP is doing a "lossy expansion" that
  increases the data volume without adding information (in fact, losing
  information compared to the raw signal).


6.5  SECTION VI-A: SERIAL FRONTHAUL
─────────────────────────────────────

When APs share a wired connection (daisy chain / serial fronthaul):

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │            SERIAL FRONTHAUL ARCHITECTURE                  │
  │                                                           │
  │  AP₁ ──wire──→ AP₂ ──wire──→ AP₃ ──wire──→ ... ──→ CPU  │
  │                                                           │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

At Level 2 (simple averaging):

  AP₁ computes ŝₖ₁ for each UE k, sends over wire to AP₂.
  AP₂ computes ŝₖ₂, ADDS to received ŝₖ₁ → sends ŝₖ₁ + ŝₖ₂ to AP₃.
  AP₃ adds ŝₖ₃ → sends ŝₖ₁ + ŝₖ₂ + ŝₖ₃ to AP₄.
  ...
  CPU receives ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ ŝₖₗ = L × ŝₖ (the Level 2 estimate, up to 1/L)

  On each wire segment, only K scalars are transmitted per data slot
  (regardless of how many APs have already contributed!).

  Total fronthaul per coherence block:
    WITHOUT serial: (τc - τₚ) × K × L
    WITH serial:    (τc - τₚ) × K        (L factor vanishes!)

At Level 3 (LSFD):

  Instead of ŝₖₗ, each AP sends the weighted estimate aₖₗ ŝₖₗ.
  AP₂ adds: aₖ₁ŝₖ₁ + aₖ₂ŝₖ₂ → sends to AP₃.
  CPU receives ∑ₗ aₖₗ ŝₖₗ = ŝₖ (the Level 3 estimate).

  Requires each AP to know its own LSFD weight aₖₗ locally.
  Same K scalars per wire segment.

Level 4 CANNOT use serial aggregation:

  Level 4 needs the RAW signal yₗ from each AP. These are different at
  each AP and cannot be meaningfully summed along the wire.
  Each AP's yₗ must reach the CPU individually.

  With serial fronthaul, Level 4 needs L × N scalars per data slot
  on the LAST wire segment (near the CPU), growing linearly with L.

  Levels 2/3 need only K scalars on every segment — CONSTANT in L.

This changes the comparison for serial fronthaul:

  ┌───────────────┬───────────────────────┬────────────────────┐
  │               │ Star Fronthaul        │ Serial Fronthaul   │
  │               │ (each AP to CPU)      │ (daisy chain)      │
  ├───────────────┼───────────────────────┼────────────────────┤
  │ Level 4 total │ τcNL                  │ N×L (per segment,  │
  │               │                       │ growing near CPU)  │
  │ Level 2/3 per │ (τc-τₚ)KL            │ (τc-τₚ)K (constant)│
  │ segment       │                       │                    │
  └───────────────┴───────────────────────┴────────────────────┘

  With serial fronthaul, Levels 2/3 become much more competitive.
  This is why the RADIO STRIPES concept [43] adopts serial fronthaul.

RADIO STRIPES CONCEPT:

  Proposed by Interdonato, Björnson, Ngo, Frenger, and Larsson (2019) [43]:
  "Ubiquitous cell-free massive MIMO communications," EURASIP JWCN.

  APs are physically embedded in a cable strip (like LED strips), which
  serves as both the physical mounting and the fronthaul connection.

  Benefits:
    • Easy deployment (just unroll the strip along walls/ceilings)
    • Natural serial fronthaul topology
    • Each AP adds its contribution sequentially
    • Fronthaul capacity requirement is constant per segment

  This makes Levels 2/3 practically viable for large-scale cell-free
  deployments, despite their lower SE compared to Level 4.

7.1  FRONTHAUL IN C-RAN
────────────────────────

  Cloud Radio Access Networks (C-RAN) pioneered the fronthaul concept:

  • China Mobile Research Institute (2011): Proposed C-RAN architecture
    with centralized baseband processing.
  • The CPRI (Common Public Radio Interface) standard defines fronthaul
    data formats and rates for connecting Remote Radio Heads to BBUs.

  Key challenge: CPRI requires sending raw I/Q samples, which demands
  very high fronthaul bandwidth (e.g., 10 Gbps for a 20 MHz LTE system
  with 100 antennas). This is the same challenge Level 4 faces.

  Compression techniques:
    • Bashar et al. (2018) [32]: Showed that channel estimates can be
      compressed well for backhaul-constrained cell-free systems.
    • Maryopi & Burr (2019) [53]: "Few-bit CSI acquisition for
      centralized cell-free massive MIMO with spatial correlation"

7.2  FRONTHAUL QUANTIZATION
────────────────────────────

  The paper acknowledges that its comparison assumes INFINITE PRECISION
  (all scalars shared with perfect accuracy). In practice:

    • Pilot signals may need higher resolution (to preserve estimation
      quality)
    • Data signals may tolerate lower resolution (MMSE combining is
      somewhat robust to quantization)
    • Pilots are a SMALL fraction of total signaling (τₚ/τc ≈ 5%)

  This is an area where Level 4 may lose some advantage in practice,
  but the paper argues the impact is modest.

EXAMPLE 1: Fronthaul Comparison
────────────────────────────────

Setup: L = 200, N = 2, K = 50, τc = 200, τₚ = 10, 20 MHz bandwidth

Level 4 per coherence block:
  Total scalars = τcNL = 200 × 2 × 200 = 80,000

Level 2/3 per coherence block:
  Total scalars = (τc-τₚ)KL = 190 × 50 × 200 = 1,900,000

Ratio: 1,900,000 / 80,000 = 23.75

Level 4 requires 23.75× LESS fronthaul!

Converting to bits (assuming 16-bit complex scalars = 32 bits each):
  Level 4: 80,000 × 32 = 2,560,000 bits per coherence block
  Level 2/3: 1,900,000 × 32 = 60,800,000 bits per coherence block

  Coherence block duration: 200 symbols / 20 MHz ≈ 10 μs (rough estimate)
  → actually τc/bandwidth = 200/(20×10⁶) is not quite right.

  Better: If Tc = 2 ms and Bc = 100 kHz:
    One coherence block = 2 ms
    Fronthaul rate for Level 4: 2.56 Mbit / 2 ms = 1.28 Gbps total
    Fronthaul rate for Level 2/3: 60.8 Mbit / 2 ms = 30.4 Gbps total

  Per AP:
    Level 4: 1.28 Gbps / 200 APs = 6.4 Mbps per AP
    Level 2/3: 30.4 Gbps / 200 APs = 152 Mbps per AP


EXAMPLE 2: Serial Fronthaul Advantage
───────────────────────────────────────

Same setup, but APs connected in a serial chain.

Level 2/3 with serial fronthaul:
  Per wire segment: (τc-τₚ) × K = 190 × 50 = 9,500 scalars per block
  This is CONSTANT regardless of how many APs are upstream!

Level 4 with serial fronthaul:
  The last segment (nearest CPU) must carry ALL APs' signals:
  200 × τcN = 200 × 400 = 80,000 scalars per block  (same as star!)

  In fact, the m-th segment carries m APs' data:
  Segment 200 (last): 200 × 400 = 80,000 scalars
  Segment 100 (middle): 100 × 400 = 40,000 scalars
  Segment 1 (first): 1 × 400 = 400 scalars

Comparison at the BOTTLENECK (last segment):
  Level 4: 80,000 scalars
  Level 2/3: 9,500 scalars

  Now Levels 2/3 require 80,000/9,500 = 8.4× LESS at the bottleneck!

This reversal shows that serial fronthaul fundamentally changes the
comparison. With serial fronthaul, Levels 2/3 are fronthaul-efficient.

From Figure 5 (approximate average values):

  ┌───────────────────────────┬──────────────┬──────────────────┐
  │ Scheme                    │ Sum SE       │ Per-UE average SE│
  │                           │ (bit/s/Hz)   │ (bit/s/Hz)       │

  Throughput per unit fronthaul (efficiency):
    Level 4: 5.54 Gbps / 1.28 Gbps fronthaul = 4.33 bit/fronthaul-bit
    Level 2: 2.60 Gbps / 30.4 Gbps fronthaul = 0.086 bit/fronthaul-bit

  Level 4 is 50× more efficient in throughput-per-fronthaul-bit!

  9. PRACTICE PROBLEMS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PROBLEM 1: Fronthaul Crossover Point
──────────────────────────────────────
For L = 100, N = 8, K = 20, τₚ = 10:

(a) At what value of τc does Level 4 begin to require LESS fronthaul
    than Levels 2/3?
(b) For τc = 200, compute the fronthaul ratio.
(c) As τc → ∞, what is the asymptotic fronthaul ratio?

Solution:

(a) Level 4 = Level 2/3 when: τcN = (τc - τₚ)K
    τc × 8 = (τc - 10) × 20
    8τc = 20τc - 200
    200 = 12τc
    τc = 200/12 = 16.67

    So for τc ≥ 17 (any practical value), Level 4 requires less.

(b) At τc = 200:
    Level 4: τcNL = 200×8×100 = 160,000
    Level 2/3: (τc-τₚ)KL = 190×20×100 = 380,000
    Ratio: 380,000/160,000 = 2.375  (Level 4 needs 2.4× less)

(c) As τc → ∞: ratio → K/N = 20/8 = 2.5


PROBLEM 2: Serial vs Star Fronthaul
─────────────────────────────────────
L = 50 APs in a chain, N = 1, K = 30, τc = 200, τₚ = 5.

(a) Star fronthaul: compute total fronthaul for Level 4 and Level 2.
(b) Serial fronthaul: compute the bottleneck (maximum) capacity per
    wire segment for Level 4 and Level 2.
(c) Which level is better with star? With serial?

Solution:

(a) Star:
    Level 4: τcNL = 200×1×50 = 10,000 scalars
    Level 2: (τc-τₚ)KL = 195×30×50 = 292,500 scalars
    → Level 4 wins (29× less)

(b) Serial bottleneck (last segment):
    Level 4: Must carry all 50 APs' raw signals per time slot
             = L × N = 50 × 1 = 50 scalars per time slot
             × τc = 200 time slots → 10,000 per block (same as star)
    Level 2: K = 30 scalars per time slot (constant)
             × (τc-τₚ) = 195 time slots → 5,850 per block

    → Level 2 wins at bottleneck! (10,000 vs 5,850)

(c) Star: Level 4 wins
    Serial: Level 2 wins at the bottleneck

    This shows the topology determines the preferred implementation.


PROBLEM 3: SIC Gain Estimation
───────────────────────────────
At Level 4 with K = 3 UEs, suppose the per-user MMSE SINRs are:
  SINR₁ = 10 (10 dB), SINR₂ = 8 (9 dB), SINR₃ = 5 (7 dB)

The sum SE with linear MMSE:
  SE_linear = log₂(1+10) + log₂(1+8) + log₂(1+5)
            = 3.459 + 3.170 + 2.585 = 9.214 bit/s/Hz

If SIC provides a 1% improvement (as observed in Fig. 5):
  SE_SIC ≈ 9.214 × 1.01 = 9.306 bit/s/Hz

The gain: 0.092 bit/s/Hz.

With 20 MHz bandwidth: 0.092 × 20 = 1.84 Mbps extra throughput.
This is negligible compared to the total 9.214 × 20 = 184 Mbps.

Is SIC worth the complexity? NO. SIC requires:
  • K = 3 sequential decoding steps (instead of 3 parallel)
  • Signal reconstruction and subtraction at each step
  • Error propagation risk (if one UE is decoded wrong, it corrupts
    all subsequent UEs)

For a 1% gain, the added complexity is not justified.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

SECTION V — NON-LINEAR DECODING:

1. MMSE-SIC (successive interference cancellation) is the capacity-
   achieving scheme for the MAC. It decodes users sequentially, subtracting
   each decoded signal before processing the next.

2. The achievable sum SE is: (1-τₚ/τc) 𝔼{log₂ det(Iₖ + P Ĥᴴ E⁻¹ Ĥ)},
   which is INDEPENDENT of the decoding order.

3. The gain over linear MMSE is only ~1%. This is because favorable
   propagation in cell-free mMIMO makes channels nearly orthogonal,
   so MMSE already cancels most inter-user interference.

4. CONCLUSION: Non-linear processing is NOT needed in cell-free mMIMO.
   Linear MMSE at Level 4 is sufficient.

5. Level 1 ≈ Level 3 in sum SE, while Level 2 is far behind. The gap
   to Level 4 (~55%) reinforces centralized processing is preferred.


SECTION VI — FRONTHAUL SIGNALING:

6. With star fronthaul (each AP connected individually to CPU):
   Level 4 requires LESS fronthaul than Levels 2/3 when N < K (typical).
   The ratio approaches K/N as τc → ∞.
   For N=1, K=40: Level 4 needs 40× less fronthaul!

7. The counterintuitive reason: Levels 2/3 EXPAND N-dimensional signals
   into K-dimensional estimates. When K >> N, this expansion dramatically
   increases fronthaul requirements.

8. With serial fronthaul (daisy-chain topology): Levels 2/3 can aggregate
   estimates along the wire, reducing fronthaul from (τc-τₚ)KL to
   (τc-τₚ)K. Level 4 cannot benefit from this, so the comparison REVERSES.

9. The RADIO STRIPES concept [43] leverages serial fronthaul to make
   distributed implementations (Levels 2/3) practically viable.

10. OVERALL: Level 4 with star fronthaul is the clear winner in BOTH SE
    and fronthaul. Serial fronthaul is the only scenario where Levels 2/3
    become competitive, and is needed for their practical viability.
