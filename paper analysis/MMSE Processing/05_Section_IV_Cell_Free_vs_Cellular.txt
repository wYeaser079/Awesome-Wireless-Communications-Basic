Imagine you're a judge comparing two sports teams:
  • Team Cellular: 4 powerful players (BSs with 100 antennas each), each
    controlling their own zone
  • Team Cell-free: 400 small players (APs with 1 antenna each) OR 100
    medium players (4 antennas each), all working together

The question: Which team wins?

The answer depends on HOW Team Cell-free plays:

  With DUMB strategy (MR combining):
    Team Cell-free LOSES. The 400 small players all try to help everyone
    but end up getting in each other's way. They'd be better off as
    400 independent small players (small cells), or not playing at all!
    Team Cellular with its 4 powerful coordinated players wins easily.

  With SMART strategy (MMSE combining):
    Team Cell-free WINS DECISIVELY. When all 400 players coordinate
    intelligently, they surround every "customer" and provide excellent
    service. No one is left behind. Even the worst-served customer in
    Team Cell-free beats the worst-served customer in Team Cellular.

But here's the catch: the previous papers about Cell-free mMIMO only
studied the DUMB strategy and claimed Cell-free was great! This paper
shows those claims were misleading because:
  1. They used the wrong playing field (macro-cell propagation model)
  2. They compared against a weak opponent (suboptimal small cells)
  3. They didn't even try the SMART strategy (MMSE)

This section provides the definitive, fair comparison.

2.1  CELLULAR NETWORK PARAMETERS
──────────────────────────────────

The cellular baseline is designed to be the STRONGEST POSSIBLE competitor:

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Cellular mMIMO Setup                              │
  ├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Number of cells: Lc = 4                           │
  │  Antennas per BS: Mc = 100                         │
  │  UEs per cell: Kc = 10                             │
  │  Total antennas: Lc × Mc = 400                     │
  │  Total UEs: Lc × Kc = K = 40                       │
  │  Pilots per cell: τₚ = Kc = 10 (unique within cell)│
  │  Pilot reuse: Factor 1 (same pilots in all cells)  │
  │  Combining: M-MMSE (strongest possible)            │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘


2.2  CELLULAR CHANNEL MODEL — EQUATIONS (31)-(34)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────

The channel from BS j to UE k in cell l:

    hₗⱼₖ ~ 𝒩ℂ(0, Rₗⱼₖ)                                          ... (31)

Where Rₗⱼₖ ∈ ℂᴹᶜˣᴹᶜ is the spatial correlation matrix and
βₗⱼₖ = tr(Rₗⱼₖ)/Mc is the large-scale fading.

MMSE channel estimation (same as Section II but for cellular):

  Estimate: ĥₗⱼᵢ ~ 𝒩ℂ(0, Rₗⱼᵢ - Cₗⱼᵢ)                         ... (32)

  Error: h̃ₗⱼᵢ ~ 𝒩ℂ(0, Cₗⱼᵢ)                                    ... (33)

                                 ⎛ Lc                    ⎞⁻¹
  Cₗⱼᵢ = Rₗⱼᵢ - pₗᵢτₚ Rₗⱼᵢ   ⎜ ∑ pₗ'ᵢτₚ Rₗ'ⱼᵢ + σ²Iₘc⎟  Rₗⱼᵢ
                                 ⎝l'=1                   ⎠        ... (34)

KEY: In cellular, pilot contamination comes from UEs in OTHER cells
using the same pilot. With pilot reuse factor 1, UE k in cell l
contaminates UE k in all other cells.


2.3  MULTI-CELL MMSE (M-MMSE) COMBINING — EQUATIONS (35)-(36)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The achievable SE of UE k in cell j:

               ⎛    τₚ ⎞
  SE^(c)ⱼₖ = ⎜1 - ──⎟ 𝔼{log₂(1 + SINR^(c)ⱼₖ)}                ... (35)
               ⎝    τc ⎠

Maximized by M-MMSE combining:

                            ⎛ Lc  Kc                      Lc Kc           ⎞⁻¹
  SINR^(c)ⱼₖ = pⱼₖ ĥⱼⱼₖᴴ  ⎜ ∑   ∑ pₗᵢĥₗⱼᵢĥₗⱼᵢᴴ + ∑  ∑ pₗᵢCₗⱼᵢ + σ²I⎟  ĥⱼⱼₖ
                            ⎝l=1 i=1                     l=1 i=1          ⎠
                             (l,i)≠(j,k)                                  ... (36)

This is the CELLULAR counterpart of Eq. (14) for Cell-free Level 4.

WHY M-MMSE (not just MMSE)?

  Standard single-cell MMSE at BS j would only suppress interference from
  UEs WITHIN cell j. M-MMSE also accounts for interference from UEs in
  OTHER cells. Specifically:

  • Single-cell MMSE: The matrix includes only {ĥⱼⱼᵢ : i = 1,...,Kc}
    (estimates of UEs in cell j's own channels to BS j)

  • M-MMSE: The matrix includes {ĥₗⱼᵢ : l = 1,...,Lc, i = 1,...,Kc}
    (estimates of ALL UEs in ALL cells as seen by BS j)

  M-MMSE requires BS j to estimate channels from UEs in OTHER cells —
  which it CAN do from pilot observations (since pilots are reused).

  M-MMSE is the strongest linear combiner for cellular mMIMO. By using
  it, the paper ensures any cell-free advantage is genuine.

  Reference: Björnson, Hoydis, Sanguinetti (2018) [9] proved M-MMSE
  achieves unlimited capacity as Mc → ∞ with spatially correlated channels.

2.5  THE 3GPP URBAN MICROCELL PROPAGATION MODEL — EQUATIONS (37)-(38)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  βₖₗ [dB] = -30.5 - 36.7 log₁₀(dₖₗ / 1m) + Fₖₗ                ... (37)

Where:
  • dₖₗ = distance between UE k and AP l (3D, including 10m height)
  • Fₖₗ ~ 𝒩(0, 4²) = shadow fading with 4 dB standard deviation
  • The model is from 3GPP TR 36.814, Table B.1.2.1-1

UNDERSTANDING THE PATH LOSS FORMULA:

  The term -36.7 log₁₀(dₖₗ) corresponds to a path loss exponent of:

    n = 36.7/10 = 3.67

  Physical meaning of different path loss exponents:

    n = 2.0: Free-space propagation (no obstructions)
    n = 2.5: Suburban environments with light clutter
    n = 3.0: Urban environments with moderate obstructions
    n = 3.67: Dense urban microcell (this paper's model)
    n = 4.0: Dense urban / indoor (COST-Hata for d > 50m)

  The value 3.67 is appropriate for micro-cell APs deployed at ~10m
  height on lamp posts in urban environments.

  The intercept -30.5 dB accounts for:
    • Antenna gains
    • Carrier frequency effects (2 GHz)
    • Constant propagation factors

SHADOW FADING:

  Fₖₗ ~ 𝒩(0, 16) represents log-normal shadow fading with σ = 4 dB.

  Shadow fading models large-scale random variations due to buildings,
  terrain, and other obstacles. The 4 dB standard deviation is typical
  for urban micro-cells (lower than macro-cells which have 7-10 dB).

  Shadowing correlation between UEs:

    𝔼{Fₖₗ Fᵢⱼ} = ⎧ 4² × 2^(-δₖᵢ / 9m)   if l = j (same AP)
                   ⎨                                              ... (38)
                   ⎩ 0                      if l ≠ j (diff. APs)

  Where δₖᵢ is the distance between UE k and UE i.

  Interpretation:
    • Two UEs close together (δₖᵢ ≈ 0) see nearly the same shadowing
      from the same AP (correlation ≈ 1)
    • UEs 9m apart have correlation 2⁻¹ = 0.5
    • UEs 30m apart have correlation 2⁻³·³ ≈ 0.1 (nearly independent)
    • Shadowing from DIFFERENT APs is independent (l ≠ j):
      justified because APs are ≥50m apart, and 2⁻⁵⁰/⁹ ≈ 0.02 ≈ 0

DISTANCE COMPUTATION:

  The distance dₖₗ includes the 10m height difference:

    dₖₗ = √(d²_horizontal + 10²)

  And is computed as the MINIMUM over wrap-around cases (to simulate
  an infinite network without edge effects).


2.6  SPATIAL CORRELATION MODEL
───────────────────────────────

Both cellular BSs and multi-antenna APs use:
  • Half-wavelength-spaced Uniform Linear Array (ULA)
  • Gaussian local scattering model with σ_φ = 15° angular spread
  • Model from [3, Sec. 2.6]

For single-antenna APs (N = 1): Rₖₗ = βₖₗ (scalar, no spatial structure)


2.7  REMARK 4: WHY THE PREVIOUS PROPAGATION MODEL WAS WRONG
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The COST-Hata model used in [16] (Eq. 39):

              ⎧ -81.2                                    dₖₗ < 10m
  βₖₗ [dB] = ⎨ -61.2 - 20 log₁₀(dₖₗ/1m)       10m ≤ dₖₗ < 50m
              ⎩ -35.7 - 35 log₁₀(dₖₗ/1m) + Fₖₗ        dₖₗ ≥ 50m  ... (39)

PROBLEMS with this model for cell-free mMIMO:

  1. DESIGNED FOR MACRO-CELLS: The COST 231 / Hata model was developed
     for BSs at 30m+ height, with UEs at least 1 km away. The model
     creators themselves stated: "must not be used for micro-cells"
     [51, Ch. 4].

  2. NO SHADOWING FOR d < 50m: In cell-free mMIMO, many UEs are within
     50m of an AP. The absence of shadowing in this range creates
     unrealistically stable channels and inflates performance.

  3. FLAT PATH LOSS FOR d < 10m: The model gives -81.2 dB for ANY
     distance less than 10m. This means a UE 1m from an AP has the same
     path loss as one 10m away — physically unreasonable.

  4. SHADOWING DECORRELATION: The decorrelation distance in [16] is 100m,
     which is 10× larger than the 3GPP model's 9m. This means shadowing
     varies much more slowly, making the comparison less realistic.

  5. IMPACT ON RESULTS: The favorable near-field model artificially
     boosts cell-free performance because APs close to UEs get
     unrealistically good channels. This contributed to the overly
     optimistic conclusions in [16].

This is a critical methodological point. By switching to the 3GPP model,
this paper provides a MUCH more realistic evaluation of cell-free mMIMO.

  Same total antennas (400) but distributed differently.

  Key differences from Fig. 2(a):

  (a) Level 4 performance DECREASES:
      With only 100 APs, the average distance from a UE to its nearest AP
      increases. Less macro diversity = lower SE.
      This shows that MORE APs (even with fewer antennas each) is better
      for Level 4.

  (b) Level 1 performance INCREASES:
      With N = 4 antennas, each AP can now do spatial interference
      suppression locally. The L-MMSE combiner at each AP inverts a 4×4
      matrix and can null up to 3 interferers.
      Level 1 is now comparable to Level 2 for weak UEs and MUCH better
      for strong UEs.

  DESIGN INSIGHT: The optimal (L, N) tradeoff depends on the level:
    • Level 4 prefers many single-antenna APs (maximizes macro diversity)
    • Level 1 prefers fewer multi-antenna APs (enables local interference
      suppression)
    • Levels 2/3 fall in between

THIS IS THE PAPER'S MOST SHOCKING RESULT:

  ┌────────────────────┬─────────────┬───────────────┐
  │ Scheme             │ 95%-likely  │ Median SE     │
  │                    │ SE (bit/s/Hz│ (bit/s/Hz)    │
  ├────────────────────┼─────────────┼───────────────┤
  │ L4 (MR)            │ ~0.5        │ ~2.0          │
  │ L3 (MR+LSFD)      │ ~0.4        │ ~1.5          │
  │ L2 (MR+avg)        │ ~0.2        │ ~0.8          │
  │ L1 (MR, small)     │ ~0.5        │ ~2.5          │
  │ Cellular M-MMSE    │ ~1.0        │ ~4.0          │
  └────────────────────┴─────────────┴───────────────┘

  DEVASTATING observations:

  (a) Level 2 with MR (the scheme from [16]) is outperformed by BOTH
      small cells (Level 1) AND Cellular mMIMO FOR EVERY SINGLE UE.

      This means: the original Cell-free mMIMO proposal LOSES to both
      of its competitors. The proclaimed advantage was an illusion created
      by the unfair comparison in [16].

  (b) Level 3 with MR (the LSFD scheme from [24]) also loses to Cellular
      mMIMO. LSFD cannot compensate for MR's fundamental weakness.

  (c) Even Level 4 with MR is worse than or comparable to Cellular mMIMO.
      Having access to ALL signals at the CPU doesn't help if you process
      them with MR — the interference overwhelms the desired signal.

  (d) Level 1 with MR actually outperforms Level 2 and Level 3 with MR!
      This is because at Level 1, each AP only serves one UE, so MR's
      inability to suppress interference doesn't matter as much (there's
      only local interference, not network-wide).

  WHY MR FAILS SO BADLY:

  With N = 1, the L-MMSE combiner at AP l is:
    v_LMMSE = ĥₖₗ / (∑ᵢ pᵢ|ĥᵢₗ|² + ∑ᵢ pᵢCᵢₗ + σ²)

  The MR combiner is: v_MR = ĥₖₗ*  (just the conjugate)

  They differ by: 1 / (∑ᵢ pᵢ|ĥᵢₗ|² + ∑ᵢ pᵢCᵢₗ + σ²)

  This is a NON-DETERMINISTIC scalar (depends on instantaneous |ĥᵢₗ|²).

  MR gives EQUAL WEIGHT to the local estimate from every AP, regardless
  of the local interference level. An AP with massive interference
  contributes just as much as an AP with no interference.

  L-MMSE automatically DOWN-WEIGHTS estimates from high-interference APs.
  This per-realization adaptation is the critical difference.

  LSFD (Level 3) cannot fix this because it uses DETERMINISTIC weights
  based on channel STATISTICS. The per-realization interference variations
  are invisible to LSFD.

2.10  REPRODUCING AND CORRECTING [16]'S RESULTS — FIGURE 4
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This subsection is a masterful piece of scientific detective work. The
authors reproduce the exact setup from [16] and systematically show why
[16]'s conclusions were misleading.

Setup (matching [16]):
  • L = 100, N = 1, K = 40, τₚ = 20
  • COST-Hata propagation model (Eq. 39)
  • Pilot assignment: greedy algorithm from [16, Sec. IV.A]

The "thick lines" in Fig. 4 are the ORIGINAL curves from [16]:
  • Level 2 with MR ("Ref. [15] (L2 MR)" in paper's notation)
  • Small cells ("Ref. [15] (Small cells)" — with [16]'s suboptimal
    AP assignment)

FIGURE 4(a): FULL POWER TRANSMISSION

  The paper adds three new curves to [16]'s original two:

  1. "Ref. [15] (Improved)": Same as [16]'s small cells, but with the
     improved AP selection (max-SE instead of max-β, multiple UEs per AP).

     Result: The gap between Cell-free and small cells SHRINKS
     significantly. About 40% of UEs prefer a different AP than the one
     [16] assigned them to.

  2. "L1 (Small cells)": Using the corrected SE expression from
     Proposition 3 (which properly accounts for pilot contamination
     conditioning) AND the improved AP assignment.

     Result: ALL UEs get HIGHER SE with small cells than with
     Level 2 MR! This completely reverses [16]'s conclusion.

  3. "L2 (L-MMSE)": Level 2 with L-MMSE combining instead of MR.

     Result: This curve is to the RIGHT of everything else — uniformly
     better SE for ALL UEs than both Level 2 MR and small cells.

  The progression tells a clear story:
    [16]'s Cell-free > [16]'s small cells  (original claim)
    → BUT: improved small cells > [16]'s Cell-free  (after fixing AP selection)
    → AND: L2 (L-MMSE) > improved small cells  (after fixing combining)

  The REAL conclusion: Cell-free mMIMO IS better than small cells, but
  ONLY if you use L-MMSE (or better) combining. With MR, the advantage
  disappears.


FIGURE 4(b): MAX-MIN POWER CONTROL

  When max-min fairness power control is applied:

  • The lower tails of all CDF curves shift RIGHT (better worst-case SE)
  • Level 2 MR with power control does outperform small cells — consistent
    with [16]'s original claim with power control

  BUT: Level 2 with L-MMSE and FULL POWER (no optimization) gives:
    • Same 95%-likely SE as Level 2 MR with max-min power control
    • 40% higher median SE than Level 2 MR with max-min
    • 3× higher median SE overall

  PROFOUND IMPLICATION: Using L-MMSE combining with simple equal power
  allocation is MORE EFFECTIVE than using MR combining with optimized
  power control.

  In other words: it's better to invest in SMARTER SIGNAL PROCESSING
  than in OPTIMIZED POWER ALLOCATION (when using a weak combiner).

  This has enormous practical implications:
    • Power control requires optimization algorithms (complex, iterative)
    • L-MMSE requires a matrix inversion (one-shot, deterministic)
    • L-MMSE is both simpler to implement AND more effective

3.1  3GPP PROPAGATION MODELS
──────────────────────────────

The 3GPP has standardized propagation models for system-level simulations:

  • 3GPP TR 36.814 (Release 9, 2017) [50]: "Further advancements for
    E-UTRA physical layer aspects" — contains the Urban Microcell (UMi)
    and Urban Macrocell (UMa) models used for LTE-Advanced evaluation.

  • The UMi model targets BSs at 10m height, inter-site distances of
    200m, and carrier frequencies of 2-6 GHz. This closely matches
    the anticipated cell-free mMIMO deployment.

  • The key parameters:
    - Path loss exponent: 3.67 (moderate urban propagation)
    - Shadow fading: 4 dB std (lower than macro-cell's 7-10 dB)
    - Decorrelation distance: 9m (short — typical for dense urban)


3.2  THE COST 231 / HATA MODEL
───────────────────────────────

  • COST Action 231 (1999) [51]: "Digital Mobile Radio Towards Future
    Generation Systems: Final Report" — developed path loss models for
    GSM/2G/3G evaluation.

  • Based on Hata's empirical model (1980) which itself was based on
    Okumura's measurements (1968) in Tokyo.

  • Designed for:
    - BS height: 30-200m (macro-cell towers)
    - UE distance: 1-20 km
    - Carrier frequency: 150-2000 MHz

  • The model creators' warning in [51, Ch. 4]: "must not be used for
    micro-cells" — yet it was adopted by [16] and became the de facto
    standard in the cell-free mMIMO literature until this paper.


3.3  CDF ANALYSIS IN WIRELESS RESEARCH
────────────────────────────────────────

The CDF of per-user SE is the standard performance metric in Massive MIMO:

  F(x) = Pr(SEₖ ≤ x)

Computed over random UE locations, channel realizations, and shadow fading.

Key percentile points:

  • 95%-likely SE (5th percentile): F(x) = 0.05
    The rate exceeded by 95% of all UEs. Represents the WORST-CASE
    performance guarantee. This is the most important metric for
    cell-free mMIMO, which aims to eliminate "dead spots."

  • 90%-likely SE (10th percentile): F(x) = 0.10
    Slightly less conservative worst-case metric.

  • Median SE (50th percentile): F(x) = 0.50
    The "typical" user experience. Important for overall throughput.

  • 5%-likely SE (95th percentile): F(x) = 0.95
    The rate achieved only by the BEST 5% of UEs. Shows peak performance.

The SHAPE of the CDF tells you about fairness:
  • Steep CDF = all UEs get similar rates = high fairness
  • Flat CDF = large variation between best and worst UEs = low fairness

Cell-free mMIMO aims for a STEEPER CDF than cellular (more uniform service).


3.4  MAX-MIN FAIRNESS
──────────────────────

Max-min fairness power control solves:

    max  min  SEₖ(p₁, ..., pₖ)
    {pₖ}  k

Subject to power constraints: 0 ≤ pₖ ≤ p_max

This is typically solved by bisection + second-order cone programming
(SOCP) or by geometric programming, as described in [16].

Effect on CDF: The CDF becomes nearly VERTICAL — all UEs get approximately
the same SE. The lower tail improves (higher guaranteed rate) but the
upper tail worsens (reduced peak rates).

This paper's argument: L-MMSE processing is a BETTER way to help weak UEs
than max-min power control. Power control with MR merely redistributes a
limited resource. L-MMSE INCREASES the total available resource by better
interference management.

4.1  PATH LOSS COMPUTATION EXAMPLE
────────────────────────────────────

Using the 3GPP UMi model (Eq. 37):

  βₖₗ [dB] = -30.5 - 36.7 log₁₀(dₖₗ) + Fₖₗ

For a UE at horizontal distance 50m from an AP at 10m height:

  dₖₗ = √(50² + 10²) = √2600 = 50.99m

  βₖₗ [dB] = -30.5 - 36.7 × log₁₀(50.99) + Fₖₗ
            = -30.5 - 36.7 × 1.7076 + Fₖₗ
            = -30.5 - 62.67 + Fₖₗ
            = -93.17 + Fₖₗ  [dB]

  Without shadowing: βₖₗ = -93.17 dB = 10⁻⁹·³¹⁷ ≈ 4.82 × 10⁻¹⁰

  SNR at this AP (p = 100mW = 20dBm, σ² = -96dBm):
    SNR = p × βₖₗ / σ² = 20 - 93.17 - (-96) = 22.83 dB ≈ 192

Compare with the COST-Hata model for the same distance:
  β [dB] = -35.7 - 35 log₁₀(50.99) + Fₖₗ
          = -35.7 - 59.77 + Fₖₗ = -95.47 + Fₖₗ [dB]

  Difference: 2.3 dB weaker with COST-Hata at 50m, BUT for d < 50m:
  COST-Hata gives -61.2-20log₁₀(d), which at d = 20m gives:
    β = -61.2 - 20×1.301 = -87.22 dB

  3GPP UMi at d = 20m (+ 10m height → d₃D = 22.36m):
    β = -30.5 - 36.7×1.3495 = -30.5 - 49.53 = -80.03 dB

  COST-Hata is 7 dB weaker at 20m! The 3GPP model predicts a STRONGER
  near-field channel, but with 4 dB shadow fading that adds variability.


4.2  M-MMSE vs CELL-FREE MMSE: STRUCTURAL COMPARISON
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Cellular M-MMSE (Eq. 36) at BS j for UE k in cell j:

  SINR = pⱼₖ ĥⱼⱼₖᴴ (Σⱼ - pⱼₖĥⱼⱼₖĥⱼⱼₖᴴ)⁻¹ ĥⱼⱼₖ

  Where Σⱼ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸᶜ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷᶜ pₗᵢ(ĥₗⱼᵢĥₗⱼᵢᴴ + Cₗⱼᵢ) + σ²I_{Mc}

  Matrix size: Mc × Mc = 100 × 100

Cell-free MMSE (Eq. 14) at the CPU for UE k:

  SINR = pₖ ĥₖᴴ (Σ - pₖĥₖĥₖᴴ)⁻¹ ĥₖ

  Where Σ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ pᵢ(ĥᵢĥᵢᴴ + Cᵢ) + σ²I_{LN}

  Matrix size: LN × LN = 400 × 400

The structural difference:
  • Cellular: BS j only sees channels TO ITSELF. Its 100 antennas provide
    angular resolution to separate UEs arriving from different directions.
  • Cell-free: CPU sees channels to ALL APs. The spatial diversity comes
    from the GEOGRAPHIC spread of APs, not from angular resolution.

  Cell-free's advantage: A UE at a cell edge in cellular is far from its
  BS. In cell-free, it's always near SOME AP. The "cell-edge" problem
  is eliminated.

  Cell-free's challenge: Each AP has few antennas (N = 1 or 4), so local
  angular resolution is poor. The power comes from combining across APs.

EXAMPLE 1: Path Loss Comparison (3GPP UMi vs COST-Hata)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Compute the path loss at distances 10m, 50m, 200m, 500m for both models.
(Ignoring height difference for simplicity, no shadowing)

3GPP UMi: β[dB] = -30.5 - 36.7 log₁₀(d)

  d = 10m:  β = -30.5 - 36.7×1.0 = -67.2 dB
  d = 50m:  β = -30.5 - 36.7×1.699 = -92.8 dB
  d = 200m: β = -30.5 - 36.7×2.301 = -114.9 dB
  d = 500m: β = -30.5 - 36.7×2.699 = -129.6 dB

COST-Hata (Eq. 39):

  d = 10m:  β = -81.2 dB (flat region)
  d = 50m:  β = -35.7 - 35×1.699 = -95.2 dB
  d = 200m: β = -35.7 - 35×2.301 = -116.2 dB
  d = 500m: β = -35.7 - 35×2.699 = -130.2 dB

  ┌────────┬────────────┬────────────┬────────────┐
  │ d (m)  │ 3GPP UMi   │ COST-Hata  │ Difference │
  ├────────┼────────────┼────────────┼────────────┤
  │ 10     │ -67.2 dB   │ -81.2 dB   │ +14.0 dB   │
  │ 50     │ -92.8 dB   │ -95.2 dB   │ +2.4 dB    │
  │ 200    │ -114.9 dB  │ -116.2 dB  │ +1.3 dB    │
  │ 500    │ -129.6 dB  │ -130.2 dB  │ +0.6 dB    │
  └────────┴────────────┴────────────┴────────────┘

  At short distances (d < 50m), the models differ dramatically:
    • At 10m: 14 dB difference! 3GPP gives 25× more power.
    • COST-Hata's flat -81.2 dB for d < 10m is especially problematic.

  Since cell-free mMIMO has many nearby APs, the near-field model
  matters enormously. The 3GPP model gives MORE power at short range
  (good), but ALSO adds 4dB shadow fading (adds uncertainty). The
  COST-Hata model's artificial suppression of near-field effects
  distorted earlier comparisons.


EXAMPLE 2: SNR at Different Network Points
────────────────────────────────────────────

Setup: p = 100 mW = 20 dBm, σ² = -96 dBm, 3GPP UMi model

Cell-free mMIMO (nearest AP typically at ~25-50m):
  At 25m: β = -30.5 - 36.7×log₁₀(26.9) = -30.5 - 52.5 = -83.0 dB
          (including 10m height: d₃D = √(625+100) = 26.9m)
  SNR = 20 - (-83.0) - (-96) = 20 + 83 + 96 = -96 + 103 = 7 dB ??

  Wait, let me recalculate:
  SNR [dB] = p[dBm] + β[dB] - σ²[dBm]
           = 20 + (-83.0) - (-96) = 20 - 83 + 96 = 33 dB

  So SNR ≈ 33 dB at the nearest AP — excellent!

Cellular mMIMO (BS is ~200-350m away for a cell-edge UE in a 500×500m cell):
  At 300m: β = -30.5 - 36.7×log₁₀(300.2) = -30.5 - 90.9 = -121.4 dB
  SNR per antenna = 20 + (-121.4) - (-96) = 20 - 121.4 + 96 = -5.4 dB

  But with 100 antennas and MR combining, the beamforming gain is ~20 dB:
  Effective SNR ≈ -5.4 + 20 = 14.6 dB

Cell-free advantage for cell-edge UEs:
  The nearest AP gives 33 dB SNR (before combining across APs).
  The cellular BS gives only 14.6 dB effective SNR.
  That's an ~18 dB advantage for cell-free — almost 100× more signal power!


EXAMPLE 3: Understanding the CDF Crossing Phenomenon
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Why do Level 1/Cellular curves cross Level 2/3 curves?

Consider two UEs in a cell-free network with L = 400, N = 1:

  UE A (weak UE): Located in a "gap" between APs. Nearest AP is 75m away.
    • Level 1: SE ≈ 1.5 bit/s/Hz (served by ONE nearby AP)
    • Level 4: SE ≈ 4.0 bit/s/Hz (all 400 APs contribute)
    • Level 2: SE ≈ 2.0 bit/s/Hz (average of all APs — many contribute 0)
    • Cellular: SE ≈ 1.0 bit/s/Hz (far from BS, cell-edge)

    Ranking: L4 > L2 > L1 > Cellular

  UE B (strong UE): Right next to AP #7, which has a great channel.
    • Level 1: SE ≈ 6.0 bit/s/Hz (AP #7 decodes independently, high SNR)
    • Level 4: SE ≈ 8.0 bit/s/Hz (still the best)
    • Level 2: SE ≈ 3.5 bit/s/Hz (averaging DILUTES the strong AP's info
                with noise from far APs)
    • Cellular: SE ≈ 5.0 bit/s/Hz (close to BS, array gain compensates)

    Ranking: L4 > L1 > Cellular > L2

  For UE B, Level 2's simple averaging HURTS — it mixes a great local
  estimate with 399 noisy estimates. Level 1 and Cellular, which use
  concentrated processing, do better for strong UEs.

  This is why the CDF curves cross: Level 2/3 help weak UEs but hurt
  strong UEs compared to Level 1/Cellular.

  Level 4 avoids this problem because MMSE combining OPTIMALLY weights
  each AP's contribution — far APs contribute near-zero weight naturally.

PROBLEM 1: Path Loss and SNR
─────────────────────────────
A cell-free mMIMO system uses the 3GPP UMi model. An AP is at height 10m.
A UE is at horizontal distance 80m.

(a) Compute the 3D distance dₖₗ.
(b) Compute the path loss βₖₗ in dB (without shadowing).
(c) Compute the received SNR if p = 100 mW and σ² = -96 dBm.
(d) If shadow fading Fₖₗ = +6 dB, what is the new path loss?
(e) If Fₖₗ = -6 dB?

Solution:

(a) dₖₗ = √(80² + 10²) = √6500 = 80.62m

(b) β = -30.5 - 36.7×log₁₀(80.62) = -30.5 - 36.7×1.9064 = -30.5 - 69.96
    β = -100.46 dB

(c) SNR = p[dBm] + β[dB] - σ²[dBm] = 20 + (-100.46) - (-96)
        = 20 - 100.46 + 96 = 15.54 dB ≈ 35.9 (linear)

(d) β = -100.46 + 6 = -94.46 dB → SNR = 21.54 dB (good shadowing helps)

(e) β = -100.46 - 6 = -106.46 dB → SNR = 9.54 dB (bad shadowing hurts)

  The 4 dB std shadow fading creates a ±6 dB range at 1.5σ, which
  corresponds to a 4× variation in received power. This is why having
  many distributed APs helps — even if one AP has bad shadowing, a
  nearby AP likely has better conditions.


PROBLEM 2: Why L-MMSE > Max-Min Power Control + MR
───────────────────────────────────────────────────
Consider a simple 2-UE, 2-AP system with N = 1.

UE 1: β₁₁ = 0.5, β₁₂ = 0.1   (close to AP 1)
UE 2: β₂₁ = 0.1, β₂₂ = 0.5   (close to AP 2)

No pilot contamination (unique pilots), σ² negligible.

(a) With MR and equal power (p₁ = p₂ = 1), which UE has lower SE?
(b) How does max-min power control adjust powers?
(c) With L-MMSE and equal power, how does the SE compare?

Solution:

(a) Both UEs are in symmetric positions, so they have the same SE
    with MR and equal power. But if there's any asymmetry (e.g., one
    UE is slightly closer to both APs), that UE gets higher SE.

    The problem with MR: at AP 1, MR for UE 1 gives v₁₁ = ĥ₁₁*.
    But UE 2 also contributes interference: ĥ₁₁* h₂₁ s₂.
    MR does nothing to suppress this.

(b) Max-min power control adjusts p₁ and p₂ to equalize SE.
    In the symmetric case, p₁ = p₂ already achieves this.
    In asymmetric cases, the stronger UE reduces power, which
    LOWERS total throughput but RAISES the minimum SE.

(c) L-MMSE at AP 1:
    v₁₁ = ĥ₁₁ / (|ĥ₁₁|² + |ĥ₂₁|² + σ²)

    The denominator is large when |ĥ₂₁|² is large (strong interference
    from UE 2). This automatically reduces the contribution from AP 1
    when interference is high.

    This provides per-realization interference management that MR + power
    control cannot achieve. The SE improvement comes "for free" — no
    iterative optimization needed.


PROBLEM 3: Interpreting CDF Curves
───────────────────────────────────
A CDF plot shows that at x = 2 bit/s/Hz:
  Level 4: F(2) = 0.05
  Level 2: F(2) = 0.30
  Cellular: F(2) = 0.15

(a) What fraction of UEs get less than 2 bit/s/Hz with each scheme?
(b) Which scheme has the best 95%-likely SE if the 95%-likely point is at
    F(x) = 0.05?
(c) If "fairness" means minimizing the gap between the 5th and 95th
    percentile users, which scheme is likely the fairest?

Solution:

(a) Level 4: 5% of UEs get < 2 bit/s/Hz
    Level 2: 30% of UEs get < 2 bit/s/Hz
    Cellular: 15% of UEs get < 2 bit/s/Hz

(b) Level 4 has the best 95%-likely SE. At F = 0.05, Level 4 is at
    x = 2 bit/s/Hz. The other schemes would have their 5th percentile
    at lower x values (since their CDF reaches 0.05 earlier).

(c) Level 2 is likely the fairest — its CDF curve is typically steeper
    (more vertical), meaning less variation between the best and worst
    UEs. However, this "fairness" comes at the cost of lower SE for
    ALL UEs compared to Level 4.

    Level 4 achieves both HIGH SE and GOOD FAIRNESS — the best of both.

2. WITH MMSE/L-MMSE (Fig. 2):
   • Level 4 dominates EVERYTHING for every single UE
   • Level 1 (small cells) with L-MMSE beats Cellular at 95%-likely point
   • More APs (L=400, N=1) is better for Level 4 (macro diversity)
   • More antennas per AP (L=100, N=4) is better for Level 1 (local

   • Even Level 4 with MR doesn't beat Cellular mMIMO
   • LSFD (Level 3) cannot compensate for MR's weakness

   • Suboptimal AP assignment in small cells
   • Inappropriate COST-Hata propagation model
   • Only MR combining was considered
   When these issues are fixed, L2 (L-MMSE) with full power outperforms
   L2 (MR) with max-min power control.

5. KEY INSIGHT: L-MMSE with equal power > MR with optimized power control.
   Smart signal processing beats smart power allocation. This has
   transformative practical implications for cell-free system design.

   cells) gives substantially different results than the COST-Hata model
   (designed for macro-cells). The cell-free mMIMO community should adopt
   the 3GPP model as the standard baseline.
