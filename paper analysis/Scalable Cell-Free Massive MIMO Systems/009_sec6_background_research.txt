1. SIMULATION SETUP AND METHODOLOGY
====================================

The evaluation uses a 2x2 km square area with wrap-around, a standard
technique in cellular and cell-free simulations to approximate an infinitely
large network and eliminate boundary effects [5], [7].  Without wrap-around,
UEs at the edge of the simulated area would see fewer APs, creating an
artificial performance imbalance unrelated to the protocol under test.

Two deployment configurations share the same total antenna budget (M=400):

  (i)  L=400 APs, N=1 antenna each  -- dense single-antenna deployment
  (ii) L=100 APs, N=4 antennas each -- sparser multi-antenna deployment

Both yield 100 antennas/km^2 alongside K=100 UEs (25 UEs/km^2).  The
propagation model follows [2, Sec. 4.1.3], which includes three-slope
pathloss, log-normal shadow fading with spatial correlation, and spatially
correlated Rayleigh fading generated from local scattering around each AP.
APs are elevated 10 m above UEs, establishing a natural minimum distance
that prevents singularities in the pathloss model.

Key parameters: coherence block tau_c=200 symbols, pilot length tau_p=10,
UL transmit power p_k=100 mW, DL AP power rho=1 W, bandwidth 20 MHz.
For UL evaluation tau_u=190 data symbols are used; for DL tau_d=190.
Results are averaged over 25 Monte Carlo network layouts with 1000 channel
realizations each, producing statistically reliable CDFs.


2. CDF ANALYSIS IN WIRELESS SYSTEM EVALUATION
==============================================

The cumulative distribution function (CDF) of per-UE spectral efficiency
(SE) is the standard tool for comparing schemes in cell-free massive MIMO
[5], [7].  Each realization of UE and AP positions produces one SE value per
UE; plotting the empirical CDF over all UEs and layouts reveals the full
performance distribution.

Two summary statistics are extracted from each CDF:

  -- 95%-likely SE (5th percentile): the SE exceeded by 95% of UEs.  This
     metric captures the experience of the weakest users and is the primary
     fairness indicator.  Cell-free massive MIMO was proposed precisely to
     improve this metric relative to cellular networks [5].

  -- Average (median) SE: represents typical system throughput.  A scheme
     may have a high median but a poor 5th percentile if it favors strong
     UEs at the expense of weak ones.

When reading CDF plots: a rightward shift of the entire curve signals
uniformly better performance; a steeper CDF indicates more uniform service
across UEs; a curve with a long left tail reveals poor fairness with some
UEs experiencing very low SE.


3. UPLINK RESULTS (FIG. 5)
===========================

Section VI-A compares two scalable schemes (P-MMSE centralized, LP-MMSE
distributed) against three non-scalable "(All)" benchmarks where every AP
serves every UE: optimal MMSE combining from [7],[30], distributed L-MMSE
from [7], and conventional MR from the original cell-free paper [5].

Key quantitative findings:

  (a) Scalable P-MMSE achieves 89% of the average SE of optimal MMSE (All).
      The 11% loss arises from two sources: limiting the number of APs per
      UE (dynamic cooperation clusters) and using the scalable, suboptimal
      cluster formation algorithm from Section V-A.  The loss is small
      because the few nearest APs capture the vast majority of received
      power for each UE, and these are precisely the APs selected by the
      clustering algorithm.

  (b) Distributed LP-MMSE average SE is 2.7x higher than MR.  This is a
      central result: MMSE-type processing is essential even in distributed
      cell-free operation, contradicting the early literature [5] that
      advocated MR for its simplicity.

  (c) LP-MMSE vs L-MMSE (All): negligible performance gap.  The scalable
      LP-MMSE, which only uses local CSI at each AP, matches the unscalable
      L-MMSE that requires global pilot-sharing information.  This validates
      the scalability framework.

  (d) Comparing L=400,N=1 with L=100,N=4: single-antenna APs are preferable
      overall.  UEs with the lowest SEs benefit the most from having many
      distributed APs (greater macro-diversity), while the strongest UEs
      achieve roughly the same SE with fewer multi-antenna APs thanks to
      more capable local interference suppression at each AP.


4. DOWNLINK RESULTS (FIG. 6)
==============================

Section VI-B focuses on two phenomena: interference-limited operation and
tightness of the capacity lower bounds (the hardening bound from
Proposition 3).

Key quantitative findings:

  (a) Interference-limited regime.  The distributed precoding schemes (MR
      and LP-MMSE) collectively use 40x more total transmit power than the
      centralized P-MMSE scheme, yet still achieve lower SE.  The reason:
      the cell-free DL is interference-limited, so raw power is far less
      valuable than spatial interference mitigation enabled by centralized
      precoding.  Adding more power simply raises both signal and
      interference proportionally.

  (b) Centralized P-MMSE substantially outperforms distributed LP-MMSE,
      particularly for L=400,N=1 (Fig. 6a).  The gap shrinks with N=4
      (Fig. 6b) because each multi-antenna AP can locally suppress
      interference toward unintended UEs, partially compensating for the
      lack of centralized coordination.

  (c) Among distributed schemes, LP-MMSE outperforms MR for 95% of UEs.
      The remaining 5% (the weakest UEs) see comparable SE under both
      schemes.

  (d) Hardening bound tightness, verified via a genie-aided case where UEs
      have perfect CSI:
        -- P-MMSE:  98% of genie-aided SE (excellent hardening)
        -- LP-MMSE: 90% of genie-aided SE (good hardening)
        -- MR:      60% of genie-aided SE (poor hardening)

      The 60% figure for MR confirms the limited channel hardening observed
      by Chen and Bjornson [47] and motivates using LP-MMSE precoding, for
      which the hardening-based capacity bound is tight, instead of
      resorting to costly DL pilot transmission.


5. MANY SINGLE-ANTENNA APs vs FEWER MULTI-ANTENNA APs
=======================================================

The comparison of L=400,N=1 versus L=100,N=4 (same total M=400 antennas)
reveals a fundamental tradeoff in cell-free deployment:

  -- Macro-diversity advantage (favors many APs, N=1):  With 400 APs the
     average distance between any UE and its nearest AP is much smaller.
     Weak UEs -- those in coverage holes or far from any AP cluster --
     benefit enormously because at least a few APs are always nearby.
     This drives up the 95%-likely (5th percentile) SE.

  -- Local interference suppression advantage (favors fewer APs, N=4):
     Multi-antenna APs can form local spatial nulls, suppressing
     interference to/from nearby UEs without centralized coordination.
     Strong UEs, already close to an AP, benefit from this cleaner
     interference environment.  The DL performance gap between centralized
     and distributed processing also shrinks with N=4.

  -- General recommendation: For deployments prioritizing uniform coverage
     and fairness (the defining promise of cell-free massive MIMO), many
     distributed single-antenna APs are preferable.  When the deployment
     must also support high peak rates for well-positioned UEs, or when
     centralized processing is unavailable, fewer multi-antenna APs offer
     a practical compromise by enabling local interference management.

This tradeoff is consistent with the broader massive MIMO literature:
Bjornson et al. [2] showed that distributing antennas improves the weakest
users' rates via macro-diversity, while co-located arrays excel at spatial
multiplexing for users already in favorable positions.
