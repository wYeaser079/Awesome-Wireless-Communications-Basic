1. THE ORIGINAL CELL-FREE MASSIVE MIMO SYSTEM MODEL
=======================================================

The canonical Cell-Free Massive MIMO system was established by Ngo, Ashikhmin,
Yang, Larsson, and Marzetta in their 2017 paper "Cell-Free Massive MIMO versus
Small Cells" [IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun., vol. 16, no. 3, pp. 1834-1850,
Mar. 2017]. The system model has the following defining characteristics:

(a) Network Architecture: M single-antenna APs and K single-antenna UEs are
    randomly distributed over a coverage area. ALL M APs coherently serve ALL
    K UEs over the same time-frequency resource. A single CPU connects to every
    AP via perfect (infinite-capacity, error-free) backhaul links. There is no
    cell boundary -- hence "cell-free."

(b) Channel Model: The channel between AP m and UE k is g_{mk} = beta_{mk}^{1/2}
    * h_{mk}, where h_{mk} ~ CN(0,1) is i.i.d. small-scale Rayleigh fading
    and beta_{mk} captures large-scale fading (pathloss + shadowing). The i.i.d.
    assumption is justified because APs are spatially distributed with distinct
    scattering environments.

(c) TDD Protocol: Each coherence block is divided into three phases:
    - Uplink pilot training (tau_p channel uses)
    - Downlink data transmission
    - Uplink data transmission
    Channel reciprocity is exploited: UL channel estimates serve DL precoding.

(d) UL Signal Processing: Each AP m locally computes the matched-filter output
    g_hat*_{mk} * y_{u,m} and forwards this scalar to the CPU. The CPU
    aggregates across all M APs:

        r_{u,k} = sum_{m=1}^{M} g_hat*_{mk} * y_{u,m}

    This is network-wide MR (matched filter) combining performed at a single
    CPU. The CPU then decodes q_k from r_{u,k}.

(e) DL Signal Processing: The m-th AP transmits:

        x_m = sqrt(rho_d) * sum_{k=1}^{K} eta_{mk}^{1/2} * g_hat*_{mk} * q_k

    This is conjugate beamforming (= MR precoding). Each AP must know ALL K
    channel estimates and ALL K data symbols. A per-AP power constraint is
    enforced: E{|x_m|^2} <= rho_d.

(f) Closed-form SE Expressions: Ngo et al. derived exact closed-form achievable
    rates for both UL and DL under finite M, K, and imperfect CSI with pilot
    contamination. These are capacity lower bounds based on worst-case
    uncorrelated additive noise (the "use-and-then-forget" bounding technique).

    The DL achievable rate for UE k [Ngo et al., Theorem 1, Eq. (24)] is:

        R^cf_{d,k} = log2(1 + SINR^dl_k)

    where the numerator contains the squared magnitude of the mean beamforming
    gain |sum_m eta_{mk}^{1/2} gamma_{mk}|^2 and the denominator contains
    beamforming gain uncertainty, multi-user interference (with pilot
    contamination terms scaled by |phi^H_{k'} phi_k|^2), and noise.

    The UL rate [Ngo et al., Theorem 2, Eq. (27)] has an analogous structure
    with gamma_{mk} = E{|g_hat_{mk}|^2} = sqrt(tau_p * rho_p) * beta_{mk} * c_{mk}
    capturing the estimation quality.

Key Limitation: Every AP must process signals for ALL K UEs. The computational
complexity per AP scales as O(K), and the fronthaul load per AP also scales
as O(K). This is the root of the scalability problem that Section III formalizes.

Source: [1] H. Q. Ngo, A. Ashikhmin, H. Yang, E. G. Larsson, and T. L. Marzetta,
"Cell-Free Massive MIMO versus Small Cells," IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun.,
vol. 16, no. 3, pp. 1834-1850, Mar. 2017.
Source: [2] arxiv.org/abs/1602.08232


2. SPECTRAL EFFICIENCY BOUNDS IN MASSIVE MIMO
=================================================

Two principal capacity bounding techniques are used in the Massive MIMO and
Cell-Free Massive MIMO literature:

(a) The Use-and-then-Forget (UatF) Bound:

    Origin: The technique was introduced by Marzetta and refined in his 2010
    seminal paper [T. L. Marzetta, "Noncooperative Cellular Wireless with
    Unlimited Numbers of Base Station Antennas," IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun.,
    vol. 9, no. 11, pp. 3590-3600, Nov. 2010] and formalized extensively in
    the textbook by Marzetta, Larsson, Yang, and Ngo [Fundamentals of Massive
    MIMO, Cambridge University Press, 2016].

    Mechanism: After using the channel estimate h_hat to design the combining
    vector v_k, the receiver "forgets" the estimate and treats the combined
    signal as if it were received over a deterministic scalar channel equal to
    the mean effective channel gain E{v_k^H * h_k}. All remaining terms --
    beamforming uncertainty, interference, noise -- are lumped into an
    uncorrelated effective noise whose variance is computed from second-order
    statistics. Since uncorrelated Gaussian noise is the worst-case noise for
    a given variance, this yields a rigorous lower bound on the ergodic
    capacity. Formally, for the UL of UE k:

        SE^ul_k = (tau_u / tau_c) * log2(1 + SINR_k)

    where

        SINR_k = |E{v_k^H h_k}|^2 / (sum_{i=1}^{K} E{|v_k^H h_i|^2}
                  - |E{v_k^H h_k}|^2 + sigma^2 E{||v_k||^2})

    The numerator is the squared mean of the desired channel (deterministic).
    The denominator contains the total variance of the received signal minus
    the deterministic part.

    Name Rationale: The channel estimate is "used" to design v_k, and then
    "forgotten" -- the decoder treats E{v_k^H h_k} as the known channel gain
    and the remainder as worst-case noise.

    Analytical Tractability: The key advantage is that for MR combining
    (v_k = h_hat_k) with i.i.d. Rayleigh fading and MMSE estimation, all
    expectations decompose into products of scalar statistics (gamma_{mk},
    beta_{mk}, c_{mk}), yielding the closed-form expressions in Ngo et al.
    Theorems 1 and 2.

(b) The Hardening Bound (for Downlink):

    In the DL, UEs typically lack CSI (no DL pilots in canonical TDD Massive
    MIMO). The "hardening bound" exploits channel hardening: when M is large,
    the effective channel gain h_k^H w_k concentrates around its mean
    E{h_k^H w_k}. The UE uses this deterministic mean as its effective channel.

    The DL SE for UE k under the hardening bound is:

        SE^dl_k = (tau_d / tau_c) * log2(1 + SINR^dl_k)

    where

        SINR^dl_k = |E{h_k^H w_k}|^2 / (sum_{i=1}^{K} E{|h_k^H w_i|^2}
                     - |E{h_k^H w_k}|^2 + sigma^2)

    This bound is tight when channel hardening holds well (i.e.,
    Var{h_k^H w_k} / |E{h_k^H w_k}|^2 is small), which happens with many
    co-located antennas. In Cell-Free Massive MIMO, each AP has few antennas,
    so channel hardening may be weak and the hardening bound becomes loose.
    This motivated the development of tighter bounds in later work (Theorem
    4.1 vs. Theorem 4.4 in the Bjornson-Hoydis-Sanguinetti monograph).

(c) Relationship to Ergodic Capacity:

    Both bounds are lower bounds on the ergodic capacity C = E{log2(1 + SINR)}.
    They arise from applying the capacity of the worst-case noise channel
    (Gaussian noise with matching second-order statistics). The UatF bound is
    equivalent to Theorem 4.4 in the monograph; the tighter bound based on
    conditional expectations (Theorem 4.1) does not require hardening but
    needs Monte Carlo evaluation. When hardening is strong, Theorems 4.1 and
    4.4 nearly coincide; when hardening is weak (as in distributed CF-mMIMO),
    Theorem 4.1 can be substantially tighter [see Figure 4.11 of the monograph].

Source: [3] E. Bjornson, J. Hoydis, and L. Sanguinetti, "Massive MIMO Networks:
Spectral, Energy, and Hardware Efficiency," Foundations and Trends in Signal
Processing, vol. 11, no. 3-4, pp. 154-655, 2017.
Source: [4] T. L. Marzetta, "Noncooperative Cellular Wireless with Unlimited
Numbers of Base Station Antennas," IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun., vol. 9,
no. 11, pp. 3590-3600, Nov. 2010.
Source: [5] T. L. Marzetta, E. G. Larsson, H. Yang, and H. Q. Ngo, Fundamentals
of Massive MIMO, Cambridge University Press, 2016.


3. MR (MAXIMUM RATIO) COMBINING AND PRECODING
=================================================

(a) Definition and Aliases:
    - UL: MR combining sets v_{kl} = h_hat_{kl} (the MMSE channel estimate).
      Equivalently called "matched filtering" because it maximizes SNR for a
      single-user channel.
    - DL: MR precoding (= conjugate beamforming) sets w_{kl} proportional to
      h_hat*_{kl}. This is the conjugate of the channel estimate, hence
      "conjugate beamforming."
    - Both are the same operation: correlate with the estimated channel.

(b) Why MR Was the Default in Early Cell-Free Papers:
    - MR requires only LOCAL CSI: AP l needs only its own channel estimates
      {h_hat_{kl}}, not the channels at other APs. This avoids CSI sharing
      over backhaul/fronthaul.
    - MR has the lowest computational complexity among linear methods: O(N)
      per UE per AP (a single inner product), versus O(N^2) or O(N^3) for
      MMSE-type methods that require matrix inversions.
    - MR yields closed-form SE expressions under i.i.d. Rayleigh fading +
      MMSE estimation (as in Ngo et al. Theorems 1-2), enabling tractable
      analysis and power control optimization.
    - Historical precedent: MR was the processing scheme in Marzetta's 2010
      paper that launched the Massive MIMO field, establishing it as the
      canonical baseline.

(c) Limitations:
    - MR maximizes signal power but IGNORES interference. In multi-user
      settings, interference can dominate, especially when users share pilots
      (pilot contamination) or when the number of users is comparable to the
      number of antennas.
    - MR does not suppress inter-user interference; its SINR saturates as
      SNR grows (interference-limited regime). MMSE combining, by contrast,
      jointly suppresses interference and noise, achieving near-optimal
      performance.
    - In CF-mMIMO, Bjornson and Sanguinetti [2020, and earlier in Bjornson,
      Sanguinetti, Hoydis, and Debbah, "Making Cell-Free Massive MIMO
      Competitive With MMSE Processing and Centralized Implementation,"
      IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun., 2020] showed that replacing MR with
      local MMSE (L-MMSE) combining at each AP can provide 2-3x SE
      improvement over MR, with only moderately higher complexity.

Source: [6] E. Bjornson, L. Sanguinetti, H. Wymeersch, J. Hoydis, and T. L.
Marzetta, "Massive MIMO is a Reality -- What is Next?" Digital Signal
Processing, vol. 94, pp. 3-20, Nov. 2019.


4. THE CONCEPT OF SCALABILITY IN CELL-FREE MASSIVE MIMO
=========================================================

(a) Scalability in Computer Science vs. Wireless Networks:
    In computer science, a system is scalable if performance does not degrade
    (or degrades gracefully) as the system size grows. More precisely, per-node
    resource consumption (computation, memory, communication) must remain
    bounded as the number of nodes increases. In wireless networks, the
    analogous requirement is that per-AP and per-UE resource consumption
    remains finite as the network size (L APs, K UEs) grows without bound.

(b) Previous Work on Scalability of Cooperative Systems:
    The scalability problem was recognized in the Network MIMO literature.
    Bjornson, Jalden, Bengtsson, and Ottersten [2011, "Optimality Properties,
    Distributed Strategies, and Measurement-Based Evaluation of Coordinated
    Multicell OFDMA Transmission," IEEE Trans. Signal Process.] showed that
    network-wide joint processing requires O(K) complexity per AP. The dynamic
    cooperation clustering (DCC) framework, formalized by Bjornson, Kountouris,
    and Debbah [2013, "Massive MIMO and Small Cells: Improving Energy
    Efficiency by Optimal Soft-Cell Coordination," Proc. ICT] and extensively
    in Bjornson, Jalden, Bengtsson, and Ottersten [2013], proposed that each
    UE is served by only a subset of nearby APs. This limits per-AP complexity
    to O(|D_l|), where |D_l| is the number of UEs associated with AP l --
    a constant independent of K.

(c) The Four Scalability Criteria (Definition 1 in Section III-B):
    Bjornson and Sanguinetti define scalability by letting K -> infinity and
    checking whether each of the following tasks remains implementable with
    FINITE per-AP complexity:

    1) Channel Estimation: AP l must estimate channels only for the UEs it
       serves, not all K UEs. In the original CF-mMIMO, AP l computes
       {h_hat_{kl} : k = 1,...,K} -- complexity O(K).

    2) Signal Processing (combining/precoding): AP l must compute combining
       vectors v_{kl} or precoding vectors w_{kl} only for its served UEs,
       and the transmitted signal sum_{k in D_l} w_{kl} s_k must have a
       bounded number of terms. In original CF-mMIMO, the sum runs over
       all K UEs -- complexity O(K).

    3) Fronthaul Signaling: AP l must exchange data symbols and CSI-related
       information only for |D_l| UEs, not K UEs. In original CF-mMIMO,
       each AP forwards K scalars in the UL and receives K data symbols in
       the DL -- fronthaul load O(K).

    4) Power Control Optimization: The optimization algorithm must have
       per-AP complexity independent of K. Network-wide max-min or
       sum-rate optimization over all K UEs has complexity growing
       polynomially (or worse) in K -- unscalable.

(d) Why O(K) Per-AP Complexity is Unacceptable:
    In a practical deployment, K may range from hundreds to millions of UEs
    across a metropolitan area. If each AP must track, estimate, and
    beamform toward every UE, the per-AP hardware (memory, processing,
    fronthaul capacity) must scale with K -- this makes the network
    economically and technically infeasible for large-scale deployment.
    The DCC solution (Lemma 1 in Section IV-B) proves that if |D_l| is
    constant as K -> infinity, the first three criteria are satisfied. The
    fourth is addressed by using distributed/heuristic power control (e.g.,
    equal power allocation, fractional power control) rather than
    network-wide optimization.

Source: [7] E. Bjornson and L. Sanguinetti, "Scalable Cell-Free Massive MIMO
Systems," IEEE Trans. Commun., vol. 68, no. 7, pp. 4247-4261, Jul. 2020.
Source: [8] E. Bjornson, N. Jalden, M. Bengtsson, and B. Ottersten, "Optimality
Properties, Distributed Strategies, and Measurement-Based Evaluation of
Coordinated Multicell OFDMA Transmission," IEEE Trans. Signal Process.,
vol. 59, no. 12, pp. 6086-6101, Dec. 2011.


5. POWER CONSTRAINTS IN CELL-FREE VS. CELLULAR SYSTEMS
=========================================================

(a) Per-AP Power Constraints:
    In Cell-Free Massive MIMO, each AP has its own power amplifier with a
    finite power budget. The DL power constraint is applied per-AP:

        E{||x_l||^2} <= rho_l,   for l = 1,...,L

    where x_l = sum_{k in D_l} w_{kl} s_k is the transmitted signal from AP l.
    This means the precoding vectors at each AP are coupled: increasing the
    power to one UE forces a reduction for others served by the same AP. In
    the Ngo et al. formulation with MR precoding, this becomes:

        sum_{k=1}^{K} eta_{mk} * gamma_{mk} <= 1,   for all m

    where eta_{mk} is the power control coefficient and gamma_{mk} = E{|g_hat_{mk}|^2}.

(b) Contrast with Cellular Systems:
    In co-located Massive MIMO, a single BS has a total power constraint
    summed across all its antennas: E{||x||^2} <= P_total. This is effectively
    a single constraint. Per-antenna constraints in co-located arrays can be
    relaxed to a sum-power constraint with negligible loss when the array is
    large (due to channel hardening of the total transmit power).

    In Cell-Free systems, there are L separate per-AP constraints. Each is
    strict (short-term, not long-term average) because a single AP with few
    antennas does NOT exhibit sufficient channel hardening to justify a
    long-term relaxation. Miretti and Stanczak [IEEE Trans. Signal Process.,
    2023] formalized this through UL-DL duality under per-AP constraints.

(c) Why DL Power Allocation is More Complex:
    - Multiple coupled constraints: The K x L power allocation variables
      {eta_{mk}} must jointly satisfy L per-AP constraints. This is far more
      constrained than the single sum-power constraint in cellular systems.
    - Network-wide optimization: Max-min fairness or sum-SE maximization
      requires solving a problem whose dimension grows as O(KL). The
      bisection-based algorithms in Ngo et al. [2017] and the SOC-based
      methods in Ngo, Tran, Duong, Matthaiou, and Larsson [2017] have
      polynomial complexity in K and L -- not scalable.
    - Per-AP constraints create coupling between UEs: power allocated to UE k
      at AP l affects the budget available for all other UEs served by AP l.
      This inter-UE coupling does not exist in cellular systems where each BS
      independently serves its own cell users.
    - Scalable alternatives: Equal power allocation (rho_k = rho_max / |D_l|),
      large-scale fading-based heuristics, and fractional power control have
      O(1) per-AP complexity and are used in the scalable framework.

Source: [9] L. Miretti and S. Stanczak, "UL-DL Duality for Cell-Free Massive
MIMO with Per-AP Power and Information Constraints," IEEE Trans. Signal
Process., 2023.
Source: [10] O. T. Demir, E. Bjornson, and L. Sanguinetti, "Foundations of
User-Centric Cell-Free Massive MIMO," Foundations and Trends in Signal
Processing, vol. 14, no. 3-4, pp. 162-472, 2021.


================================================================================
SUMMARY OF KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR SECTION III
================================================================================

1. The original Cell-Free Massive MIMO (Ngo et al., 2017) is an idealized
   system where ALL APs serve ALL UEs via MR processing through a single CPU
   with infinite-capacity backhaul. This provides an upper bound on cooperative
   gains but is fundamentally unscalable.

2. The SE expressions use the UatF capacity bounding technique (originating
   from Marzetta 2010/2016), which "forgets" the channel estimate after
   combining vector design and treats the effective channel as deterministic.
   The DL hardening bound further assumes the UE uses only the mean effective
   channel gain. Both are rigorous lower bounds on ergodic capacity.

3. MR combining/precoding is analytically elegant but ignores interference,
   making it the simplest but weakest linear processing option. Its dominance
   in early CF-mMIMO papers was driven by tractability, not optimality.

4. Scalability is formally defined via four criteria (estimation, processing,
   fronthaul, power control), all of which must have O(1) per-AP complexity
   as K -> infinity. The original CF-mMIMO fails all four, motivating the
   DCC-based user-centric framework proposed in the rest of the paper.

5. Per-AP power constraints in CF-mMIMO create inter-UE coupling absent in
   cellular systems, making DL power control inherently harder and requiring
   distributed/heuristic algorithms for scalability.
