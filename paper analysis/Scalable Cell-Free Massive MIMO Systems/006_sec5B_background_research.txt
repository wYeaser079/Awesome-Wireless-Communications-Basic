1. CENTRALIZED vs. DISTRIBUTED PROCESSING
==========================================

Bjornson and Sanguinetti [7] defined four cooperation levels for CF-mMIMO
in "Making Cell-Free Massive MIMO Competitive" (IEEE Trans. Wireless
Commun., 2020). The present paper focuses on the two most relevant:

  Level 4 (Centralized):  Each AP l forwards its raw received pilot signals
    {y^{pilot}_{tl}} and data signal y^{ul}_l to the CPU over the fronthaul.
    The CPU performs all channel estimation and data detection jointly across
    all L APs.  Fronthaul load per AP per coherence block: (tau_p + tau_u)N
    complex scalars.  This is efficient when the coherence block tau_c is
    large because the overhead of sending tau_p*N pilot samples is amortized
    over many data symbols [7, Sec. III].

  Level 3 (Distributed):  AP l locally estimates channels {hat{h}_{il} :
    i in D_l} and computes per-UE soft estimates hat{s}_{kl} = v^H_{kl}
    D_{kl} y^{ul}_l.  Only the scalar estimates {hat{s}_{kl} : k in D_l}
    are forwarded.  Fronthaul load: tau_u |D_l| complex scalars per
    coherence block, upper-bounded by tau_u * tau_p (Assumption 1).

When is centralized better?  Level 4 enables joint MMSE combining across
all AP antennas, which captures the full macro-diversity.  It dominates
when tau_c is large (tau_u >> tau_p), making the pilot-forwarding cost
negligible [7].  Level 4 also permits full MMSE or P-MMSE combining.

When is distributed sufficient?  Level 3 is preferred when fronthaul
capacity is limited or when each AP has enough antennas to suppress local
interference via LP-MMSE.  It avoids sending raw pilot signals and scales
naturally because per-AP signaling is bounded by tau_p [1, Table I].


2. MMSE COMBINING AND THE GENERALIZED RAYLEIGH QUOTIENT
=========================================================

The instantaneous UL SINR for UE k with combining vector v_k is [1, Eq. 18]:

  SINR^{(ul,1)}_k = p_k |v^H_k D_k hat{h}_k|^2 /
    [ sum_{i != k} p_i |v^H_k D_k hat{h}_i|^2 + v^H_k Z_k v_k ]

This is a generalized Rayleigh quotient of the form v^H a a^H v / v^H B v,
where a = sqrt(p_k) D_k hat{h}_k and B = sum_{i != k} p_i D_k hat{h}_i
hat{h}^H_i D_k + Z_k.  By [2, Lemma B.10], this ratio is maximized by
v_k = B^{-1} a (or B^{dagger} a when B is singular), yielding

  v^{MMSE}_k = p_k [ sum_i p_i D_k hat{h}_i hat{h}^H_i D_k + Z_k ]^{dagger}
               D_k hat{h}_k.                                          (Eq. 20)

This v_k simultaneously maximizes the instantaneous SINR and minimizes the
conditional MSE = E{|s_k - hat{s}_k|^2 | {hat{h}_i}}, establishing the
equivalence between SINR-optimal combining and Wiener filtering [2, Sec. 4.1].
The result traces back to the classical Wiener filter and its MIMO extension
via the matrix inversion lemma; see Verdu, "Multiuser Detection," Cambridge
University Press, 1998, Ch. 5.

Computational cost:  MMSE combining requires inverting a (N|M_k|) x (N|M_k|)
matrix and computing K channel estimates at every AP serving UE k.  Both
operations scale with K, making full MMSE unscalable (Table II in [1]).


3. PARTIAL MMSE (P-MMSE) -- THE SCALABLE INNOVATION
=====================================================

Key insight from [30] (Interdonato et al., "Local Partial Zero-Forcing
Precoding for Cell-Free Massive MIMO," IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun., 2020):
interference at UE k is dominated by UEs whose serving clusters overlap
with UE k's cluster.  UEs served by entirely disjoint AP sets contribute
weakly because their signals are spatially separated.

P-MMSE restricts the MMSE summation to the set [1, Eq. 22]:

  P_k = { i : D_k D_i != 0_{LN} }

i.e., UEs sharing at least one AP with UE k.  The P-MMSE combiner is:

  v^{P-MMSE}_k = p_k [ sum_{i in P_k} p_i D_k hat{h}_i hat{h}^H_i D_k
                 + Z'_k ]^{dagger} D_k hat{h}_k               (Eq. 23)

Scalability guarantee:  |P_k| <= (tau_p - 1)|M_k| + 1.  Each AP serves at
most tau_p UEs (Assumption 1), and |M_k| is the number of APs in UE k's
cluster, which is bounded by design.  Hence |P_k| is bounded independent of
K, and the matrix dimension N|M_k| x N|M_k| does not grow with K.

Performance:  Simulations in [1, Fig. 3] show P-MMSE achieves 98% of full
MMSE performance while having scalable complexity.  It vastly outperforms MR
combining, particularly for cell-edge UEs where interference suppression is
most needed.  P-MMSE coincides with MMSE when UE k is served by all APs.


4. LOCAL P-MMSE (LP-MMSE) COMBINING
=====================================

In the distributed architecture (Level 3), AP l has access only to its local
channel estimates {hat{h}_{il} : i in D_l}.  LP-MMSE combining [1, Eq. 29]:

  v^{LP-MMSE}_{kl} = p_k [ sum_{i in D_l} p_i (hat{h}_{il} hat{h}^H_{il}
                     + C_{il}) + sigma^2 I_N ]^{-1} hat{h}_{kl}

Critical differences from L-MMSE in [7]:
  - L-MMSE sums over ALL K UEs, requiring global CSI at each AP (unscalable).
  - LP-MMSE sums only over D_l (at most tau_p UEs), using only local CSI.

Complexity per AP:  LP-MMSE requires inverting an N x N matrix (not NL x NL),
with cost O(N^3) per AP.  This is dramatically cheaper than centralized
P-MMSE (O((N|M_k|)^3)).  For typical values N = 4, |M_k| = 10, the matrix
dimension drops from 40 x 40 to 4 x 4.

LP-MMSE vs. MR:  Even with single-antenna APs (N = 1), LP-MMSE provides
interference-aware combining by weighting the local estimate according to
inter-user interference statistics.  With N >= 4, LP-MMSE exploits spatial
degrees of freedom to null dominant interferers locally, vastly outperforming
MR [1, Fig. 3], [7, Fig. 4].


5. THE USE-AND-THEN-FORGET (UatF) BOUND
=========================================

In distributed processing, the CPU receives only soft estimates hat{s}_k =
sum_l hat{s}_{kl} without access to channel estimates.  The standard
capacity bound from Proposition 1 (which conditions on {hat{h}_i}) is
therefore inapplicable.  Instead, Proposition 2 applies the UatF technique.

Formal construction:  The received signal after combining is decomposed as

  hat{s}_k = E{v^H_k D_k h_k} * s_k  +  (effective noise)

where the deterministic scalar E{v^H_k D_k h_k} acts as the "known" channel,
and all remaining terms -- including the deviation v^H_k D_k h_k - E{v^H_k
D_k h_k} -- are lumped into worst-case uncorrelated Gaussian noise.  This
yields the SE in (27)-(28) with SINR expressed entirely in terms of
expectations over channel realizations [2, Th. 4.4].

When is UatF tight?
  - Strong channel hardening:  When NL is large, v^H_k D_k h_k concentrates
    around its mean, so the "forgotten" information is small.  The gap
    between UatF and the genie bound (Proposition 1) becomes negligible.
  - Co-located massive MIMO with M >> K antennas exhibits strong hardening
    [2, Sec. 2.4], making UatF nearly exact.

When is UatF loose?
  - Few antennas per AP (N = 1 or 2):  The product v^H_k D_k h_k fluctuates
    significantly around its mean, and treating this fluctuation as noise is
    pessimistic.  Chen and Bjornson (IEEE Trans. Commun., 2021) showed UatF
    can underestimate rates by 0.5-1.0 bit/s/Hz in such regimes.
  - Weak channel hardening:  Cell-free deployments with spatially distributed
    single-antenna APs exhibit limited hardening [45] (Chen and Bjornson,
    "Channel Hardening and Favorable Propagation in Cell-Free Massive MIMO
    with Stochastic Geometry," IEEE Globecom, 2018).

Connection to DL hardening bound:  The DL SE in Section V-C uses the
"hardening bound" [2, Th. 4.6], which is the DL dual of UatF.  Both replace
the instantaneous effective channel by its mean and treat deviations as
noise.  They become tight under the same conditions (large NL, strong
hardening) and loose under the same conditions (few antennas, distributed
geometry).
