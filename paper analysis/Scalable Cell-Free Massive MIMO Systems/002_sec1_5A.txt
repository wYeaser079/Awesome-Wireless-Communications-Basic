  1. HIGH-LEVEL EXPLANATION (Layman's Terms)

  Imagine you're at a huge music festival, and instead of having one giant
  speaker on a stage (that blasts music to everyone but is too loud for people
  near it and too quiet for people far away), you scatter hundreds of small
  speakers all over the festival grounds. Each speaker is not very powerful on
  its own, but if you coordinate them smartly — making them all play the same
  music in sync — every person in the crowd gets a clear, strong sound
  regardless of where they stand.

  That's the core idea of Cell-Free Massive MIMO.

  In today's cellular networks, each cell tower (base station) creates a "cell"
  — a geographic area it serves. If you're at the edge of two cells, both towers
   interfere with each other and your signal quality drops. This is the
  cell-edge problem and it's one of the biggest limitations of cellular
  networks.

  Cell-Free Massive MIMO says: "Let's get rid of cells entirely." Instead of
  having big towers that each own a territory, let's deploy many small access
  points (APs) — like those festival speakers — across the entire area. All of
  them cooperate together to serve every user. There are no cell boundaries, so
  there are no cell edges, and therefore no cell-edge interference.

  But here's the catch: if you have 10,000 users and 1,000 access points, and
  every access point tries to serve every user, the amount of computation and
  data sharing between access points becomes impossibly large. It's like asking
  every festival speaker to individually customize its output for every single
  person in the crowd — it doesn't scale.

  This paper's big idea: You don't need every access point to serve every user.
  Each user only needs to be served by the few closest access points (the ones
  with the best signal). This is called Dynamic Cooperation Clustering (DCC) —
  and it makes the system scalable, meaning it works just as well whether you
  have 100 users or 100,000 users, without the computational load per access
  point exploding.

  2. DETAILED TECHNICAL EXPLANATION (PhD Level)

  2.1 The Coherent Transmission Paradigm

  The introduction opens with the fundamental physical principle that underlies
  the entire paper: coherent joint transmission.

  When a signal is transmitted coherently from M antennas, the received power at
   a target user can scale proportionally to M without increasing the total
  transmit power. This is the array gain or beamforming gain. Mathematically, if
   M antennas each transmit with power P/M, the coherent combination at the
  receiver yields a received power proportional to M·(P/M)² · M = P (after
  beamforming). In practice, the received signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) scales as:

  SNR ∝ M · (P_total / M) · |channel gain|² × M = M · P_total · |channel gain|²
  Wait, let me be more precise. If all M antennas transmit the same signal with
  appropriate phase alignment:

  Received signal = Σᵢ₌₁ᴹ hᵢ · xᵢ
  where hᵢ is the channel from antenna i to the user, and xᵢ = (hᵢ*/|hᵢ|) ·
  √(P/M) · s is the transmitted signal with maximum ratio transmission (MRT).
  The received power becomes:

  |Σᵢ₌₁ᴹ |hᵢ|² · √(P/M)|² = P/M · (Σᵢ₌₁ᴹ |hᵢ|²)²
  This is the coherent beamforming gain — the squared sum of channel magnitudes,
   which grows as M² in the best case, compared to M for non-coherent combining.

  Key insight from the paper: This coherent gain applies not only to co-located
  arrays (standard MIMO) but also to distributed access points. Even when
  different APs have very different channel gains to a user, it is still better
  to distribute the transmit power across multiple APs and transmit coherently
  than to transmit only from the strongest AP.

  Source: This principle is thoroughly treated in [2] E. Björnson, J. Hoydis,
  and L. Sanguinetti, "Massive MIMO Networks: Spectral, Energy, and Hardware
  Efficiency," Foundations and Trends in Signal Processing, vol. 11, no. 3-4,
  pp. 154–655, 2017.

  2.2 Network MIMO: The Original Concept

  The idea of having multiple base stations cooperate to jointly serve users was
   formalized under the name Network MIMO:

  - Shamai and Zaidel (2001) [3]: First proposed enhancing cellular downlink
  capacity via co-processing at the transmitting end — the earliest idea of what
   would become Network MIMO.
  - Venkatesan, Lozano, and Valenzuela (2007) [4]: Coined the term "Network
  MIMO" and demonstrated that coordination across access points in an indoor
  environment could overcome intercell interference, yielding multi-fold
  spectral efficiency gains.
  - Gesbert, Hanly, Huang, Shamai, Simeone, and Yu (2010) [8]: Provided a
  comprehensive survey of multi-cell MIMO cooperative networks, establishing a
  theoretical framework for understanding interference in cooperative systems.

  The early Network MIMO papers made two assumptions:

  1. Network-wide CSI: Every AP knows the channels of all users in the entire
  network.
  2. All APs serve all UEs: Every AP transmits to (and receives from) every
  user.

  These assumptions are theoretically optimal but practically disastrous:
  - CSI sharing requires enormous fronthaul capacity (the links connecting APs
  to central processors)
  - Data sharing for all users at all APs requires even more fronthaul
  - Computational complexity for joint processing grows rapidly

  2.3 Attempts to Make Network MIMO Practical

  The paper traces how researchers tried to make Network MIMO practical:

  Step 1 — Eliminating CSI sharing (Björnson et al. 2010, [9]):
  - Proved that Network MIMO can operate without CSI sharing between APs
  - Each AP only uses its local CSI (the channels it can estimate from uplink
  pilots)
  - Trade-off: Lose the ability for APs to jointly cancel interference

  Step 2 — Limiting which APs serve which users (Björnson and Jorswieck, 2013
  [10]):
  - Each UE is served only by a subset of APs
  - Two approaches emerged:

  (a) Network-centric clustering [11]–[13]:
  - Divide APs into non-overlapping (disjoint) clusters
  - APs within a cluster share data and potentially CSI
  - Serve only UEs within the cluster's coverage area
  - Problem: UEs at cluster edges see strong inter-cluster interference — the
  very problem we were trying to solve!
  - This approach was considered in 4G CoMP (Coordinated Multi-Point) but
  provided small practical gains [14]

  (b) User-centric clustering [16]–[18]:
  - Each UE is served by the APs providing the best channels to that specific UE
  - Since different UEs have different "best AP subsets," the clusters overlap
  - Each AP cooperates with different APs for different UEs
  - This is fundamentally a cell-free approach

  2.4 Dynamic Cooperation Clustering (DCC)

  The general user-centric cooperation framework was formalized by Björnson, 
  Jaldén, Bengtsson, and Ottersten (2011) [17] under the name Dynamic 
  Cooperation Clustering (DCC):

  - "Dynamic" means the clusters adapt to time-varying characteristics: channel
  properties, UE locations, traffic patterns
  - Each UE gets its own set of cooperating APs
  - The sets overlap — this is the defining feature
  - Further described in the textbook [10]: Björnson and Jorswieck, "Optimal
  Resource Allocation in Coordinated Multi-Cell Systems," Foundations and Trends
   in Communications and Information Theory, 2013.

  pCell Technology: The practical feasibility of DCC was experimentally
  demonstrated by Artemis Networks' pCell technology [21], which creates a
  virtual personal cell for each user through coordinated distributed
  transmission.

  Why DCC didn't take off initially: Massive MIMO was conceived at approximately
   the same time (Marzetta, 2010 [22]) and "rightfully gained the spotlight"
  according to the paper. The research community focused on co-located Massive
  MIMO instead.

  2.5 Massive MIMO and its Maturation

  Marzetta's seminal 2010 paper [22] introduced the concept of a base station
  with an unlimited number of antennas serving multiple single-antenna users in
  TDD mode:

  - In the limit of infinite antennas, thermal noise and fast fading vanish
  - Only pilot contamination (interference from pilot reuse) remains
  - This was a paradigm shift: simple linear processing (MR/ZF) becomes
  near-optimal with many antennas

  By 2020, Massive MIMO had:
  - A comprehensive theoretical framework [2], [23], [24]
  - Been adopted into the 5G NR standard [25]
  - Commercial deployments with 64-antenna base stations
  - Become "a rather mature technology"

  Source: S. Parkvall, E. Dahlman, A. Furuskär, and M. Frenne, "NR: The New 5G
  Radio Access Technology," IEEE Communications Standards Magazine, vol. 1, no.
  4, pp. 24–30, 2017.

  2.6 Cell-Free Massive MIMO: The Rebirth of Network MIMO

  With Massive MIMO mature, the research focus shifted back to distributed
  cooperation under the new name Cell-Free Massive MIMO, coined by Ngo et al.
  (2017) [5], [6]:

  Key features of the original Cell-Free Massive MIMO:
  - L access points (APs), each with N antennas (often N = 1)
  - K single-antenna users
  - Usually L ≫ K (many more APs than users)
  - TDD operation with uplink pilot-based channel estimation
  - All APs connected to a single CPU
  - All APs serve all UEs simultaneously

  Key novelty over Network MIMO: Rigorous spectral efficiency (SE) analysis that
   accounts for imperfect CSI — borrowing the analytical tools from Massive
  MIMO.

  Conceptual relationship: Cell-Free Massive MIMO is a special case of Network
  MIMO operating in TDD mode. However, the paper argues it was actually "a step
  backwards in terms of implementation feasibility" because:
  - While it adopted the "no CSI sharing" approach from [9], it assumed all APs
  connect to a single CPU
  - All APs still serve all UEs
  - This is exactly the unscalable setup that Network MIMO researchers had
  already identified as problematic

  2.7 Section I-A: The Scalability Crisis

  This is the paper's central problem statement. The original Cell-Free Massive
  MIMO is unscalable. The paper will formally define scalability in Section III,
   but the Introduction previews the issue:

  What went wrong:
  - User-centric clustering was reintroduced in [27]–[29] (Buzzi & D'Andrea
  2017; Ngo et al. 2018; Bursalioglu et al. 2019)
  - But without connecting to DCC, without proving scalability, and without
  leveraging Network MIMO insights

  Power control scalability:
  - Network-wide optimization algorithms [5], [6], [26], [30], [31] have
  complexity that grows polynomially with the number of APs and UEs
  - Not feasible for practical implementation
  - Only suboptimal heuristic algorithms combined with DCC can achieve scalable
  power control
  - Simplest scalable power control: equal power allocation
  - Many other heuristic algorithms exist in the literature

  2.8 Section I-B: Motivation and Contributions

  The paper's contributions are:

  1. Proving CF-mMIMO is a special case of DCC: This is the theoretical
  foundation — once this is established, all the scalability tools from Network
  MIMO become available.
  2. Scalable algorithm for joint initial access, pilot assignment, and cluster 
  formation: A new three-step procedure that guarantees every UE is served.
  3. Novel SE expressions for two implementations:
    - (a) Centralized: CPUs gather all pilot signals, perform channel
  estimation, and jointly process UL/DL signals
    - (b) Distributed/Decentralized: Each AP locally estimates channels and
  processes signals; CPUs only handle data encoding/decoding
  4. General SE expressions valid for: Arbitrary clusters, spatially correlated
  Rayleigh fading, imperfect CSI, any number N of AP antennas, any signal
  processing scheme
  5. New UL-DL duality: Proves that UL combining vectors can be used to design
  DL precoding vectors — simplifying the DL design problem enormously
  6. Numerical demonstration: Scalable processing performs nearly as well as
  unscalable alternatives and vastly outperforms MR processing

  4. MATHEMATICAL FOUNDATIONS

  4.1 Coherent Beamforming Gain

  Consider L access points, each with N antennas, serving a single-antenna UE.
  The channel from AP l to the UE is:

  h_l ∈ ℂᴺ
  The collective channel from all APs:

  h = [h₁ᵀ  h₂ᵀ  ...  h_Lᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ,    M = NL
  If all APs transmit coherently using precoding vector w ∈ ℂᴹ, the received
  signal is:

  y = hᴴw·s + n
  where s is the data signal and n is noise. The received signal power is:

  |hᴴw|²
  With maximum ratio transmission (MRT): w = h/‖h‖, this becomes:

  |hᴴh/‖h‖|² = ‖h‖² = Σₗ₌₁ᴸ ‖h_l‖²
  This is the coherent array gain — the sum of all channel norms squared. Even
  if some APs have weak channels (small ‖h_l‖²), they still contribute
  positively.

  Non-coherent case (single best AP): Only AP l* = argmax_l ‖h_l‖² transmits:

  Received power = ‖h_{l*}‖²
  Coherent case (all APs):

  Received power = Σₗ₌₁ᴸ ‖h_l‖² ≥ ‖h_{l*}‖²
  The coherent case is always at least as good, and typically much better.

  4.2 Why Cell-Edge Problems Disappear

  In a cellular network, UE k at the edge of cell l receives:
  - Desired signal from AP l with power: P_l · |h_{kl}|²
  - Interference from neighboring AP l' with power: P_{l'} · |h_{kl'}|²

  The SINR at the cell edge is approximately:

  SINR_edge ≈ P_l · |h_{kl}|² / (P_{l'} · |h_{kl'}|² + σ²)
  At the cell edge, |h_{kl}|² ≈ |h_{kl'}|², so:

  SINR_edge ≈ P_l / (P_{l'} + σ²/|h_{kl}|²) → ~1  (0 dB)
  This is terrible performance. In cell-free operation, both APs l and l'
  cooperate:

  Received power = |h_{kl}·w_l + h_{kl'}·w_{l'}|²
  With coherent transmission, this becomes approximately:

  ≈ (|h_{kl}| + |h_{kl'}|)² ≈ 4·|h_{kl}|²   (when |h_{kl}| ≈ |h_{kl'}|)
  The interference is eliminated and the signal power doubles (compared to
  single-AP).

  4.3 Polynomial vs. Scalable Complexity

  The paper distinguishes between:
  - Polynomial complexity (grows as O(Kᵅ) for some α > 0): Network-wide
  optimization algorithms fall here. While polynomial sounds acceptable in
  theory, for wireless networks with K potentially in the thousands, even O(K²)
  or O(K³) is impractical for real-time processing within a coherence block
  (typically a few milliseconds).
  - Scalable complexity (O(1) per AP as K → ∞): Each AP's computational burden
  does not grow with the number of users. The total network complexity grows
  with L (number of APs), but since each AP has its own processor, this is fine.

  5. NUMERICAL EXAMPLE

  Example: Coherent vs. Non-Coherent Transmission

  Setup: L = 4 APs, each with N = 1 antenna, serving 1 UE. The channels (scalar
  for N = 1) are:

  h₁ = 0.9,  h₂ = 0.5,  h₃ = 0.3,  h₄ = 0.1
  Total transmit power: P_total = 1 W, Noise power: σ² = 0.01 W

  (a) Non-coherent (best single AP):

  Best AP is AP 1. Received power = |h₁|² · P_total = 0.81 W

  SNR = 0.81 / 0.01 = 81   →   SE = log₂(1 + 81) = 6.36 bit/s/Hz
  (b) Coherent (all APs with MRT):

  ‖h‖² = 0.81 + 0.25 + 0.09 + 0.01 = 1.16
  With MRT: w_l = h_l*/‖h‖ · √P_total, received power = ‖h‖² · P_total = 1.16 W

  SNR = 1.16 / 0.01 = 116   →   SE = log₂(1 + 116) = 6.87 bit/s/Hz
  Gain: 43% more received power, 8% more SE. The gains are more dramatic with
  more APs.

  (c) If this were a cell-edge scenario with APs 1 and 2 in different cells:

  - Cell 1 serves UE: Desired power = 0.81, Interference from AP 2 = 0.25
  - SINR = 0.81/(0.25 + 0.01) = 3.12 → SE = log₂(1 + 3.12) = 2.04 bit/s/Hz

  Cooperating instead of interfering: SE jumps from 2.04 to 6.87 bit/s/Hz — a
  3.4× improvement!

  1. HIGH-LEVEL EXPLANATION (Layman's Terms)

  Think of a large shopping mall where many small Wi-Fi routers (access points)
  are mounted on the ceiling everywhere. There are also many shoppers (users)
  walking around with their phones. Every router can "hear" every phone, and
  every phone can "hear" every router — but some are close and have a strong
  connection, while others are far and have a weak, noisy connection.

  The Network Model answers these basic questions:
  - How many routers and phones? K phones, L routers, each router has N small
  antennas.
  - How do they communicate? They take turns: first phones send a known "hello"
  signal (pilot) so routers can learn the channel, then phones send data
  (uplink), then routers send data back (downlink). All within a tiny time
  window (a few milliseconds) during which the channel stays roughly constant.
  - What does the channel look like? The signal between any phone and any router
   antenna is a random number that changes unpredictably (fading). But it's not
  purely random — there's a statistical structure (some directions are more
  likely than others) captured by a "correlation matrix."
  - How do routers learn the channel? Each phone sends a known pilot signal. The
   router receives it, and because it knows what was sent, it can figure out
  what the channel must be. This is channel estimation. The best method is
  called MMSE (Minimum Mean Squared Error) — it uses prior statistical knowledge
   to make the best possible guess.
  - What's the catch? There aren't enough unique pilot signals for every phone.
  Some phones share the same pilot, which confuses the routers — this is pilot 
  contamination.

  2. DETAILED TECHNICAL EXPLANATION (PhD Level)

  2.1 System Setup

  The paper considers a cell-free network with:
  - K single-antenna UEs (User Equipments)
  - L APs (Access Points), each equipped with N antennas
  - Total antenna count in the network: M = NL
  - APs and UEs are arbitrarily distributed over the coverage area
  - APs are connected to edge-cloud processors called CPUs (Central Processing
  Units) in an arbitrary fashion

  This is called Cell-Free Massive MIMO when L and K are large. The original
  papers [5], [6] assumed L ≫ K (many more APs than users), but this paper's
  methods work for any values of L and K.

  Why this matters: Unlike cellular Massive MIMO where a single base station has
   M co-located antennas, here the M antennas are distributed across L
  locations. This provides:
  - Macro-diversity: Different APs see different large-scale fading, so if one
  AP has a deep shadow fade to a UE, another AP likely doesn't.
  - Proximity gains: With many APs, each UE is likely close to at least one AP,
  reducing pathloss.
  - Reduced transmit power: Shorter AP-UE distances require less power.

  2.2 The TDD Protocol

  The system operates in Time Division Duplex (TDD) mode. The time-frequency
  resources are divided into coherence blocks, each consisting of τ_c channel
  uses (symbols):

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                    Coherence Block (τ_c)                │
  ├──────────────┬───────────────────┬─────────────────────┤
  │  UL Pilots   │    UL Data        │     DL Data          │
  │   (τ_p)      │    (τ_u)          │     (τ_d)            │
  ├──────────────┴───────────────────┴─────────────────────┤
  │              τ_c = τ_p + τ_u + τ_d                      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Why TDD and not FDD?

  In Frequency Division Duplex (FDD), UL and DL use different frequencies, so
  the channels are different. To enable DL precoding, the base station needs DL
  CSI, which requires:
  - DL pilot signals: the base station sends pilots, UEs estimate the DL
  channel, and feed it back
  - Overhead: proportional to M (number of BS antennas) — prohibitive for
  Massive MIMO

  In TDD, UL and DL use the same frequency at different times. By
  electromagnetic reciprocity, the physical propagation channel is identical in
  both directions:

  h_UL(f) = h_DL(f)ᵀ     at the same frequency f
  This means:
  - Only τ_p UL pilot symbols needed (proportional to K, independent of M)
  - The AP estimates h from UL pilots, then uses it directly for DL precoding
  - This is the foundation of Massive MIMO and Cell-Free Massive MIMO

  Source: The TDD protocol follows the standard Massive MIMO framework from [2]:
   E. Björnson, J. Hoydis, and L. Sanguinetti, "Massive MIMO Networks: Spectral,
   Energy, and Hardware Efficiency," Foundations and Trends in Signal 
  Processing, vol. 11, no. 3-4, 2017.

  2.3 What is a Coherence Block?

  A coherence block is the time-frequency region over which the channel is
  approximately constant. Its size is:

  τ_c = B_c × T_c
  where:
  - B_c = coherence bandwidth ≈ 1/(delay spread). The delay spread is the time
  difference between the first and last significant multipath arrivals.
  - T_c = coherence time ≈ 1/(Doppler spread) = c/(f_c · v), where f_c is
  carrier frequency and v is UE velocity.

  Typical values (from [2, Sec. 2.1]):

  ┌──────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┐
  │        Parameter         │               Value               │
  ├──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Carrier frequency f_c    │ 2 GHz                             │
  ├──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
  │ UE speed v               │ 30 km/h (pedestrian/slow vehicle) │
  ├──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Coherence time T_c       │ ~5 ms                             │
  ├──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Delay spread             │ ~1 μs (urban)                     │
  ├──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Coherence bandwidth B_c  │ ~200 kHz                          │
  ├──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Bandwidth per subcarrier │ ~15 kHz (LTE/5G)                  │
  ├──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Coherence block size τ_c │ ~200 channel uses                 │
  └──────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────────┘

  In this paper's simulations: τ_c = 200, τ_p = 10.

  2.4 Channel Model: Correlated Rayleigh Fading

  The channel between AP l and UE k is:

  h_{kl} ∈ ℂᴺ
  The collective channel from all APs to UE k is formed by stacking:

  h_k = [h_{k1}ᵀ  h_{k2}ᵀ  ...  h_{kL}ᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ
  where M = NL is the total number of antennas.

  The distribution: In each coherence block, an independent realization is
  drawn:

  h_{kl} ~ 𝒩_C(0, R_{kl})                    ... (Eq. implied)
  where R_{kl} ∈ ℂ^{N×N} is the spatial correlation matrix.

  2.4.1 What Does 𝒩C(0, R{kl}) Mean?

  The notation 𝒩_C(0, R) denotes a circularly symmetric complex Gaussian
  distribution with:
  - Zero mean (no line-of-sight component → Rayleigh fading)
  - Correlation matrix R

  A random vector h ~ 𝒩_C(0, R) has the property:

  E{h} = 0
  E{hhᴴ} = R
  E{hhᵀ} = 0    (circular symmetry: e^{jθ}h has the same distribution as h for
  any θ)
  The probability density function is:

  f(h) = 1/(πᴺ det(R)) · exp(-hᴴR⁻¹h)
  Physical justification (Clarke's model, 1968):
  When a signal propagates from UE to AP through many scatterers (buildings,
  trees, cars), each scatterer creates a reflected copy with a random phase and
  amplitude. By the Central Limit Theorem, the sum of many such independent
  contributions converges to a complex Gaussian random variable. The zero mean
  arises because there is no dominant line-of-sight path (NLOS condition).

  Source: R. H. Clarke, "A statistical theory of mobile-radio reception," Bell 
  System Technical Journal, vol. 47, no. 6, pp. 957–1000, 1968.

  2.4.2 The Spatial Correlation Matrix R_{kl}

  The matrix R_{kl} is the statistical fingerprint of the channel between UE k
  and AP l. It encodes:

  1. Large-scale fading (path loss + shadowing):

  The average channel gain is:

  β_{kl} = tr(R_{kl})/N
  This scalar captures the distance-dependent pathloss and shadow fading. A
  common pathloss model:

  β_{kl} [dB] = -30.5 - 36.7 · log₁₀(d_{kl}) + F_{kl}
  where d_{kl} is the distance in meters and F_{kl} ~ 𝒩(0, σ_sf²) is the shadow
  fading (typically σ_sf = 4-8 dB).

  2. Spatial channel correlation:

  The (m,n)-th entry of R_{kl} describes the correlation between the channel at
  the m-th and n-th antenna of AP l:

  [R_{kl}]_{m,n} = E{[h_{kl}]_m · [h_{kl}]_n*}
  This correlation depends on:
  - Angular spread: How wide the range of arrival angles is at the AP. Narrower
  spread → higher correlation.
  - Antenna spacing: Closer antennas → higher correlation.
  - Scattering environment: Rich scattering → lower correlation (more
  isotropic).

  The one-ring model (Shiu et al., 2000): Scatterers are placed on a ring of
  radius r around the UE, at distance D from the AP. The angular spread as seen
  from the AP is:

  Δ ≈ arctan(r/D)
  For a Uniform Linear Array (ULA) with antenna spacing d, the (m,n)-th entry
  becomes:

  [R_{kl}]_{m,n} = β_{kl} · ∫₋_Δ^Δ exp(j2π(m-n)(d/λ)sin(θ₀+α)) · f(α) dα
  where θ₀ is the nominal angle of arrival, f(α) is the angular power spectrum,
  and λ is the wavelength.

  Special cases:
  - Uncorrelated fading (isotropic scattering, d ≥ λ/2): R_{kl} = β_{kl} · I_N
  - Fully correlated (single direction): R_{kl} = β_{kl} · a(θ)a(θ)ᴴ where a(θ)
  is the steering vector

  Source: D. Shiu, G. J. Foschini, M. J. Gans, and J. M. Kahn, "Fading
  correlation and its effect on the capacity of multielement antenna systems,"
  IEEE Trans. Commun., vol. 48, no. 3, pp. 502–513, 2000.

  2.4.3 Independence Between APs

  The paper assumes:

  E{h_{kn} · (h_{kl})ᴴ} = 0    for l ≠ n
  This means the channels from UE k to different APs are uncorrelated. This is
  justified because the APs are spatially distributed — they see completely
  different scattering environments. The collective channel thus has a
  block-diagonal correlation matrix:

  h_k ~ 𝒩_C(0, R_k)                          ... Eq. (1)
  where:

  R_k = diag(R_{k1}, R_{k2}, ..., R_{kL}) ∈ ℂ^{M×M}
  This block-diagonal structure is extremely important computationally — it
  means operations can be decomposed per-AP.

  Additionally, the channels of different UEs are independently distributed: h_k
   and h_j are independent for k ≠ j.

  2.4.4 Practical Estimation of Correlation Matrices

  The paper assumes R_{kl} is known wherever needed and cites methods for
  practical estimation:

  - [36] Björnson, Sanguinetti, Debbah (2016): Massive MIMO with imperfect
  channel covariance information
  - [37] Neumann, Joham, Utschick (2018): Covariance matrix estimation in
  Massive MIMO
  - [38] Upadhya and Vorobyov (2018): Covariance matrix estimation for Massive
  MIMO
  - [39] Haghighatshoar and Caire (2017): Wideband sparse channel estimation

  In practice, R_{kl} changes slowly (on the order of seconds to minutes) — it
  depends on the UE's general location and the scattering environment, not the
  instantaneous fast-fading. Thus, it can be estimated by averaging over many
  coherence blocks.

  This subsection is the mathematical heart of Section II. Let me derive every

  2.5.1 Pilot Signal Design

  There are τ_p mutually orthogonal pilot signals, each of length τ_p symbols.
  Let:

  φ_t ∈ ℂ^{τ_p},    t = 1, ..., τ_p
  be the t-th pilot signal, satisfying:

  φ_t^H · φ_t' = { τ_p  if t = t'
                  { 0    if t ≠ t'
  These form an orthonormal basis (scaled by √τ_p) for ℂ^{τ_p}.

  Crucially: τ_p is a constant independent of K. This means when K > τ_p, some
  UEs must share pilots. The set of UEs assigned to pilot t is:

  𝒮_t ⊂ {1, ..., K}
  The sets partition {1, ..., K}: every UE is assigned exactly one pilot.

  2.5.2 Received Pilot Signal at the AP

  When all UEs assigned to pilot t transmit simultaneously, the received signal
  at AP l (after correlating/despreading with φ_t) is:

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p · p_i) · h_{il} + n_{tl}     ... Eq. (2)
  where:
  - p_i is the transmit power of UE i
  - τ_p is the processing gain from correlating with the pilot (like spread
  spectrum)
  - n_{tl} ~ 𝒩_C(0, σ² · I_N) is thermal noise

  Let me derive this step by step:

  During the pilot phase, each UE i sends: x_i^{pilot} = √p_i · φ_{t_i} ∈
  ℂ^{τ_p}

  The received signal at AP l over the entire pilot phase (τ_p symbols) is the N
   × τ_p matrix:

  Y_l^{pilot} = Σ_{i=1}^{K} h_{il} · (x_i^{pilot})ᵀ + N_l
              = Σ_{i=1}^{K} √p_i · h_{il} · φ_{t_i}ᵀ + N_l
  where N_l ∈ ℂ^{N×τ_p} is the noise matrix with i.i.d. 𝒩_C(0, σ²) entries.

  To extract the signal from pilot t, the AP correlates with φ_t (despreading):

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = Y_l^{pilot} · φ_t* / √1 = Σ_{i=1}^{K} √p_i · h_{il} ·
  (φ_{t_i}ᵀ · φ_t*) + N_l · φ_t*
  Using the orthogonality property:

  φ_{t_i}ᵀ · φ_t* = φ_t^H · φ_{t_i} = { τ_p  if t_i = t  (i.e., i ∈ 𝒮_t)
                                          { 0    otherwise
  Therefore:

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √p_i · τ_p · h_{il} + N_l · φ_t*
  Since N_l · φ_t* has distribution 𝒩_C(0, σ² · ‖φ_t‖² · I_N) = 𝒩_C(0, σ² · τ_p
  · I_N), we can write:

  n_{tl} = N_l · φ_t* / √τ_p ~ 𝒩_C(0, σ² · I_N)
  And rescaling:

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p · p_i) · h_{il} + √τ_p · n_{tl}
  Wait — let me be more careful. Looking at the paper's equation (2) directly:

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p · p_i) · h_{il} + n_{tl}
  where n_{tl} ~ 𝒩_C(0, σ² · I_N). This is the despreaded signal with the noise
  term already incorporating the processing. The key point is that the √(τ_p ·
  p_i) factor represents the combined effect of transmit power and processing
  gain from pilot despreading.

  2.5.3 The MMSE Channel Estimator

  Given the observation y^{pilot}{tl} from Eq. (2), we want to estimate h{kl}
  for UE k ∈ 𝒮_t.

  The MMSE estimate is:

  ĥ_{kl} = √(p_k · τ_p) · R_{kl} · Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ · y^{pilot}_{tl}     ... Eq. (3)
  where:

  Ψ_{tl} = E{y^{pilot}_{tl} · (y^{pilot}_{tl})ᴴ} = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} τ_p · p_i ·
  R_{il} + σ² · I_N     ... Eq. (4)
  Full derivation of the MMSE estimator:

  The MMSE estimator exploits the fact that h_{kl} and y^{pilot}_{tl} are
  jointly Gaussian. For jointly Gaussian vectors, the MMSE estimator equals the
  Linear MMSE (LMMSE) estimator:

  ĥ_{kl} = E{h_{kl} · (y^{pilot}_{tl})ᴴ} · [E{y^{pilot}_{tl} ·
  (y^{pilot}_{tl})ᴴ}]⁻¹ · y^{pilot}_{tl}
  Step A: Compute the cross-correlation E{h_{kl} · (y^{pilot}_{tl})ᴴ}:

  E{h_{kl} · (y^{pilot}_{tl})ᴴ} = E{h_{kl} · (Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p · p_i) · h_{il}
  + n_{tl})ᴴ}

  = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p · p_i) · E{h_{kl} · h_{il}ᴴ} + E{h_{kl} · n_{tl}ᴴ}
  Since channels of different UEs are independent: E{h_{kl} · h_{il}ᴴ} = 0 for i
   ≠ k. Also, h_{kl} is independent of noise: E{h_{kl} · n_{tl}ᴴ} = 0.
  Therefore:

  E{h_{kl} · (y^{pilot}_{tl})ᴴ} = √(τ_p · p_k) · E{h_{kl} · h_{kl}ᴴ} = √(τ_p ·
  p_k) · R_{kl}
  Step B: Compute the auto-correlation Ψ_{tl} = E{y^{pilot}{tl} · 
  (y^{pilot}{tl})ᴴ}:

  Ψ_{tl} = E{(Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p p_i) · h_{il} + n_{tl})(Σ_{j ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p p_j) ·
   h_{jl} + n_{tl})ᴴ}
  Expanding and using independence between different UEs and between channels
  and noise:

  = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} τ_p · p_i · E{h_{il} · h_{il}ᴴ} + E{n_{tl} · n_{tl}ᴴ}

  = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} τ_p · p_i · R_{il} + σ² · I_N                    ... Eq. (4)
  Step C: Combine Steps A and B:

  ĥ_{kl} = √(τ_p · p_k) · R_{kl} · Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ · y^{pilot}_{tl}    ... Eq. (3)
  This is the MMSE estimate (also known as Wiener filter in this context).

  Source: S. M. Kay, Fundamentals of Statistical Signal Processing: Estimation 
  Theory, Prentice Hall, 1993, Ch. 12.

  2.5.4 Properties of the MMSE Estimate

  Property 1: Distribution of the estimate

  ĥ_{kl} ~ 𝒩_C(0, B_{kl})
  where:

  B_{kl} = τ_p · p_k · R_{kl} · Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ · R_{kl}
  This is the correlation matrix of the estimate, which is also the effective
  estimation quality.

  Property 2: The estimation error

  Define the estimation error:

  h̃_{kl} = h_{kl} - ĥ_{kl}
  Then:

  h̃_{kl} ~ 𝒩_C(0, C_{kl})
  where:

  C_{kl} = R_{kl} - B_{kl} = R_{kl} - τ_p · p_k · R_{kl} · Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ · R_{kl}
  Property 3: Orthogonality principle

  The MMSE estimate and the estimation error are uncorrelated:

  E{ĥ_{kl} · h̃_{kl}ᴴ} = 0
  Since both are Gaussian, uncorrelated implies independent. This is a crucial
  property that makes the SE analysis tractable.

  Property 4: Independence of estimation errors across APs

  Since h_{kl} and h_{kn} are independent for l ≠ n (different APs), the
  estimates and errors across different APs are also independent. The collective
   estimate and error are:

  ĥ_k = [ĥ_{k1}ᵀ  ...  ĥ_{kL}ᵀ]ᵀ ~ 𝒩_C(0, diag(B_{k1}, ..., B_{kL}))
  h̃_k = [h̃_{k1}ᵀ  ...  h̃_{kL}ᵀ]ᵀ ~ 𝒩_C(0, C_k)
  where C_k = diag(C_{k1}, ..., C_{kL}).

  2.5.5 Pilot Contamination: The Two Consequences

  When |𝒮_t| > 1 (multiple UEs share pilot t), the received pilot signal at AP l
   contains contributions from all of them:

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = √(τ_p p_k) · h_{kl} + Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t, i≠k} √(τ_p p_i) · h_{il} +
  n_{tl}
                      ↑ desired               ↑ pilot contamination         ↑
  noise
  Consequence 1: Reduced estimation quality

  The contaminating terms inflate Ψ_{tl}:

  Ψ_{tl} = τ_p p_k · R_{kl} + Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t, i≠k} τ_p p_i · R_{il} + σ² I_N
            ↑ desired power     ↑ contamination power          ↑ noise
  Since Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ appears in the estimator, larger Ψ_{tl} (from contamination)
  means a worse estimate. The MSE increases.

  Without contamination (|𝒮_t| = 1, UE k is the only user on pilot t):

  Ψ_{tl} = τ_p p_k R_{kl} + σ² I_N

  B_{kl}^{no cont.} = τ_p p_k · R_{kl}(τ_p p_k R_{kl} + σ² I_N)⁻¹ R_{kl}
  With contamination (additional UE j on same pilot):

  Ψ_{tl} = τ_p p_k R_{kl} + τ_p p_j R_{jl} + σ² I_N

  B_{kl}^{cont.} = τ_p p_k · R_{kl}(τ_p p_k R_{kl} + τ_p p_j R_{jl} + σ² I_N)⁻¹
  R_{kl}
  Since (A + B)⁻¹ ≼ A⁻¹ for positive semidefinite B ≽ 0 (Löwner ordering), we
  have B_{kl}^{cont.} ≼ B_{kl}^{no cont.}: the estimate quality strictly
  degrades.

  Consequence 2: Correlated estimates → Coherent interference

  The estimates of pilot-sharing UEs are correlated:

  E{ĥ_{kl} · ĥ_{jl}ᴴ} ≠ 0    for j ∈ 𝒮_t, j ≠ k
  Specifically:

  E{ĥ_{kl} · ĥ_{jl}ᴴ} = τ_p√(p_k p_j) · R_{kl} · Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ · R_{jl} · Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ ·
  [τ_p² p_k p_j · R_{kl} · Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ · ... ]
  Actually, more precisely, the correlation arises because both estimates are
  linear functions of the same observation y^{pilot}{tl}. When the AP beamforms 
  toward UE k using ĥ{kl}, it inadvertently beamforms toward UE j as well,
  creating coherent interference that scales with N (the number of antennas per
  AP) — at the same rate as the desired signal.

  This is the phenomenon identified by Marzetta (2010) [22] as the fundamental
  limitation of Massive MIMO (later shown to be overcomable by Björnson et al.
  [41]).

  2.5.6 Computational Complexity of Channel Estimation

  The paper notes an important practical detail: the matrix √(p_k τ_p) · R_{kl}
  · Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ in Eq. (3) depends only on channel statistics (R_{kl}, p_k, τ_p,
  Ψ_{tl}), which are constant over many coherence blocks. Therefore:

  Precomputation (done once, per statistics change):
  - Compute Ψ_{tl}⁻¹: requires N³ operations (matrix inversion)
  - Compute √(p_k τ_p) · R_{kl} · Ψ_{tl}⁻¹: requires N³ operations (matrix
  multiplication)
  - Store this N × N matrix

  Per coherence block:
  - Compute y^{pilot}_{tl} (despreading): requires N · τ_p multiplications per
  pilot
  - Compute ĥ_{kl} = (precomputed matrix) · y^{pilot}_{tl}: requires N²
  multiplications per UE

  This is efficient and, under DCC where each AP serves at most τ_p UEs, the
  per-block complexity is bounded by τ_p · N² — independent of K.

  1. HIGH-LEVEL EXPLANATION (Layman's Terms)

  Imagine a large shopping mall with many small Wi-Fi routers (access points)
  mounted on the ceiling throughout the building, and many people with 
  smartphones (users) walking around. This section describes the "rules of the
  game" — how the routers and phones communicate.

  The setup is simple:
  - There are K people (users/UEs), each with a phone that has one antenna.
  - There are L routers (access points/APs), each with N small antennas.
  - The routers are connected to powerful computers called CPUs (Central
  Processing Units) that help coordinate everything.

  The wireless channel between any router and any phone is like an invisible,
  constantly changing tunnel. The signal bounces off walls, furniture, people —
  creating multiple copies of itself that arrive at different times and angles.
  Sometimes these copies add up nicely (strong signal), sometimes they cancel
  out (weak signal). This random fluctuation is called fading.

  How do the routers learn the channel? They can't see the invisible tunnel
  directly. Instead, they use a clever trick called pilot signals: each phone
  sends a known, pre-agreed signal (like a specific ring tone) to the routers.
  Since the routers know exactly what was sent, they can figure out what the
  channel did to the signal — and thus "estimate" the channel.

  The problem: there are only a limited number of unique ring tones (pilot
  signals) — typically far fewer than the number of phones. So multiple phones
  must share the same ring tone. When two phones send the same ring tone
  simultaneously, the routers get confused — they can't perfectly tell which
  channel belongs to which phone. This confusion is called pilot contamination,
  and it's one of the biggest challenges in these systems.

  The time structure: Everything happens in short time windows called coherence 
  blocks. Within each block, the channel stays approximately constant (the
  person hasn't moved much). Each block is divided into three phases:
  1. Pilot phase: Phones send their ring tones so routers can learn channels
  2. Uplink phase: Phones send actual data to routers
  3. Downlink phase: Routers send data to phones

  2. DETAILED TECHNICAL EXPLANATION (PhD Level)

  2.1 System Architecture

  The paper considers a cell-free network with:

  - K single-antenna UEs (User Equipments)
  - L APs (Access Points), each equipped with N antennas
  - M = NL total antennas in the coverage area
  - APs connected to edge-cloud processors called CPUs

  The connection between APs and CPUs is "in an arbitrary fashion" — the paper
  deliberately leaves the network topology flexible (this is elaborated in
  Section V-E). The key architectural point: this setup enables coherent joint 
  transmission and reception across the entire coverage area.

  Definition: The system is called Cell-Free Massive MIMO when both L and K are
  large. While it is often assumed L ≫ K (many more APs than users), the methods
   in this paper hold for any values of L and K.

  Physical interpretation: Unlike cellular networks where each base station
  autonomously serves its own cell, here all APs can potentially cooperate to
  serve any UE. There are no cell boundaries — hence "cell-free."

  2.2 TDD Protocol and Coherence Block Structure

  The system operates in Time-Division Duplex (TDD) mode. This is critical for
  two reasons:

  (a) Channel Reciprocity: In TDD, both uplink and downlink transmissions occur
  on the same frequency but at different times. Due to electromagnetic
  reciprocity, the physical propagation channel is identical in both directions.
   This means:

  h_UL(f) = h_DL(f)    (at the same carrier frequency f)
  This allows APs to estimate the channel from UL pilots and use that estimate
  for DL precoding — without needing separate DL training.

  (b) Pilot Overhead Scales with K, not M: In TDD, only the UEs need to send
  pilots (K pilots), not the APs (which would require M = NL pilots in FDD).
  Since K ≪ M typically, TDD is far more efficient.

  The coherence block is the fundamental time-frequency resource unit. The
  channel is assumed constant within one coherence block (block fading model).
  Each coherence block consists of τ_c channel uses, divided as:

  τ_c = τ_p + τ_u + τ_d
  where:
  - τ_p = channel uses for UL pilot transmission (channel estimation)
  - τ_u = channel uses for UL data transmission
  - τ_d = channel uses for DL data transmission

  Physical basis for τ_c: The coherence block size is the product of the
  coherence time T_c and coherence bandwidth B_c:

  τ_c = B_c × T_c
  where:
  - Coherence time: T_c ≈ c / (f_c · v), where c is the speed of light, f_c is
  the carrier frequency, v is the UE velocity
    - At f_c = 2 GHz, v = 3 km/h (walking): T_c ≈ 25 ms
    - At f_c = 2 GHz, v = 120 km/h (driving): T_c ≈ 0.625 ms
  - Coherence bandwidth: B_c ≈ 1/τ_rms, where τ_rms is the RMS delay spread
    - Urban environment: τ_rms ≈ 1-5 μs → B_c ≈ 200 kHz - 1 MHz

  Example: B_c = 200 kHz, T_c = 1 ms → τ_c = 200 channel uses (the value used in
   the paper's simulations)

  Source: E. Björnson, J. Hoydis, and L. Sanguinetti, "Massive MIMO Networks:
  Spectral, Energy, and Hardware Efficiency," Foundations and Trends in Signal 
  Processing, vol. 11, no. 3-4, 2017, Sec. 2.1.

  2.3 Channel Model: Correlated Rayleigh Fading

  The channel between AP l and UE k is denoted:

  h_{kl} ∈ ℂᴺ
  This is an N-dimensional complex vector (one entry per antenna at AP l). The
  collective channel from ALL APs to UE k is:

  h_k = [h_{k1}ᵀ  h_{k2}ᵀ  ...  h_{kL}ᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ
  where M = NL is the total number of antennas.

  The fading model: In each coherence block, an independent realization is
  drawn:

  h_{kl} ~ 𝒩_C(0, R_{kl})                ... (1-local)
  where R_{kl} ∈ ℂᴺˣᴺ is the spatial correlation matrix.

  What the Gaussian distribution models: The zero-mean circularly symmetric
  complex Gaussian distribution models the small-scale fading — the rapid random
   fluctuations caused by multipath propagation. The physical justification
  comes from Clarke's model (1968):

  - A mobile UE is surrounded by many scatterers
  - Each scatterer reflects the signal, creating a propagation path with random
  phase
  - By the Central Limit Theorem, the superposition of many independent random
  phasors converges to a complex Gaussian distribution
  - Zero mean: no dominant line-of-sight (LOS) component (NLOS scenario)
  - Circular symmetry: the phases of the scatterer contributions are uniformly
  distributed over [0, 2π)

  The envelope |h_{kl,n}| (for each antenna element n) follows a Rayleigh 
  distribution — hence the name "Rayleigh fading."

  Source: R. H. Clarke, "A statistical theory of mobile-radio reception," Bell 
  System Technical Journal, vol. 47, no. 6, pp. 957–1000, 1968.

  2.4 The Spatial Correlation Matrix R_{kl} — Deep Dive

  The positive semi-definite matrix R_{kl} ∈ ℂᴺˣᴺ is perhaps the single most
  important quantity in the entire paper. It encodes everything about the
  large-scale propagation characteristics:

  (a) Geometric pathloss: The average power decay with distance d:

  PL(d) ∝ d^{-α}
  where α is the pathloss exponent (typically 2–5). This affects tr(R_{kl})/N =
  β_{kl}, the average channel gain.

  (b) Shadow fading: Random log-normal variations caused by large obstacles
  (buildings, hills):

  Shadow fading ~ 10^{σ_SF · Z / 10},    Z ~ 𝒩(0,1)
  where σ_SF is the shadow fading standard deviation (typically 4–10 dB).

  (c) Antenna gains: Directional patterns of the AP antennas.

  (d) Spatial channel correlation: The statistical coupling between channel
  coefficients at different antenna elements.

  How spatial correlation arises physically: Consider a Uniform Linear Array
  (ULA) at the AP with antenna spacing d and a UE at angle θ. If multipath
  arrives within angular spread Δ around θ:

  [R_{kl}]_{m,n} = β_{kl} ∫ e^{j2π(m-n)(d/λ)sin(φ)} f(φ) dφ
  where f(φ) is the angular power spectrum and λ is the wavelength. When Δ is
  small (few scatterers near the UE), adjacent antenna elements see highly
  correlated channels. When Δ is large (rich scattering), correlation is low.

  Special cases:
  - Uncorrelated fading (isotropic scattering, large angular spread): R_{kl} =
  β_{kl} · I_N
  - Fully correlated (single path, Δ → 0): R_{kl} = β_{kl} · a(θ)a(θ)ᴴ, where
  a(θ) is the array response vector — rank-1 matrix
  - General case: R_{kl} has rank between 1 and N, with eigenvalues reflecting
  the angular power distribution

  The one-ring model (Shiu et al., 2000): Places scatterers on a ring of radius
  r around the UE at distance D from the AP. The angular spread seen by the AP
  is Δ ≈ arctan(r/D). This model is widely used in Massive MIMO analysis.

  Source: D. Shiu, G. J. Foschini, M. J. Gans, and J. M. Kahn, "Fading
  correlation and its effect on the capacity of multielement antenna systems,"
  IEEE Trans. Commun., vol. 48, no. 3, pp. 502–513, 2000.

  2.5 Independence Across APs

  The paper assumes:

  E{h_{kn} (h_{kl})ᴴ} = 0    for l ≠ n
  That is, the channel vectors from UE k to different APs are statistically 
  independent. The justification: APs are spatially distributed in the network,
  so they see completely different scattering environments. The scatterers near
  AP l and AP n are different, leading to independent fading realizations.

  This means the collective correlation matrix is block-diagonal:

  R_k = diag(R_{k1}, R_{k2}, ..., R_{kL}) ∈ ℂᴹˣᴹ          ... (Eq. 1)
  so that:

  h_k ~ 𝒩_C(0, R_k)
  Why block-diagonal structure matters: It dramatically simplifies channel
  estimation — each AP can estimate its own channels independently, without
  needing information from other APs.

  2.6 Assumption on Correlation Matrix Knowledge

  The paper states: "We assume the spatial correlation matrices {R_{kl}} are
  available wherever needed."

  This is a standard assumption in the Massive MIMO literature. The
  justification is that R_{kl} changes on a much slower timescale than the
  instantaneous channel h_{kl}:

  - h_{kl} changes every coherence block (milliseconds)
  - R_{kl} changes over large-scale coherence intervals (seconds to minutes) —
  only when UE moves significantly or the scattering environment changes

  Therefore, R_{kl} can be estimated using many samples collected over multiple
  coherence blocks. The paper cites four methods for practical correlation
  matrix estimation:

  1. Björnson, Sanguinetti, and Debbah (2016) [36]: Massive MIMO with imperfect
  covariance information
  2. Neumann, Joham, and Utschick (2018) [37]: Covariance matrix estimation in
  Massive MIMO
  3. Upadhya and Vorobyov (2018) [38]: Alternative covariance estimation
  approach
  4. Haghighatshoar and Caire (2017) [39]: Wideband sparse channel estimation

  SECTION II-A: PILOT TRANSMISSION AND CHANNEL ESTIMATION

  2.7 Pilot Signal Structure

  There are τ_p mutually orthogonal pilot signals, each of length τ_p (they form
   a τ_p × τ_p orthogonal matrix). The crucial design choice:

  τ_p is a CONSTANT, independent of K
  This is essential for scalability. If τ_p grew with K, the pilot overhead
  would eventually consume the entire coherence block.

  Pilot assignment: Since there are only τ_p pilots but K > τ_p users (in
  general), multiple users must share the same pilot. Let:

  𝒮_t ⊂ {1, ..., K}
  denote the subset of UEs assigned to pilot t, for t = 1, ..., τ_p.

  Orthogonality: The τ_p pilot signals φ₁, ..., φ_{τ_p} ∈ ℂ^{τ_p} satisfy:

  φᵢᴴ φⱼ = { τ_p  if i = j
            { 0    if i ≠ j
  This means when a UE transmits pilot t and the AP correlates (despreads) the
  received signal with φ_t, all other pilots are perfectly removed — only
  signals from UEs sharing pilot t remain.

  2.8 Received Pilot Signal (Equation 2)

  When UEs in set 𝒮_t simultaneously transmit pilot t, the received signal at AP
   l after despreading is:

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p · p_i) · h_{il} + n_{tl}          ... (Eq.
  2)
  where:
  - p_i is the transmit power of UE i (in Watts)
  - τ_p is the processing gain (from the pilot length)
  - h_{il} ∈ ℂᴺ is the channel from UE i to AP l
  - n_{tl} ~ 𝒩_C(0, σ² I_N) is the thermal noise after despreading

  Derivation of Equation (2):

  Before despreading, the received signal at AP l during the pilot phase is (for
   all τ_p time slots):

  Y^{pilot}_l = Σ_{k=1}^{K} √(p_k) · h_{kl} · φ_{t_k}ᵀ + N_l ∈ ℂᴺˣᵗᵖ
  where t_k is the pilot index assigned to UE k and N_l ∈ ℂᴺˣᵗᵖ is the noise
  matrix.

  To extract the signal from pilot t, we despread (correlate) by multiplying
  with φ_t*/τ_p:

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = Y^{pilot}_l · φ_t* / √1

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = Y^{pilot}_l · φ_t*

                  = Σ_{k: t_k = t} √(p_k) · h_{kl} · τ_p + N_l · φ_t*
  Actually, the standard formulation from [2, Sec. 3] gives equation (2)

  Each UE k assigned to pilot t transmits the signal √(p_k) · φ_t ∈ ℂ^{τ_p} over
   τ_p channel uses. The received signal at AP l across all τ_p pilot channel
  uses is:

  Y_l = Σ_{k=1}^{K} h_{kl} √(p_k) φ_{t_k}ᵀ + N_l ∈ ℂᴺˣᵗᵖ
  Despreading with pilot t (multiply by φ_t*/√τ_p and scale):

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = Y_l · φ_t* / √τ_p · √τ_p = ...
  The standard result after proper normalization gives:

  y^{pilot}_{tl} = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p p_i) h_{il} + n_{tl}
  The key takeaway: After despreading, only UEs sharing the same pilot t

  2.9 MMSE Channel Estimation (Equation 3)

  Given the received pilot signal y^{pilot}{tl}, the MMSE estimate of h{kl} for
  k ∈ 𝒮_t is:

  ĥ_{kl} = √(p_k τ_p) · R_{kl} · Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ · y^{pilot}_{tl}          ... (Eq. 3)
  where:

  Ψ_{tl} = E{y^{pilot}_{tl} (y^{pilot}_{tl})ᴴ} = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} τ_p p_i R_{il} + σ²
   I_N     ... (Eq. 4)
  Detailed Derivation of the MMSE Estimator:

  The MMSE estimate of a random vector x given observation y when both are
  jointly Gaussian is (from Kay, 1993, Ch. 12):

  x̂_{MMSE} = E{x} + C_{xy} C_{yy}⁻¹ (y - E{y})
  For our problem:
  - x = h_{kl} with E{h_{kl}} = 0 and Cov(h_{kl}) = R_{kl}
  - y = y^{pilot}{tl} with E{y^{pilot}{tl}} = 0

  The cross-correlation is:

  C_{xy} = E{h_{kl} (y^{pilot}_{tl})ᴴ}
         = E{h_{kl} (Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p p_i) h_{il} + n_{tl})ᴴ}
         = √(τ_p p_k) · E{h_{kl} h_{kl}ᴴ}     (since channels of different UEs
  are independent)
         = √(τ_p p_k) · R_{kl}
  The auto-correlation is:

  C_{yy} = E{y^{pilot}_{tl} (y^{pilot}_{tl})ᴴ}
         = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} τ_p p_i · E{h_{il} h_{il}ᴴ} + σ² I_N
         = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} τ_p p_i R_{il} + σ² I_N
         = Ψ_{tl}
  Therefore:

  ĥ_{kl} = C_{xy} C_{yy}⁻¹ y = √(p_k τ_p) R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ y^{pilot}_{tl}     ✓
  Properties of the MMSE estimate:

  (i) Distribution of the estimate:

  ĥ_{kl} ~ 𝒩_C(0, p_k τ_p R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ R_{kl})
  The covariance of the estimate is:

  E{ĥ_{kl} ĥ_{kl}ᴴ} = p_k τ_p R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ R_{kl}
  (ii) Estimation error:

  h̃_{kl} = h_{kl} - ĥ_{kl} ~ 𝒩_C(0, C_{kl})
  where:

  C_{kl} = R_{kl} - p_k τ_p R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ R_{kl}
  (iii) Orthogonality principle: The MMSE guarantee is:

  E{h̃_{kl} ĥ_{kl}ᴴ} = 0
  The estimation error is uncorrelated with the estimate. For Gaussian vectors,
  uncorrelated implies independent. This property is crucial for deriving SE
  expressions later.

  (iv) Mean Squared Error (MSE):

  MSE = E{‖h̃_{kl}‖²} = tr(C_{kl}) = tr(R_{kl}) - p_k τ_p tr(R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹
  R_{kl})
  2.10 The Precomputation Insight

  The paper makes an important practical observation. The matrix:

  √(p_k τ_p) R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ ∈ ℂᴺˣᴺ
  in equation (3) depends only on the channel statistics (R_{kl}, Ψ_{tl}, p_k,
  τ_p), which are fixed throughout the communication (they change on a much
  slower timescale than the instantaneous channel). Therefore:

  - This matrix can be precomputed once and stored at AP l
  - The cost of precomputation is O(N³) for the matrix inverse, done once per
  large-scale coherence interval
  - In each coherence block, the estimation only requires:
    - Computing y^{pilot}_{tl}: N τ_p complex multiplications per pilot sequence
    - Multiplying the precomputed matrix with y^{pilot}_{tl}: N² complex
  multiplications per UE

  This is a key practical insight — the "expensive" part (matrix inversion) is
  done rarely, while the per-block computation is cheap.

  2.11 Pilot Contamination — The Two-Headed Problem

  When multiple UEs share pilot t (i.e., |𝒮t| > 1), the MMSE estimate ĥ{kl} is
  contaminated by the channels of the other pilot-sharing UEs. Looking at
  equation (3):

  ĥ_{kl} = √(p_k τ_p) R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ · [Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} √(τ_p p_i) h_{il} + n_{tl}]

  = p_k τ_p R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ h_{kl}                    ← desired component
  + √(p_k τ_p) R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t, i≠k} √(τ_p p_i) h_{il}    ←
  contamination
  + √(p_k τ_p) R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ n_{tl}                 ← noise
  Consequence 1 — Reduced estimation quality: The contaminating channels
  increase the effective noise floor in the estimation. The MSE increases:

  MSE_contaminated > MSE_no_contamination
  Specifically, without contamination (|𝒮_t| = {k} only):

  Ψ_{tl} = τ_p p_k R_{kl} + σ² I_N
  With contamination:

  Ψ_{tl} = τ_p p_k R_{kl} + Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t, i≠k} τ_p p_i R_{il} + σ² I_N
  The additional terms make Ψ_{tl} "larger," which makes Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ "smaller,"
  reducing the estimate quality.

  Consequence 2 — Correlated estimates (coherent interference): The MMSE
  estimates of pilot-sharing UEs become statistically correlated:

  E{ĥ_{kl} ĥ_{il}ᴴ} ≠ 0    for i, k ∈ 𝒮_t, i ≠ k
  This correlation means that when AP l beamforms toward UE k using ĥ_{kl}, it
  inadvertently also beamforms toward UE i. This creates coherent interference
  that scales with the number of antennas N — at the same rate as the desired
  signal. This is the phenomenon identified by Marzetta (2010) as the
  fundamental limitation of Massive MIMO.

  However, Björnson, Hoydis, and Sanguinetti (2018) [41] proved that with
  spatially correlated fading, MMSE combining can separate pilot-sharing UEs if
  their spatial correlation matrices R_{kl} and R_{il} are sufficiently
  different — meaning pilot contamination is NOT a fundamental limit.

  Source: T. L. Marzetta, "Noncooperative Cellular Wireless with Unlimited
  Numbers of Base Station Antennas," IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun., vol. 9, no.
  11, 2010.

  4. MATHEMATICAL DEEP DIVE

  4.1 The Circularly Symmetric Complex Gaussian Distribution

  A random vector h ∈ ℂᴺ is circularly symmetric complex Gaussian with
  covariance R if:

  h ~ 𝒩_C(0, R)
  This means:
  - E{h} = 0
  - E{h hᴴ} = R (covariance matrix)
  - E{h hᵀ} = 0 (pseudo-covariance is zero — this is the circular symmetry
  condition)

  Circular symmetry means that h and e^{jθ} h have the same distribution for any
   θ. Physically, this means the phase of each channel component is uniformly
  distributed — no preferred direction in the complex plane.

  The probability density function is:

  f(h) = 1/(πᴺ det(R)) · exp(-hᴴ R⁻¹ h)
  Key properties used throughout the paper:

  (i) Linear transformation: If h ~ 𝒩_C(0, R) and A is a deterministic matrix,
  then:

  Ah ~ 𝒩_C(0, A R Aᴴ)
  (ii) Conditional distribution: If [h₁ᵀ h₂ᵀ]ᵀ is jointly Gaussian with
  covariance blocks R₁₁, R₁₂, R₂₁, R₂₂, then:

  h₁ | h₂ ~ 𝒩_C(R₁₂ R₂₂⁻¹ h₂,  R₁₁ - R₁₂ R₂₂⁻¹ R₂₁)
  (iii) Independence from uncorrelation: For jointly Gaussian vectors, E{h₁ h₂ᴴ}
   = 0 implies h₁ and h₂ are independent.

  4.2 Eigendecomposition of R_{kl} — Geometric Interpretation

  Since R_{kl} is Hermitian positive semi-definite, it has an
  eigendecomposition:

  R_{kl} = U_{kl} Λ_{kl} U_{kl}ᴴ
  where:
  - U_{kl} = [u₁ u₂ ... u_N] ∈ ℂᴺˣᴺ is unitary (columns are orthonormal
  eigenvectors)
  - Λ_{kl} = diag(λ₁, λ₂, ..., λ_N) with λ₁ ≥ λ₂ ≥ ... ≥ λ_N ≥ 0

  Geometric meaning: The eigenvectors u_n represent the spatial directions from
  which signal energy arrives at the AP. The eigenvalues λ_n represent the power
   along each direction.

  - If the channel has rank r < N (only r significant eigenvalues), the channel
  lives in an r-dimensional subspace. MMSE estimation exploits this by
  projecting away the noise in the (N-r) noise-only dimensions.
  - β_{kl} = tr(R_{kl})/N = (Σₙ λ_n)/N is the average channel gain per antenna.

  4.3 Step-by-Step Derivation: MMSE Estimation Quality

  Let's derive the estimate covariance and error covariance explicitly.

  Starting point: ĥ_{kl} = √(p_k τ_p) R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ y^{pilot}_{tl}

  Estimate covariance:

  E{ĥ_{kl} ĥ_{kl}ᴴ} = p_k τ_p R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ E{y^{pilot}_{tl}
  (y^{pilot}_{tl})ᴴ} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ R_{kl}
                      = p_k τ_p R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ Ψ_{tl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ R_{kl}
                      = p_k τ_p R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ R_{kl}
  Let's call this:

  B_{kl} ≜ p_k τ_p R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ R_{kl}          (estimate covariance)
  Error covariance:

  C_{kl} = R_{kl} - B_{kl} = R_{kl} - p_k τ_p R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ R_{kl}
  Normalized MSE per antenna:

  NMSE = tr(C_{kl}) / tr(R_{kl}) = 1 - p_k τ_p tr(R_{kl} Ψ_{tl}⁻¹ R_{kl}) /
  tr(R_{kl})
  4.4 Special Case: Uncorrelated Fading (N = 1 or R_{kl} = β_{kl} I_N)

  When R_{kl} = β_{kl} I_N (uncorrelated fading) or N = 1 (single antenna),
  everything simplifies to scalars.

  For N = 1:
  - h_{kl} ~ 𝒩C(0, β{kl}) is a scalar
  - Ψ_{tl} = Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮t} τ_p p_i β{il} + σ² is a scalar
  - ĥ_{kl} = √(p_k τ_p) β_{kl} / Ψ_{tl} · y^{pilot}_{tl}

  The estimate covariance becomes:

  E{|ĥ_{kl}|²} = p_k τ_p β_{kl}² / Ψ_{tl} = p_k τ_p β_{kl}² / (Σ_{i ∈ 𝒮_t} τ_p
  p_i β_{il} + σ²)
  And the error variance:

  E{|h̃_{kl}|²} = β_{kl} - p_k τ_p β_{kl}² / Ψ_{tl}
  This is the classical result from the original Cell-Free Massive MIMO paper
  [5].

  5. NUMERICAL EXAMPLES

  Example 1: MMSE Channel Estimation with Single-Antenna AP

  Setup: N = 1, τ_p = 5, AP l, UE k on pilot t = 1.

  Channel statistics:
  - β_{kl} = 0.01 (= -20 dB, moderate distance)
  - β_{il} = 0.001 (= -30 dB, far UE sharing same pilot)
  - p_k = p_i = 100 mW = 0.1 W
  - σ² = -94 dBm = 3.98 × 10⁻¹⁰ W (for 20 MHz bandwidth)

  Step 1: Compute Ψ_{tl}:

  Ψ_{tl} = τ_p p_k β_{kl} + τ_p p_i β_{il} + σ²
         = 5 × 0.1 × 0.01 + 5 × 0.1 × 0.001 + 3.98 × 10⁻¹⁰
         = 0.005 + 0.0005 + 0.000000000398
         ≈ 0.0055
  (Note: the noise term is negligible compared to the pilot signals — the system
   is interference-limited, not noise-limited.)

  Step 2: Compute estimate quality:

  E{|ĥ_{kl}|²} = p_k τ_p β_{kl}² / Ψ_{tl} = 0.5 × 0.0001 / 0.0055 = 0.00909
  Step 3: Compute estimation error:

  E{|h̃_{kl}|²} = β_{kl} - E{|ĥ_{kl}|²} = 0.01 - 0.00909 = 0.00091
  Step 4: Normalized MSE:

  NMSE = 0.00091 / 0.01 = 0.091 = 9.1%
  Interpretation: Even with pilot contamination from one other UE, the
  estimation is quite good (91% of the channel energy is captured). This is
  because the contaminating UE has 10× weaker channel.

  Example 2: Impact of Pilot Contamination Severity

  Same setup, but now vary β_{il} (the contaminator's channel gain):

  ┌───────────────────┬────────┬──────────────┬───────┬─────────────────────┐
  │      β_{il}       │ Ψ_{tl} │ E{|ĥ_{kl}|²} │ NMSE  │   Interpretation    │
  ├───────────────────┼────────┼──────────────┼───────┼─────────────────────┤
  │ 0 (no             │ 0.005  │ 0.01000      │ 0.0%  │ Perfect estimation  │
  │ contamination)    │        │              │       │ (noise negligible)  │
  ├───────────────────┼────────┼──────────────┼───────┼─────────────────────┤
  │ 0.001 (-30 dB)    │ 0.0055 │ 0.00909      │ 9.1%  │ Mild contamination  │
  ├───────────────────┼────────┼──────────────┼───────┼─────────────────────┤
  │ 0.005 (-23 dB)    │ 0.0075 │ 0.00667      │ 33.3% │ Moderate            │
  │                   │        │              │       │ contamination       │
  ├───────────────────┼────────┼──────────────┼───────┼─────────────────────┤
  │ 0.01 (-20 dB,     │ 0.0100 │ 0.00500      │ 50.0% │ Severe              │
  │ same as UE k)     │        │              │       │ contamination       │
  ├───────────────────┼────────┼──────────────┼───────┼─────────────────────┤

  Key insight: Pilot contamination is most severe when the contaminating UE has
  a comparable or stronger channel to the AP. If the contaminating UE is far
  away (weak β_{il}), the impact is small. This is why Cell-Free Massive MIMO,
  with its distributed APs, naturally reduces pilot contamination — each AP
  mainly "sees" nearby UEs.

  Example 3: Multi-Antenna AP (N = 4)

  Setup: N = 4 antennas, τ_p = 5, 2 UEs sharing pilot t.

  Spatial correlation matrices (using a simplified model):

  R_{kl} = β_{kl} · R_spatial,k    where β_{kl} = 0.01

  R_{il} = β_{il} · R_spatial,i    where β_{il} = 0.008
  Suppose the UEs have different angular directions:

  R_spatial,k = [1.0  0.8  0.4  0.1;
                 0.8  1.0  0.8  0.4;
                 0.4  0.8  1.0  0.8;
                 0.1  0.4  0.8  1.0]

  R_spatial,i = [1.0  0.3  0.0  -0.1;
                 0.3  1.0  0.3   0.0;
                 0.0  0.3  1.0   0.3;
                -0.1  0.0  0.3   1.0]
  These represent UEs arriving from different angular directions (different
  correlation structures).

  Ψ_{tl} = τ_p p_k R_{kl} + τ_p p_i R_{il} + σ² I₄
         = 0.5 × 0.01 × R_spatial,k + 0.5 × 0.008 × R_spatial,i + σ² I₄
  The MMSE estimator can exploit the different spatial signatures of the two
  UEs:
  - Even though both UEs send the same pilot, their channels arrive from
  different directions
  - The MMSE estimator uses the spatial correlation matrix R_{kl} as prior 
  information to project the received signal onto UE k's subspace
  - This partially resolves the pilot contamination — something not possible
  with single-antenna APs!

  This is why the paper considers multi-antenna APs (N > 1) — spatial
  correlation provides additional degrees of freedom for separating
  pilot-sharing UEs.

  6. PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  Problem 1: Basic MMSE Estimation

  Consider N = 1, τ_p = 10, one AP, one UE (no pilot contamination). β_{kl} =
  0.005, p_k = 200 mW, σ² = 10⁻⁹ W.

  (a) Compute Ψ_{tl}.
  (b) Compute E{|ĥ_{kl}|²}.
  (c) Compute the NMSE.
  (d) What happens to the NMSE as p_k → ∞?

  Solutions:

  (a) Ψ_{tl} = τ_p p_k β_{kl} + σ² = 10 × 0.2 × 0.005 + 10⁻⁹ = 0.01 + 10⁻⁹ ≈
  0.01

  (b) E{|ĥ_{kl}|²} = p_k τ_p β_{kl}² / Ψ_{tl} = 0.2 × 10 × 0.000025 / 0.01 =
  0.005

  (c) NMSE = (β_{kl} - E{|ĥ_{kl}|²}) / β_{kl} = (0.005 - 0.005) / 0.005 ≈ 0
  (essentially perfect)

  (d) As p_k → ∞: Ψ_{tl} → τ_p p_k β_{kl}, and E{|ĥ_{kl}|²} → p_k τ_p β_{kl}² /
  (τ_p p_k β_{kl}) = β_{kl}. So NMSE → 0. Without pilot contamination, infinite 
  SNR gives perfect estimation.

  Problem 2: Pilot Contamination Effect

  Same as Problem 1, but now a second UE i shares the same pilot with β_{il} =
  0.005 (same channel gain) and p_i = 200 mW.

  (a) Compute the new Ψ_{tl}.
  (b) Compute E{|ĥ_{kl}|²} and NMSE.
  (c) What happens to the NMSE as p_k, p_i → ∞ together?

  Solutions:

  (a) Ψ_{tl} = τ_p p_k β_{kl} + τ_p p_i β_{il} + σ² = 0.01 + 0.01 + 10⁻⁹ ≈ 0.02

  (b) E{|ĥ_{kl}|²} = 2 × 0.000025 / 0.02 = 0.0025. NMSE = (0.005 - 0.0025)/0.005
   = 50%

  (c) As p_k, p_i → ∞: Ψ_{tl} → τ_p(p_k β_{kl} + p_i β_{il}), and:

  E{|ĥ_{kl}|²} → p_k τ_p β_{kl}² / (τ_p(p_k β_{kl} + p_i β_{il}))
                 = β_{kl}² / (β_{kl} + β_{il})
                 = β_{kl}/2   (when β_{kl} = β_{il})

  NMSE → 1 - β_{kl}/(2β_{kl}) = 1/2 = **50%**
  Key insight: With pilot contamination from an equally strong UE, the NMSE
  saturates at 50% regardless of how much power you use. Pilot contamination is 
  an interference-limited phenomenon — more power does not help!

  Problem 3: Computational Complexity

  An AP has N = 4 antennas and serves UEs on τ_p = 10 pilots.

  (a) How many complex multiplications are needed per coherence block for the
  despreading operation (computing y^{pilot}_{tl} for all pilots)?
  (b) How many for the MMSE estimation of all served UEs (matrix-vector
  multiplications only, assuming precomputed matrices)?
  (c) What is the total per AP? How does it scale with K?

  Solutions:

  (a) Despreading: N × τ_p multiplications per pilot × τ_p pilots = N × τ_p² = 4
   × 100 = 400

  (Actually, more precisely: N τ_p per pilot sequence, and there are τ_p
  sequences, so total = N τ_p × τ_p = N τ_p². But the paper states N τ_p per
  pilot sequence.)

  Correct interpretation: y^{pilot}_{tl} = Y_l · φ_t* requires N τ_p
  multiplications per pilot sequence t. For all τ_p pilots: N τ_p² = 4 × 100 = 
  400 multiplications.

  (b) Per UE: N² = 16 multiplications (matrix-vector product). Maximum UEs per
  AP = τ_p = 10. Total: 10 × 16 = 160 multiplications.

  (c) Total per AP: 400 + 160 = 560 complex multiplications per coherence block.
   This does NOT depend on K — it only depends on τ_p and N, both constants.
  This is the scalability feature.

  SECTION III: DEFINITION OF SCALABILITY AND REVIEW OF THE ORIGINAL FORM OF 
  CELL-FREE MASSIVE MIMO

  Paper: "Scalable Cell-Free Massive MIMO Systems" — Björnson & Sanguinetti 
  (2020)

  ---
  1. HIGH-LEVEL EXPLANATION (Layman's Terms)

  Imagine you run a pizza delivery company. You have L delivery drivers (access
  points) and K customers (users).

  The original Cell-Free system works like this: every driver is responsible for
   delivering to every customer. Each driver must:
  - Know the address of every customer (channel estimation for all K)
  - Prepare a separate order for every customer (precoding for all K)
  - Report back to headquarters about every customer (fronthaul for all K)
  - Be part of a master plan that optimizes delivery across all customers (power
   control for all K)

  When you have 10 customers, this works fine. But imagine 10,000 customers —
  each driver now needs 10,000 addresses, 10,000 prepared orders, sends 10,000
  reports... The workload per driver explodes. This is not scalable.

  What scalability means: A system is scalable if adding more customers does not
   increase the workload of any individual driver. Each driver should only
  handle a fixed, bounded number of customers — say, the 5–10 nearest ones. The
  total work across all drivers grows (you need more drivers), but each driver's
   individual burden stays constant.

  Section III does three things:
  1. Shows exactly how the original Cell-Free Massive MIMO processes uplink and
  downlink signals
  2. Formally defines "scalability" as a mathematical property
  3. Proves the original system fails all four scalability criteria

  ---
  2. DETAILED TECHNICAL EXPLANATION (PhD Level)

  2.1 Context: The Original Cell-Free Massive MIMO

  The original Cell-Free Massive MIMO was proposed by Ngo, Ashikhmin, Yang,
  Larsson, and Marzetta in 2017 [5], [6], [30]. The defining characteristic: all
   L APs simultaneously serve all K UEs in both uplink (UL) and downlink (DL).
  There is a single CPU connected to all APs.

  This section reviews that system model to expose its scalability flaws.

  2.2 Section III-A: Uplink Data Transmission

  The Received UL Signal (Equation 5)

  During UL data transmission, UE i transmits signal sᵢ ∈ ℂ with power pᵢ (i.e.,
   𝔼{|sᵢ|²} = pᵢ). The received signal at AP l is:

  $$\textbf{y}ₗᵘˡ = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \textbf{h}ᵢₗ sᵢ + \textbf{n}ₗ$$

  In proper Unicode:

  **y**ₗᵘˡ  =  ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ  **h**ᵢₗ sᵢ  +  **n**ₗ                    ... (Eq. 5)
  where:
  - yₗᵘˡ ∈ ℂᴺ is the received signal vector at AP l (N entries, one per antenna)
  - hᵢₗ ∈ ℂᴺ is the channel from UE i to AP l
  - sᵢ ∈ ℂ is the transmitted signal from UE i with power pᵢ
  - nₗ ~ 𝒩ℂ(0, σ²Iₙ) is additive white Gaussian noise

  Physical interpretation: AP l's antennas receive a superposition of all K
  users' signals, each distorted by its own channel hᵢₗ, plus thermal noise. The
   AP cannot directly separate the signals — it needs signal processing
  (combining) to extract each user's data.

  Network-Wide UL Decoding (Equation 6)

  In the original Cell-Free Massive MIMO, the decoding of UE k's signal is done
  network-wide across all APs:

  Step 1: AP l selects a receive combining vector vₖₗ ∈ ℂᴺ for UE k.

  Step 2: AP l computes the local inner product vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ ∈ ℂ (a scalar).

  Step 3: The network estimates sₖ by summing the local contributions from all L
   APs:

  ŝₖ  =  ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ  **v**ₖₗᴴ **y**ₗᵘˡ                              ... (Eq. 6, first
   line)
  Expanding by substituting Eq. (5):

  ŝₖ  =  ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ  **v**ₖₗᴴ  ( ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ **h**ᵢₗ sᵢ  +  **n**ₗ )

  =  ( ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ **v**ₖₗᴴ **h**ₖₗ ) sₖ   +   ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ ( ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ **v**ₖₗᴴ **h**ᵢₗ ) sᵢ
     +   ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ **v**ₖₗᴴ **n**ₗ
  Now define the collective combining vector and channel:

  **v**ₖ  =  [**v**ₖ₁ᵀ  ...  **v**ₖₗᵀ]ᵀ  ∈  ℂᴹ
  **h**ₖ  =  [**h**ₖ₁ᵀ  ...  **h**ₖₗᵀ]ᵀ  ∈  ℂᴹ
  **n**   =  [**n**₁ᵀ   ...  **n**ₗᵀ ]ᵀ  ∈  ℂᴹ
  Then Eq. (6) simplifies to:

  ŝₖ  =  **v**ₖᴴ **h**ₖ · sₖ   +   ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ  **v**ₖᴴ **h**ᵢ · sᵢ   +   **v**ₖᴴ
  **n**
          ╰─── desired signal ───╯   ╰──── multi-user interference ────╯   ╰──
  noise ──╯
  Critical observation: This is mathematically equivalent to a single-cell
  Massive MIMO system with M = NL antennas serving K users! The "combined
  channels" are {vₖᴴ hᵢ : i = 1,...,K}. All single-cell Massive MIMO SE results
  directly apply.

  The only difference from cellular Massive MIMO: the combining vectors {vₖₗ}
  should preferably use only local CSI — i.e., AP l should design vₖₗ using only
   the channel estimates it can acquire locally from the pilot phase.

  MR Combining — The Default Choice

  The most popular choice in the original papers [5], [26], [27] is Maximum 
  Ratio (MR) combining:

  **v**ₖₗ  =  ĥₖₗ                                                ... (MR
  combining)
  where ĥₖₗ is the MMSE estimate from Eq. (3) of Section II.

  Why MR?
  - Maximizes the desired signal power |vₖᴴ hₖ|² in the numerator of the SINR
  - Uses only local CSI (each AP uses its own estimate)
  - Very low complexity: no matrix inversions needed
  - Yields closed-form SE expressions

  Why MR is suboptimal: It completely ignores the interference terms ∑ᵢ≠ₖ |vₖᴴ
  hᵢ|² in the SINR denominator. It maximizes signal power but does nothing to
  suppress interference. Later work by Björnson & Sanguinetti [7] showed that
  MMSE combining can achieve 2–3× higher SE than MR.

  Source: H. Q. Ngo, A. Ashikhmin, H. Yang, E. G. Larsson, and T. L. Marzetta,
  "Cell-Free Massive MIMO versus Small Cells," IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun.,
  vol. 16, no. 3, pp. 1834–1850, 2017.

  2.3 Section III-A: Downlink Data Transmission

  The Received DL Signal (Equation 7)

  Let wᵢₗ ∈ ℂᴺ denote the precoding vector that AP l assigns to UE i. During DL
  transmission, the received signal at UE k is:

  yₖᵈˡ  =  ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ  **h**ₖₗᴴ  ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ  **w**ᵢₗ ςᵢ   +   nₖ       ... (Eq. 7, first
  form)
  Using the collective channel hₖ and collective precoding wᵢ = [wᵢ₁ᵀ ... wᵢₗᵀ]ᵀ
   ∈ ℂᴹ:

  yₖᵈˡ  =  **h**ₖᴴ  ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ  **w**ᵢ ςᵢ   +   nₖ                 ... (Eq. 7,
  compact form)
  where:
  - ςᵢ ∈ ℂ is the unit-power data signal for UE i (𝔼{|ςᵢ|²} = 1)
  - wᵢ = [wᵢ₁ᵀ ... wᵢₗᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ is the collective precoding vector
  - nₖ ~ 𝒩ℂ(0, σ²) is receiver noise (scalar, since UE has 1 antenna)

  Expanding the desired and interference terms:

  yₖᵈˡ  =  **h**ₖᴴ **w**ₖ · ςₖ   +   ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ  **h**ₖᴴ **w**ᵢ · ςᵢ   +   nₖ
            ╰── desired signal ──╯   ╰──── inter-user interference ────╯   ╰
  noise ╯
  Mathematical equivalence: Since hₖ ~ 𝒩ℂ(0, Rₖ) with block-diagonal Rₖ, this is
   equivalent to a DL single-cell Massive MIMO system with correlated fading [2,
   Sec. 2.3.1].

  MR Precoding — The Default Choice (Equation 8)

  The standard choice is MR precoding (also called conjugate beamforming):

  **w**ᵢₗ  =  √ρᵢ  ·  ĥᵢₗ  /  √( 𝔼{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²} )                ... (Eq. 8)
  where:
  - ρᵢ ≥ 0 is the transmit power allocated to UE i
  - ĥᵢₗ is the MMSE channel estimate
  - √(𝔼{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²}) is a normalization factor ensuring 𝔼{‖wᵢₗ‖²} = ρᵢ

  Why this normalization? The MR precoding vector points in the direction of the
   estimated channel. The normalization ensures the average transmit power
  allocated to UE i at AP l equals ρᵢ. Without normalization, the transmit power
   would depend on the random channel realization, making power control
  impossible.

  DL Power constraints: Unlike UL where each UE has its own power constraint pᵢ,
   in DL each AP has a total power budget ρ. AP l must satisfy:

  ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ  𝔼{‖**w**ᵢₗ‖²}  ≤  ρ
  This is a per-AP constraint, not a sum-power constraint. It couples all UEs
  served by the same AP — increasing power to one UE reduces the budget for
  others at that AP. This makes DL power optimization fundamentally harder than
  in co-located Massive MIMO.

  Source: E. Nayebi, A. Ashikhmin, T. L. Marzetta, H. Yang, and B. D. Rao,
  "Precoding and Power Optimization in Cell-Free Massive MIMO Systems," IEEE 
  Trans. Wireless Commun., vol. 16, no. 7, 2017.

  2.4 Section III-B: The Scalability Issue — Definition 1

  This is the conceptual heart of the entire paper. The authors propose a
  rigorous test for scalability.

  The thought experiment: Let K → ∞ (the number of UEs grows without bound).
  Check which of the following four tasks remain practically implementable at
  each AP:

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  FOUR SCALABILITY CRITERIA (per AP, as K → ∞)                   │
  ├──────┬───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  1.  │  Signal processing for channel estimation                │
  │  2.  │  Signal processing for data reception/transmission       │
  │  3.  │  Fronthaul signaling for data and CSI sharing            │
  │  4.  │  Power control optimization                              │
  └──────┴───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  Definition 1 (Scalability): A Cell-Free Massive MIMO network is scalable if
  all four tasks have finite complexity and resource requirements for each AP as
   K → ∞.

  In mathematical notation: for each AP l, the computational cost and fronthaul
  requirements must satisfy:

  Complexityₗ(K)  =  𝒪(1)     as K → ∞
  Fronthaulₗ(K)   =  𝒪(1)     as K → ∞
  That is, the per-AP requirements must be bounded by a constant independent of
  K.

  2.5 Why the Original Cell-Free Massive MIMO FAILS All Four Criteria

  The paper systematically proves that the original system is unscalable:

  Failure 1: Channel Estimation

  AP l must compute estimates {ĥₖₗ : k = 1, ..., K} for all K UEs.

  - Per-UE cost: N² complex multiplications (matrix-vector product with
  precomputed matrix)
  - Total cost at AP l: K · N²
  - As K → ∞: complexity → ∞ ✗

  Failure 2: Signal Processing (Precoding/Combining)

  UL: AP l needs to compute {vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ : k = 1,...,K} using K different
  combining vectors.
  - Per-UE cost: N multiplications (inner product)
  - Total: K · N → ∞ as K → ∞ ✗

  DL: AP l needs to form the transmitted signal:

  **x**ₗᵈˡ  =  ∑ₖ₌₁ᴷ  **w**ₖₗ ςₖ
  - Each term requires N multiplications (scalar × vector)
  - The summation has K terms → ∞ as K → ∞ ✗

  Even with the lowest-complexity MR precoding, AP l must:
  1. Take K channel estimates
  2. Normalize each as in Eq. (8)
  3. Form the sum of K precoded signals

  All require 𝒪(K) operations.

  Failure 3: Fronthaul Signaling

  DL: AP l needs to receive K data signals {ςₖ : k = 1,...,K} from the CPU.
  - Fronthaul load: K complex scalars per time slot → ∞ as K → ∞ ✗

  UL: AP l must forward K processed signals {vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ : k = 1,...,K} to the
  CPU.
  - Fronthaul load: K complex scalars per time slot → ∞ as K → ∞ ✗

  Failure 4: Power Control

  Any non-trivial network-wide power optimization has complexity that grows with
   K:

  - Linear programs: 𝒪(K²·⁵) to 𝒪(K³·⁵) (interior point methods)
  - Convex programs: similar polynomial growth
  - Max-min fairness: requires solving a sequence of convex feasibility
  problems, each with K variables

  Even the simplest non-trivial optimization grows at least linearly with K → ∞
  as K → ∞ ✗

  2.6 Remark 1: What Scalability Actually Means in Practice

  The paper includes a crucial remark that clarifies what the definition does
  and does not require:

  What is kept finite: The complexity and fronthaul per individual AP.

  What is allowed to grow: The total network complexity and fronthaul, which
  will be proportional to L (number of APs).

  Why this makes sense: Since LN ≫ K for the network to be practically useful, K
   → ∞ requires also L → ∞ (more users means you need more APs to serve them).
  The total complexity diverges, but as long as:
  - Each AP has a local processor of sufficient but finite capacity
  - Each AP has a fronthaul connection of sufficient but finite bandwidth

  ...then each AP can carry out its tasks irrespective of how large the network 
  becomes.

  Analogy: Think of the internet. Each router has finite processing power and a
  finite number of ports. The total internet complexity is enormous, but each
  router handles a bounded number of connections. The internet scales because
  complexity is distributed, not centralized.

  This is exactly the paradigm shift this paper proposes for Cell-Free Massive
  MIMO: move from centralized (everything at one CPU) to distributed (each AP
  handles bounded work).

  3.2 The SE Bound History

  The SE bounds used in this paper come from a rich tradition:

  The Use-and-then-Forget (UatF) Bound (used for UL SE in the distributed case):
  - Origin: Implicit in Marzetta (2010) [22]; formalized by Marzetta, Larsson,
  Yang, and Ngo in Fundamentals of Massive MIMO (Cambridge, 2016)
  - Idea: The channel estimate is "used" to design vₖ and then "forgotten." The
  decoder treats 𝔼{vₖᴴ hₖ} as the known deterministic effective channel.
  Everything else (estimation error, interference, noise) is treated as
  worst-case uncorrelated Gaussian noise.
  - Why it's used: It yields closed-form SEs that depend only on channel
  statistics (not instantaneous realizations), enabling tractable analysis.
  - Reference: [2, Theorem 4.4]: Björnson, Hoydis, Sanguinetti, Foundations and 
  Trends in Signal Processing, 2017.

  The Hardening Bound (used for DL SE):
  - Origin: Used since early Massive MIMO papers [22], [53], [54]
  - Idea: The UE knows only the average effective channel gain 𝔼{hₖᴴ wₖ}, not
  the instantaneous value. This is reasonable when channel hardening occurs —
  i.e., the effective channel gain concentrates around its mean.
  - When it's tight: In co-located Massive MIMO with many antennas (strong
  hardening). Can be loose in Cell-Free Massive MIMO when N (antennas per AP) is
   small [47].
  - Reference: [2, Theorem 4.6].

  Ergodic capacity (the true maximum achievable rate) is unknown for these
  multi-user MIMO systems. All SE expressions in the paper are lower bounds on
  the true capacity — guaranteed achievable rates.

  3.3 Scalability in Computer Science vs. Wireless Networks

  The concept of scalability is well-established in computer science:
  - Horizontal scalability: Add more machines to handle more load (each machine
  does bounded work)
  - Vertical scalability: Make individual machines more powerful (eventually
  hits limits)

  Wireless network scalability is analogous:
  - Scalable: Each AP does 𝒪(1) work; add more APs for more users (horizontal)
  - Unscalable: Each AP does 𝒪(K) work; must make APs more powerful as K grows
  (vertical)

  The original CF-mMIMO requires vertical scaling — fundamentally unsustainable.

  3.4 Why MR Was the Default (and Why It's Not Enough)

  MR combining/precoding was the standard in early Cell-Free papers because:
  1. Simplicity: vₖₗ = ĥₖₗ requires no matrix inversions
  2. Local CSI only: Each AP uses only its own estimate
  3. Closed-form SEs: Enables analytical tractability
  4. Historical precedent: Marzetta's original Massive MIMO paper [22] used MR
  and showed it's asymptotically optimal as M → ∞ in the single-cell case

  However, MR has a fundamental flaw: it ignores interference. The SINR with MR
  combining is:

  SINRₖᴹᴿ  =  pₖ |**v**ₖᴴ **h**ₖ|²  /  ( ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ pᵢ |**v**ₖᴴ **h**ᵢ|²  +  σ²
  ‖**v**ₖ‖² )
  MR maximizes the numerator |vₖᴴ hₖ|² but does nothing to minimize the
  interference terms |vₖᴴ hᵢ|² in the denominator. MMSE combining optimally
  balances both.

  Source: E. Björnson and L. Sanguinetti, "Making Cell-Free Massive MIMO
  Competitive with MMSE Processing and Centralized Implementation," IEEE Trans. 
  Wireless Commun., vol. 19, no. 1, pp. 77–90, 2020.

  1. HIGH-LEVEL EXPLANATION (Layman's Terms)

  Imagine a restaurant kitchen where every chef prepares food for every table.
  With 10 tables, this is manageable. But when the restaurant grows to 1,000
  tables, each chef now has to remember 1,000 orders, prepare 1,000 dishes
  simultaneously, and communicate about 1,000 plates with the head chef. The
  workload per chef grows linearly with the number of tables — eventually, the
  chefs are overwhelmed and the kitchen collapses.

  That's the scalability problem with the original Cell-Free Massive MIMO.

  In the original system (proposed in 2017 by Ngo et al.), every access point
  (AP) was responsible for every single user. When users send data to the APs
  (uplink), each AP processes signals from ALL users. When the APs send data to
  users (downlink), each AP transmits signals intended for ALL users. Every AP
  also estimates the channels of ALL users and shares data about ALL users over
  its connection (fronthaul) to a central computer (CPU).

  This works fine in small simulations with 10-50 users. But in a real city with
   thousands or millions of users, the work each AP must do grows without bound.
   The system is unscalable.

  What does "scalable" mean? This section provides a precise definition: a
  system is scalable if no individual AP's workload increases when you add more
  users to the network. The total network work can grow (you add more APs), but
  each AP's job stays constant.

  The section first shows you the original system model (how signals flow in UL
  and DL), then systematically proves it fails ALL four scalability criteria.

  2. DETAILED TECHNICAL EXPLANATION (PhD Level)

  2.1 Section III-A: Uplink Data Transmission (Original Model)

  During UL data transmission, every UE transmits simultaneously. The received
  signal at AP l is:

  $$\textbf{y}ₗᵘˡ = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \textbf{h}ᵢₗ sᵢ + \textbf{n}ₗ$$

  Written in proper Unicode:

  ▎ yₗᵘˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + nₗ  ∈ ℂᴺ        ... (Eq. 5)

  where:
  - yₗᵘˡ ∈ ℂᴺ is the received signal vector at AP l (N entries, one per antenna)
  - hᵢₗ ∈ ℂᴺ is the channel from UE i to AP l
  - sᵢ ∈ ℂ is the signal transmitted by UE i, with power E{|sᵢ|²} = pᵢ
  - nₗ ~ 𝒩ᶜ(0, σ²Iₙ) is the thermal noise at AP l

  Physical meaning: Each AP receives a superposition of ALL K users' signals,
  corrupted by noise. This is the fundamental challenge — the signals are mixed
  together and must be separated.

  2.1.1 Network-Wide UL Decoding (Equation 6)

  In the original Cell-Free Massive MIMO [5], [30], all APs cooperate to decode
  every UE's signal. The process:

  Step 1: AP l selects a receive combining vector vₖₗ ∈ ℂᴺ for UE k.

  Step 2: AP l computes the local inner product vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ (a scalar).

  Step 3: The network estimates sₖ by summing the local inner products from ALL
  L APs:

  ▎ ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ        ... (Eq. 6, first line)

  Substituting equation (5) into this:

  ▎ ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ (∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + nₗ)

  ▎ = (∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ hₖₗ) sₖ + ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ (∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ hᵢₗ) sᵢ + ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ nₗ

  Now define the collective vectors by stacking all per-AP vectors:

  ▎ vₖ = [vₖ₁ᵀ  vₖ₂ᵀ  ...  vₖₗᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ     (collective combining vector)
  ▎ hₖ = [hₖ₁ᵀ  hₖ₂ᵀ  ...  hₖₗᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ     (collective channel vector)
  ▎ n = [n₁ᵀ  n₂ᵀ  ...  nₗᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ          (collective noise vector)

  Then equation (6) becomes compactly:

  ▎ ŝₖ = vₖᴴ hₖ sₖ + ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ vₖᴴ hᵢ sᵢ + vₖᴴ n        ... (Eq. 6)
  ▎       ⎣⎦      ⎣________⎦      ⎣⎦
  ▎       desired signal    multi-user interference      noise

  Critical observation from the paper: This expression is mathematically
  identical to a single-cell Massive MIMO system with combined effective
  channels {vₖᴴ hᵢ : i = 1,...,K}. The entire Cell-Free network looks like one
  giant virtual base station with M = NL antennas. All the SE results from the
  Massive MIMO literature [2, Sec. 4.1] directly apply.

  The difference from cellular: In a cellular system, the combining vector vₖ
  would be designed using full CSI of all channels at one co-located base
  station. In Cell-Free, each AP can only use its local CSI — the channels it
  can estimate from the UL pilots. This constrains the design of vₖₗ to depend
  only on {ĥᵢₗ : i = 1,...,K}.

  2.1.2 MR Combining: The Default Choice

  The most popular combining in the original Cell-Free papers is Maximum Ratio 
  (MR) combining:

  ▎ vₖₗ = ĥₖₗ        (MR combining)

  where ĥₖₗ is the MMSE estimate of hₖₗ from Section II-A.

  Why MR?
  - Extremely simple — just use the channel estimate directly
  - Each AP only needs its own local CSI
  - Maximizes the desired signal power |vₖᴴ hₖ|²
  - Yields closed-form SE expressions (Corollary 2 in the paper)

  Why MR is suboptimal:
  - It completely ignores interference — the denominator of the SINR
  - It does not suppress signals from other UEs
  - More advanced schemes like MMSE combining provide 2-3× higher SE [7]

  Historical note: MR combining is also known as the matched filter (from
  radar/detection theory) or conjugate beamforming (when used in DL). It dates
  back to the 1940s in radar signal processing. In the Massive MIMO context,
  Marzetta (2010) [22] showed that MR becomes near-optimal when M → ∞ in an
  interference-free setting, but this optimality does not hold with realistic
  pilot contamination and finite M.

  2.2 Section III-A: Downlink Data Transmission (Original Model)

  Let wᵢₗ ∈ ℂᴺ denote the precoding vector that AP l assigns to UE i. During DL,
   the received signal at UE k is:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ wᵢₗ ςᵢ + nₖ        ... (Eq. 7)

  Using collective vectors wₖ = [wₖ₁ᵀ  ...  wₖₗᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = hₖᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ wᵢ ςᵢ + nₖ        ... (Eq. 7, compact)

  where:
  - ςᵢ ∈ ℂ is the unit-power data signal for UE i (E{|ςᵢ|²} = 1)
  - wᵢ ∈ ℂᴹ is the collective precoding vector for UE i
  - nₖ ~ 𝒩ᶜ(0, σ²) is the receiver noise at UE k

  Expanding the terms:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = hₖᴴ wₖ ςₖ + ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ hₖᴴ wᵢ ςᵢ + nₖ
  ▎         ⎣_⎦       ⎣____________⎦      ⎣⎦
  ▎         desired signal    inter-user interference   noise

  Again, this is mathematically equivalent to a single-cell DL Massive MIMO
  system [2, Sec. 4.3].

  2.2.1 MR Precoding (Equation 8)

  The most popular choice in the original Cell-Free papers is MR precoding (also
   called conjugate beamforming):

  ▎ wᵢₗ = √ρᵢ · ĥᵢₗ / √(E{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²})        ... (Eq. 8)

  where:
  - ρᵢ ≥ 0 is the transmit power allocated to UE i
  - ĥᵢₗ is the MMSE channel estimate
  - The denominator √(E{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²}) normalizes the precoding vector so that ρᵢ
  directly controls the transmit power

  Why normalization? Without it, the transmit power would depend on the random
  channel realization. The normalization ensures:

  ▎ E{‖wᵢₗ‖²} = ρᵢ · E{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²} / E{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²} = ρᵢ

  So ρᵢ is exactly the average transmit power allocated to UE i at AP l.

  Key difference from UL: In the UL, each UE has its own power constraint (pᵢ).
  In the DL, each AP has a power constraint — the total power transmitted by AP
  l must satisfy:

  ▎ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ E{‖wᵢₗ‖²} ≤ ρₘₐₓ

  This is a per-AP constraint that couples the power allocation across all UEs
  served by the same AP — making DL power optimization fundamentally harder than
   UL.

  2.3 Section III-B: The Scalability Issue — Formal Definition

  This is the most important conceptual contribution of the section. The paper
  asks: What happens as K → ∞?

  Four tasks must remain implementable at each AP:

  1. Channel estimation: Computing ĥₖₗ for all UEs k
  2. Signal processing: Computing combining vectors vₖₗ (UL) or precoding
  vectors wₖₗ (DL), and applying them
  3. Fronthaul signaling: Sharing data and CSI between APs and CPUs
  4. Power control: Optimizing the transmit power allocation

  ▎ Definition 1 (Scalability): A Cell-Free Massive MIMO network is scalable if 
  all four tasks have finite complexity and resource requirements for each AP as
   K → ∞.

  Let me unpack what "finite as K → ∞" means: If a task requires O(K) operations
   per AP, then as K doubles, each AP's workload doubles. As K → ∞, the workload
   → ∞. This is unscalable. If a task requires O(1) operations per AP (constant,
   independent of K), it is scalable.

  2.4 Why the Original Cell-Free Massive MIMO Fails — All Four Criteria

  The paper proves that the original system fails every single criterion:

  Failure 1: Channel Estimation is Unscalable

  AP l must compute channel estimates {ĥₖₗ : k = 1, ..., K} for ALL K UEs.

  From Section II-A, each MMSE estimate requires N² complex multiplications
  (matrix-vector product with the precomputed statistical matrix). For K UEs:

  ▎ Complexity = K · N²  →  ∞ as K → ∞  ❌

  Why ALL K? Because in the original model, every AP serves every UE. Even if
  most UEs are far from AP l (very weak channels), the AP still computes their
  estimates.

  Failure 2: Signal Processing is Unscalable

  Downlink: AP l must form the transmitted signal:

  ▎ xₗᵈˡ = ∑ₖ₌₁ᴷ wₖₗ ςₖ

  This summation requires K vector additions (each of dimension N). Even with
  the simplest MR precoding, the AP must:
  - Take K channel estimates
  - Normalize each one as in Eq. (8)
  - Sum K precoding vectors multiplied by data symbols

  ▎ Complexity = O(K · N) → ∞ as K → ∞  ❌

  Uplink: Similarly, AP l must compute K inner products {vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ : k = 1, ...,
   K}:

  ▎ Complexity = K · N → ∞ as K → ∞  ❌

  Failure 3: Fronthaul Signaling is Unscalable

  Downlink: AP l must receive K data signals {ςₖ : k = 1, ..., K} from the CPU.

  ▎ Fronthaul load = K complex scalars per time slot → ∞ as K → ∞  ❌

  Uplink: AP l must forward K processed signals {vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ : k = 1, ..., K} to
  the CPU.

  ▎ Fronthaul load = K complex scalars per time slot → ∞ as K → ∞  ❌

  Failure 4: Power Control is Unscalable

  Any non-trivial network-wide power optimization involves K optimization
  variables (one per UE, at minimum). Even convex optimization problems have
  polynomial complexity:

  - Linear programs: O(K³·⁵) via interior-point methods
  - Second-order cone programs (SOCPs): O(K³) to O(K⁴)
  - Semidefinite programs (SDPs): even worse

  ▎ Complexity = O(Kᵅ) for some α > 0 → ∞ as K → ∞  ❌

  Specific examples from the literature:
  - Max-min fairness optimization in [5], [30], [31]: bisection over SOCPs →
  O(K³) per iteration
  - Sum SE maximization: often non-convex, even harder

  2.5 Remark 1: What Does Total Complexity Look Like?

  The paper provides a crucial clarification in Remark 1:

  ▎ "The total computational complexity and fronthaul requirements will then be 
  independent of K but proportional to L."

  Under scalability:
  - Per AP: O(1) as K → ∞ ✓
  - Total network: O(L) as K → ∞ — grows with the number of APs

  Since the network must have LN ≫ K to be useful, K → ∞ also requires L → ∞. So
   the total complexity does diverge. But since each AP has its own local
  processor and its own fronthaul link of finite capacity, each AP can handle
  its tasks regardless of network size.

  Analogy: A highway system is scalable if each toll booth can handle its
  traffic regardless of how many total cars are on the highway. The total number
   of toll booths must grow with traffic, but each booth's workload is bounded.

  3.1 The Birth of Cell-Free Massive MIMO (2017)

  The original Cell-Free Massive MIMO was proposed in two key papers:

  Ngo, Ashikhmin, Yang, Larsson, and Marzetta (2017) [5]:
  - Title: "Cell-Free Massive MIMO versus Small Cells"
  - Published: IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun., vol. 16, no. 3
  - System: M single-antenna APs, K single-antenna UEs, all connected to one CPU
  - Processing: MR combining (UL), conjugate beamforming (DL)
  - Power control: Max-min fairness optimization
  - Key result: Cell-Free Massive MIMO provides 5× higher 95%-likely SE than
  small cells
  - Implicit assumption: ALL APs serve ALL UEs → unscalable

  Nayebi, Ashikhmin, Marzetta, Yang, and Rao (2017) [6]:
  - Title: "Precoding and Power Optimization in Cell-Free Massive MIMO Systems"
  - Published: IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun., vol. 16, no. 7
  - Extended [5] with zero-forcing precoding and more sophisticated power
  control
  - Key result: ZF significantly outperforms conjugate beamforming
  - Still assumed all APs serve all UEs → unscalable

  Björnson and Sanguinetti (2020) [7]:
  - Title: "Making Cell-Free Massive MIMO Competitive with MMSE Processing and
  Centralized Implementation"
  - Published: IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun., vol. 19, no. 1
  - Introduced L-MMSE and centralized MMSE combining
  - Showed 2-3× SE improvement over MR
  - Still not scalable

  3.2 SE Bounds: A Critical Toolkit

  The SE expressions used throughout this paper come from the Massive MIMO
  literature. Understanding them is essential:

  The "Use-and-then-Forget" (UatF) Bound (used for UL with distributed
  processing and for DL):

  Origin: The technique dates to Marzetta (2010) [22] and was formalized in [2,
  Th. 4.4]. The idea:

  1. The combining/precoding is designed using the channel estimate ĥₖ ("use")
  2. For SE computation, we "forget" the estimate — treat E{vₖᴴ hₖ} as a known
  deterministic constant
  3. All remaining terms (estimation error, interference) are treated as
  worst-case uncorrelated noise

  This yields a lower bound on the ergodic capacity that does not require the UE
   to know the instantaneous effective channel — only its mean. The bound is:

  ▎ SEₖ = (τᵤ/τᶜ) log₂(1 + SINRₖ)

  where SINRₖ depends only on statistical expectations (no instantaneous channel
   knowledge needed at the decoder).

  Why UatF? It gives achievable rates with practical receivers. The UE can
  decode without estimating the instantaneous effective channel — it only needs
  the long-term average, which can be learned over many coherence blocks.

  The Hardening Bound (used for DL, Proposition 3):

  This is a variant of UatF where the UE knows only E{hₖᴴ wₖ}. It is tight when
  channel hardening occurs — when the effective channel gain hₖᴴ wₖ has small
  fluctuations around its mean. In co-located Massive MIMO with M → ∞, this
  holds strongly. In Cell-Free Massive MIMO with small N per AP, hardening can
  be weak, making the bound loose [47].

  Source: E. Björnson, J. Hoydis, and L. Sanguinetti, "Massive MIMO Networks,"
  Foundations and Trends in Signal Processing, 2017, Theorems 4.1, 4.4, 4.6.

  4. MATHEMATICAL DEEP DIVE

  4.1 The UL Signal Model — Component by Component

  Starting from the received signal at AP l:

  ▎ yₗᵘˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + nₗ

  Let's decompose sᵢ = √pᵢ · s̃ᵢ where s̃ᵢ has unit power (E{|s̃ᵢ|²} = 1). Then:

  ▎ yₗᵘˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ √pᵢ · hᵢₗ · s̃ᵢ + nₗ

  The AP applies combining vₖₗᴴ to get the scalar:

  ▎ vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ √pᵢ · vₖₗᴴ hᵢₗ · s̃ᵢ + vₖₗᴴ nₗ

  Summing over all APs:

  ▎ ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ = vₖᴴ hₖ · √pₖ · s̃ₖ + ∑ᵢ≠ₖ vₖᴴ hᵢ · √pᵢ · s̃ᵢ + vₖᴴ n

  The instantaneous SINR at the CPU for UE k (treating interference as noise):

  ▎ SINRₖᵘˡ = pₖ |vₖᴴ hₖ|² / (∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ pᵢ |vₖᴴ hᵢ|² + σ² ‖vₖ‖²)

  The achievable SE (treating the estimate as known, the error and interference
  as noise):

  ▎ SEₖᵘˡ = (τᵤ/τᶜ) · E{log₂(1 + SINRₖᵘˡ)}  [bit/s/Hz]

  The pre-log factor τᵤ/τᶜ accounts for the fraction of the coherence block used
   for UL data (the pilot phase τₚ and DL phase τ_d are overhead for UL).

  4.2 The DL Signal Model — Component by Component

  Each AP l transmits:

  ▎ xₗᵈˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ wᵢₗ ςᵢ

  The received signal at UE k:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ xₗᵈˡ + nₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ wᵢₗ ςᵢ + nₖ

  Rearranging (swap summation order):

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ (∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ wᵢₗ) ςᵢ + nₖ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hₖᴴ wᵢ · ςᵢ + nₖ

  Separating the desired and interfering terms:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = hₖᴴ wₖ · ςₖ + ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ hₖᴴ wᵢ · ςᵢ + nₖ

  The effective channel gain for the desired signal is: hₖᴴ wₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ wₖₗ

  This is a sum of L terms, one per AP. Each term is the inner product between
  the channel and the precoding vector at that AP.

  4.3 MR Precoding Normalization — Derivation of Equation (8)

  The MR precoding vector before normalization is:

  ▎ w̃ᵢₗ = ĥᵢₗ

  The average power this would transmit:

  ▎ E{‖w̃ᵢₗ‖²} = E{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²} = tr(E{ĥᵢₗ ĥᵢₗᴴ}) = tr(pᵢτₚ Rᵢₗ Ψₜᵢₗ⁻¹ Rᵢₗ)

  To make the transmit power equal to ρᵢ, we normalize:

  ▎ wᵢₗ = √ρᵢ · ĥᵢₗ / √(E{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²})        ... (Eq. 8)

  Verification:

  ▎ E{‖wᵢₗ‖²} = ρᵢ · E{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²} / E{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²} = ρᵢ  ✓

  4.4 Why Does the Equivalence to Cellular Massive MIMO Hold?

  The paper states that (6) and (7) are "mathematically equivalent" to
  single-cell Massive MIMO. Let me make this precise.

  For UL: The signal model in (6) can be written as:

  ▎ ŝₖ = vₖᴴ hₖ sₖ + ∑ᵢ≠ₖ vₖᴴ hᵢ sᵢ + vₖᴴ n

  This is identical to a co-located Massive MIMO base station with M antennas,
  receiving from K single-antenna UEs, where:
  - The base station's channel to UE k is hₖ ∈ ℂᴹ
  - The base station applies combining vₖ ∈ ℂᴹ
  - The channel correlation is Rₖ = diag(Rₖ₁,...,Rₖₗ) — block-diagonal due to AP
   independence

  For DL: Similarly, (7) matches a DL Massive MIMO system with correlated fading
   [2, Sec. 2.3.1], where the base station with M antennas precodes for K users.

  The key difference: In cellular Massive MIMO, all M antennas are co-located,
  so full CSI is available at one point. In Cell-Free, the M antennas are
  distributed across L APs, so each AP only has local CSI. This constrains the
  combining/precoding design.

  5. NUMERICAL EXAMPLES

  Example 1: Quantifying Unscalability

  Setup: L = 400 APs, N = 1 antenna each, τₚ = 10, τᶜ = 200, bandwidth = 20 MHz.

  Question: What are the per-AP requirements for K = 100 vs K = 10,000 users?

  (a) Channel estimation:
  - Per UE: N² = 1 multiplication (N = 1, so it's just scalar multiplication)
  - K = 100: 100 multiplications per AP per coherence block
  - K = 10,000: 10,000 multiplications per AP per coherence block
  - Growth factor: 100×

  (b) Fronthaul (UL, per coherence block):
  - Each AP sends K complex scalars per UL time slot
  - UL time slots: τᵤ = 190
  - K = 100: 100 × 190 = 19,000 complex scalars
  - K = 10,000: 10,000 × 190 = 1,900,000 complex scalars
  - Growth factor: 100×

  (c) Fronthaul bandwidth requirement:
  - Each complex scalar = 2 real numbers × (e.g., 16 bits each) = 32 bits
  - Coherence block duration ≈ τᶜ / bandwidth = 200 / (20 × 10⁶) = 10 μs
  - K = 100: 19,000 × 32 bits / 10 μs = 60.8 Mbit/s per AP
  - K = 10,000: 1,900,000 × 32 bits / 10 μs = 6,080 Mbit/s = 6.08 Gbit/s per AP

  At K = 10,000, each AP needs a 6 Gbit/s fronthaul link just for UL data — this
   is impractical for most deployments!

  Example 2: Demonstrating the UL Signal Model

  Setup: L = 3 APs, N = 1 (single antenna), K = 2 UEs.

  Channel realizations (scalar for N = 1):

  h₁₁ = 0.8 + 0.3j    (UE 1 → AP 1)
  h₁₂ = 0.2 − 0.1j    (UE 1 → AP 2)
  h₁₃ = 0.05 + 0.02j   (UE 1 → AP 3)
  h₂₁ = 0.1 − 0.2j    (UE 2 → AP 1)
  h₂₂ = 0.6 + 0.5j    (UE 2 → AP 2)
  h₂₃ = 0.3 − 0.1j    (UE 2 → AP 3)
  Transmitted signals: s₁ = 1 + 0j, s₂ = 0 + 1j (unit power, for simplicity)
  Noise: nₗ = 0 (ignore noise for this example)

  Step 1: Received signals at each AP (Eq. 5):

  y₁ᵘˡ = h₁₁·s₁ + h₂₁·s₂ = (0.8+0.3j)·1 + (0.1−0.2j)·j = 0.8+0.3j + 0.2+0.1j =
  1.0+0.4j
  y₂ᵘˡ = h₁₂·s₁ + h₂₂·s₂ = (0.2−0.1j)·1 + (0.6+0.5j)·j = 0.2−0.1j − 0.5+0.6j =
  −0.3+0.5j
  y₃ᵘˡ = h₁₃·s₁ + h₂₃·s₂ = (0.05+0.02j)·1 + (0.3−0.1j)·j = 0.05+0.02j + 0.1+0.3j
   = 0.15+0.32j
  Step 2: MR combining for UE 1 (v₁ₗ = ĥ₁ₗ, assume perfect CSI: ĥ₁ₗ = h₁ₗ):

  v₁₁ᴴ y₁ᵘˡ = (0.8−0.3j)(1.0+0.4j) = 0.8+0.32j−0.3j−0.12j² = 0.8+0.12+0.02j =
  0.92+0.02j
  v₁₂ᴴ y₂ᵘˡ = (0.2+0.1j)(−0.3+0.5j) = −0.06+0.1j−0.03j+0.05j² = −0.06−0.05+0.07j
   = −0.11+0.07j
  v₁₃ᴴ y₃ᵘˡ = (0.05−0.02j)(0.15+0.32j) = 0.0075+0.016j−0.003j−0.0064j² =
  0.0075+0.0064+0.013j = 0.0139+0.013j
  Step 3: Network-wide estimate (Eq. 6):

  ŝ₁ = ∑ₗ₌₁³ v₁ₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ = 0.92+0.02j + (−0.11+0.07j) + 0.0139+0.013j
      = 0.8239 + 0.103j
  Verification — desired signal component: v₁ᴴ h₁ = ∑ₗ |h₁ₗ|² = 0.73 + 0.05 +
  0.0029 = 0.7829

  This is close to the real part of ŝ₁ (which should be ≈ |h₁|² × s₁ = 0.7829),
  showing MR combining effectively extracts the desired signal. The imaginary
  part (0.103j) is primarily interference from UE 2.

  Example 3: Per-AP Power Constraint in DL

  Setup: AP l serves K = 3 UEs with MR precoding. Maximum AP power: ρₘₐₓ = 1 W.

  Channel estimates: ĥ₁ₗ = 0.5, ĥ₂ₗ = 0.3, ĥ₃ₗ = 0.1 (scalar, N = 1)

  E{|ĥᵢₗ|²} ≈ |ĥᵢₗ|² for this example: 0.25, 0.09, 0.01.

  MR precoding (Eq. 8): wᵢₗ = √ρᵢ · ĥᵢₗ / √(E{|ĥᵢₗ|²})

  Power constraint: ∑ᵢ₌₁³ ρᵢ ≤ ρₘₐₓ = 1 W

  With equal power allocation: ρ₁ = ρ₂ = ρ₃ = 1/3 W

  w₁ₗ = √(1/3) × 0.5/√0.25 = √(1/3) × 1.0 = 0.577
  w₂ₗ = √(1/3) × 0.3/√0.09 = √(1/3) × 1.0 = 0.577
  w₃ₗ = √(1/3) × 0.1/√0.01 = √(1/3) × 1.0 = 0.577
  Notice: After normalization, all UEs get the same precoding magnitude —
  despite UE 3 having a much weaker channel. The normalization in Eq. (8)
  removes the channel quality from the precoding direction. The power ρᵢ
  controls the power allocation, not the precoding structure.

  Total power: ∑ᵢ E{|wᵢₗ|²} = 3 × (1/3) = 1.0 W = ρₘₐₓ ✓

  6. PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  Problem 1: UL Combining with MR

  Consider L = 2 APs, N = 1, K = 2 UEs, p₁ = p₂ = 0.1 W, σ² = 10⁻³ W.

  Channels: h₁₁ = 0.6, h₁₂ = 0.1, h₂₁ = 0.2, h₂₂ = 0.7.
  Assume perfect CSI (ĥᵢₗ = hᵢₗ) and MR combining (vₖₗ = ĥₖₗ).

  (a) Compute ŝ₁ using Eq. (6) when s₁ = 1, s₂ = −1, and noise is zero.
  (b) Compute the instantaneous SINR for UE 1: SINR₁ = p₁|v₁ᴴ h₁|² / (p₂|v₁ᴴ
  h₂|² + σ²‖v₁‖²)
  (c) Compute the SE: SE₁ = log₂(1 + SINR₁)

  Solutions:

  (a) Collective vectors: v₁ = [0.6, 0.1]ᵀ, h₁ = [0.6, 0.1]ᵀ, h₂ = [0.2, 0.7]ᵀ

  ŝ₁ = **v**₁ᴴ(**h**₁s₁ + **h**₂s₂)
      = **v**₁ᴴ **h**₁ · s₁ + **v**₁ᴴ **h**₂ · s₂
      = (0.6×0.6 + 0.1×0.1)(1) + (0.6×0.2 + 0.1×0.7)(−1)
      = (0.36 + 0.01)(1) + (0.12 + 0.07)(−1)
      = 0.37 − 0.19 = **0.18**
  (b) SINR₁ = p₁|v₁ᴴh₁|² / (p₂|v₁ᴴh₂|² + σ²‖v₁‖²)

  |**v**₁ᴴ**h**₁|² = |0.37|² = 0.1369
  |**v**₁ᴴ**h**₂|² = |0.19|² = 0.0361
  ‖**v**₁‖² = 0.36 + 0.01 = 0.37

  SINR₁ = 0.1 × 0.1369 / (0.1 × 0.0361 + 0.001 × 0.37)
         = 0.01369 / (0.00361 + 0.00037)
         = 0.01369 / 0.00398
         = **3.44** (5.37 dB)
  (c) SE₁ = log₂(1 + 3.44) = log₂(4.44) = 2.15 bit/s/Hz

  (Note: this is instantaneous SE for one channel realization; the ergodic SE
  would average over many.)

  Problem 2: Scalability Check

  A Cell-Free network has L = 200 APs, N = 4, τₚ = 10.

  (a) In the original model (all APs serve all UEs), how many channel estimates
  must AP l compute when K = 500?
  (b) What is the per-AP UL fronthaul load (complex scalars per time slot) when
  K = 500?
  (c) If we use DCC and each AP serves at most τₚ = 10 UEs, answer (a) and (b)
  again.
  (d) Compute the fronthaul bandwidth for (b) and (c), assuming 32 bits per
  complex scalar, 20 MHz bandwidth, τᶜ = 200.

  Solutions:

  (a) Original: K = 500 estimates, each requiring N² = 16 mults. Total: 500 × 16
   = 8,000 multiplications.

  (b) Original: K = 500 complex scalars per UL time slot.

  (c) DCC: At most τₚ = 10 estimates. Total: 10 × 16 = 160 multiplications (50×
  reduction!).
      Fronthaul: At most 10 complex scalars per UL time slot (50× reduction!).

  (d) Coherence block duration: τᶜ / BW = 200/(20×10⁶) = 10 μs. UL time slots
  per block: τᵤ = 190.

  - Original (b): 500 × 190 × 32 bits / 10 μs = 304 Mbit/s per AP
  - DCC (c): 10 × 190 × 32 bits / 10 μs = 6.08 Mbit/s per AP

  The DCC version needs only a standard Ethernet link; the original needs a
  high-speed fiber link!

  Problem 3: DL Per-AP Power Constraint

  AP l has ρₘₐₓ = 1 W and serves K = 4 UEs with MR precoding.

  Channel gains: β₁ₗ = 0.01, β₂ₗ = 0.005, β₃ₗ = 0.002, β₄ₗ = 0.001.

  (a) If using equal power allocation (ρᵢ = ρₘₐₓ/K), what power does each UE
  get?
  (b) If using the proportional allocation ρᵢₗ = ρₘₐₓ · √βᵢₗ / ∑ⱼ √βⱼₗ (from Eq.
   43 of the paper), compute each ρᵢₗ.
  (c) Which scheme gives more power to weaker UEs?

  Solutions:

  (a) Equal: ρᵢ = 1/4 = 0.25 W for all i.

  (b) Compute √βᵢₗ: √0.01 = 0.1, √0.005 = 0.0707, √0.002 = 0.0447, √0.001 =
  0.0316.
  Sum = 0.2470

  ρ₁ₗ = 1.0 × 0.1/0.2470 = 0.405 W
  ρ₂ₗ = 1.0 × 0.0707/0.2470 = 0.286 W
  ρ₃ₗ = 1.0 × 0.0447/0.2470 = 0.181 W
  ρ₄ₗ = 1.0 × 0.0316/0.2470 = 0.128 W
  Check: 0.405 + 0.286 + 0.181 + 0.128 = 1.000 W ✓

  (c) Equal power gives more to weaker UEs (same power regardless of channel
  quality). The proportional scheme (b) gives more power to stronger UEs — UE 1
  gets 3.2× more power than UE 4. This seems counterintuitive, but the rationale
   is: the precoding already steers the signal; the power allocation ensures the
   strongest channels are exploited most efficiently. Fairness can be addressed
  through max-min power control.

  SECTION IV: DYNAMIC COOPERATION CLUSTERING (DCC)

  Paper: "Scalable Cell-Free Massive MIMO Systems" — Björnson & Sanguinetti 
  (2020)

  ---
  1. HIGH-LEVEL EXPLANATION (Layman's Terms)

  Remember the pizza delivery analogy from Section III? Every driver was
  delivering to every customer — that was the scalability disaster.

  Dynamic Cooperation Clustering (DCC) is the solution framework. The idea is
  elegantly simple:

  Each customer is served only by the few nearest drivers. Each driver only
  serves the few nearest customers.

  Imagine you live in a large city. You order pizza. Instead of every driver in
  the city preparing your order, only the 2-3 drivers nearest to your house are
  assigned to you. Similarly, each driver is only responsible for the 5-10
  customers nearest to them.

  The key word is "dynamic" — the assignments change based on:
  - Where customers are located (if you move, different drivers serve you)
  - Channel conditions (if a building blocks the signal from one AP, another AP
  takes over)
  - Network load (busy APs hand off users to less busy neighbors)

  The "clusters" overlap. Your house might be served by Drivers A, B, and C.
  Your neighbor might be served by Drivers B, C, and D. Driver B and C serve
  both of you — the clusters overlap. There are no rigid boundaries like in
  traditional cells.

  The magic is a simple on/off switch: for each AP-UE pair, there's a switch
  that says "AP l serves UE i" (switch ON) or "AP l does NOT serve UE i" (switch
   OFF). These switches are captured mathematically by diagonal matrices Dᵢₗ.

  This section shows that:
  1. The original Cell-Free Massive MIMO (where every switch is ON) is just one
  extreme case
  2. Traditional cellular networks (where only one AP per UE is ON) is another
  extreme
  3. DCC encompasses everything in between
  4. If you limit how many "ON switches" each AP has, you get scalability

  ---
  2. DETAILED TECHNICAL EXPLANATION (PhD Level)

  2.1 The DCC Framework — Origin and Core Idea

  The DCC framework was proposed by Björnson, Jaldén, Bengtsson, and Ottersten
  (2011) [17] to enable:

  ▎ "unified analysis of anything from interference channels to Network MIMO"

  The genius of the framework is that it captures ALL possible cooperation
  strategies using a single mathematical object: a set of diagonal selection 
  matrices.

  Definition: For each UE i = 1,...,K and each AP l = 1,...,L, define a diagonal
   matrix:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ ∈ ℂᴺˣᴺ     (N × N diagonal matrix)

  The j-th diagonal element of Dᵢₗ is:

  ▎ [Dᵢₗ]ⱼⱼ = { 1  if the j-th antenna of AP l is allowed to transmit to / 
  decode from UE i
  ▎              { 0  otherwise

  Physical meaning: Dᵢₗ is a binary switch matrix that controls which antennas
  at AP l participate in serving UE i.

  2.2 Special Cases of Dᵢₗ

  Case 1: Full service — AP l serves UE i with all N antennas:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = Iₙ     (N × N identity matrix — all antennas ON)

  Case 2: No service — AP l does not serve UE i at all:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ     (N × N zero matrix — all antennas OFF)

  Case 3: Partial service — Only some antennas of AP l serve UE i:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = diag(1, 0, 1, 0)     (example for N = 4: antennas 1 and 3 are ON)

  In practice, the paper mostly uses Case 1 or Case 2 (full AP-level selection),
   leading to the simplified Eq. (11). Per-antenna selection is theoretically
  possible but rarely used.

  2.3 The Block-Diagonal Aggregate Matrix Dₖ

  For UE k, stack all L matrices into a block-diagonal aggregate:

  ▎ Dₖ = diag(Dₖ₁, Dₖ₂, ..., Dₖₗ) ∈ ℂᴹˣᴹ

  This M × M block-diagonal matrix acts as a network-wide mask that selects
  which of the M = NL total antennas participate in serving UE k.

  2.4 Two Key Set Definitions

  Set ℳᵢ — the set of APs serving UE i:

  ▎ ℳᵢ ⊂ {1, ..., L}     where l ∈ ℳᵢ  ⟺  Dᵢₗ ≠ 0ₙ

  This is the "AP cluster for UE i" — the user-centric cluster shown in Fig. 2
  of the paper.

  Set 𝒟ₗ — the set of UEs served by AP l (Equation 12):

  ▎ 𝒟ₗ = { i : tr(Dᵢₗ) ≥ 1, i ∈ {1, ..., K} }          ... (Eq. 12)

  This is the "UE set of AP l" — the set of UEs that AP l needs to allocate
  resources to.

  These two sets are duals of each other: ℳᵢ tells you "which APs serve UE i"
  (UE's perspective), while 𝒟ₗ tells you "which UEs does AP l serve" (AP's
  perspective).

  2.5 Section IV-A: UL and DL Data Transmission with DCC

  The DCC framework modifies the signal models from Section III by inserting the
   selection matrices.

  2.5.1 UL Signal Model with DCC (Equation 9)

  The physical received signal at AP l does not change — all APs still
  physically receive signals from all UEs:

  ▎ yₗᵘˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + nₗ          (same as Eq. 5)

  However, the DCC framework changes which APs participate in decoding. Only APs
   in ℳₖ process UE k's signal. The estimate of sₖ becomes:

  ▎ ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ          ... (Eq. 9, first line)

  Using the collective vectors:

  ▎ ŝₖ = vₖᴴ Dₖ hₖ sₖ + ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ vₖᴴ Dₖ hᵢ sᵢ + vₖᴴ Dₖ n     ... (Eq. 9)

  Compare with the original Eq. (6):

  ┌────────────────┬──────────────────┬───────────────────┐
  │                │ Original (Eq. 6) │    DCC (Eq. 9)    │
  ├────────────────┼──────────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ Desired signal │ vₖᴴ hₖ sₖ        │ vₖᴴ Dₖ hₖ sₖ      │
  ├────────────────┼──────────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ Interference   │ ∑ᵢ≠ₖ vₖᴴ hᵢ sᵢ   │ ∑ᵢ≠ₖ vₖᴴ Dₖ hᵢ sᵢ │
  ├────────────────┼──────────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ Noise          │ vₖᴴ n            │ vₖᴴ Dₖ n          │
  └────────────────┴──────────────────┴───────────────────┘

  The insertion of Dₖ is the only difference. It zeroes out the contributions
  from APs not serving UE k.

  Key insight about the UL: Even though AP l physically receives signals from
  ALL UEs, when Dₖₗ = 0ₙ, the contribution vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ = 0ₙᵀ yₗᵘˡ = 0. So AP l
   simply does not participate in decoding UE k.

  2.5.2 DL Signal Model with DCC (Equation 10)

  The received DL signal at UE k becomes:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ Dᵢₗ wᵢₗ ςᵢ + nₖ          ... (Eq. 10)

  Using collective vectors:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = hₖᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ Dᵢ wᵢ ςᵢ + nₖ          ... (Eq. 10, compact)

  Key mechanism: When Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ, then Dᵢₗ wᵢₗ = 0ₙ — AP l does not transmit to UE
   i. Only APs with l ∈ ℳᵢ (i.e., Dᵢₗ ≠ 0ₙ) actually transmit the signal
  intended for UE i.

  Similarly for UL combining: vᵢₗᴴ Dᵢₗ = 0ₙᵀ when Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ, so only APs in ℳᵢ
  apply receive combining for UE i.

  2.6 DCC Unifies Multiple Architectures

  The beauty of DCC is that it captures many different network architectures by
  choosing {D₁, ..., Dₖ} differently:

  Architecture 1: Original Cell-Free Massive MIMO

  ▎ Dᵢ = Iₘ   ∀ i          (i.e., Dᵢₗ = Iₙ ∀ i, l)

  Every AP serves every UE with all antennas. Eq. (9) reduces to Eq. (6) and Eq.
   (10) reduces to Eq. (7).

  Architecture 2: User-Centric Cell-Free Massive MIMO [27]

  Let ℳᵢ ⊂ {1, ..., L} be the subset of APs serving UE i. Then:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = { Iₙ   if l ∈ ℳᵢ              ... (Eq. 11)
  ▎          { 0ₙ   if l ∉ ℳᵢ

  This is exactly the setup considered in [17] (the original DCC paper) and
  later in [27] (Buzzi & D'Andrea's user-centric Cell-Free approach).

  Architecture 3: Fog Massive MIMO [29]

  Bursalioglu et al. (2019) proposed the "Fog Massive MIMO" architecture for
  DL-only transmission. It uses 𝒜ᵢ instead of ℳᵢ to denote the AP subset, but is
   otherwise identical to Eq. (11) — a direct instance of DCC.

  Architecture 4: Traditional Cellular Network (not in paper, but implied)

  Each UE is served by exactly one AP (the "home" base station):

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = { Iₙ   if l = l*(i)     (one specific AP)
  ▎          { 0ₙ   otherwise

  This gives |ℳᵢ| = 1 for all i — each UE has a cluster of size 1 (its cell).

  Architecture 5: Network-Centric Clustering (disjoint, like 4G CoMP)

  APs are partitioned into non-overlapping groups {𝒞₁, ..., 𝒞ₘ}:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = { Iₙ   if l ∈ 𝒞ₘ and UE i is in cluster m's coverage
  ▎          { 0ₙ   otherwise

  The clusters are disjoint: 𝒞ₘ ∩ 𝒞ₘ' = ∅ for m ≠ m'. This creates hard cluster
  boundaries with inter-cluster interference.

  Unified view:

  Interference        Cellular          Disjoint           User-Centric
  Full Cell-Free
  Channel            (1 AP/UE)         CoMP Clusters       DCC/CF-mMIMO
  (All APs/All UEs)
    ←───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  ───→
   Dᵢₗ mostly 0     Dᵢₗ=Iₙ for 1 AP   Dᵢₗ=Iₙ in cluster  Dᵢₗ=Iₙ for nearby
  Dᵢ=Iₘ ∀i
   No cooperation     Minimal coop.      Moderate coop.      Smart coop.
    Full coop.
                                                             (SCALABLE)
    (UNSCALABLE)

  2.7 Section IV-B: A Partial Solution to the Scalability Issue

  Now comes the critical scalability result.

  Lemma 1

  ▎ If the cardinality |𝒟ₗ| is constant as K → ∞ for l = 1, ..., L, then the 
  Cell-Free Massive MIMO network with DCC satisfies the first three conditions 
  in Definition 1.

  Proof (from the paper):

  If |𝒟ₗ| is constant as K → ∞, then:

  Criterion 1 (Channel estimation): AP l only needs to compute channel estimates
   for |𝒟ₗ| UEs (not all K UEs). Complexity = |𝒟ₗ| · N² = O(1) as K → ∞. ✓

  Criterion 2 (Signal processing): AP l only computes combining/precoding
  vectors for |𝒟ₗ| UEs:
  - UL: |𝒟ₗ| inner products, each N multiplications → |𝒟ₗ| · N = O(1) ✓
  - DL: sum of |𝒟ₗ| precoded signals → |𝒟ₗ| · N = O(1) ✓

  Criterion 3 (Fronthaul): AP l only sends/receives data for |𝒟ₗ| UEs → O(1)
  scalars per time slot ✓

  What about Criterion 4 (Power control)? Lemma 1 does NOT cover this. Power
  control scalability must be achieved separately — for example, by using
  heuristic algorithms like equal power allocation or the fractional allocation
  in Eq. (43).

  The practical question: How do we choose the sets 𝒟ₗ in a scalable way while
  guaranteeing that every UE is served by at least one AP? This is addressed in
  Section V.

  3.2 The 4G CoMP Failure — Lessons Learned

  4G LTE-Advanced (3GPP Release 11) introduced Coordinated Multi-Point (CoMP)
  with three modes:

  1. Joint Transmission (JT): Multiple cells transmit the same data to a
  cell-edge UE
  2. Dynamic Point Selection (DPS): Only one cell transmits at a time, but the
  cell can change dynamically
  3. Coordinated Scheduling/Beamforming (CS/CB): Cells coordinate scheduling but
   don't share data

  In DCC notation:
  - JT: Dᵢₗ = Iₙ for all l in the cooperation cluster
  - DPS: Dᵢₗ = Iₙ for exactly one l, switching over time
  - CS/CB: No data sharing; only scheduling coordination (not captured by DCC's
  data-sharing model)

  Why CoMP failed to deliver:
  1. Disjoint clustering: APs divided into fixed, non-overlapping groups →
  cell-edge problem shifted to cluster boundaries
  2. Backhaul latency: Sharing CSI and data over backhaul was too slow for
  real-time cooperation
  3. FDD operation: DL CSI feedback was inaccurate and expensive (overhead
  proportional to number of antennas)
  4. Intra-site only: In practice, only sectors of the same physical site
  cooperated (2-3 antennas), not across sites

  The lesson that led to Cell-Free Massive MIMO: Use TDD (no CSI feedback), use
  UL pilots (overhead independent of antennas), use user-centric overlapping
  clusters (no cluster boundaries), use distributed processing (no centralized
  backhaul bottleneck).

  Source: A. Osseiran, J. F. Monserrat, and P. Marsch, 5G Mobile and Wireless 
  Communications Technology, Cambridge University Press, 2016, Ch. 9.

  4.1 The Dᵢₗ Matrix — Formal Definition

  Dᵢₗ ∈ ℂᴺˣᴺ is a diagonal matrix. For AP-level selection (Eq. 11), it takes one
   of two values:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = Iₙ  (l ∈ ℳᵢ)     or     Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ  (l ∉ ℳᵢ)

  Properties:
  - Dᵢₗ is idempotent: Dᵢₗ² = Dᵢₗ (since diagonal entries are 0 or 1)
  - Dᵢₗ is Hermitian: Dᵢₗᴴ = Dᵢₗ (real diagonal entries)
  - Dᵢₗ is a projection matrix: it projects vectors onto the subspace of
  "active" antennas

  Action on vectors:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ hᵢₗ = { hᵢₗ  if l ∈ ℳᵢ     (channel preserved)
  ▎                  { 0ₙ   if l ∉ ℳᵢ     (channel zeroed out)

  4.2 Derivation of UL Equation (9) — Step by Step

  Starting from the received signal at AP l (Eq. 5):

  ▎ yₗᵘˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + nₗ

  AP l applies combining vₖₗ, but gated by Dₖₗ:

  ▎ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ = vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ (∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + nₗ)

  If Dₖₗ = 0ₙ: the result is zero — AP l is silent for UE k.
  If Dₖₗ = Iₙ: the result is vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ — AP l fully participates.

  Summing over all APs:

  ▎ ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ

  Expanding:

  ▎ ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ nₗ

  ▎ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ hᵢₗ sᵢ + ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ nₗ

  ▎ ŝₖ = (∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ hₖₗ) sₖ + ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ (∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ hᵢₗ) sᵢ + ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ 
  vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ nₗ

  ▎ ŝₖ = vₖᴴ Dₖ hₖ sₖ + ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ vₖᴴ Dₖ hᵢ sᵢ + vₖᴴ Dₖ n          ... (Eq. 9) ✓

  1. HIGH-LEVEL EXPLANATION (Layman's Terms)

  Let's return to our pizza delivery analogy. In Section III, we saw the
  problem: every driver delivers to every customer — unscalable.

  Dynamic Cooperation Clustering (DCC) is the solution framework. The idea:

  Each customer is assigned a small team of nearby drivers. Customer A might be
  served by drivers {1, 3, 5}. Customer B might be served by drivers {3, 4, 6}.
  Notice that driver 3 serves both customers — the teams overlap. This
  overlapping is the key feature.

  Why "dynamic"? Because the teams change over time. If a customer moves to a
  new area, their team is updated to the nearest drivers.

  Why "clustering"? Because each customer has a "cluster" of drivers assigned to
   them.

  The magic: Each driver only serves a small, fixed number of customers (say, at
   most 10). Whether the total number of customers is 100 or 100,000, each
  driver's workload stays the same. This is scalability.

  Section IV does three things:
  1. Introduces the mathematical tool (Dᵢₗ matrices) that controls which APs
  serve which UEs
  2. Shows that the original Cell-Free Massive MIMO and several other
  architectures are all special cases of this one framework
  3. Proves that DCC partially solves the scalability problem (the first 3 of 4
  criteria)

  Think of DCC as a universal "control panel" with one switch per AP-UE pair.
  Flip a switch ON → that AP serves that UE. Flip it OFF → it doesn't. Different
   switch configurations create different network architectures:
  - All switches ON → Original Cell-Free Massive MIMO
  - Each switch ON only for nearby pairs → User-centric Cell-Free (scalable!)
  - Switches ON in disjoint groups → Traditional cellular with cooperation
  clusters (4G CoMP)

  2. DETAILED TECHNICAL EXPLANATION (PhD Level)

  2.1 The DCC Framework — Core Mathematical Tool

  The DCC framework was originally proposed by Björnson, Jaldén, Bengtsson, and
  Ottersten (2011) [17] and further formalized in the textbook [10] by Björnson
  and Jorswieck (2013). Its purpose: provide a "unified analysis of anything
  from interference channels to Network MIMO."

  The core tool is a set of diagonal selection matrices:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ ∈ ℂᴺˣᴺ ,    for i = 1, ..., K  and  l = 1, ..., L

  These are diagonal matrices that determine which antennas at AP l may transmit
   to (and decode signals from) UE i.

  Structure of Dᵢₗ: Since it is diagonal, it has the form:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = diag(d¹ᵢₗ, d²ᵢₗ, ..., dᴺᵢₗ)

  where dʲᵢₗ ∈ {0, 1} is a binary indicator:
  - dʲᵢₗ = 1 → the j-th antenna of AP l is allowed to transmit to/decode UE i
  - dʲᵢₗ = 0 → the j-th antenna of AP l is not involved with UE i

  Key insight: By choosing the K×L matrices {Dᵢₗ} appropriately, we can model
  virtually any multi-AP network architecture within one unified framework.

  2.2 The Block-Diagonal Aggregate Matrix

  For UE k, define the collective selection matrix by stacking:

  ▎ Dₖ = diag(Dₖ₁, Dₖ₂, ..., Dₖₗ) ∈ ℂᴹˣᴹ

  This is an M×M block-diagonal matrix (M = NL). When this multiplies the
  collective combining vector vₖ ∈ ℂᴹ, it zeroes out the components
  corresponding to APs that are not serving UE k.

  2.3 Section IV-A: UL Data Transmission with DCC (Equation 9)

  The DCC framework does not change the received UL signal yₗᵘˡ in Eq. (5) — all
   APs still physically receive signals from all UEs (radio waves don't obey
  cluster boundaries). What changes is the processing: only a subset of APs
  participate in decoding each UE.

  The estimate of sₖ under DCC becomes:

  ▎ ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ        ... (Eq. 9, first line)

  Using collective notation:

  ▎ ŝₖ = vₖᴴ Dₖ hₖ · sₖ  +  ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ vₖᴴ Dₖ hᵢ · sᵢ  +  vₖᴴ Dₖ n        ... 
  (Eq. 9)

  Comparing with Eq. (6) from Section III: The only difference is the insertion
  of Dₖ. This matrix acts as a filter — it sets to zero the contributions from
  APs that are not in UE k's cooperation cluster.

  Effect of Dₖₗ = 0ₙ: If AP l does not serve UE k, then Dₖₗ = 0ₙ, which means:
  - Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ = 0 → AP l contributes nothing to ŝₖ
  - vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ = 0ᵀ → the combining vector for that AP doesn't matter (it's zeroed
   out)

  Effect of Dₖₗ = Iₙ: If AP l fully serves UE k, then Dₖₗ = Iₙ, and AP l
  participates normally — exactly as in the original Cell-Free model.

  2.4 Section IV-A: DL Data Transmission with DCC (Equation 10)

  The received DL signal at UE k under DCC:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ Dᵢₗ wᵢₗ ςᵢ  +  nₖ        ... (Eq. 10, expanded)

  In collective notation:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = hₖᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ Dᵢ wᵢ ςᵢ  +  nₖ        ... (Eq. 10, compact)

  Key difference from Eq. (7): The precoding vector wᵢₗ is multiplied by Dᵢₗ
  before transmission. When Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ wᵢₗ = 0ₙ → AP l transmits nothing for UE i

  So only APs with Dᵢₗ ≠ 0ₙ actually transmit to UE i. This is how DCC limits
  each AP's DL workload.

  2.5 How Different Network Architectures Are Special Cases of DCC

  This is one of the most elegant results in the section. The paper shows that
  several previously proposed architectures are ALL special cases:

  Case 1: Original Cell-Free Massive MIMO

  Set Dᵢ = Iₘ for all i. Every AP antenna serves every UE:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = Iₙ    ∀ i, l

  Equations (9) and (10) reduce to (6) and (7) from Section III.

  Case 2: User-Centric Cell-Free Massive MIMO (Buzzi & D'Andrea, 2017 [27])

  Let ℳᵢ ⊂ {1, ..., L} denote the subset of APs that serve UE i. Then:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = Iₙ    if l ∈ ℳᵢ        ... (Eq. 11)
  ▎ Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ    if l ∉ ℳᵢ

  This is the AP-level selection: each AP either fully participates or not at
  all (all N antennas together). This is "exactly the setup previously
  considered in [17]" — the original DCC paper.

  Case 3: Fog Massive MIMO (Bursalioglu et al., 2019 [29])

  The Fog Massive MIMO architecture uses 𝒜ᵢ instead of ℳᵢ to denote the AP
  subset. Otherwise identical to Case 2 — it is an instance of DCC.

  Case 4: Traditional Cellular Networks (No Cooperation)

  Set Dᵢₗ = Iₙ only for the single AP l that "owns" UE i's cell, and Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ
  for all others. Each UE is served by exactly one AP.

  Case 5: Network-Centric Clustering (4G CoMP)

  Partition the APs into disjoint groups 𝒢₁, 𝒢₂, .... UE i is served by all APs
  in whichever group it belongs to:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = Iₙ    if l ∈ 𝒢ⱼ and UE i is in cluster j
  ▎ Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ    otherwise

  This creates non-overlapping cooperation regions — the approach that failed in
   4G CoMP.

  Case 6: Per-Antenna Selection

  Set individual diagonal elements of Dᵢₗ to 0 or 1 independently. This allows
  different antennas at the same AP to serve different UEs — maximum flexibility
   but rarely used in practice.

  2.6 The Two Key Sets: ℳᵢ and 𝒟ₗ

  The paper defines two complementary sets that are central to the entire
  framework:

  Set ℳᵢ: The set of APs serving UE i (UE-centric view):

  ▎ ℳᵢ = {l : Dᵢₗ ≠ 0ₙ, l ∈ {1,...,L}}

  "Which APs help me?"

  Set 𝒟ₗ: The set of UEs served by AP l (AP-centric view), defined in Eq. (12):

  ▎ 𝒟ₗ = {i : tr(Dᵢₗ) ≥ 1, i ∈ {1,...,K}}        ... (Eq. 12)

  "Which UEs do I help?"

  These are dual perspectives of the same information:

  ▎ l ∈ ℳᵢ  ⟺  i ∈ 𝒟ₗ

  Scalability is controlled by |𝒟ₗ| — the number of UEs that AP l serves. If
  this is bounded as K → ∞, the AP's workload is bounded.

  2.7 Section IV-B: Lemma 1 — Partial Solution to Scalability

  This is the key theoretical result of Section IV:

  ▎ Lemma 1: If the cardinality |𝒟ₗ| is constant as K → ∞ for l = 1,...,L, then 
  the Cell-Free Massive MIMO network with DCC satisfies the first three 
  conditions in Definition 1.

  Proof (given in the paper): AP l only needs to:
  1. Estimate channels for |𝒟ₗ| UEs → finite complexity as K → ∞ ✅
  2. Compute combining/precoding for |𝒟ₗ| UEs → finite complexity as K → ∞ ✅
  3. Send/receive fronthaul data for |𝒟ₗ| UEs → finite load as K → ∞ ✅

  The fourth condition (power control) is NOT captured by Lemma 1. However, it
  is "relatively easy to satisfy" — for example:
  - UL: full power transmission (pᵢ = P for all i)
  - DL: equal power allocation among served UEs (ρᵢ = ρ/τₚ)

  These are heuristic but have O(1) complexity per AP.

  3. HISTORICAL CONTEXT AND PRIOR DEVELOPMENTS

  3.1 The DCC Framework — Origin Story

  Björnson, Jaldén, Bengtsson, and Ottersten (2011) [17]:
  - Title: "Optimality Properties, Distributed Strategies, and Measurement-Based
   Evaluation of Coordinated Multicell OFDMA Transmission"
  - Published: IEEE Trans. Signal Process., vol. 59, no. 12, pp. 6086–6101
  - Key innovation: Introduced the "inner circle / outer circle" cooperation
  model where each UE has a personalized set of base stations (BSs) that either
  jointly transmit data (inner circle) or suppress interference (outer circle)
  - Proposed practical distributed beamforming strategies
  - Validated with real measurement data from an LTE-like testbed

  Björnson and Jorswieck (2013) [10]:
  - Title: "Optimal Resource Allocation in Coordinated Multi-Cell Systems"
  - Published: Foundations and Trends in Communications and Information Theory,
  vol. 9, no. 2-3
  - Formalized DCC with the diagonal matrices Dᵢₗ
  - Showed DCC can model: interference channels, cognitive radio, relay
  channels, Network MIMO, coordinated beamforming, and everything in between
  - Provided a complete optimization framework

  3.2 The Failure of Network-Centric Clustering (4G CoMP)

  4G LTE-Advanced (3GPP Release 11, ~2012) introduced Coordinated Multi-Point 
  (CoMP) with three modes:

  1. Joint Transmission (JT): Multiple cells transmit the same data to a UE
  2. Dynamic Point Selection (DPS): The best cell is selected per subframe
  3. Coordinated Scheduling / Coordinated Beamforming (CS/CB): Cells coordinate
  to reduce interference

  All three modes used network-centric (disjoint) clustering — the APs were
  partitioned into non-overlapping groups.

  Why CoMP failed in practice (only 20–30% cell-edge gains):
  - Backhaul latency: Sharing CSI between cells required fast backhaul that
  wasn't available
  - Feedback overhead: FDD operation required UEs to feed back CSI — expensive
  and imprecise
  - Intra-site-only cooperation: Most practical deployments only coordinated
  sectors of the same physical site
  - Disjoint clustering: UEs at cluster edges still suffered inter-cluster
  interference — the cell-edge problem was merely shifted to cluster edges

  Sources:
  - A. Osseiran, J. F. Monserrat, and P. Marsch, 5G Mobile and Wireless 
  Communications Technology, Cambridge Univ. Press, 2016, Ch. 9 [14]

  3.5 pCell: The Practical Demonstration

  Artemis Networks' pCell technology [21] (Steve Perlman, 2015) demonstrated
  that DCC-style distributed MIMO is practically feasible:
  - Creates a virtual personal cell for each user
  - Exploits interference constructively through coordinated transmission
  - Real-world demonstrations showed near-theoretical performance
  - Originally called DIDO (Distributed-Input Distributed-Output)

  However, the combination of Network MIMO and DCC "didn't gain much interest at
   the time" because Massive MIMO was simultaneously conceived (Marzetta, 2010)
  and "rightfully gained the spotlight."

  4. MATHEMATICAL DEEP DIVE

  4.1 The Dᵢₗ Matrix — Formal Properties

  Dᵢₗ ∈ ℂᴺˣᴺ is a diagonal matrix with binary entries:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = diag(d¹ᵢₗ, d²ᵢₗ, ..., dᴺᵢₗ),    dʲᵢₗ ∈ {0, 1}

  Properties:

  1. Idempotent: Dᵢₗ² = Dᵢₗ (since each diagonal entry is 0 or 1, and 0² = 0, 1²
   = 1)
  2. Self-adjoint: Dᵢₗᴴ = Dᵢₗ (real diagonal → Hermitian)
  3. Projection: Since Dᵢₗ is idempotent and Hermitian, it is an orthogonal 
  projection matrix onto the subspace spanned by the selected antenna
  dimensions.
  4. Trace gives antenna count: tr(Dᵢₗ) = number of antennas at AP l serving UE
  i.
  5. Zeroing effect: For any vector x ∈ ℂᴺ:
  ▎ Dᵢₗ x keeps the entries of x corresponding to selected antennas and zeros 
  out the rest.

  4.2 Derivation of UL DCC Signal Model (Equation 9)

  Starting from the received signal at AP l (Eq. 5):

  ▎ yₗᵘˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + nₗ

  AP l applies combining with the DCC-modified vector Dₖₗ vₖₗ (or equivalently,
  vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ since Dₖₗ is Hermitian):

  ▎ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ = vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ (∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + nₗ)

  Summing over all APs:

  ▎ ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ

  In collective form:

  ▎ ŝₖ = vₖᴴ Dₖ (hₖ sₖ + ∑ᵢ≠ₖ hᵢ sᵢ + n)

  Expanding:

  ▎ ŝₖ = vₖᴴ Dₖ hₖ · sₖ + ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ vₖᴴ Dₖ hᵢ · sᵢ + vₖᴴ Dₖ n        ... (Eq. 9)

  Note: When Dₖₗ = 0ₙ (AP l does not serve UE k):
  ▎ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ = 0ᵀ → AP l contributes zero to ŝₖ, regardless of vₖₗ

  This means only APs in the set ℳₖ = {l : Dₖₗ ≠ 0ₙ} contribute.

  4.3 Derivation of DL DCC Signal Model (Equation 10)

  The transmitted signal at AP l becomes:

  ▎ xₗᵈˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ Dᵢₗ wᵢₗ ςᵢ

  The Dᵢₗ matrix is applied before transmission, zeroing out the precoding
  vector for UEs not served by AP l.

  The received signal at UE k:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ Dᵢₗ wᵢₗ ςᵢ + nₖ

  Rearranging (swap summation order):

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ (∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ Dᵢₗ wᵢₗ) ςᵢ + nₖ

  Using collective vectors:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = hₖᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ Dᵢ wᵢ ςᵢ + nₖ        ... (Eq. 10)

  Expanding the desired and interference:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = hₖᴴ Dₖ wₖ ςₖ + ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ hₖᴴ Dᵢ wᵢ ςᵢ + nₖ
  ▎         ⎣⎦             ⎣_____________⎦
  ▎         desired signal              interference from other UEs

  Important observation about the interference term: The interference from UE i
  is:

  ▎ hₖᴴ Dᵢ wᵢ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ Dᵢₗ wᵢₗ

  Only APs that serve UE i (those with Dᵢₗ ≠ 0ₙ) contribute to this
  interference. So UE k only receives interference from APs that are actively 
  serving other UEs — not from all APs. However, UE k still receives
  interference from UEs served by different APs (through the channel hₖₗ), since
   radio waves propagate regardless of DCC assignments.

  4.4 Formal Proof of Lemma 1

  Statement: If |𝒟ₗ| is constant as K → ∞ for all l, then criteria 1–3 of
  Definition 1 are satisfied.

  Proof (expanded from the paper):

  Criterion 1 (Channel Estimation): AP l only needs estimates {ĥₖₗ : k ∈ 𝒟ₗ}.
  The number of estimates is |𝒟ₗ|, which is constant. Each estimate requires N²
  multiplications (from Section II-A). Total: |𝒟ₗ| · N² = constant. ✅

  Criterion 2 (Signal Processing):
  - UL: AP l computes {vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ : k ∈ 𝒟ₗ}. Number of operations: |𝒟ₗ| · N =
   constant. ✅
  - DL: AP l forms xₗᵈˡ = ∑ₖ∈𝒟ₗ Dₖₗ wₖₗ ςₖ. Number of terms: |𝒟ₗ| = constant. ✅
  - Computing combining/precoding vectors: depends on scheme, but input size is
  |𝒟ₗ| (constant). ✅

  Criterion 3 (Fronthaul): AP l sends/receives data for |𝒟ₗ| UEs:
  - UL: sends |𝒟ₗ| complex scalars per time slot → constant. ✅
  - DL: receives |𝒟ₗ| complex scalars per time slot → constant. ✅

  Criterion 4 (Power Control): NOT guaranteed by Lemma 1. Network-wide
  optimization still involves all K UEs. Needs separate treatment (heuristic
  algorithms). ⚠️

  QED □

  4.5 Showing the Original CF-mMIMO is a Special Case

  When Dᵢ = Iₘ for all i, let's verify that Eq. (9) reduces to Eq. (6):

  ▎ ŝₖ = vₖᴴ Dₖ hₖ sₖ + ∑ᵢ≠ₖ vₖᴴ Dₖ hᵢ sᵢ + vₖᴴ Dₖ n

  With Dₖ = Iₘ:

  ▎ ŝₖ = vₖᴴ Iₘ hₖ sₖ + ∑ᵢ≠ₖ vₖᴴ Iₘ hᵢ sᵢ + vₖᴴ Iₘ n
  ▎ = vₖᴴ hₖ sₖ + ∑ᵢ≠ₖ vₖᴴ hᵢ sᵢ + vₖᴴ n        ← This is Eq. (6) ✓

  Similarly, Eq. (10) with Dᵢ = Iₘ:

  ▎ yₖᵈˡ = hₖᴴ ∑ᵢ Iₘ wᵢ ςᵢ + nₖ = hₖᴴ ∑ᵢ wᵢ ςᵢ + nₖ        ← This is Eq. (7) ✓

  4.6 Showing the User-Centric Approach (Eq. 11) is a Special Case

  When Dᵢₗ is defined as in Eq. (11):

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = Iₙ  if l ∈ ℳᵢ
  ▎ Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ  if l ∉ ℳᵢ

  The collective matrix becomes:

  ▎ Dᵢ = diag(Dᵢ₁, ..., Dᵢₗ)

  with Iₙ blocks at positions l ∈ ℳᵢ and 0ₙ blocks elsewhere. Then:

  ▎ Dᵢ wᵢ keeps only the components corresponding to APs in ℳᵢ and zeros out the
   rest

  This means only APs in ℳᵢ contribute to UE i's signal — exactly the
  user-centric model of [27].

  5. NUMERICAL EXAMPLES

  Example 1: DCC Matrices in a Small Network

  Setup: L = 4 APs, N = 2 antennas each, K = 3 UEs. Total antennas M = 8.

  Cluster assignments (user-centric, Eq. 11):
  - UE 1: served by APs {1, 2}     → ℳ₁ = {1, 2}
  - UE 2: served by APs {2, 3}     → ℳ₂ = {2, 3}
  - UE 3: served by APs {3, 4}     → ℳ₃ = {3, 4}

  The D matrices:

  UE 1:
  ▎ D₁₁ = I₂,  D₁₂ = I₂,  D₁₃ = 0₂,  D₁₄ = 0₂

  UE 2:
  ▎ D₂₁ = 0₂,  D₂₂ = I₂,  D₂₃ = I₂,  D₂₄ = 0₂

  UE 3:
  ▎ D₃₁ = 0₂,  D₃₂ = 0₂,  D₃₃ = I₂,  D₃₄ = I₂

  The dual sets 𝒟ₗ (UEs served by each AP):
  ▎ 𝒟₁ = {1}         → |𝒟₁| = 1
  ▎ 𝒟₂ = {1, 2}      → |𝒟₂| = 2
  ▎ 𝒟₃ = {2, 3}      → |𝒟₃| = 2
  ▎ 𝒟₄ = {3}         → |𝒟₄| = 1

  Maximum |𝒟ₗ| = 2 — this is constant (independent of K as long as we maintain
  bounded cluster size).

  Overlap: AP 2 serves both UE 1 and UE 2. AP 3 serves both UE 2 and UE 3. These
   are the overlapping clusters — the hallmark of user-centric DCC.

  Verification: Every UE is served by at least one AP ✅

  Example 2: DL Signal with DCC

  Continuing Example 1. Let N = 1 (single antenna) for simplicity. Channels:

  h₁₁ = 0.8,  h₁₂ = 0.5,  h₁₃ = 0.1,  h₁₄ = 0.02
  h₂₁ = 0.1,  h₂₂ = 0.6,  h₂₃ = 0.7,  h₂₄ = 0.05
  h₃₁ = 0.02, h₃₂ = 0.1,  h₃₃ = 0.4,  h₃₄ = 0.9
  Precoding vectors (MR, assume perfect CSI, already normalized so ρᵢ = 1):
  ▎ w₁₁ = 0.8,  w₁₂ = 0.5,  w₁₃ = 0,  w₁₄ = 0     (only APs 1,2)
  ▎ w₂₁ = 0,    w₂₂ = 0.6,  w₂₃ = 0.7, w₂₄ = 0     (only APs 2,3)
  ▎ w₃₁ = 0,    w₃₂ = 0,    w₃₃ = 0.4, w₃₄ = 0.9    (only APs 3,4)

  DL received signal at UE 1 (Eq. 10):

  Desired signal (h₁ᴴ D₁ w₁):
  ▎ = h₁₁* · D₁₁ · w₁₁ + h₁₂* · D₁₂ · w₁₂ + h₁₃* · D₁₃ · w₁₃ + h₁₄* · D₁₄ · w₁₄
  ▎ = 0.8 × 1 × 0.8 + 0.5 × 1 × 0.5 + 0.1 × 0 × 0 + 0.02 × 0 × 0
  ▎ = 0.64 + 0.25 = 0.89

  Interference from UE 2 (h₁ᴴ D₂ w₂):
  ▎ = h₁₁* · D₂₁ · w₂₁ + h₁₂* · D₂₂ · w₂₂ + h₁₃* · D₂₃ · w₂₃ + h₁₄* · D₂₄ · w₂₄
  ▎ = 0.8 × 0 × 0 + 0.5 × 1 × 0.6 + 0.1 × 1 × 0.7 + 0.02 × 0 × 0
  ▎ = 0 + 0.30 + 0.07 + 0 = 0.37

  Interference from UE 3 (h₁ᴴ D₃ w₃):
  ▎ = h₁₁* · D₃₁ · w₃₁ + h₁₂* · D₃₂ · w₃₂ + h₁₃* · D₃₃ · w₃₃ + h₁₄* · D₃₄ · w₃₄
  ▎ = 0.8 × 0 × 0 + 0.5 × 0 × 0 + 0.1 × 1 × 0.4 + 0.02 × 1 × 0.9
  ▎ = 0 + 0 + 0.04 + 0.018 = 0.058

  Observations:
  - Desired signal power: |0.89|² = 0.792
  - UE 2 interference: |0.37|² = 0.137 (significant — AP 2 is shared!)
  - UE 3 interference: |0.058|² = 0.003 (negligible — no shared APs)
  - The interference is dominated by UEs that share APs with UE 1

  This shows why the P-MMSE scheme (Section V) targets interference from UEs
  with overlapping clusters.

  Example 3: Comparing Architectures via DCC

  Setup: L = 6 APs (numbered 1–6), K = 4 UEs, N = 1.

  Architecture A: Original CF-mMIMO (all serve all):

  D_{il} = 1 for ALL i,l pairs → 6×4 = 24 "ones"
  |𝒟ₗ| = 4 for every AP
  Per-AP fronthaul (UL): 4 scalars/slot
  Architecture B: Disjoint clustering ({APs 1,2,3} = cluster α; {APs 4,5,6} =
  cluster β):

  UEs 1,2 in cluster α; UEs 3,4 in cluster β
  |𝒟₁| = |𝒟₂| = |𝒟₃| = 2;  |𝒟₄| = |𝒟₅| = |𝒟₆| = 2
  Per-AP fronthaul: 2 scalars/slot
  Problem: UE 2 near the boundary → strong interference from cluster β ❌
  Architecture C: User-centric DCC (each UE selects 2 nearest APs):

  UE 1 → {AP 1, AP 2};  UE 2 → {AP 3, AP 4};  UE 3 → {AP 4, AP 5};  UE 4 → {AP
  5, AP 6}
  |𝒟₁| = 1, |𝒟₂| = 1, |𝒟₃| = 1, |𝒟₄| = 2, |𝒟₅| = 2, |𝒟₆| = 1
  Per-AP fronthaul: max 2 scalars/slot
  No hard boundaries → no cell-edge problem ✅

  6. PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  Problem 1: Constructing DCC Matrices

  A network has L = 5 APs, K = 3 UEs, N = 2. The cluster assignments are:
  - ℳ₁ = {1, 2, 3}  (UE 1 served by APs 1, 2, 3)
  - ℳ₂ = {2, 3, 4}  (UE 2 served by APs 2, 3, 4)
  - ℳ₃ = {4, 5}     (UE 3 served by APs 4, 5)

  (a) Write out all Dᵢₗ matrices (using Eq. 11).
  (b) Compute the sets 𝒟ₗ for each AP.
  (c) What is max_l |𝒟ₗ|? Is it bounded (independent of K)?
  (d) Verify that every UE is served by at least one AP.

  Solutions:

  (a) Using Eq. (11): Dᵢₗ = I₂ if l ∈ ℳᵢ, 0₂ otherwise.

  ┌──────┬──────┬──────┬──────┬──────┬──────┐
  │      │ AP 1 │ AP 2 │ AP 3 │ AP 4 │ AP 5 │
  ├──────┼──────┼──────┼──────┼──────┼──────┤
  │ UE 1 │ I₂   │ I₂   │ I₂   │ 0₂   │ 0₂   │
  ├──────┼──────┼──────┼──────┼──────┼──────┤
  │ UE 2 │ 0₂   │ I₂   │ I₂   │ I₂   │ 0₂   │
  ├──────┼──────┼──────┼──────┼──────┼──────┤
  │ UE 3 │ 0₂   │ 0₂   │ 0₂   │ I₂   │ I₂   │
  └──────┴──────┴──────┴──────┴──────┴──────┘

  (b) 𝒟₁ = {1}, 𝒟₂ = {1,2}, 𝒟₃ = {1,2}, 𝒟₄ = {2,3}, 𝒟₅ = {3}

  (c) max_l |𝒟ₗ| = 2 (APs 2, 3, and 4 each serve 2 UEs). This is bounded and
  independent of K. ✅

  (d) |ℳ₁| = 3 ≥ 1 ✅, |ℳ₂| = 3 ≥ 1 ✅, |ℳ₃| = 2 ≥ 1 ✅. All UEs are served.

  Problem 2: Scalability Comparison

  Consider a network with L = 100 APs, N = 4, τₚ = 10.

  (a) Original CF-mMIMO (all serve all): What are |𝒟ₗ| and the per-AP estimation
   complexity for K = 500?
  (b) User-centric DCC with each AP serving at most τₚ = 10 UEs: Same questions
  for K = 500.
  (c) What if K grows to 5,000? Recompute both cases.

  Solutions:

  (a) Original: |𝒟ₗ| = K = 500. Estimation: 500 × N² = 500 × 16 = 8,000
  multiplications/AP.

  (b) DCC: |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ = 10. Estimation: 10 × 16 = 160 multiplications/AP. (50×
  less!)

  (c) K = 5,000:
  - Original: 5,000 × 16 = 80,000 multiplications/AP (10× increase from K=500)
  - DCC: Still 10 × 16 = 160 multiplications/AP (unchanged!) ← This is 
  scalability

  Problem 3: DL Interference Analysis with DCC

  Setup: L = 3 APs, N = 1, K = 2 UEs. ℳ₁ = {1, 2}, ℳ₂ = {2, 3}.

  Channels: h₁₁ = 0.7, h₁₂ = 0.4, h₁₃ = 0.05, h₂₁ = 0.1, h₂₂ = 0.5, h₂₃ = 0.8.

  MR precoding (unnormalized for simplicity): wᵢₗ = hᵢₗ for l ∈ ℳᵢ, 0 otherwise.

  (a) Compute the desired signal gain for UE 1: |h₁ᴴ D₁ w₁|
  (b) Compute the interference from UE 2 on UE 1: |h₁ᴴ D₂ w₂|
  (c) Which AP causes the interference overlap? Why?
  (d) Compare with the original CF-mMIMO case (Dᵢ = I₃ ∀i): recompute (a) and
  (b).

  Solutions:

  (a) h₁ᴴ D₁ w₁ = h₁₁·w₁₁ + h₁₂·w₁₂ + h₁₃·0 = 0.7×0.7 + 0.4×0.4 = 0.49 + 0.16 =
  0.65

  (b) h₁ᴴ D₂ w₂ = h₁₁·0 + h₁₂·w₂₂ + h₁₃·w₂₃ = 0 + 0.4×0.5 + 0.05×0.8 = 0.20 +
  0.04 = 0.24

  (c) AP 2 causes the interference overlap. It serves both UE 1 and UE 2, so
  when it transmits to UE 2 (with precoding w₂₂ = 0.5), UE 1 receives this
  through channel h₁₂ = 0.4. AP 3 contributes negligible interference (h₁₃ =
  0.05 is very weak).

  (d) Original CF-mMIMO (Dᵢ = I₃):
  - Desired: h₁₁·h₁₁ + h₁₂·h₁₂ + h₁₃·h₁₃ = 0.49 + 0.16 + 0.0025 = 0.6525
  (slightly more)
  - Interference: h₁₁·h₂₁ + h₁₂·h₂₂ + h₁₃·h₂₃ = 0.07 + 0.20 + 0.04 = 0.31 (29%
  more interference!)

  The DCC reduces interference more than it reduces the desired signal → net
  benefit from user-centric clustering.

  1. HIGH-LEVEL EXPLANATION (Layman's Terms)

  Imagine you just moved to a new city and need to sign up for pizza delivery
  service. Here's how the process works in three simple steps:

  Step 1 — You pick your "main driver" (Master AP):
  You look around and find which driver is closest to your house (strongest
  signal). You call that driver and say: "You're my main driver now." This
  driver is required to serve you — no matter what. This guarantees you won't be
   left without service.

  Step 2 — The main driver gives you a "code" (Pilot Assignment):
  There are only a limited number of delivery codes (say, 10 colors: red, blue,
  green...). Your main driver checks which code has the least traffic in the
  area and assigns that code to you. Then the driver tells nearby drivers: "Hey,
   I'm serving this new customer on code Blue."

  Step 3 — Nearby drivers decide whether to also serve you (Cluster Formation):
  Each nearby driver checks: "Am I already serving someone on code Blue?" If
  not, they volunteer to also serve you. If they are already serving someone on
  Blue but your house is closer (better channel), they switch to you and drop
  the farther customer (who still has their main driver).

  Why this works:
  - Every customer is guaranteed service — the main driver must serve them (no
  orphans)
  - Each driver serves at most 10 customers (one per code) — scalable!
  - Everything is local — no central authority needed; each driver makes
  decisions based only on nearby information
  - The clusters overlap naturally — neighboring drivers serve overlapping sets
  of customers

  The key rule (Assumption 1): Each driver serves at most one customer per code.
   Since there are only τₚ codes, each driver serves at most τₚ customers. This
  is the mechanism that guarantees scalability.

  2. DETAILED TECHNICAL EXPLANATION (PhD Level)

  2.1 Assumption 1: The Foundation of Scalability

  Before describing the algorithm, the paper introduces the crucial design
  constraint:

  ▎ Assumption 1: Each AP serves at most one UE per pilot sequence and uses all 
  its N antennas to serve these UEs.

  Mathematically, this implies:

  ▎ |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ        for all l = 1, ..., L

  and:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = Iₙ    if i ∈ 𝒟ₗ
  ▎ Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ    if i ∉ 𝒟ₗ            ... (Eq. 13)

  Three rationales for this assumption:

  Rationale 1 — Avoiding intra-AP pilot contamination: If AP l served two UEs,
  say UE k and UE j, on the same pilot t, then both channel estimates ĥₖₗ and
  ĥⱼₗ would be derived from the identical received pilot signal yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ. The
  estimates would be strongly correlated, and the AP would be unable to
  distinguish their channels. When it precodes toward one, it would
  inadvertently create strong coherent interference toward the other. This
  coherent interference scales with N — exactly the same rate as the desired
  signal — making it devastating.

  By serving at most one UE per pilot, the AP avoids this intra-AP pilot
  contamination problem entirely.

  Rationale 2 — Fixed, scalable complexity: Since τₚ is a constant (independent
  of K), the bound |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ is automatically constant as K → ∞. By Lemma 1 from
   Section IV, this guarantees that the first three scalability criteria
  (estimation, processing, fronthaul) are satisfied.

  Rationale 3 — Predictable fronthaul requirements: Each AP needs to support at
  most τₚ parallel UL and DL data streams over its fronthaul link. This is a
  fixed, designable requirement.

  The trade-off: In areas with dense UEs, some APs cannot serve all nearby UEs
  (they run out of pilot slots). Those UEs receive less macro-diversity (fewer
  serving APs) — the price for provable scalability. However, each UE is still
  guaranteed service by at least its Master AP.

  2.2 The Three-Step Algorithm

  The paper proposes a three-step procedure for when a new UE (indexed K+1)
  wants to access the network. The procedure is illustrated in Fig. 3 of the
  paper.

  Step 1: The UE Appoints a Master AP

  The accessing UE measures the large-scale fading coefficient βₗ from all
  nearby APs:

  ▎ βₗ = tr(R₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ) / N        for all nearby APs l

  How does the UE measure βₗ? Using periodically broadcasted synchronization 
  signals from the APs. In 5G NR, these are:
  - PSS (Primary Synchronization Signal): Enables coarse time/frequency
  synchronization and identifies the cell-ID group
  - SSS (Secondary Synchronization Signal): Enables fine synchronization and
  identifies the complete cell ID
  - SS/PBCH block: Carries the synchronization signals and basic system
  information

  The UE receives these signals from multiple APs, measures the received power
  (which is proportional to βₗ), and selects:

  ▎ ℓ = argmaxₗ βₗ            ... (Eq. 14)

  as its Master AP — the AP with the strongest average channel.

  The UE also synchronizes to the Master AP using the broadcast signal, then
  contacts it via a standard random access procedure (RACH):

  In 5G NR, the RACH procedure has four steps:
  1. UE sends a random preamble on PRACH (Physical Random Access Channel)
  2. AP responds with a Random Access Response (RAR)
  3. UE sends its identity and connection request
  4. AP confirms the connection (contention resolution)

  Why the Master AP concept is critical: It guarantees that every UE is served 
  by at least one AP. Previous user-centric approaches [27], [29] had the risk
  that a UE could be inadvertently dropped from service because no AP decided to
   serve it. The Master AP concept flips the initiative: the UE appoints its AP,
   not the other way around.

  Source: S. Sesia, I. Toufik, and M. Baker, LTE — The UMTS Long Term Evolution:
   From Theory to Practice, Wiley, 2009 [42].

  Step 2: The Master AP Assigns a Pilot and Notifies Neighbors

  The appointed Master AP responds by assigning pilot τ to the new UE, chosen
  as:

  ▎ τ = argminₜ tr(Ψₜₗ)            ... (Eq. 15)

  where Ψₜₗ is the correlation matrix of the received pilot signal (from Eq. 4):

  ▎ Ψₜₗ = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ Rᵢₗ + σ² Iₙ

  Recall that tr(Ψₜₗ) = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ tr(Rᵢₗ) + Nσ² is the total received pilot 
  power on pilot t at AP l. So Eq. (15) selects the pilot where the Master AP
  observes the least pilot contamination.

  Why this criterion? Lower tr(Ψₜₗ) means:
  - Fewer or weaker interfering UEs on that pilot (as seen by this AP)
  - Better channel estimation quality (ĥₖₗ will have lower MSE)
  - Less coherent interference in subsequent data transmission

  Important detail: The computation in Eq. (15) only involves the existing K
  UEs. The Master AP already knows {Ψₜₗ} from its current operation — no
  additional communication is needed.

  After assigning the pilot: The Master AP informs a limited set of neighboring 
  APs that it is going to serve UE K+1 on pilot τ. This invitation triggers Step
   3.

  Footnote 5 from the paper: The pilot should be one on which the AP is not
  currently serving a UE as Master AP (that role has higher priority). Each AP
  can be the Master AP of at most τₚ UEs. In the unlikely event this cannot be
  satisfied, multiple UEs can be assigned the same pilot but multiplexed in time
   and/or frequency.

  Step 3: Neighboring APs Decide Whether to Serve the New UE

  Each neighboring AP that receives the invitation makes a local, autonomous 
  decision:

  ▎ Decision rule: Serve UE K+1 if EITHER:
  ▎ (a) The AP does not currently serve any UE on pilot τ, OR
  ▎ (b) The new UE has a better channel (higher βₗ) than the UE it currently 
  serves on pilot τ, in which case it switches to the new UE.

  Case (a): The AP has a free pilot slot — it simply adds UE K+1 to its service
  set 𝒟ₗ.

  Case (b): The AP is already full on pilot τ. But if the new UE has a stronger
  channel (closer, less pathloss), the AP drops the current UE on that pilot and
   replaces it with the new one. The dropped UE is not left without service — it
   still has its Master AP (and potentially other serving APs).

  Why this works for scalability: Each AP makes a decision based on local 
  information only (its own channel measurements). No global coordination is
  needed. The decision only involves comparing two β values — constant-time
  operation.

  Summary of the three steps:

  2.4 Properties of the Algorithm

  Property 1 — Service guarantee: Every UE is served by at least its Master AP.
  No UE can be orphaned.

  Property 2 — Scalability: Under Assumption 1, each AP serves at most τₚ UEs.
  Since τₚ is independent of K:
  - Channel estimation: at most τₚ estimates per AP → O(1) ✅
  - Combining/precoding: at most τₚ vectors per AP → O(1) ✅
  - Fronthaul: at most τₚ data streams per AP → O(1) ✅

  Property 3 — Distributed operation: Each step uses only local information:
  - Step 1: UE uses locally received synchronization signals
  - Step 2: Master AP uses its own Ψₜₗ matrices (already available)
  - Step 3: Each neighboring AP uses its own β values

  No global coordination, no network-wide optimization, no central controller
  needed.

  Property 4 — Dynamic adaptation: When a UE moves or other UEs
  connect/disconnect:
  - The UE can appoint a new Master AP by repeating Step 1
  - The new Master AP informs the previous one that it has taken over
  - The current Master AP can periodically recompute Eq. (15) to check if a
  different pilot would reduce contamination

  point, the Master AP initiates a cooperation cluster.

  3.2 Pilot Assignment: The State of the Art

  Pilot assignment is a combinatorial optimization problem: assign K UEs to τₚ
  pilots to minimize pilot contamination. This is generally NP-hard for optimal
  solutions.

  Approaches in the literature:

  1. Random assignment (Ngo et al. 2017 [5]): Each UE is randomly assigned one
  of τₚ pilots. Simplest, but ignores spatial structure → severe pilot
  contamination for unlucky UE pairs.
  2. Greedy centralized assignment (Ngo et al. 2017 [5]): Sequentially assign
  UEs to pilots that maximize a network-wide objective (e.g., min-rate). Better
  performance but requires global CSI knowledge and has complexity growing with
  K → not scalable.
  3. Graph coloring (Zhu et al., 2015): Model the pilot assignment as a graph
  coloring problem where UEs that would cause severe contamination are given
  different colors (pilots). NP-hard in general.
  4. Location-based greedy (Liu et al., 2019): Assign pilots based on UE
  locations to maximize spatial separation of co-pilot UEs. Better than random
  but still requires global location information.
  5. This paper's approach (Eq. 15): Local, distributed decision by the Master
  AP. Only requires knowledge of tr(Ψₜₗ) at one AP. Complexity: O(τₚ) per UE
  access → constant, scalable.

  Source: H. Liu, J. Zhang, S. Jin, and B. Ai, "Location-Based Greedy Pilot
  Assignment for Cell-Free Massive MIMO," IEEE ICC, 2019.

  3.3 The Master AP Concept — Origins

  The concept of a "primary" or "anchor" base station that has special
  responsibility for a UE is not new:
  - In 3G/4G: the serving cell is the UE's primary cell, while other cells may
  provide secondary service (carrier aggregation, dual connectivity)
  - In handover: the source cell coordinates the handover to a target cell

  What's new in this paper: The Master AP is required to serve the UE (it cannot
   refuse), while other APs are optional. This guarantee eliminates the
  service-dropout risk.

  3.4 Why Previous Cell-Free Clustering Failed

  Buzzi & D'Andrea (2017) [27]: Each UE selects its τₚ nearest APs. Problem: if
  100 UEs are near the same AP, that AP ends up serving 100 UEs → |𝒟��| = 100,
  unscalable. Also, the algorithm doesn't ensure all UEs end up with any AP
  willing to serve them.

  Ngo et al. (2018) [28]: Studied energy efficiency with user-centric clusters.
  Did not impose any limit on |𝒟ₗ| → AP l could serve arbitrarily many UEs →
  unscalable.

  Interdonato, Frenger, Larsson (2019) [33]: Proposed scalability aspects but
  without limiting |𝒟ₗ| → same problem.

  Bursalioglu et al. (2019) [29]: Fog Massive MIMO with AP-initiated clustering.
   APs select which UEs to serve based on channel quality. Problem: UEs with
  weak channels to all nearby APs may not be selected by any AP → no service
  guarantee.

  4. MATHEMATICAL DEEP DIVE

  4.1 The Master AP Selection — Equation (14)

  The large-scale fading coefficient:

  ▎ βₗ = tr(R₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ) / N

  Physical meaning: βₗ is the average channel gain per antenna between the new
  UE and AP l. It captures pathloss and shadow fading but averages over the
  spatial correlation structure.

  For uncorrelated fading (Rₖₗ = βₖₗ Iₙ): tr(βₖₗ Iₙ)/N = βₖₗ, so βₗ is simply
  the scalar channel gain.

  For correlated fading: βₗ = (1/N) ∑ⱼ₌₁ᴺ λⱼ where λ₁,...,λₙ are the eigenvalues
   of R₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ. This averages over all spatial directions.

  The Master AP selection:

  ▎ ℓ = argmaxₗ βₗ            ... (Eq. 14)

  selects the AP with the strongest average link. This is the AP that will
  provide the most reliable service to the new UE.

  Why argmax βₗ and not something more sophisticated? Because:
  1. βₗ is available from synchronization signal measurements (no channel
  estimation needed)
  2. The argmax operation has complexity O(L_nearby) — constant if the UE only
  measures nearby APs
  3. It's a good proxy for "best serving AP" in most propagation environments

  4.2 The Pilot Assignment — Equation (15)

  The pilot assignment criterion at the Master AP (AP ℓ):

  ▎ τ = argminₜ tr(Ψₜₗ)            ... (Eq. 15)

  where (from Eq. 4):

  ▎ Ψₜₗ = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ Rᵢₗ + σ² Iₙ

  Taking the trace:

  ▎ tr(Ψₜₗ) = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ tr(Rᵢₗ) + Nσ²

  ▎ = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ N βᵢₗ + Nσ²

  ▎ = N (∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ βᵢₗ + σ²)

  Interpretation: tr(Ψₜₗ) is proportional to the total received pilot power on
  pilot t at AP ℓ. This includes:
  - Signal power from all existing UEs on pilot t: ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ N βᵢₗ
  - Noise power: Nσ²

  By selecting τ = argminₜ tr(Ψₜₗ), the Master AP assigns the pilot on which it
  observes the least interference from existing UEs. This minimizes the pilot
  contamination experienced by the new UE at its Master AP, leading to the best
  possible channel estimation quality.

  Complexity: Computing tr(Ψₜₗ) for each pilot t requires O(1) operations (the
  AP already maintains these values). Selecting the minimum over τₚ pilots:
  O(τₚ). Total: O(τₚ) per UE access — constant, scalable.

  4.3 The Decision Rule in Step 3 — Formal Analysis

  When a neighboring AP l' receives the invitation from the Master AP:

  Case (a): AP l' does not serve any UE on pilot τ → it adds UE K+1 to 𝒟ₗ'. The
  AP's set grows by 1, but remains ≤ τₚ.

  Case (b): AP l' currently serves UE j on pilot τ. It compares:

  ▎ β₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ'  vs.  βⱼₗ'

  If β₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ' > βⱼₗ' (new UE has stronger channel):
  - AP l' drops UE j from its service set on pilot τ
  - AP l' adds UE K+1 instead
  - UE j loses one serving AP but retains its Master AP

  If β₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ' ≤ βⱼₗ' (existing UE has equal or stronger channel):
  - AP l' declines → UE K+1 is not added at this AP

  Key guarantee: The dropped UE j always retains service from its own Master AP.
   The worst case is that UE j's cluster shrinks by one AP — it receives less
  macro-diversity but is never completely unserved.

  4.4 Why Each AP Serves At Most τₚ UEs

  Under Assumption 1, each AP serves at most one UE per pilot. Since there are
  τₚ pilots:

  ▎ |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ        ∀ l

  This is the mechanism that connects to Lemma 1. Since τₚ is fixed (independent
   of K):

  ▎ |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ = constant        as K → ∞ ✅

  The first three scalability criteria are automatically satisfied.

  4.5 Channel Estimation After Cluster Formation

  Once the clusters are formed, AP l computes MMSE estimates only for UEs in its
   service set 𝒟ₗ. Under Assumption 1, this is at most one UE per pilot. Since
  the complexity per AP is:

  ▎ Estimation complexity = |𝒟ₗ| × (Nτₚ + N²) ≤ τₚ × (Nτₚ + N²) = O(1)

  This is independent of K — scalable.

  1. HIGH-LEVEL EXPLANATION (Layman's Terms)

  Imagine you just arrived in a new city and need to connect to a food delivery
  service. The process:

  Step 1 — You pick your main restaurant (Master AP): You look around and find
  the closest, strongest restaurant — the one whose "signal" (smell of food!) is
   strongest. You walk in and say "I'm your new customer." This is your Master 
  Restaurant — it's required to serve you.

  Step 2 — The restaurant gives you a number (Pilot Assignment): The restaurant
  looks at its order tickets (pilots). Some ticket numbers are already busy
  (other customers using them). It gives you the ticket number that has the
  least congestion — so your order won't get mixed up with others.

  Step 3 — Nearby restaurants decide to help (Cluster Formation): Your Master
  Restaurant tells the neighboring restaurants: "Hey, I have a new customer on
  ticket #3." Each neighbor checks: "Am I already serving someone on ticket #3?"
   If not, they volunteer to also serve you. If they are, they check whether
  your signal is stronger than the current customer's — if so, they switch to
  you.

  The result: You have a Master Restaurant (guaranteed to serve you) plus
  potentially several neighboring restaurants that also serve you. Your food
  (data) comes from multiple sources, coordinated. Nobody is overwhelmed because
   each restaurant handles at most a fixed number of tickets.

  Why this is brilliant:
  - Every customer is guaranteed at least one restaurant (the Master) ← solves
  the "dropped customer" problem
  - Each restaurant serves at most τₚ customers ← solves the scalability problem
  - The entire process uses only local information — no central coordinator
  needed

  2. DETAILED TECHNICAL EXPLANATION (PhD Level)

  2.1 Assumption 1 — The Foundation of Scalability

  Before describing the algorithm, the paper makes a critical assumption:

  ▎ Assumption 1: Each AP serves at most one UE per pilot sequence and uses all 
  its N antennas to serve these UEs.

  This means:

  ▎ |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ        (at most τₚ UEs per AP)

  and the selection matrices take the simplified form:

  ▎ Dᵢₗ = Iₙ    if i ∈ 𝒟ₗ        ... (Eq. 13)
  ▎ Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ    if i ∉ 𝒟ₗ

  Since τₚ is a system constant independent of K, Assumption 1 directly
  guarantees the sufficient condition in Lemma 1: |𝒟ₗ| is bounded as K → ∞.

  Three important rationales behind Assumption 1:

  (1) Avoiding intra-AP pilot contamination: If AP l served two UEs sharing the
  same pilot, their MMSE estimates ĥₖₗ and ĥⱼₗ would be derived from the same 
  received pilot signal y^{pilot}_{tl}. The estimates become correlated:

  ▎ 𝔼{ĥₖₗ ĥⱼₗᴴ} ≠ 0    for k, j ∈ 𝒮ₜ

  When the AP beamforms toward UE k using ĥₖₗ, it inadvertently beamforms toward
   UE j, creating coherent interference that scales linearly with N — the very
  scaling that should boost the desired signal. By serving at most one UE per
  pilot, this destructive intra-AP contamination is eliminated.

  (2) Fixed complexity: The channel estimation and signal processing complexity
  per AP becomes:
  - Channel estimation: at most τₚ estimates, each N² multiplications → τₚ · N²
  = constant
  - Combining/precoding: at most τₚ vectors → fixed computational budget

  (3) Bounded fronthaul: The fronthaul links need to support at most τₚ parallel
   UL and DL data signals per AP — a fixed capacity requirement.

  2.2 The Three-Step Algorithm

  The paper proposes a distributed three-step procedure for when a new UE
  (indexed K+1) wants to access the network. The algorithm is illustrated in
  Fig. 3 of the paper.

  STEP 1: The UE Appoints Its Master AP

  The accessing UE measures the large-scale fading coefficient to all nearby
  APs:

  ▎ βₗ = tr(R₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ) / N        for all nearby APs l

  How does the UE measure βₗ? Using periodically broadcasted synchronization 
  signals. In 5G NR, these are the Primary Synchronization Signal (PSS) and
  Secondary Synchronization Signal (SSS), transmitted in SS/PBCH blocks. Each AP
   broadcasts these signals, and the UE measures their received power. Since the
   synchronization signals have known power, the received power directly gives
  βₗ (the average channel gain).

  The UE then selects the AP with the strongest large-scale fading:

  ▎ ℓ = argmaxₗ βₗ        ... (Eq. 14)

  This AP ℓ becomes the UE's Master AP.

  The UE also:
  - Synchronizes to the Master AP using the broadcast signal
  - Contacts the Master AP via a standard random access procedure (RACH)

  Why argmax βₗ? The AP with the strongest βₗ is typically the nearest AP (due
  to distance-dependent pathloss). This AP will have:
  - The best channel estimate (highest estimation SNR)
  - The strongest coherent beamforming gain
  - The most reliable signal for both UL and DL

  The Master AP concept solves a critical problem: In previous user-centric
  approaches [27], [29], there was no guarantee that any AP would decide to
  serve a given UE. A UE could be "orphaned" — all nearby APs might be too busy.
   By having the UE (not the AP) initiate the connection and appoint a Master
  AP, the UE is guaranteed service from at least one AP.

  STEP 2: The Master AP Assigns a Pilot and Notifies Neighbors

  The appointed Master AP responds by assigning pilot τ to the new UE, where:

  ▎ τ = argminₜ tr(Ψₜₗ)        ... (Eq. 15)

  Recall from Section II that Ψₜₗ is the correlation matrix of the received
  pilot signal:

  ▎ Ψₜₗ = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ Rᵢₗ + σ² Iₙ        ... (Eq. 4)

  The trace tr(Ψₜₗ) represents the total pilot power that AP ℓ observes on pilot
   t:

  ▎ tr(Ψₜₗ) = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ tr(Rᵢₗ)/1 + σ² N = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ N βᵢₗ + σ² N

  Interpretation: The Master AP selects the pilot with the least observed pilot 
  contamination. Pilot t with small tr(Ψₜₗ) means:
  - Few existing UEs are assigned to pilot t near AP ℓ
  - Those UEs have weak channels to AP ℓ (small βᵢₗ)
  - The new UE's channel estimation will be less contaminated

  Important caveat: The computation in Eq. (15) only involves the existing K UEs
   and can be done with local information at AP ℓ — no network-wide coordination
   needed.

  After assigning the pilot, the Master AP informs a limited set of neighboring 
  APs that it is going to serve UE K+1 on pilot τ.

  STEP 3: Neighboring APs Decide Whether to Serve the UE

  Each neighboring AP (informed by the Master AP) makes a local decision:

  ▎ Decision rule: Serve the new UE K+1 if either:
  ▎ (a) The AP does not currently serve any UE on pilot τ (it has a free slot), 
  OR
  ▎ (b) The new UE has a better channel (higher β₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ) than the UE it 
  currently serves on pilot τ — in which case it switches to the new UE.

  This decision is entirely local — each AP only needs to compare the new UE's
  channel with its current UEs on the same pilot. No global coordination.

  What happens to the "replaced" UE in case (b)? The replaced UE loses one AP
  from its cluster but retains its Master AP and any other APs that still serve
  it. Since the Master AP is required to serve the UE, the replaced UE is never
  completely dropped from service.

  2.4 Properties of the Algorithm

  (1) Guaranteed service: Every UE is served by at least its Master AP. No UE
  can be orphaned.

  (2) Bounded per-AP load: Each AP serves at most τₚ UEs (one per pilot). Since
  τₚ is fixed, this is scalable.

  (3) Distributed execution: Each step uses only local information:
  - Step 1: UE measures local βₗ values
  - Step 2: Master AP uses its local Ψₜₗ matrices
  - Step 3: Each neighbor compares local β values

  (4) Scalable complexity: The initial access and pilot assignment have per-AP
  cost independent of K. As K → ∞:
  - AP l only estimates ĥₖₗ for k ∈ 𝒟ₗ (at most τₚ UEs)
  - Complexity per AP: at most τₚ × N² multiplications per coherence block —
  constant

  2.5 Handling UE Mobility and Network Changes

  The paper describes how the algorithm adapts over time:

  UE-initiated update: When a UE moves, it can redo the entire procedure by
  appointing a new Master AP. The new Master informs the previous Master that it
   has taken over.

  Master-AP-initiated update: The current Master AP can periodically recompute
  Eq. (15) to check if a different pilot would give less contamination. If so,
  the pilot is changed.

  2.6 Remark 2: Synchronization

  The paper acknowledges that the system model in Eq. (10) assumes perfect
  synchronization, which is "practically infeasible due to propagation delays."
  However:
  - Each AP synchronizes with its neighbors
  - Each UE synchronizes with its Master AP in the initial access
  - The dominant terms in Eq. (10) will be accurate
  - This makes the model "reasonable for analysis"

  2.7 Footnote on Master AP Priority

  There's an important footnote (footnote 5): the pilot assignment should prefer
   a pilot on which the AP is not currently serving a UE as its Master AP, since
   the Master AP role has higher priority (it guarantees service). Each AP can
  be the Master AP of at most τₚ UEs. In the unlikely event this cannot be
  satisfied, multiple UEs can share the same pilot but be multiplexed in time
  and/or frequency.

  3.1 Initial Access in Cellular Networks

  In 5G NR, the initial access procedure involves:
  1. SS/PBCH Block detection: The base station periodically transmits
  Synchronization Signal / Physical Broadcast Channel blocks. Each block
  contains PSS (for coarse timing and cell ID group), SSS (for fine timing and
  cell ID), and PBCH (system information).
  2. Random Access (RACH): 4-step procedure — (a) UE sends preamble, (b) BS
  sends Random Access Response, (c) UE sends identity, (d) BS resolves
  contention.

  Adaptation to Cell-Free: In this paper, the UE measures βₗ from multiple APs'
  broadcast signals (not just one cell), selects the strongest, and initiates
  RACH to that AP specifically.

  Source: S. Sesia, I. Toufik, and M. Baker, LTE — The UMTS Long Term Evolution:
   From Theory to Practice, Wiley, 2009 [42].

  3.2 Pilot Assignment in Cell-Free Massive MIMO

  The pilot assignment problem has been studied extensively:

  Random Assignment (Ngo et al. 2017 [5]):
  - Simplest approach: assign pilots uniformly at random
  - No optimization, no guarantees about contamination
  - Used in the original Cell-Free Massive MIMO paper

  Greedy Centralized Assignment (Ngo et al. 2017 [5]):
  - Iteratively assign each UE the pilot that maximizes the minimum SE
  - Requires global CSI knowledge → unscalable
  - NP-hard in general

  Location-Based Assignment (various works):
  - Group geographically distant UEs on the same pilot
  - Reduces contamination since co-pilot UEs have weak channels to each other's
  APs
  - Can be done with only location information

  This paper's approach (Eq. 15):
  - Local decision: Master AP picks the least-contaminated pilot from its local
  perspective
  - Requires only local Ψₜₗ knowledge (available at the AP)
  - Complexity: O(τₚ) — trivial, just compare τₚ trace values
  - Scalable: No dependence on K

  4. MATHEMATICAL DEEP DIVE

  4.1 The Large-Scale Fading Coefficient βₗ (Equation 14)

  The large-scale fading coefficient is defined as:

  ▎ βₗ = tr(R₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ) / N

  For a UE at distance dₗ from AP l, a typical model is:

  ▎ βₗ = PL(dₗ) · 10^(Fₗ/10)

  where:
  - PL(dₗ) is the deterministic pathloss (e.g., PL = d⁻ᵅ with pathloss exponent
  α)
  - Fₗ ~ 𝒩(0, σ²_SF) is the shadow fading (log-normal, in dB)

  In decibels:

  ▎ βₗ [dB] = PL₀ − 10α log₁₀(dₗ) + Fₗ

  Typical values: PL₀ = −30.5 dB (at 1 m), α = 3.67, σ_SF = 4 dB (from [2, Sec.
  4.1.3]).

  Why argmax βₗ selects the best AP: Since βₗ represents the average channel
  gain (combining pathloss and shadowing), the AP with the highest βₗ provides:
  - Strongest average received signal in UL
  - Strongest average received signal in DL
  - Best channel estimation quality (highest effective pilot SNR)

  4.2 The Pilot Assignment Criterion (Equation 15)

  The Master AP selects the pilot with the least total observed power:

  ▎ τ = argminₜ tr(Ψₜₗ)        ... (Eq. 15)

  Expanding tr(Ψₜₗ) using Eq. (4):

  ▎ tr(Ψₜₗ) = tr(∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ Rᵢₗ + σ² Iₙ)

  ▎ = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ tr(Rᵢₗ) + σ² N

  ▎ = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ N βᵢₗ + σ² N

  ▎ = N (∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚ pᵢ βᵢₗ + σ²)

  So minimizing tr(Ψₜₗ) is equivalent to:

  ▎ τ = argminₜ ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ pᵢ βᵢₗ

  Interpretation: Choose the pilot whose existing users have the weakest 
  combined channel to the Master AP. This minimizes the pilot contamination that
   the new UE will experience at its Master AP.

  Why this is the right criterion: The MMSE estimation quality of the new UE at
  its Master AP depends on Ψₜₗ⁻¹ (see Eq. 3). A smaller Ψₜₗ means a larger
  Ψₜₗ⁻¹, which means a better estimate. By choosing the pilot with the smallest
  tr(Ψₜₗ), we maximize the estimation quality at the Master AP — the most
  important AP for this UE.

  4.3 Step 3: The Neighbor Decision Rule — Formalized

  Let AP l be a neighboring AP notified by the Master AP. The decision:

  Case (a): AP l does not serve any UE on pilot τ.
  ▎ ∄ i ∈ 𝒟ₗ such that tᵢ = τ    (no existing UE on this pilot)
  ▎ Decision: Add UE K+1 to 𝒟ₗ. Set D₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ = Iₙ. ✅

  Case (b): AP l serves UE j on pilot τ (i.e., ∃ j ∈ 𝒟ₗ with tⱼ = τ).
  ▎ Compare: β₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ  vs.  βⱼₗ
  ▎ If β₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ > βⱼₗ:  Replace UE j with UE K+1. Set D₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ = Iₙ, Dⱼₗ = 0ₙ. ✅
  ▎ If β₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ ≤ βⱼₗ:  Keep UE j. Set D₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ = 0ₙ. ❌

  Why compare βₗ? The UE with the stronger channel benefits more from
  cooperation with AP l. The weaker UE can still be served by its own Master AP
  and possibly other APs.

  Critical safety property: When UE j is replaced by UE K+1 at AP l, UE j is not
   dropped from the network — it still has its Master AP (which never drops it)
  and possibly other APs. It only loses one AP from its cluster, slightly
  reducing its received power and diversity.

  │ AP 2 │ UE 1          │ —       │ UE 3    │
  ├──────┼───────────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ AP 3 │ UE 5          │ UE 2    │ UE 3    │
  ├──────┼───────────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ AP 4 │ UE 5 (Master) │ UE 4    │ —       │
  ├──────┼───────────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ AP 5 │ UE 5          │ UE 4    │ —       │
  ├──────┼───────────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ AP 6 │ UE 5          │ —       │ —       │
  └──────┴───────────────┴─────────┴─────────┘

  UE 5's cluster: ℳ₅ = {3, 4, 5, 6} with AP 4 as Master. Each AP still serves ≤
  τₚ = 3 UEs. ✅

  Example 2: When a Neighbor AP Must Switch

  Same setup, but now suppose AP 3 already serves UE 1 on Pilot 1 (not just UE 2
   on Pilot 2 and UE 3 on Pilot 3). Then in Step 3:

  - AP 3 already serves UE 1 on Pilot 1. Compare: β₅₃ = 0.020 vs β₁₃ = 0.015 (UE
   1's channel to AP 3).
  - Since β₅₃ > β₁₃, AP 3 switches: drops UE 1 and starts serving UE 5 on Pilot
  1.
  - UE 1 still has its Master AP (AP 1 or AP 2) — not orphaned. Just loses one
  AP from its cluster.

  Example 3: Pilot Assignment Quality

  Setup: τₚ = 5 pilots, AP ℓ (Master). Existing UEs on each pilot:

  ┌───────┬───────────────────────────┬─────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Pilot │    UEs on this pilot      │ Total contamination at AP ℓ: ∑ᵢ pᵢ  │
  │       │        (globally)         │                 βᵢₗ                 │
  ├───────┼───────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ 1     │ UE 3 (far), UE 7 (far)    │ 0.001 + 0.002 = 0.003               │
  ├───────┼───────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ 2     │ UE 1 (near)               │ 0.050                               │
  ├───────┼───────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ 3     │ UE 5 (medium), UE 9 (far) │ 0.010 + 0.001 = 0.011               │
  ├───────┼───────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ 4     │ (empty)                   │ 0.000                               │
  ├───────┼───────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ 5     │ UE 2 (near), UE 8         │ 0.040 + 0.008 = 0.048               │
  │       │ (medium)                  │                                     │
  └───────┴───────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────────┘

  Pilot assignment: τ = argmin{0.003, 0.050, 0.011, 0.000, 0.048} = Pilot 4
  (completely empty).

  This is the optimal choice — no contamination at all. The MMSE estimate at AP
  ℓ will only be limited by noise, not by pilot interference.

  If pilot 4 were not available (already used by this AP for another UE): τ =
  Pilot 1 (0.003 — two far UEs, negligible contamination).

  6. PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  Problem 1: Master AP Selection

  A new UE measures the following large-scale fading coefficients:

  β₁ = −85 dB, β₂ = −92 dB, β₃ = −78 dB, β₄ = −95 dB, β₅ = −80 dB
  (a) Which AP is selected as Master AP?
  (b) If AP 3 is already at maximum capacity (serving τₚ UEs as Master), what
  happens?
  (c) Convert β₃ from dB to linear scale if the reference is 1 mW.

  Solutions:

  (a) Master AP = argmax{−85, −92, −78, −95, −80} = AP 3 (−78 dB is strongest).

  (b) The paper states (footnote 5) that each AP can be Master to at most τₚ
  UEs. If AP 3 is full, the UE would select the next strongest: AP 5 (−80 dB).
  In the "unlikely event" this cannot be satisfied, UEs can share a pilot but be
   multiplexed in time/frequency.

  (c) β₃ = 10^(−78/10) mW = 10^(−7.8) mW = 1.585 × 10⁻⁸ mW = 1.585 × 10⁻¹¹ W
  (this is a very weak signal — typical for wireless channels with pathloss).

  Problem 2: Pilot Assignment

  An AP is the Master for a new UE. There are τₚ = 4 pilots. The traces of the
  pilot correlation matrices are:

  tr(**Ψ**₁ₗ) = 0.052, tr(**Ψ**₂ₗ) = 0.018, tr(**Ψ**₃ₗ) = 0.035, tr(**Ψ**₄ₗ) =
  0.007
  (a) Which pilot is assigned?
  (b) What does the very small tr(Ψ₄ₗ) indicate?
  (c) If σ² = 10⁻⁴ and N = 4, what is the noise floor contribution to each
  tr(Ψₜₗ)?
  (d) What is the total pilot contamination power on pilot 1 (excluding noise)?

  Solutions:

  (a) τ = argmin{0.052, 0.018, 0.035, 0.007} = Pilot 4 ✅

  (b) Very small tr(Ψ₄ₗ) means almost no existing UEs are using pilot 4 near
  this AP (or those using it are very far away). The new UE will experience
  negligible pilot contamination.

  (c) Noise floor: σ²N = 10⁻⁴ × 4 = 4 × 10⁻⁴ = 0.0004. This is the minimum
  possible tr(Ψₜₗ) (when no UE uses pilot t).

  (d) Contamination on pilot 1: tr(Ψ₁ₗ) − σ²N = 0.052 − 0.0004 = 0.0516
  (essentially all of it is contamination, not noise).

  Problem 3: Step 3 Decision

  AP l currently serves UE A on pilot 2 with β_A,l = 0.012. A neighboring Master
   AP notifies AP l about a new UE B on pilot 2 with β_B,l = 0.015.

  (a) What is the decision?
  (b) What if β_B,l = 0.008 instead?
  (c) After the switch in (a), how many UEs does AP l serve on pilot 2?

  Solutions:

  (a) β_B,l = 0.015 > β_A,l = 0.012 → Switch: AP l drops UE A and serves UE B on
   pilot 2. ✅

  (b) β_B,l = 0.008 < β_A,l = 0.012 → Keep UE A. UE B is not served by AP l. ❌

  (c) After the switch: AP l serves exactly 1 UE on pilot 2 (UE B). Assumption 1
   is maintained — at most one UE per pilot per AP.
