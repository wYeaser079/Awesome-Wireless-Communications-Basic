1. UL-DL DUALITY IN MIMO SYSTEMS
=================================

UL-DL duality states that any set of SINRs achievable on the uplink with
a given total power budget is also achievable on the downlink with the same
total power, though individual per-UE power allocations differ.

Origins.  Rashid-Farrokhi et al. [IEEE JSAC, vol. 16, no. 8, 1998] first
exploited this structure for joint transmit beamforming and power control.
Boche and Schubert [IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory, vol. 50, no. 10, 2004]
established a general duality framework solving the multiuser downlink
beamforming problem with individual SINR constraints by transforming it
into an equivalent UL problem.  Viswanath and Tse [IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory,
vol. 49, no. 8, 2003] proved UL-DL duality at the capacity level, showing
that the sum capacity of the vector Gaussian broadcast channel equals that
of the dual MAC under a sum-power constraint.

Extension to cell-free systems.  Classical duality assumes a co-located
array with a sum-power constraint.  In cell-free massive MIMO every AP l
has an individual power budget rho_dl, yielding L per-AP constraints
instead of one global constraint.  Proposition 4 in [1] shows that UL-DL
duality still holds in this setting when DCC matrices {D_i} restrict which
APs serve which UEs.  The precoding vectors are constructed as

  w_i = v_i / sqrt( E{ v_i^H D_i v_i } )                        (Eq. 33)

where v_i is the UL combining vector from Section V-B.  The DL power
coefficients {rho_k} are then obtained by solving a linear system that
enforces equal SINR in both directions under per-AP power constraints.
Because the per-AP constraints couple all UEs served by the same AP, the
DL power allocation differs from the UL allocation -- this is captured by
the matrix equation in Proposition 4.  Miretti et al. [IEEE Trans. Signal
Process., 2024] recently generalized this duality to include per-AP
information constraints (limited fronthaul), proving optimality of team
MMSE precoding in user-centric cell-free networks.


2. THE HARDENING BOUND FOR DL SE (PROPOSITION 3)
==================================================

Proposition 3 gives the DL SE for UE k as

  SE_k^{dl} = (tau_d / tau_c) log2(1 + SINR_k^{dl})

with

  SINR_k^{dl} = rho_k |E{ h_k^H D_k w_k }|^2 /
    [ sum_i rho_i E{ |h_k^H D_i w_i|^2 } - rho_k |E{ h_k^H D_k w_k }|^2
      + sigma_dl^2 ]

The numerator uses E{ h_k^H D_k w_k } -- the mean effective channel -- as
the "known" signal component.  The UE does not know the instantaneous
realization h_k^H D_k w_k; it knows only its statistical average.  All
deviation from this mean is treated as worst-case uncorrelated noise,
following the hardening bound methodology in [2, Th. 4.6].

When is the bound tight?  When channel hardening is strong (large NL,
correlated fading, MMSE-type precoding), the instantaneous effective gain
concentrates tightly around its mean, and the noise term is small.  In
co-located massive MIMO with M >> K, the bound is nearly exact [2].

When is it loose?  Remark 3 in [1] warns that the bound can be "rather
loose" in cell-free massive MIMO when N (antennas per AP) is small.  With
MR precoding and single-antenna APs (N = 1), the effective channel
h_k^H D_k w_k exhibits heavy fluctuations.  Chen and Bjornson [47] (IEEE
Trans. Commun., vol. 66, no. 11, 2018) quantified this, showing that
channel hardening in cell-free systems requires either large N or very
dense AP deployments.  With LP-MMSE precoding and N >= 4, the effective
channel distribution has bounded support (Fig. 4 in [1]), substantially
tightening the bound.


3. PRECODING DESIGN IN CELL-FREE SYSTEMS
==========================================

Downlink precoding is inherently harder than uplink combining because each
AP must simultaneously serve multiple UEs, and its transmit signal is the
superposition of all UE-directed beams.  This creates a joint design
problem constrained per AP:

  sum_{i in D_l} rho_i E{ || w_{il} ||^2 } <= rho_dl,  for each AP l

Under a centralized architecture (Level 4), the CPU computes all precoding
vectors {w_i} from global CSI and sends the composite DL signal to each AP
via the fronthaul.  This permits full P-MMSE or MMSE precoding.

Under a distributed architecture (Level 3), each AP l locally computes
{w_{il} : i in D_l} from its own channel estimates.  Only the power
coefficients {rho_i} need coordination.  LP-MMSE precoding is the natural
choice here, matching the LP-MMSE combiner from UL (by duality).

The UL-DL duality motivates using UL combiners directly as DL precoders:
rather than solving the coupled DL optimization from scratch, one solves
the decoupled UL combining problem and then maps the solution via (Eq. 33).
This avoids the NP-hard nature of general multiuser DL beamforming and
produces high-quality precoders with scalable complexity.


4. CHANNEL HARDENING IN CELL-FREE MASSIVE MIMO
================================================

Channel hardening means the effective channel gain h_k^H w_k concentrates
around its mean as the number of antennas grows.  Formally, it is measured
by the normalized variance:

  delta_k = Var{ h_k^H w_k } / | E{ h_k^H w_k } |^2

When delta_k -> 0, the channel "hardens" to a deterministic scalar, and
the hardening bound becomes tight.

In co-located massive MIMO with M antennas and MR combining, the law of
large numbers ensures delta_k = O(1/M), yielding strong hardening for
M >= 64 [2, Sec. 2.4].

In cell-free deployments, antennas are distributed across L APs with N
antennas each.  Even though the total count LN may be large, the
contribution of distant APs is attenuated by pathloss, so only a few
nearby APs provide strong channel gains.  This concentration of effective
array gain weakens the averaging effect.  Chen and Bjornson [47] proved
via stochastic geometry that:
  (i)   hardening requires small pathloss exponents or large N;
  (ii)  single-antenna APs (N = 1) yield weak hardening even with L large;
  (iii) N = 5-10 per AP substantially improves the hardening metric.

Fig. 4 in [1] illustrates this dramatically: with LP-MMSE precoding, the
PDF of the normalized effective channel has bounded support (finite
variance), whereas MR precoding produces an exponential tail (heavy
fluctuations).  The practical implication is that LP-MMSE precoding not
only improves SE through interference suppression but also tightens the
SE bound itself.


5. DL POWER ALLOCATION
========================

Per-AP power constraints.  Each AP l has a maximum DL power rho_dl,
yielding L separate constraints.  This differs from cellular MIMO where a
single sum-power constraint applies to the entire array.

Centralized equal allocation.  For centralized precoding with the
normalized precoders in (Eq. 33), the equal allocation rho_i = rho_dl /
tau_p for all i satisfies every per-AP constraint because each AP serves
at most tau_p UEs (Assumption 1) and the precoders are norm-normalized.

Distributed fractional allocation.  For distributed precoding, Interdonato
et al. [48] (Proc. IEEE ICC, 2019) proposed the heuristic (Eq. 43 in [1]):

  rho_{ik} proportional to ( beta_{kl}^{alpha} / sum_{i'} beta_{i'l}^{alpha} )

where beta_{kl} is the large-scale fading coefficient and alpha in [0, 1]
is a fractional exponent.  This allocates more power to UEs with stronger
channels to each AP, mimicking fractional power control from cellular
uplink.  The allocation is computed locally per AP using only long-term
statistics, making it fully scalable.

Network-wide optimization.  Jointly optimizing {rho_k} across all APs
and UEs for max-min fairness or sum-SE requires solving a global problem
coupling all L per-AP constraints.  This is inherently unscalable:  the
problem dimension grows with LK, and centralized coordination of power
coefficients across all APs contradicts the scalable design philosophy.
Fractional allocation avoids this by distributing the decision.
