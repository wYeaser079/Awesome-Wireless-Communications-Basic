1. UPLINK POWER CONTROL IN CELL-FREE MASSIVE MIMO
===================================================

Full power transmission (p_i = P for all i) is the simplest strategy: every
UE transmits at maximum power regardless of channel conditions.  Bjornson
and Sanguinetti [7] (IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun., vol. 19, no. 2, 2020)
showed this is "good for both strong and weak UEs" in cell-free systems
because, unlike cellular networks, macro-diversity from many distributed
APs eliminates the near-far effect.  Strong UEs are heard by many APs;
weak UEs are heard by fewer APs but still benefit from full power reaching
those closest APs.  Full power is therefore a reasonable default when the
primary concern is simplicity rather than strict fairness.

Max-min fairness power control aims to maximize the minimum SE across all
UEs, producing uniform service quality.  Ngo et al. [5] (IEEE Trans.
Commun., vol. 65, no. 4, 2017) formulated this for cell-free massive MIMO
as a quasi-concave optimization solved via bisection: at each step an SOCP
feasibility problem is checked.  Bashar et al. [31] (IEEE Trans. Commun.,
vol. 67, no. 10, 2019) extended max-min to include fronthaul quantization
constraints, solving the problem via geometric programming (GP) combined
with an iterative receiver-filter / power-allocation decomposition.

Fractional power control was introduced to cell-free systems by Nikbakht
and Lozano [34] (Proc. IEEE ICC, 2019).  It generalizes the cellular
fractional power control policy p_i = P * (f(beta_i))^alpha, where
beta_i is a large-scale fading measure and alpha in [0, 1] is a single
control parameter.  At alpha = 0 all UEs use full power; at alpha = 1 full
channel inversion is recovered.  The scheme relies only on large-scale
statistics, is fully distributed, and provides a smooth trade-off between
average SE and fairness.

Why network-wide UL power optimization is unscalable.  Max-min SINR via
bisection + SOCP has complexity growing in the total number of UEs K and
APs L; all UEs and APs must be jointly considered.  Every new UE added to
the network changes the global feasibility problem.  Fractional or full
power control is computed per-UE using only local statistics, satisfying
the scalability definition in [1, Def. 2].


2. DOWNLINK POWER ALLOCATION
==============================

Per-AP constraints.  Each AP l has maximum DL power rho, yielding L
independent constraints rather than one global sum-power constraint.

Equal power allocation: rho_i = rho / tau_p for all UEs i.  Because each
AP serves at most tau_p pilot-sharing groups (Assumption 1 in [1]) and the
precoding vectors w_{il} are unit-normalized (Eq. 33 in [1]), the power
consumed at AP l is sum_{i in D_l} rho_i E{ ||w_{il}||^2 } <= tau_p *
(rho / tau_p) * 1 = rho.  Every AP automatically satisfies its constraint
regardless of the channel realization.  This is the simplest scalable DL
power rule.

Fractional allocation from Interdonato et al. [33] (Proc. IEEE ICC, 2019):

  rho_{kl} = rho * sqrt(beta_{kl}) / sum_{i in D_l} sqrt(beta_{il})

The numerator weights UE k proportional to sqrt(beta_{kl}), so UEs with
stronger large-scale fading to AP l receive more power from that AP.  The
denominator normalizes so that AP l's total power equals rho.  This is a
locally-computed heuristic: each AP only needs its own large-scale fading
coefficients, making it scalable.

Comparison with water-filling.  Classical water-filling allocates power
inversely proportional to channel quality -- pouring more power into weaker
sub-channels until all reach the same level.  The fractional allocation
does the opposite: it gives more power to stronger channels at each AP.
The rationale is that in a multi-UE multi-AP DL setting, each AP should
concentrate its limited budget on UEs it can most effectively serve rather
than wasting power on distant UEs.  This is closer to "inverse water-
filling" or matched-filter power loading.

Network-wide DL power optimization.  Ngo et al. [5], Nayebi et al. [6]
(IEEE Trans. Wireless Commun., vol. 16, no. 10, 2017), and Bjornson and
Sanguinetti [10] (Proc. Asilomar, 2019) all formulated global DL power
optimization, typically max-min or max-sum-SE, involving all L per-AP
constraints jointly.  These require centralized knowledge of all channels
and yield problems whose dimension scales with L * K -- unscalable by the
paper's definition.  Dual decomposition [10, Sec. 4.3] partially
distributes the computation but still requires iterative message-passing
across all APs and a global dual variable update, making it impractical
at scale.


3. NETWORK TOPOLOGY OPTIONS
=============================

Star topology.  The original cell-free massive MIMO formulation [5] assumed
all L APs connected to a single CPU via dedicated fronthaul links.  This
is the simplest logical architecture but creates a single point of failure
and a fronthaul bottleneck that scales as O(L).

Multiple CPUs / edge-cloud processors.  APs are partitioned into groups,
each connected to one of several edge CPUs.  This mirrors the Cloud-RAN
(C-RAN) architecture studied by China Mobile Research Institute (C-I et
al. [55], IEEE Commun. Mag., vol. 52, no. 2, 2014) and surveyed by Peng
et al. [56] (IEEE Wireless Commun., vol. 22, no. 6, 2015), where Remote
Radio Heads (RRHs) connect to a centralized Baseband Unit (BBU) pool.
The key difference is that C-RAN retains cell boundaries while cell-free
systems do not.  Multiple CPUs reduce per-link fronthaul load but
introduce inter-CPU coordination overhead for UEs served by APs in
different groups.

Local processing at each AP.  Each AP acts as its own processor: it
estimates channels locally, computes combining/precoding locally, and
decodes data locally.  This is the classic cellular base station model
repurposed for a dense small-cell deployment.  No fronthaul is needed for
data processing, but inter-AP cooperation (the defining feature of cell-
free) is forfeited unless APs exchange information over backhaul.

Master AP as CPU.  The paper [1] introduces a key insight: for each UE k,
the "Master AP" (the AP with the strongest large-scale fading to UE k,
denoted m_k) performs the CPU tasks for that UE.  This includes final
signal combining (UL), power coefficient selection (DL), and SE
computation.  The Master AP aggregates partial local estimates from other
serving APs via the fronthaul, but only from those in UE k's DCC --
not from all APs.  Different UEs may have different Master APs, fully
distributing the CPU role across the network.

The pCell architecture.  Artemis Networks' pCell system [21] (Forenza et
al., Proc. Asilomar, 2015) is a commercial implementation of distributed
MIMO.  It uses software-defined radios as distributed APs connected to a
centralized cloud processor that jointly computes precoding across all
APs, creating a personal cell (pCell) for each UE.  pCell demonstrated
real-time cooperative transmission over commodity hardware, validating the
cell-free concept, but its centralized design corresponds to a single-CPU
star topology -- not scalable by the criteria in [1].

The CPU as a "set of tasks."  Section V-E in [1] redefines the CPU not as
a physical box but as a collection of processing tasks: final combining,
power allocation, SE computation, handover decisions.  These tasks can be
distributed arbitrarily across the network infrastructure.  Any physical
node -- a dedicated server, an edge processor, or even an AP itself --
can execute CPU tasks for a subset of UEs.  This abstraction decouples the
logical architecture from the physical deployment and is essential for
scalability: adding UEs or APs redistributes tasks without redesigning
the network topology.


4. SCALABLE VS. UNSCALABLE POWER CONTROL ALGORITHMS
=====================================================

Unscalable algorithms:
  - Max-min SINR: bisection + SOCP, O(K^3.5) per iteration [5]
  - Sum-SE maximization: non-convex, typically solved via SCA or WMMSE
    with complexity coupling all K UEs and L APs
  - Weighted MMSE (WMMSE): iterates between MMSE filters and power
    updates over the full network [10]

Scalable heuristics:
  - Full UL power (p_i = P): zero computational cost
  - Equal DL power (rho_i = rho / tau_p): zero computational cost
  - Fractional power control [34]: per-UE computation using local stats
  - Fractional DL allocation [33]: per-AP computation using local stats

The paper's philosophy.  Any heuristic power rule -- however simple -- is
acceptable as long as it can be embedded in the DCC framework without
violating the scalability definition (Def. 2 in [1]).  The key requirement
is that each AP and each UE can compute its power coefficients using only
information from its own DCC, not from the entire network.  Optimality is
explicitly sacrificed for scalability; the SE loss is compensated by the
gains from LP-MMSE combining/precoding and well-designed DCCs.
