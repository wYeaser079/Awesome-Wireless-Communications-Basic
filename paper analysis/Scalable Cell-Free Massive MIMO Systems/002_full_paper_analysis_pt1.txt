● Section I: Introduction — Structured Summary

  ---
  1.1 The Big Picture: What Is This Paper About?

  Imagine a city where instead of a few powerful cell towers each "owning" a
  region of users, you have hundreds of small access points (APs) on lampposts,
  buildings, and rooftops — all cooperating to serve every user jointly. No user
   is at a "cell edge" because there are no cells. That's Cell-Free Massive 
  MIMO.

  The problem? Previous formulations of this idea required every AP to know
  about and process signals for every user in the network. If you have 10,000
  users, every single AP must handle 10,000 data streams. That's like asking
  every post office in a country to sort mail for every household — it doesn't
  scale.

  This paper asks: How do we keep the benefits of cell-free operation while 
  making the system practical for arbitrarily large networks?

  1.4 Key Contributions Claimed

  The paper makes four main contributions:

  1. Unification: Proves that Cell-Free Massive MIMO is a special case of the
  DCC framework from Network MIMO literature, bridging two previously
  disconnected research communities.
  2. Scalable Initial Access Algorithm: A 3-step procedure for joint initial
  access, pilot assignment, and cluster formation that is provably scalable —
  every UE is guaranteed service by at least one AP (Master AP).
  3. Scalable Signal Processing:
    - P-MMSE (Partial MMSE) combining/precoding for centralized operation
    - LP-MMSE (Local Partial MMSE) for distributed operation
    - Both outperform MR and approach unscalable MMSE performance
  4. UL-DL Duality: A new duality result (Proposition 4) enabling DL precoding
  vectors to be designed directly from UL combining vectors — transferring
  scalability from UL to DL.

  1.5 Context: Why "Cell-Free" Resurged

  The paper explains the timing:
  - 2010s: Massive MIMO (co-located) gained spotlight → led to 5G NR standard
  [25]
  - Post-2017: With Massive MIMO mature, research shifted back to
  distributed/cooperative operation
  - Rebranding: "Network MIMO" → "Cell-Free Massive MIMO" — same concept but now
   with imperfect CSI analysis and TDD operation
  - Key novelty of CF-mMIMO over Network MIMO: SE analysis with imperfect CSI
  - Key step backward: Lost the DCC and scalability aspects that Network MIMO
  had begun developing

  This paper rectifies that step backward.

  Key Takeaways

  - Cell-Free Massive MIMO eliminates cell edges by having distributed APs
  jointly serve all users, but previous formulations had O(K) complexity per AP
  — fundamentally unscalable
  - The paper bridges two research threads (CF-mMIMO and Network MIMO/DCC) that

  - Scalability is formally defined: per-AP complexity must stay finite as K → ∞

  - The user-centric (overlapping cluster) approach is essential — disjoint

  - The paper's position in the literature is as a "missing link" connecting the
   scalability tools of Network MIMO with the modern CF-mMIMO framework

  2. Can the DCC framework be adapted for dynamic/mobile scenarios where cluster

  3. What is the optimal trade-off between cluster size and performance — is

  more APs to a user's cluster?
  4. How does this framework interact with 3GPP standardization efforts around
  Coordinated Multipoint (CoMP) in Release 18+ and the emerging cell-free

  Part A: System Geometry, Channel Model & Statistical Framework

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  What problem does this solve?

  Before you can design algorithms for a cell-free network, you need a precise
  mathematical description of the physical reality: How do signals travel from
  APs to users? What does the network look like? How do we model the randomness
  of wireless channels?

  The Analogy: Think of a concert hall with hundreds of small speakers (APs)
  mounted throughout the ceiling, and an audience of listeners (UEs) scattered
  across the floor. Each speaker-listener pair has a unique acoustic channel —
  affected by distance (sound gets weaker farther away), obstacles (pillars,
  other people), and reflections off walls. The "network model" is the
  mathematical blueprint of this entire acoustic environment.

  Key simplifications the authors make:
  - Each listener has a single ear (single-antenna UE)
  - Each speaker has N small driver units (N-antenna AP)
  - The acoustic environment changes randomly over time in "blocks" — during
  each block the channel is fixed, then it changes independently to a new random
   realization
  - The speakers are connected to sound engineers (CPUs) via cables (fronthaul)

  Why it matters: Every SE expression, every combining/precoding design, and
  every scalability claim later in the paper rests on this model. If the model
  is wrong or unrealistic, everything built on it collapses.

  Layer 3: Rigorous Technical Deep Dive

  3.1 Network Geometry

  [ASSUMPTION 1] The network consists of:
  - K single-antenna UEs (user equipments)
  - L APs (access points), each with N antennas
  - Total antenna count: M = NL
  - APs and UEs are arbitrarily distributed over the coverage area
  - APs are connected to edge-cloud processors called CPUs

  This is deliberately general — no grid structure, no hexagonal cells, no
  assumptions on AP placement regularity.

  [ASSUMPTION 2] The system is "Cell-Free Massive MIMO" when L and K are large.
  The original papers [5], [6] assumed L ≫ K, but the methods in this paper hold
   for any L, K values.

  3.2 TDD Protocol Structure

  [ASSUMPTION 3] The system operates in Time-Division Duplex (TDD) mode.

  Each coherence block has τ_c channel uses, divided as:

  ┌──────────────┬──────────────────────┬──────────────────────┐
  │   UL Pilots  │      UL Data        │      DL Data         │
  │     τₚ       │        τᵤ           │        τ_d           │
  └──────────────┴──────────────────────┴──────────────────────┘
                τ_c = τₚ + τᵤ + τ_d

  Physical meaning of τ_c: The coherence block is the time-frequency region over
   which the channel can be treated as approximately constant. It depends on:
  - Coherence time T_c ≈ 1/(2f_D) where f_D is the maximum Doppler spread
  - Coherence bandwidth B_c ≈ 1/τ_rms where τ_rms is the RMS delay spread
  - τ_c ≈ T_c × B_c (number of independent signal dimensions)

  Why TDD? Channel reciprocity — the UL channel equals the DL channel (within
  the coherence block). This means UL pilot-based channel estimates can be
  directly used for DL precoding, avoiding the need for DL pilot overhead that
  scales with M (which would be enormous in Massive MIMO).

  3.3 Channel Model

  The channel between AP l and UE k is:

  hₖₗ ∈ ℂᴺ

  This is an N-dimensional complex vector. The collective channel from all L APs
   to UE k is:

  hₖ = [hₖ₁ᵀ  hₖ₂ᵀ  ...  hₖₗᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ

  [ASSUMPTION 4] In each coherence block, the channel is drawn independently
  from a correlated Rayleigh fading distribution:

  hₖₗ ~ 𝒩_ℂ(0, Rₖₗ)                                   — Eq. (1a)

  where Rₖₗ ∈ ℂᴺˣᴺ is the spatial correlation matrix.

  What Rₖₗ encodes:
  - tr(Rₖₗ)/N = βₖₗ — the average channel gain (captures path loss + shadowing)
  - Eigenstructure of Rₖₗ — the spatial directivity (which directions signals
  arrive from/depart to)
  - Rank of Rₖₗ — the angular spread (rank 1 = single path, full rank =
  isotropic scattering)

  [ASSUMPTION 5] Channels from different APs are independent:

  𝔼{hₖₙ(hₖₗ)ᴴ} = 0 for l ≠ n

  Justification: APs are spatially distributed throughout the network (meters to
   hundreds of meters apart), so their local scattering environments are
  independent. This is in contrast to co-located arrays where antennas are
  wavelengths apart.

  Consequence: The collective correlation matrix is block-diagonal:

  Rₖ = diag(Rₖ₁, Rₖ₂, ..., Rₖₗ) ∈ ℂᴹˣᴹ            — Eq. (1b)

  This block-diagonal structure is computationally powerful — it means
  operations on the M×M matrix decompose into L independent operations on N×N
  matrices.

  [ASSUMPTION 6] Different UEs' channels are independently distributed:

  hₖ and hⱼ are independent for k ≠ j

  [ASSUMPTION 7] The spatial correlation matrices {Rₖₗ} are known wherever
  needed.

  This is a standard assumption in Massive MIMO. In practice, these matrices
  change slowly (on the order of seconds to minutes) and can be estimated from
  uplink measurements over many coherence blocks. References [36]–[39] provide
  practical algorithms.

  3.4 Understanding the Correlation Matrix — A Deeper Look

  The Gaussian distribution hₖₗ ~ 𝒩_ℂ(0, Rₖₗ) models:
  - Small-scale fading — the random phase shifts and amplitude variations due to
   multipath propagation (captured by the Gaussian randomness)
  - Large-scale fading — geometric path loss, shadowing, antenna gains, and
  spatial channel correlation (captured by Rₖₗ)

  The eigendecomposition of Rₖₗ reveals the channel's spatial structure:

  Rₖₗ = Uₖₗ Λₖₗ Uₖₗᴴ

  where:
  - Uₖₗ ∈ ℂᴺˣᴺ — eigenvectors (dominant spatial directions)
  - Λₖₗ = diag(λ₁, λ₂, ..., λₙ) — eigenvalues (power along each direction)

  Special cases:
  - Uncorrelated fading (isotropic): Rₖₗ = βₖₗIₙ — all directions equally
  likely. This was assumed in the original CF-mMIMO papers [5], [6]
  - Fully correlated (LoS, rank-1): Rₖₗ = Nβₖₗa(θ)a(θ)ᴴ — single dominant path.
  All energy in one spatial direction
  - General (this paper): Arbitrary Rₖₗ — handles any propagation environment

  β₁₁ = 10^((-30.5 - 36.7·log₁₀(20))/10)
       = 10^((-30.5 - 47.8)/10)
       = 10^(-7.83)
       = 1.48 × 10⁻⁸  (≈ -78.3 dB)

       = 10^((-30.5 - 69.9)/10)

       = 10^((-30.5 - 79.9)/10)
       = 10^(-11.04)

       = 10^((-30.5 - 90.9)/10)
       = 10^(-12.14)

  This demonstrates a crucial physical reality: the channel gain drops by over 4
   orders of magnitude between the nearest and farthest AP. This is precisely
  why scalability works — distant APs contribute negligibly, so ignoring them
  (via DCC clustering) costs almost nothing in performance.

  Correlation matrix example for uncorrelated fading:

  For UE 1, AP 1 with uncorrelated Rayleigh fading (N=2):

  R₁₁ = β₁₁ · I₂ = 1.48×10⁻⁸ · [1 0; 0 1]

  Correlation matrix example with spatial correlation (ULA, Gaussian angular 
  spread):

  For a Uniform Linear Array (ULA) with antenna spacing d = λ/2 and angular
  standard deviation σ_φ = 15°, the (m,n)-th element of Rₖₗ follows:

  [Rₖₗ]ₘₙ = βₖₗ · exp(j2π(m-n)d/λ · sin(φₖₗ)) · exp(-σ_φ²/2 · (2π(m-n)d/λ · 
  cos(φₖₗ))²)

  For UE 1, AP 1 with angle of arrival φ₁₁ = 30°, σ_φ = 15° (≈0.26 rad), N=2:

  [R₁₁]₁₁ = β₁₁ = 1.48×10⁻⁸

  [R₁₁]₁₂ = β₁₁ · exp(jπ sin(30°)) · exp(-0.26²/2 · (π cos(30°))²)
           = 1.48×10⁻⁸ · exp(jπ/2) · exp(-0.0338 · 7.40)
           = 1.48×10⁻⁸ · j · exp(-0.250)
           = 1.48×10⁻⁸ · j · 0.779
           = j · 1.153×10⁻⁸

  [R₁₁]₂₁ = [R₁₁]₁₂* = -j · 1.153×10⁻⁸

  So:

  R₁₁ = 1.48×10⁻⁸ · [ 1.000    j·0.779 ]
                       [-j·0.779  1.000   ]

  The off-diagonal magnitude (0.779) indicates high spatial correlation — the
  two antennas "see" a similar channel because the angular spread is narrow.
  With wider angular spread (σ_φ → ∞), the off-diagonal → 0 and we recover R =
  βI.

  Part B: Pilot Transmission and Channel Estimation

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  The problem: APs need to know the channels to their served UEs in order to
  beamform (in DL) or combine signals (in UL). But the channel changes every
  coherence block — so the APs must re-learn the channels continually.

  The analogy: Imagine you're in a dark room with several flashlights (UEs) and
  several cameras (APs). To figure out where each flashlight is, you ask each
  flashlight to blink with a unique pattern (pilot signal). The cameras observe
  the blinking patterns and triangulate positions. But you only have τₚ distinct
   patterns. If you have more flashlights than patterns, some must share
  patterns — and the cameras can't easily tell them apart. This is pilot 
  contamination.

  Why it matters: Channel estimation quality directly determines how well the
  system can beamform. Poor estimates → poor beamforming → low SE. And the way
  pilots are shared determines which UEs contaminate each other — a recurring
  theme throughout the paper.

  Two effects of pilot contamination (from [2, Sec. 3.3.2]):
  1. Reduced estimation quality — the estimate is noisier, making coherent
  transmission less effective
  2. Correlated estimates — estimates of pilot-sharing UEs become correlated,
  causing coherent interference that scales with N (just like the desired
  signal)

  The second effect is particularly dangerous and has received much attention
  [22], [40], [41]. However, [41] proved that pilot contamination does NOT
  impose a fundamental capacity limit when optimal processing is used — it only
  limits suboptimal schemes like MR.

  Layer 3: Rigorous Technical Deep Dive

  3.5 Pilot Signal Structure

  [ASSUMPTION 8] There are τₚ mutually orthogonal pilot signals, each of length
  τₚ. The value τₚ is a constant independent of K.

  This is critical for scalability — the pilot resource does not grow with the
  number of users. The consequence is that when K > τₚ, pilots must be reused
  (shared).

  Let S_t ⊂ {1, ..., K} denote the subset of UEs assigned to pilot t, where t ∈
  {1, ..., τₚ}.

  3.6 Received Pilot Signal

  When UEs sharing pilot t transmit simultaneously, the received signal at AP l
  after despreading (correlating with the known pilot sequence) is:

  yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ = ∑_{i∈Sₜ} √(τₚpᵢ) hᵢₗ + nₜₗ                — Eq. (2)

  Variable definitions:
  - yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ ∈ ℂᴺ — received pilot signal at AP l for pilot t (after
  despreading)
  - pᵢ — transmit power of UE i (in Watts)
  - τₚ — pilot length (also the processing gain from despreading)
  - hᵢₗ — channel from UE i to AP l
  - nₜₗ ~ 𝒩_ℂ(0, σ²Iₙ) — thermal noise
  - σ² — noise power

  Physical meaning: AP l observes a superposition of channels from ALL UEs
  sharing pilot t, scaled by their respective powers and the processing gain
  √τₚ, plus noise. The AP cannot separate these contributions — this is the
  source of pilot contamination.

  Why √(τₚpᵢ) and not just √pᵢ? The factor √τₚ is the despreading gain. When a
  pilot of length τₚ is correlated with the matching sequence, the signal power
  is boosted by τₚ while noise remains at σ². This is the same principle as in
  CDMA/spread-spectrum systems.

  3.7 MMSE Channel Estimation

  For UE k assigned to pilot t (i.e., k ∈ S_t), the MMSE estimate of hₖₗ is:

  ĥₖₗ = √(pₖτₚ) Rₖₗ Ψₜₗ⁻¹ yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ                 — Eq. (3)

  where the correlation matrix of the received pilot signal is:

  Ψₜₗ = 𝔼{yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ (yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ)ᴴ} = ∑_{i∈Sₜ} τₚpᵢRᵢₗ + σ²Iₙ    — Eq. (4)

  Step-by-step derivation of MMSE estimate:

  The MMSE estimator minimizes 𝔼{‖hₖₗ - ĥₖₗ‖²}. For jointly Gaussian random
  vectors, the MMSE estimate of hₖₗ given observation yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ is (standard
  Bayesian estimation theory):

  ĥₖₗ = 𝔼{hₖₗ (yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ)ᴴ} · [𝔼{yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ (yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ)ᴴ}]⁻¹ · yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ

  Computing the cross-correlation:

  𝔼{hₖₗ (yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ)ᴴ} = 𝔼{hₖₗ (∑_{i∈Sₜ} √(τₚpᵢ) hᵢₗ + nₜₗ)ᴴ}

  Since channels of different UEs are independent and noise is independent of
  channels:

  = √(pₖτₚ) · 𝔼{hₖₗ hₖₗᴴ} = √(pₖτₚ) · Rₖₗ

  Therefore:

  ĥₖₗ = √(pₖτₚ) Rₖₗ Ψₜₗ⁻¹ yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ   ✓

  Key properties of the MMSE estimate:

  (P1) The estimate is distributed as:
  ĥₖₗ ~ 𝒩_ℂ(0, pₖτₚ Rₖₗ Ψₜₗ⁻¹ Rₖₗ)

  (P2) The estimation error h̃ₖₗ = hₖₗ - ĥₖₗ is distributed as:
  h̃ₖₗ ~ 𝒩_ℂ(0, Cₖₗ)

  where the error correlation matrix is:
  Cₖₗ = Rₖₗ - pₖτₚ Rₖₗ Ψₜₗ⁻¹ Rₖₗ

  (P3) The estimate and error are uncorrelated (orthogonality principle):
  𝔼{ĥₖₗ h̃ₖₗᴴ} = 0

  (P4) The matrix √(pₖτₚ) Rₖₗ Ψₜₗ⁻¹ in Eq. (3) depends only on statistics (not
  instantaneous channel realizations). It can be precomputed once and stored at
  AP l — negligible computational cost over time.

  3.8 Computational Complexity of Channel Estimation

  Per pilot sequence at AP l:

  ┌──────────────────┬──────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────┐
  │    Operation     │      Complexity      │          Description          │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
  │ Despreading      │ Nτₚ complex          │ Correlate received signal     │
  │                  │ multiplications      │ with pilot                    │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
  │ MMSE estimate    │ N² complex           │ Multiply precomputed matrix   │
  │ per UE           │ multiplications      │ with despreaded signal        │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
  │ Ψₜₗ⁻¹            │ O(N³) one-time cost  │ Matrix inversion, done        │
  │ (precomputed)    │                      │ offline                       │
  └──────────────────┴──────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┘

  Total per coherence block per AP: Nτₚ (despreading) + N² per served UE
  (estimation)

  In the original CF-mMIMO (all APs estimate all K UEs): complexity = K × N² per
   AP → grows with K → unscalable.

  With DCC (AP l estimates only UEs in 𝒟ₗ): complexity = |𝒟ₗ| × N² per AP →
  bounded by τₚN² → scalable.

  3.9 Pilot Contamination — Mathematical Structure

  When UE k shares pilot t with other UEs, the estimate ĥₖₗ becomes:

  ĥₖₗ = √(pₖτₚ) Rₖₗ Ψₜₗ⁻¹ (∑_{i∈Sₜ} √(τₚpᵢ) hᵢₗ + nₜₗ)

  Expanding:

  ĥₖₗ = pₖτₚ Rₖₗ Ψₜₗ⁻¹ hₖₗ  +  √(pₖτₚ) Rₖₗ Ψₜₗ⁻¹ ∑_{i∈Sₜ, i≠k} √(τₚpᵢ) hᵢₗ  +  
  √(pₖτₚ) Rₖₗ Ψₜₗ⁻¹ nₜₗ

           ⎡ Desired component ⎤   ⎡ Pilot contamination  ⎤   ⎡ Noise component
  ⎤
  ĥₖₗ  =  ⎣ (from UE k)       ⎦ + ⎣ (from co-pilot UEs)  ⎦ + ⎣ (from thermal)  ⎦

  The pilot contamination term means that when AP l beamforms toward UE k using
  ĥₖₗ, it inadvertently also beamforms toward every UE i ∈ S_t sharing the same
  pilot. This creates coherent interference that does not vanish as N → ∞ with
  MR processing.

  Correlation between co-pilot estimates:

  𝔼{ĥₖₗ ĥᵢₗᴴ} = pₖpᵢτₚ² Rₖₗ Ψₜₗ⁻¹ Rᵢₗ  ≠ 0 for i, k ∈ S_t

  This non-zero cross-correlation is the mathematical signature of pilot
  contamination.

  Layer 4: Numerical Example (Channel Estimation)

  Continuing from Part A with UE 1, AP 1:

  ┌──────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │    Parameter     │                        Value                         │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ N                │ 2                                                    │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ τₚ               │ 10 pilots                                            │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ p₁ = p₂ = ... =  │ 100 mW = 0.1 W                                       │
  │ P                │                                                      │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ σ²               │ -94 dBm = 3.98 × 10⁻¹³ W (at 20 MHz BW, noise figure │
  │                  │  9 dB)                                               │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ β₁₁              │ 1.48 × 10⁻⁸ (-78.3 dB)                               │
  └──────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Assume uncorrelated fading (R₁₁ = β₁₁I₂) and UE 1 is the only user on pilot 1
  (no pilot contamination):

  Step 1: Compute Ψ₁₁ (correlation matrix of received pilot at AP 1 for pilot 1)

  Ψ₁₁ = τₚ p₁ R₁₁ + σ² I₂
        = 10 × 0.1 × 1.48×10⁻⁸ · I₂ + 3.98×10⁻¹³ · I₂
        = (1.48×10⁻⁸ + 3.98×10⁻¹³) · I₂
        ≈ 1.48×10⁻⁸ · I₂

  The noise term is negligible (5 orders of magnitude smaller) — this is a
  high-SNR pilot regime for the nearest AP.

  Step 2: Compute the MMSE estimation matrix

  √(p₁τₚ) R₁₁ Ψ₁₁⁻¹ = √(0.1 × 10) × 1.48×10⁻⁸ · I₂ × (1.48×10⁻⁸)⁻¹ · I₂
                               = 1.0 × I₂

  Almost perfect estimation! The MMSE estimate ≈ the true channel for this close
   AP.

  Step 3: Compute estimation quality (normalized MSE)

  NMSE = tr(C₁₁) / tr(R₁₁) = 1 - p₁τₚ · tr(R₁₁ Ψ₁₁⁻¹ R₁₁) / tr(R₁₁)

  For the scalar (uncorrelated) case:

  NMSE = 1 - τₚp₁β₁₁/(τₚp₁β₁₁ + σ²)
       = σ²/(τₚp₁β₁₁ + σ²)
       = 3.98×10⁻¹³ / 1.48×10⁻⁸
       ≈ 2.69 × 10⁻⁵  (≈ -45.7 dB)

  Excellent estimation quality — NMSE ≈ 0.003%.

  Now for a distant AP (UE 1, AP 4):

  β₁₄ = 7.24 × 10⁻¹³

  NMSE = σ² / (τₚp₁β₁₄ + σ²) = 3.98×10⁻¹³ / (7.24×10⁻¹² + 3.98×10⁻¹³)
       = 3.98×10⁻¹³ / 7.64×10⁻¹²
       ≈ 0.052  (≈ -12.8 dB)

  Much worse estimation — 5.2% NMSE. The distant AP cannot estimate UE 1's
  channel well.

  Now add pilot contamination (UE 2 shares pilot 1):

  With β₂₁ = path loss from UE 2 to AP 1. If UE 2 is 200m from AP 1:

  β₂₁ = 10^((-30.5 - 36.7·log₁₀(200))/10) = 10^(-11.43) = 3.72 × 10⁻¹²

  Ψ₁₁ = τₚp₁β₁₁I₂ + τₚp₂β₂₁I₂ + σ²I₂
        = (1.48×10⁻⁸ + 3.72×10⁻¹¹ + 3.98×10⁻¹³) · I₂
        ≈ 1.48×10⁻⁸ · I₂

  The contamination from UE 2 is negligible at AP 1 because β₂₁ ≪ β₁₁ (UE 2 is
  far from AP 1). This is a characteristic of cell-free networks — pilot 
  contamination is naturally mitigated by the large path loss differences across
   distributed APs.

  Summary of estimation quality across APs for UE 1:

  ┌──────────────────────┬───────────┬───────────┬────────────┬───────────┐
  │                      │   AP 1    │   AP 2    │   AP 3     │   AP 4    │
  │                      │   (20m)   │   (80m)   │   (150m)   │  (300m)   │
  ├──────────────────────┼───────────┼───────────┼────────────┼───────────┤
  │ βₖₗ [dB]             │ -78.3     │ -100.4    │ -110.4     │ -121.4    │
  ├──────────────────────┼───────────┼───────────┼────────────┼───────────┤
  │ NMSE (no             │ 0.003%    │ 4.2%      │ 30.4%      │ 84.6%     │
  │ contamination)       │           │           │            │           │
  ├──────────────────────┼───────────┼───────────┼────────────┼───────────┤
  │ Useful for           │ Excellent │ Good      │ Marginal   │ No        │
  │ beamforming?         │           │           │            │           │
  └──────────────────────┴───────────┴───────────┴────────────┴───────────┘

  This table tells the whole scalability story: Only AP 1 and AP 2 have
  meaningful channel knowledge about UE 1. Including AP 3 and AP 4 in UE 1's
  cluster adds complexity with almost no benefit. This is why DCC works — you
  only need the nearby APs.

  ---
  Key Takeaways

  - The channel model uses correlated Rayleigh fading with block structure —
  standard in Massive MIMO but general enough to handle any propagation
  environment through the correlation matrix Rₖₗ
  - The block-diagonal structure of the collective correlation matrix (channels
  from different APs are independent) is what makes distributed processing
  possible and computationally tractable
  - MMSE channel estimation provides the optimal linear estimate; its quality is
   governed by the ratio τₚpₖβₖₗ/σ² (pilot SNR) and degrades with pilot
  contamination from co-pilot UEs
  - The estimation quality drops dramatically with distance — a 15× increase in
  distance leads to ~43 dB loss in channel gain, making distant APs nearly
  useless for a given UE. This physical fact is the foundation of why DCC-based
  scalability works
  - The precomputable statistical matrix √(pₖτₚ)RₖₗΨₜₗ⁻¹ means the
  per-coherence-block estimation cost is just one matrix-vector multiplication
  (N² operations per UE per AP)

  Open Research Questions

  1. How robust is the MMSE estimator to correlation matrix mismatch? In
  practice, Rₖₗ must be estimated from data — what is the SE degradation when
  the estimated correlation matrix has errors?
  2. Can deep learning-based channel estimators outperform MMSE in cell-free
  settings where the Gaussian channel model may not perfectly hold (e.g., with
  LoS components or sparse scattering at mmWave/THz)?
  3. What is the optimal pilot length τₚ as a function of network density and
  mobility? The paper treats it as a fixed constant — is there a principled way
  to adapt it?
  4. How does wideband (OFDM) operation change the estimation framework? Each
  subcarrier has a different channel realization in frequency-selective fading —
   does the estimation complexity scale with the number of subcarriers?
  5. Can pilot contamination be exploited (rather than just mitigated) by using
  the correlated estimates to perform joint detection of co-pilot users?

● Section III: Definition of Scalability and Review of the Original Form of

  This section serves as the problem statement of the paper — it precisely 
  defines what "scalable" means and then systematically demonstrates that the

  Part A: Uplink and Downlink Data Transmission (Original CF-mMIMO)

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  What is the "original" Cell-Free Massive MIMO?

  Picture a company where every employee (AP) must handle paperwork for every
  customer (UE). When you have 10 customers, that's manageable. When you have
  10,000 customers, every employee is overwhelmed — each must maintain files on
  all 10,000, process requests from all 10,000, and communicate about all 10,000
   through the company's internal mail system (fronthaul). This is the original
  CF-mMIMO: all APs serve all UEs, always.

  The UL picture: All K UEs transmit simultaneously. Every AP l receives a
  superposition of all K signals. Each AP computes a local combining operation
  for each UE and sends the result to a CPU, which adds them up to decode each
  UE's data.

  The DL picture: The CPU wants to send data to all K UEs. It tells every AP
  what to transmit. Each AP creates a signal that is a weighted sum of all K
  data streams, where the weights (precoding vectors) are designed to steer
  energy toward the intended UEs.

  Why this works in theory: With many distributed APs and good channel
  estimates, the combined signal from all APs creates strong effective channels
  to each UE while interference is suppressed by spatial diversity.

  Why this fails in practice: The "all serve all" assumption means every metric
  — computation, memory, fronthaul — scales linearly (or worse) with K.

  Layer 3: Rigorous Technical Deep Dive

  3.1 Uplink Signal Model

  During UL data transmission, each UE k transmits a signal sₖ ∈ ℂ with power pₖ
   = 𝔼{|sₖ|²}. The received signal at AP l is:

  yₗᵘˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + nₗ                               — Eq. (5)

  Variable definitions:
  - yₗᵘˡ ∈ ℂᴺ — received signal vector at AP l
  - hᵢₗ ∈ ℂᴺ — channel from UE i to AP l
  - sᵢ ∈ ℂ — transmitted data symbol from UE i
  - nₗ ~ 𝒩_ℂ(0, σ²Iₙ) — additive white Gaussian noise at AP l

  Physical meaning: AP l hears everything — signals from all K UEs mixed
  together, plus noise. The challenge is to extract each individual UE's signal
  from this mixture.

  3.2 Network-Wide UL Decoding

  AP l selects a receive combining vector vₖₗ ∈ ℂᴺ for UE k and computes the
  local inner product vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ. The network then estimates sₖ by summing across
   all APs:

  ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ                                      — Eq. (6a)

  Expanding by substituting (5):

  ŝₖ = (∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ hₖₗ) sₖ + ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ (∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ hᵢₗ) sᵢ + ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ nₗ

  Using collective vectors vₖ = [vₖ₁ᵀ ... vₖₗᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ, hᵢ = [hᵢ₁ᵀ ... hᵢₗᵀ]ᵀ ∈
  ℂᴹ, n = [n₁ᵀ ... nₗᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ:

  ŝₖ = vₖᴴ hₖ sₖ  +  ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ vₖᴴ hᵢ sᵢ  +  vₖᴴ n         — Eq. (6b)

        ┌─────────────┐   ┌──────────────────────────┐   ┌────────────┐
  ŝₖ =  │ Desired      │ + │ Inter-user interference   │ + │ Noise      │
        │ vₖᴴhₖ · sₖ  │   │ Σᵢ≠ₖ vₖᴴhᵢ · sᵢ         │   │ vₖᴴn       │
        └─────────────┘   └──────────────────────────┘   └────────────┘

  Critical observation (stated in the paper): Equation (6b) is mathematically
  equivalent to a single-cell Massive MIMO system with "combined channels"
  {vₖᴴhᵢ : i = 1,...,K}. All the SE analysis from the Massive MIMO literature
  [2, Sec. 4.1] directly applies. The difference is only in how the combining
  vectors {vₖₗ} are designed.

  3.3 Combining Vector Choices in Original CF-mMIMO

  Maximum Ratio (MR) combining — the most popular choice [5], [26], [27]:

  vₖₗᴹᴿ = ĥₖₗ                                                — Eq. (implied)

  - Uses the channel estimate directly as the combining vector
  - Intuition: Maximizes the power of the desired signal vₖᴴhₖ (by matched
  filtering)
  - Downside: Completely ignores interference — does not try to suppress ∑ᵢ≠ₖ
  vₖᴴhᵢ sᵢ
  - Complexity: Very low (just use the estimate)

  Local MMSE (L-MMSE) combining — considered in [7]:

  vₖₗᴸ⁻ᴹᴹˢᴱ = pₖ (∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ pᵢ(ĥᵢₗĥᵢₗᴴ + Cᵢₗ) + σ²Iₙ)⁻¹ ĥₖₗ

  - Each AP independently suppresses interference from all K UEs
  - Better SE than MR
  - Problem: Requires channel estimates for all K UEs at AP l → unscalable

  Network-wide MMSE combining — also in [7]:

  vₖᴹᴹˢᴱ = pₖ (∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ pᵢ ĥᵢĥᵢᴴ + Z)⁻¹ ĥₖ

  - Centralized processing using all channels from all APs
  - Optimal linear combining (maximizes SINR)
  - Problem: Requires M×M matrix inversion where M = NL → extremely high
  complexity

  3.4 Downlink Signal Model

  Let wᵢₗ ∈ ℂᴺ denote the precoding vector that AP l assigns to UE i. The
  received signal at UE k is:

  yₖᵈˡ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ wᵢₗ ςᵢ + nₖ = hₖᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ wᵢ ςᵢ + nₖ    — Eq. (7)

  Variable definitions:
  - yₖᵈˡ ∈ ℂ — received scalar signal at UE k (single antenna)
  - ςᵢ ∈ ℂ — unit-power data signal for UE i: 𝔼{|ςᵢ|²} = 1
  - wᵢ = [wᵢ₁ᵀ ... wᵢₗᵀ]ᵀ ∈ ℂᴹ — collective precoding vector for UE i
  - nₖ ~ 𝒩_ℂ(0, σ²) — receiver noise

  MR precoding (standard choice [5], [6]):

  wᵢₗ = √ρᵢ · ĥᵢₗ / √(𝔼{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²})                       — Eq. (8)

  where ρᵢ ≥ 0 is the transmit power allocated to UE i. The normalization
  √(𝔼{‖ĥᵢₗ‖²}) ensures that the expected transmit power per AP is controlled.

  Again, mathematically equivalent to single-cell Massive MIMO DL [2, Sec. 4.3]
  with correlated fading. The same SE expressions apply.

  3.5 The Equivalence to Single-Cell Massive MIMO — Why It Matters

  This equivalence is a powerful theoretical insight. It means:

  1. All known SE bounds from Massive MIMO carry over directly
  2. All known combining/precoding optimality results apply
  3. The only design freedom unique to cell-free is the choice of which APs
  participate (DCC) and how the combining/precoding vectors are structured
  across APs

  But it also reveals the fundamental tension: single-cell Massive MIMO assumes
  one base station processes everything centrally. In cell-free, "centralized"
  processing means signals must travel over fronthaul links to a CPU —
  introducing bottlenecks that don't exist in co-located arrays.

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  The thought experiment: What happens when we let K → ∞ (the number of users
  grows without bound)?

  Imagine a city that keeps growing — more and more residents move in. A
  scalable postal system means each post office handles a bounded number of
  parcels regardless of the city's total population (because each office only
  serves its neighborhood). An unscalable system requires every post office to
  touch every parcel — as the population grows, every post office eventually
  collapses under the load.

  The formal definition says: Scalability means that no single AP sees its
  workload grow unboundedly as the network adds more users. The total network
  workload grows (more APs are needed), but each AP's burden stays finite.

  3.7 Definition 1 (Scalability) — The Paper's Central Criterion

  Definition 1 (Scalability). A Cell-Free Massive MIMO network is scalable if 
  all four above-listed tasks have finite complexity and resource requirements 
  for each AP as K → ∞.

  Mathematical formulation: For each AP l, define:
  - C₁(l, K) = complexity of channel estimation at AP l
  - C₂(l, K) = complexity of signal processing at AP l
  - C₃(l, K) = fronthaul data rate required by AP l
  - C₄(l, K) = complexity of power control involving AP l

  The network is scalable if and only if:

  ∀ task j ∈ {1,2,3,4}, ∀ AP l:  lim_{K→∞} Cⱼ(l, K) < ∞

  [ASSUMPTION — implicit]: As K → ∞, we also have L → ∞ (more APs are deployed
  to serve more users), maintaining a finite ratio of antennas per UE (NL/K > 1
  for the system to be useful).

  3.8 Systematic Failure Analysis of Original CF-mMIMO

  The paper proves that the original "all serve all" CF-mMIMO fails every single
   criterion:

  ---
  Task 1 FAILURE: Channel Estimation

  In original CF-mMIMO, AP l must compute:

  {ĥₖₗ : k = 1, ..., K}

  That is, all K channel estimates. Per estimate: N² complex multiplications.
  Total:

  C₁(l, K) = K · N² → ∞ as K → ∞    ✗ NOT SCALABLE

  Physical meaning: Every AP must know how to communicate with every user in the
   entire network — even users kilometers away with negligible channels.

  ---
  Task 2 FAILURE: Signal Processing

  UL: AP l must compute K inner products {vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ : k = 1,...,K}:

  C₂ᵘˡ(l, K) = K · N → ∞ as K → ∞    ✗ NOT SCALABLE

  DL: AP l must form the transmitted signal:

  xₗᵈˡ = ∑ₖ₌₁ᴷ wₖₗ ςₖ

  This is a sum of K terms, each requiring N complex multiplications:

  C₂ᵈˡ(l, K) = K · N → ∞ as K → ∞    ✗ NOT SCALABLE

  Even with the simplest MR precoding (wₖₗ = √ρₖ ĥₖₗ / √(𝔼{‖ĥₖₗ‖²})), the AP
  still needs K channel estimates and must normalize each one — complexity K·N.

  ---
  Task 3 FAILURE: Fronthaul Signaling

  UL: AP l must forward K processed signals {vₖₗᴴ yₗᵘˡ : k = 1,...,K} to the CPU
   — that's K complex scalars per channel use, or K · τᵤ per coherence block:

  C₃ᵘˡ(l, K) = K · τᵤ → ∞ as K → ∞    ✗ NOT SCALABLE

  DL: AP l must receive K data signals {ςₖ : k = 1,...,K} from the CPU — that's
  K · τ_d per coherence block:

  C₃ᵈˡ(l, K) = K · τ_d → ∞ as K → ∞    ✗ NOT SCALABLE

  Physical meaning: The cable connecting each AP to the network must carry data
  proportional to the total number of users in the entire network. No real
  fronthaul link can support this indefinitely.

  ---
  Task 4 FAILURE: Power Control

  Network-wide power optimization in [5], [6], [26], [30], [31] involves solving
   optimization problems with K or more variables:

  Example: max-min fairness → K SINR constraints, K power variables
  Complexity: polynomial in K (e.g., O(K³) for convex solvers)

  C₄(l, K) = O(K^α) for some α ≥ 1 → ∞ as K → ∞    ✗ NOT SCALABLE

  Even if an individual AP doesn't solve the optimization itself, the result
  must be communicated back to each AP — which again scales with K.

  Original CF-mMIMO: ALL 4 TASKS FAIL SCALABILITY

  Task 2: Signal Processing C₂ = O(K·N)      ──→ ∞  ✗
  Task 3: Fronthaul         C₃ = O(K·τᵤ)     ──→ ∞  ✗
  Task 4: Power Control     C₄ = O(Kᵅ), α≥1  ──→ ∞  ✗

  3.10 Remark 1: What Scalability Does and Does Not Mean

  The paper includes an important clarification in Remark 1:

  The total computational complexity and fronthaul requirements across the 
  entire network will diverge as K → ∞ (since L → ∞ too). But as long as each AP
   has a local processor and a fronthaul connection of sufficient but finite 
  capacity, each AP can carry out its tasks regardless of how large the network 
  is.

  Analogy: The total amount of mail delivered by a national postal service grows
   as the population grows. But each individual post office only handles its
  local neighborhood — its workload stays bounded. You just add more post
  offices as the city expands.

  This means:
  - Per-AP complexity: Finite ✓ (the scalability requirement)
  - Total network complexity: Proportional to L (grows, but linearly with
  infrastructure)
  - Practical implication: You can deploy the network incrementally — adding APs
   to cover new areas without upgrading existing APs

  │ DL fronthaul      │ 100 × 190 = 19,000 scalars = 152 KB │ Manageable │
  └───────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────────┴────────────┘

  Coherence block duration at 20 MHz: τ_c / (20×10⁶) = 200 / (20×10⁶) = 10 μs

  Fronthaul data rate for UL: 152 KB / 10 μs = 15.2 GB/s ... already exceeding 
  100 Mbps by 150×!

  Wait — let's be more careful. The coherence block spans both time and
  frequency. With τ_c = 200 in a system with 20 MHz bandwidth:
  - If one coherence block spans ~1 ms in time and ~200 kHz in frequency
  - Number of coherence blocks per second per frequency: 1000
  - Number of frequency blocks in 20 MHz: 100
  - Total coherence blocks per second: 100,000

  Fronthaul per second (UL only):
  100 UEs × 190 channel uses × 8 bytes × 100,000 blocks = 15.2 TB/s

  This is absurdly beyond any practical fronthaul. Even with compression, the
  scaling with K is fatal.

  Case 2: K = 1,000 UEs (modest urban deployment)

  Everything scales 10×:
  - Channel estimation: 1,000 multiplications per AP per block
  - Fronthaul: 10× the already-impossible rate

  Case 3: K = 10,000 UEs (dense urban / stadium)

  100× the baseline — completely unimaginable per AP.

  Now compare with DCC (preview of the solution):

  If each AP serves at most |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ = 10 UEs:

  ┌────────────────────────┬──────────────────┬───────────┬────────────┐
  │          Task          │     K = 100      │ K = 1,000 │ K = 10,000 │
  ├────────────────────────┼──────────────────┼───────────┼────────────┤
  │ Ch. estimation         │ 10               │ 10        │ 10         │
  ├────────────────────────┼──────────────────┼───────────┼────────────┤
  │ Combining vectors      │ 10               │ 10        │ 10         │
  ├────────────────────────┼──────────────────┼───────────┼────────────┤
  │ Fronthaul scalars (UL) │ 10 × 190 = 1,900 │ 1,900     │ 1,900      │
  └────────────────────────┴──────────────────┴───────────┴────────────┘

  Constant regardless of K! This is the power of scalability — the per-AP cost
  is fixed at its value for τₚ users, no matter how large the network grows.

  Practical fronthaul calculation with DCC:

  10 UEs × 190 × 8 bytes × 100,000 blocks/s = 1.52 GB/s

  Still substantial but within the range of modern fiber or high-speed Ethernet
  fronthaul. And critically, it does not grow as you add more users to the
  network.

  ---
  Key Takeaways

  - The original CF-mMIMO signal model is mathematically equivalent to 
  single-cell Massive MIMO — all known SE results transfer directly, but the
  distributed nature creates fronthaul bottlenecks not present in co-located
  arrays
  - Definition 1 formalizes scalability as finite per-AP complexity as K → ∞ — a
   clean, binary criterion that any algorithm can be tested against. This is the
   paper's first major contribution: turning an informal concern into a rigorous
   design requirement
  - The original CF-mMIMO fails all four scalability criteria: channel
  estimation, signal processing, fronthaul, and power control all grow with K
  - The numerical analysis reveals that even for moderate K = 100, the fronthaul
   requirements of "all serve all" are orders of magnitude beyond practical
  links — the problem is not just theoretical but immediately practical
  - The physical reason scalability is achievable (previewed): path loss causes
  99%+ of a UE's signal power to come from the nearest few APs. Ignoring distant
   APs costs negligible performance but eliminates the K-dependence

  Open Research Questions

  1. Is the K → ∞ limit the right scalability criterion? In practice, networks
  have finite K. Would a complexity-per-SE metric (efficiency rather than
  absolute boundedness) be more operationally useful?
  2. Can fronthaul compression techniques (e.g., Wyner-Ziv coding,
  quantize-and-forward as in [45]) relax the fronthaul scalability requirement
  enough to make "all serve all" viable in some regimes?
  3. How does the scalability definition interact with heterogeneous AP 
  capabilities? If some APs have more powerful processors or higher-capacity
  fronthaul, should they serve more UEs? The current definition treats all APs
  symmetrically.
  4. What is the minimum ratio L/K (AP density) required for the DCC-based
  scalable approach to work without significant performance loss? Is there a
  phase transition?
  5. Does the scalability definition need modification for multi-carrier (OFDM)?
   With F subcarriers, does the complexity scale as |𝒟ₗ| × F — and if F is large
   (e.g., 1000+ in 5G NR), does this create a new bottleneck?

● Section IV: Dynamic Cooperation Clustering (DCC)

  This section is the conceptual bridge between the problem (unscalable 
  CF-mMIMO) and the solution (scalable CF-mMIMO). It introduces the DCC 
  framework that unifies multiple network architectures under one mathematical 
  formalism. It will be covered in 2 parts:

  - Part A: The DCC framework — signal models and unifying perspective
  - Part B: Partial solution to scalability via DCC — Lemma 1 and its

  ---
  Part A: The DCC Framework — Signal Models and Unifying Perspective

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  What is Dynamic Cooperation Clustering?

  Imagine a large restaurant with many waiters (APs) and many tables of diners
  (UEs). In the "original CF-mMIMO" model, every waiter must serve every table —
   clearly impractical as the restaurant grows. DCC is the sensible alternative:
   each table is assigned a specific team of nearby waiters, and each waiter
  only serves the tables in their zone. The teams can overlap — waiter #5 might
  serve tables 3, 7, and 12, while waiter #6 serves tables 7, 12, and 15. Table
  7 gets attention from both waiters #5 and #6, which is the cooperative
  benefit.

  The word "dynamic" means these assignments can change over time — as diners
  leave or arrive, or as waiters' workloads shift, the teams are reconfigured.

  What problem does this solve?

  DCC provides a single mathematical framework that can describe:
  - The original CF-mMIMO (every AP serves every UE)
  - User-centric CF-mMIMO (each UE served by a subset of APs)
  - Cellular networks (each UE served by exactly one AP)
  - Any intermediate configuration

  By expressing everything through a set of binary selection matrices {Dᵢₗ}, the
   framework unifies analysis and makes it possible to systematically design
  scalable algorithms.

  Why it matters: Without DCC, every new network architecture requires its own
  signal model, its own SE derivations, and its own scalability analysis. DCC
  provides a "plug-and-play" formalism — change the matrices {Dᵢₗ} and the
  entire system behavior changes, while the SE expressions remain valid.

  Layer 3: Rigorous Technical Deep Dive

  4.1 The DCC Matrices — Core Mathematical Object

  The DCC framework introduces a set of diagonal matrices:

  Dᵢₗ ∈ ℝᴺˣᴺ, for i = 1,...,K and l = 1,...,L

  Definition: The j-th diagonal element of Dᵢₗ is:

  [Dᵢₗ]ⱼⱼ = { 1   if the j-th antenna of AP l is allowed to
              {     transmit to / decode signals from UE i
              { 0   otherwise

  This is the most general formulation possible for per-antenna cooperation 
  decisions. Each individual antenna at each AP can independently decide whether
   to participate in serving each UE.

  The collective DCC matrix for UE k across all APs:

  Dₖ = diag(Dₖ₁, Dₖ₂, ..., Dₖₗ) ∈ ℝᴹˣᴹ

  This is a block-diagonal binary matrix of size M × M that acts as a selection 
  mask over the entire antenna array.

  4.2 Modified UL Signal Model with DCC

  The physical received signal at AP l is unchanged — all APs still physically
  receive all signals:

  yₗᵘˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ hᵢₗ sᵢ + nₗ                               — same as Eq. (5)

  However, only a subset of APs participate in signal detection for each UE. The
   estimate of sₖ becomes:

  ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ                                — Eq. (9a)

  Expanding and using collective notation:

  ŝₖ = vₖᴴ Dₖ hₖ sₖ  +  ∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ vₖᴴ Dₖ hᵢ sᵢ  +  vₖᴴ Dₖ n     — Eq. (9b)

  Comparison with the original model (Eq. 6b):

  Original:  ŝₖ = vₖᴴ hₖ sₖ  +  Σᵢ≠ₖ vₖᴴ hᵢ sᵢ  +  vₖᴴ n

  DCC:       ŝₖ = vₖᴴ Dₖ hₖ sₖ  +  Σᵢ≠ₖ vₖᴴ Dₖ hᵢ sᵢ  +  vₖᴴ Dₖ n
                       ↑                     ↑                    ↑
                    Selection            Selection            Selection
                    matrix               matrix               matrix

  The only difference is the insertion of Dₖ — a selection matrix that zeros out
   contributions from non-participating APs/antennas. This is mathematically
  elegant: the same signal model handles all configurations.

  Key property: When Dₖₗ = 0ₙ (AP l does not serve UE k):
  - Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ = 0 (AP l's signal is ignored for UE k)
  - vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ = 0ᵀ (combining contributes nothing)

  So only APs with Dₖₗ ≠ 0ₙ contribute to the detection of UE k.

  4.3 Modified DL Signal Model with DCC

  The DL received signal at UE k becomes:

  yₖᵈˡ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ hₖₗᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ Dᵢₗ wᵢₗ ςᵢ + nₖ = hₖᴴ ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ Dᵢ wᵢ ςᵢ + nₖ    — Eq. 
  (10)

  Physical meaning: AP l only transmits precoded signals for UEs that it is
  assigned to serve. If Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ, then Dᵢₗ wᵢₗ = 0 — AP l transmits nothing for
  UE i. No power is wasted, no interference is generated.

  4.4 Unification: How DCC Subsumes Multiple Architectures

  The power of DCC is that different choices of {D₁, ..., Dₖ} produce
  fundamentally different network architectures:

  Case 1: Original Cell-Free Massive MIMO

  Dᵢ = Iₘ   ∀i

  All antennas at all APs serve all UEs. Equations (9) and (10) reduce to (6)
  and (7).

      AP₁  AP₂  AP₃  AP₄
  UE₁  ■    ■    ■    ■       ■ = serves
  UE₂  ■    ■    ■    ■
  UE₃  ■    ■    ■    ■

  Case 2: User-Centric CF-mMIMO (Buzzi & D'Andrea [27])

  Let ℳᵢ ⊂ {1,...,L} denote the subset of APs serving UE i. Then:

  Dᵢₗ = Iₙ  if l ∈ ℳᵢ;   Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ  if l ∉ ℳᵢ          — Eq. (11)

  This is the setup previously considered in [17] — all N antennas at an AP
  either serve a UE or they don't.

      AP₁  AP₂  AP₃  AP₄
  UE₁  ■    ■    ·    ·       ■ = serves, · = does not
  UE₂  ·    ■    ■    ·       (overlapping clusters!)
  UE₃  ·    ·    ■    ■

  Case 3: Fog Massive MIMO (Bursalioglu et al. [29])

  Identical to Case 2 but with notation 𝒜ᵢ instead of ℳᵢ. DL-only analysis in
  the original paper.

  Case 4: Conventional Cellular Network

  Each UE served by exactly one AP:

  ℳᵢ = {l*(i)}    where l*(i) = arg maxₗ βᵢₗ

      AP₁  AP₂  AP₃  AP₄
  UE₁  ■    ·    ·    ·       Each UE has exactly one AP
  UE₂  ·    ■    ·    ·       = traditional cellular
  UE₃  ·    ·    ·    ■

  Case 5: Disjoint Cooperation Clusters (4G CoMP)

  APs are partitioned into non-overlapping groups:

      AP₁  AP₂  AP₃  AP₄
  UE₁  ■    ■    ·    ·       Cluster 1: {AP₁, AP₂}
  UE₂  ■    ■    ·    ·       Cluster 2: {AP₃, AP₄}
  UE₃  ·    ·    ■    ■

  UEs at cluster boundaries (e.g., between AP₂ and AP₃) suffer severe
  inter-cluster interference.

  Case 6: Per-Antenna Selection (most general DCC)

  Individual antennas can be assigned differently:

  Dᵢₗ = diag(1, 0) — only antenna 1 of AP l serves UE i

  This level of granularity is theoretically possible but rarely used in
  practice.

  The hierarchy:

  Most general ──────────────────────────────────── Most specific

  Per-antenna    User-centric    Disjoint       Cellular    Original
  DCC            DCC (Eq. 11)    clusters       (1 AP/UE)   CF-mMIMO
  (arbitrary     (Iₙ or 0ₙ      (non-overlap)              (Dᵢ = Iₘ)
  diagonal)      per AP)
     ⊃              ⊃               ⊃              ⊃           =

  All are special cases of the DCC framework. This is the paper's first major 
  conceptual contribution: proving that CF-mMIMO ⊂ DCC.

  Using MR combining (v₁ₗ = ĥ₁ₗ):

  ŝ₁ = v₁ᴴ D₁ h₁ s₁ + ∑ᵢ₌₂⁴ v₁ᴴ D₁ hᵢ sᵢ + v₁ᴴ D₁ n

  Since D₁ only selects APs 1 and 2:

  ŝ₁ = (ĥ₁₁ᴴh₁₁ + ĥ₁₂ᴴh₁₂) s₁ + ∑ᵢ₌₂⁴ (ĥ₁₁ᴴhᵢ₁ + ĥ₁₂ᴴhᵢ₂) sᵢ + (ĥ₁₁ᴴn₁ + ĥ₁₂ᴴn₂)

  The interference from UE 3 and UE 4 enters through APs 1 and 2 — even though
  these UEs are served by different APs, their signals are still physically 
  received at APs 1 and 2.

  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  The question: We've introduced DCC matrices that can limit which APs serve
  which UEs. But does this actually solve the scalability problem?

  The answer (Lemma 1): Yes, for 3 out of 4 tasks, if the number of UEs per AP
  stays bounded.

  Going back to the restaurant analogy: if every waiter is contractually limited
   to serving at most τₚ tables, then no matter how many tables the restaurant
  adds (by expanding the building), no individual waiter's workload ever exceeds
   τₚ tables. The only remaining question is: how do you assign waiters to
  tables (and set prices, i.e., power control)?

  Layer 3: Rigorous Technical Deep Dive

  4.6 The AP Serving Set

  Definition (Eq. 12): The set of UEs served by at least one antenna at AP l:

  𝒟ₗ = {i : tr(Dᵢₗ) ≥ 1,  i ∈ {1,...,K}}

  Physical meaning: 𝒟ₗ is the "workload" of AP l — the list of UEs it must
  handle. If tr(Dᵢₗ) ≥ 1, at least one antenna at AP l is assigned to UE i.

  For the special case of Eq. (11) where Dᵢₗ ∈ {Iₙ, 0ₙ}:

  𝒟ₗ = {i : l ∈ ℳᵢ} = {i : AP l is in UE i's cluster}

  4.7 Lemma 1 — The Scalability Bridge

  Lemma 1. If the cardinality |𝒟ₗ| is constant as K → ∞ for l = 1,...,L, then 
  the Cell-Free Massive MIMO network with DCC satisfies the first three 
  conditions in Definition 1.

  Proof (from the paper, expanded):

  Task 1 (Channel estimation): AP l only needs to compute channel estimates for
  UEs in 𝒟ₗ:
  {ĥₖₗ : k ∈ 𝒟ₗ}

  Number of estimates: |𝒟ₗ|
  Complexity per estimate: N² (matrix-vector multiplication with precomputed
  statistical matrix)
  Total: |𝒟ₗ| · N²

  If |𝒟ₗ| is constant as K → ∞, then |𝒟ₗ| · N² is constant. ✓

  Task 2 (Signal processing): AP l only needs to compute combining/precoding for
   UEs in 𝒟ₗ:
  - UL: compute {vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ : k ∈ 𝒟ₗ} — |𝒟ₗ| inner products of dimension N
  - DL: form ∑ₖ∈𝒟ₗ wₖₗ ςₖ — sum of |𝒟ₗ| precoded signals

  Complexity: O(|𝒟ₗ| · N) — constant if |𝒟ₗ| is constant. ✓

  Task 3 (Fronthaul signaling): AP l only exchanges data for UEs in 𝒟ₗ:
  - UL: sends |𝒟ₗ| processed signals per channel use → |𝒟ₗ| · τᵤ per coherence
  block
  - DL: receives |𝒟ₗ| data symbols per channel use → |𝒟ₗ| · τ_d per coherence
  block

  Both are constant if |𝒟ₗ| is constant. ✓

  Task 4 (Power control) — NOT covered by Lemma 1:

  Network-wide power optimization still involves all K UEs as variables.
  However, the paper notes this is "relatively easy to satisfy" — simple
  heuristics like:
  - UL: full power transmission pᵢ = P for all i
  - DL: equal power allocation among served UEs

  These have O(|𝒟ₗ|) complexity per AP — constant and scalable.

  The complete scalability picture:

                          Original CF-mMIMO    DCC with |Dₗ| bounded
                          ─────────────────    ─────────────────────
  Task 1 (estimation)     O(K · N²)    ✗      O(|Dₗ| · N²)     ✓
  Task 2 (processing)     O(K · N)     ✗      O(|Dₗ| · N)      ✓
  Task 3 (fronthaul)      O(K · τᵤ)   ✗      O(|Dₗ| · τᵤ)     ✓
  Task 4 (power ctrl)     O(Kᵅ)       ✗      O(|Dₗ|) heuristic ✓
                                               ─────────────────
                                               ALL SCALABLE!

  4.8 The Remaining Challenge: How to Choose 𝒟ₗ?

  Lemma 1 tells us what property to achieve (bounded |𝒟ₗ|) but not how to
  achieve it. The challenges are:

  1. Guaranteed service: Every UE must be in at least one AP's serving set.
  Otherwise a UE could be dropped entirely.
  2. Quality of service: The UEs should be assigned to APs with good channels,
  not random APs.
  3. Pilot management: UEs sharing the same pilot at the same AP cause pilot
  contamination — ideally, each AP serves at most one UE per pilot.
  4. Distributed decision-making: The assignment must be done without
  centralized coordination (which itself would be unscalable).

  Previous attempts and their deficiencies (identified in the paper):

  ┌──────────────────────┬─────────────────┬───────────────────────────────┐
  │        Paper         │    Approach     │            Problem            │
  ├──────────────────────┼─────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
  │ Buzzi & D'Andrea     │ Each UE picks   │ No guarantee that all UEs are │
  │ [27]                 │ best APs        │  served — an AP might not     │
  │                      │                 │ reciprocate                   │
  ├──────────────────────┼─────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
  │ Bursalioglu et al.   │ Same approach   │ Same problem — no guaranteed  │
  │ [29]                 │ (Fog MIMO)      │ service                       │
  ├──────────────────────┼─────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
  │ Ngo et al. [28],     │ Each UE picks   │                               │
  │ Interdonato et al.   │ APs, no limit   │ 𝒟ₗ                            │
  │ [33]                 │ on              │                               │
  └──────────────────────┴─────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┘

  The paper resolves all these issues in Section V with a novel 3-step algorithm
   (covered in the next section).

  4.9 The Practical Question: What Should |𝒟ₗ| Be?

  Under Assumption 1 (introduced in Section V but previewed here):

  Each AP serves at most one UE per pilot sequence → |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ

  With τₚ being a constant (e.g., 10), this automatically satisfies Lemma 1's
  condition.

  The rationale (three reasons from the paper):

  1. Pilot contamination avoidance: If an AP serves two UEs on the same pilot,
  it cannot distinguish their channels — causing severe mutual interference.
  2. Bounded complexity: |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ ensures all four tasks have complexity at
  most O(τₚ · N²) per AP — independent of K.
  3. Bounded fronthaul: Each AP needs to support at most τₚ parallel UL and DL
  data streams.

  Key Takeaways

  - DCC is a unifying framework that describes the original CF-mMIMO,
  user-centric CF-mMIMO, Fog Massive MIMO, disjoint clustering, and conventional
   cellular — all as special cases through the choice of binary diagonal
  matrices {Dᵢₗ}
  - The modified signal models (Eqs. 9, 10) differ from the originals (Eqs. 6,
  7) only by the insertion of Dₖ — a mathematically clean "selection mask" that
  zeros out non-participating APs
  - Lemma 1 proves that bounded |𝒟ₗ| (number of UEs per AP) is sufficient for
  scalability of Tasks 1-3. The proof is elementary but the result is
  foundational — it converts the scalability problem into a cluster design 
  problem
  - The remaining challenge is algorithmic: how to assign UE-AP clusters that
  are bounded in size, guarantee service to all UEs, and manage pilots — all in
  a distributed fashion. This is addressed in Section V
  - The physical justification is path loss: signal power concentrates at nearby
   APs (99%+ from the closest 2-3 APs in typical deployments), so ignoring
  distant APs costs negligible SE while eliminating the K-dependence in
  complexity

  Open Research Questions

  1. Is the per-antenna granularity of DCC (arbitrary diagonal Dᵢₗ**) ever worth
   the additional design complexity** compared to the simpler per-AP binary
  decision (Dᵢₗ ∈ {Iₙ, 0ₙ})? Could hybrid beamforming architectures benefit from
   per-antenna DCC?
  2. Can DCC be extended to dynamic/time-varying clusters where ℳₖ changes
  within a coherence block? This could be relevant for extremely mobile users
  (vehicular, UAV) where the best AP set changes rapidly.
  3. What is the information-theoretic capacity loss from DCC compared to full
  cooperation (all serve all)? Lemma 1 guarantees scalability but does not
  quantify the performance gap — can we derive closed-form capacity loss bounds
  as a function of |𝒟ₗ|?
  4. How does the DCC framework extend to multi-layer heterogeneous networks
  where APs have different capabilities (macro + small cells + RIS)?
  5. Can machine learning be used to optimize the DCC matrices {Dᵢₗ} in
  real-time, going beyond simple distance-based heuristics to account for
  traffic patterns, interference topology, and QoS requirements?

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  What problem does this solve?

  When a new phone (UE) turns on in a cell-free network, three things must
  happen simultaneously:

  1. Initial access: The phone must connect to the network (find an AP to "talk
  to")
  2. Pilot assignment: The phone must be given a unique-ish training sequence so
   APs can learn its channel
  3. Cluster formation: A set of APs must agree to serve this phone

  In cellular networks, this is simple — you connect to the strongest cell
  tower, period. In cell-free networks, it's profoundly harder because there are
   no cells, multiple APs must cooperate, and no single entity has global
  knowledge.

  The analogy: Imagine you're a new student (UE) arriving at a large university
  with many tutors (APs). You need to:
  1. Pick a primary advisor (Master AP) — the tutor with the best connection to
  you
  2. Get assigned to a study group (pilot) — your advisor picks the one with the
   least crowding
  3. Recruit additional tutors (cluster) — your advisor invites nearby tutors to
   also help you, and they agree if they have capacity

  The critical guarantee: every student gets at least one tutor (the Master AP).
   No student is accidentally left without service.

  Why previous approaches failed:
  - Buzzi & D'Andrea [27], Bursalioglu et al. [29]: UE picks APs, but APs might
  all reject → UE gets no service
  - Ngo et al. [28], Interdonato et al. [33]: No limit on APs' serving load →
  unscalable

  Layer 3: Rigorous Technical Deep Dive

  5.1 Assumption 1 — The Scalability Anchor

  Assumption 1. Each AP serves at most one UE per pilot sequence and uses all 
  its N antennas to serve these UEs.

  Mathematical consequences:

  (a) |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ for all l = 1,...,L

  (b) The DCC matrices take the simplified form:

  Dᵢₗ = Iₙ  if i ∈ 𝒟ₗ;   Dᵢₗ = 0ₙ  if i ∉ 𝒟ₗ           — Eq. (13)

  (c) Since τₚ is independent of K, Lemma 1 is automatically satisfied → Tasks 
  1-3 are scalable.

  Three-fold rationale for Assumption 1:

  (R1) Pilot contamination avoidance: If AP l serves two UEs k and j on the same
   pilot t:
  - ĥₖₗ and ĥⱼₗ are correlated (from Sec. II)
  - When AP l beamforms to UE k, it inadvertently beamforms to UE j
  - This creates strong coherent interference that scales with N
  - Avoiding same-pilot co-service at an AP eliminates this worst-case
  contamination

  (R2) Fixed complexity: Channel estimation needs at most τₚ estimates per AP.
  Signal processing needs at most τₚ combining/precoding vectors. Both are
  constant.

  (R3) Bounded fronthaul: Each AP needs fronthaul capacity for at most τₚ
  parallel data streams in both UL and DL.

  5.2 The Three-Step Algorithm

  Let K+1 be the index of a new UE accessing the network. The algorithm proceeds
   as:

  ---
  STEP 1: UE Appoints Master AP

  The accessing UE measures the large-scale fading coefficient to all nearby
  APs:

  βₗ = tr(R₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ) / N

  using periodically broadcasted synchronization signals (analogous to PSS/SSS
  in 5G NR). Then the UE selects:

  ℓ = arg max_l  βₗ                                              — Eq. (14)

  as its Master AP — the AP with the strongest average channel.

  Implementation details:
  - The UE can measure βₗ passively from broadcast signals (no uplink
  transmission needed yet)
  - The UE synchronizes to the Master AP's timing
  - The UE contacts the Master AP via a standard random access procedure
  [42]–[44]

  Why the strongest AP? The AP with the highest βₗ will have:
  - Best channel estimation quality (highest pilot SNR)
  - Largest contribution to the desired signal power
  - Shortest propagation delay (important for synchronization)

  Scalability: This step involves only the new UE and its nearby APs —
  independent of K. ✓

  ---
  STEP 2: Master AP Assigns Pilot

  The Master AP (AP ℓ) selects the pilot with the least contamination observed
  at AP ℓ:

  τ = arg min_t  tr(Ψₜₗ)                                       — Eq. (15)

  where Ψₜₗ is the pilot signal correlation matrix from Eq. (4):

  Ψₜₗ = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚpᵢRᵢₗ + σ²Iₙ

  Physical meaning of tr(Ψₜₗ**):**

  tr(Ψₜₗ) = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚpᵢ tr(Rᵢₗ) + Nσ² = ∑ᵢ∈𝒮ₜ τₚpᵢNβᵢₗ + Nσ²

  This is proportional to the total pilot power the Master AP receives on pilot
  t from existing UEs. Minimizing this means choosing the pilot where the Master
   AP "hears" the least interference from existing users — giving the new UE the
   best estimation quality at its most important AP.

  Important constraint (from footnote 5): The selected pilot should ideally be
  one on which the Master AP is not already serving a UE as Master AP, since
  that role has higher priority. Each AP can be the Master AP of at most τₚ UEs.

  After pilot assignment: The Master AP informs a limited set of neighboring APs
   that it will serve UE K+1 on pilot τ.

  Scalability: The computation in Eq. (15) involves only τₚ correlation matrices
   already stored at AP ℓ — independent of K. ✓

  ---
  STEP 3: Neighboring APs Decide to Join Cluster

  Each neighboring AP (informed by the Master AP) independently decides whether
  to serve UE K+1. The decision rule:

  IF (AP does NOT serve any UE on pilot τ):
      → ACCEPT: Add UE K+1 to its serving set on pilot τ

  ELSE IF (new UE has better channel than currently served UE on pilot τ):
      → SWITCH: Replace the weaker UE with UE K+1 on pilot τ

  ELSE:
      → REJECT: Continue serving the existing UE on pilot τ

  "Better channel" means: β₍ₖ₊₁₎ₗ > βⱼₗ where UE j is the currently served UE on
   pilot τ at this AP.

  Key properties of this step:

  (P1) Guaranteed service: Even if all neighboring APs reject, the Master AP is 
  required to serve UE K+1. This resolves the dropped-service problem of [27],
  [29].

  (P2) At most one UE per pilot per AP: The decision rule enforces Assumption 1
  — an AP either has a free slot on pilot τ or switches to the stronger UE.

  (P3) Distributed: Each AP makes its own decision based only on local
  information (its own channel gains). No centralized coordination required.

  (P4) Scalable: Each AP only communicates with a fixed number of neighbors —
  independent of K.

  Scalability: ✓

  5.3 Algorithm Properties — A Formal Assessment

  Theorem (implicit): The proposed 3-step algorithm is scalable according to
  Definition 1.

  Proof sketch:
  - Step 1: O(1) computation per UE (measure nearby APs)
  - Step 2: O(τₚ) computation at Master AP (evaluate τₚ pilots)
  - Step 3: O(1) decision per neighboring AP (compare two channel gains)
  - Result: |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ for all l → Lemma 1 satisfied → Tasks 1-3 scalable
  - Combined with heuristic power control → Task 4 scalable
  - Every UE has at least one serving AP (Master AP) → guaranteed service ✓

  5.4 Dynamic Reconfiguration

  The paper addresses what happens when UEs move or network conditions change:

  UE-initiated reconfiguration:
  - UE measures new βₗ values
  - If a new AP has a stronger channel → UE appoints new Master AP
  - Procedure restarts as if the UE is newly accessing
  - New Master AP informs the previous Master AP of the handover

  Master AP-initiated pilot reassignment:
  - Master AP periodically re-evaluates Eq. (15)
  - If a different pilot τ' would yield less contamination than the current
  pilot
  - The pilot is changed → cluster may be reconfigured

  Remark 2 (Synchronization): The signal model (Eq. 10) assumes perfect
  synchronization, which is infeasible due to propagation delays. However, the
  paper argues:
  - Each AP synchronizes with its neighbors
  - The UE synchronizes with its Master AP in Step 1
  - The dominant terms in the signal model are from nearby (synchronized) APs
  - Contributions from distant (potentially unsynchronized) APs are weak anyway
  - Therefore, the model is "reasonable for analysis"

  Part B: Uplink Data Transmission — Centralized and Distributed Combining

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  Two operating modes for UL decoding:

  Centralized: Each AP sends its raw received signals to the CPU. The CPU has
  all the data and can jointly process everything — like a classroom exam where
  every student (AP) sends their answer sheets to one teacher (CPU) who grades
  holistically.

  Distributed: Each AP locally processes its signals and sends only a compressed
   summary (one number per UE) to the CPU. Like students grading their own work
  and only reporting final scores — the teacher gets less information but the
  system is more efficient.

  The design question: What combining vectors should be used in each mode, and
  are they scalable?

  ---
  Layer 2: Historical Context & Literature Linkage

  Combining Schemes — Evolution in CF-mMIMO
  ─────────────────────────────────────────
  MR (Matched Filter)          [5] Ngo et al. 2017
    Simple, distributed         → Low complexity, poor interference mgmt
    vₖₗ = ĥₖₗ                  → Coherent interference scales with N

  L-MMSE (Local MMSE)          [7] Björnson & Sanguinetti 2020
    Distributed, interference   → Better SE, but needs all K estimates
    suppression at each AP       → UNSCALABLE (K channel estimates per AP)

  MMSE (Centralized)           [7], [30]
    Optimal, network-wide       → Best SE, but M×M matrix inversion
                                 → UNSCALABLE (complexity grows with K)

  P-MMSE (THIS PAPER)
    Centralized, partial info   → Near-MMSE SE with only |Pₖ| ≤ τₚ|Mₖ|
                                   interferers considered → SCALABLE

  LP-MMSE (THIS PAPER)
    Distributed, partial info   → Near-L-MMSE SE with only |Dₗ| ≤ τₚ
                                   UEs considered per AP → SCALABLE

  Layer 3: Rigorous Technical Deep Dive

  5.5 Centralized Combining — Proposition 1

  In centralized mode, AP l ∈ ℳₖ forwards its raw signals to the CPU:
  - Pilot signals: {yₜₗᵖⁱˡᵒᵗ : t = 1,...,τₚ} → τₚN complex scalars
  - UL data signals: yₗᵘˡ → τᵤN complex scalars per coherence block

  The CPU performs channel estimation and data detection centrally.

  Proposition 1. When the MMSE channel estimates are known, an achievable SE of
  UE k is:

  SEₖ⁽ᵘˡ'¹⁾ = (τᵤ/τ_c) · 𝔼{log₂(1 + SINRₖ⁽ᵘˡ'¹⁾)}                — Eq. (17)

  where the instantaneous effective SINR is:

  SINRₖ⁽ᵘˡ'¹⁾ = pₖ|vₖᴴDₖĥₖ|² / (∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ pᵢ|vₖᴴDₖĥᵢ|² + vₖᴴZₖvₖ)    — Eq. (18)

  with:

  Zₖ = Dₖ (∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ pᵢCᵢ + σ²ᵤₗIₗₙ) Dₖ

  Anatomy of Eq. (18):

           pₖ |vₖᴴ Dₖ ĥₖ|²              ← Desired signal power
      ─────────────────────────────────────   (uses channel ESTIMATE ĥₖ)
      Σᵢ≠ₖ pᵢ|vₖᴴ Dₖ ĥᵢ|²              ← Interference from estimated channels
      + vₖᴴ Zₖ vₖ                        ← Estimation error + noise

  The numerator uses ĥₖ (the channel estimate, not the true channel). The term
  Zₖ captures the uncertainty due to estimation errors (Cᵢ = error covariance)
  and thermal noise.

  The key insight: only the entries of Dₖĥᵢ where Dₖ is nonzero matter. The CPU
  only has partial channel knowledge — it knows Dᵢĥᵢ (Eq. 16 from Section V-B),
  with unknown estimates represented by zeros.

  5.6 MMSE Combining — Optimal but Unscalable

  Since Eq. (18) is a generalized Rayleigh quotient in vₖ, the SINR-maximizing
  combiner is:

  vₖᴹᴹˢᴱ = pₖ (∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ pᵢDₖĥᵢĥᵢᴴDₖ + Zₖ)† Dₖĥₖ        — Eq. (20)

  yielding maximum SINR:

  SINRₖ⁽ᵘˡ'¹⁾ = pₖĥₖᴴDₖ (∑ᵢ₌₁,ᵢ≠ₖᴷ pᵢDₖĥᵢĥᵢᴴDₖ + Zₖ)† Dₖĥₖ    — Eq. (21)

  Why it's called MMSE: This combiner minimizes:

  MSEₖ = 𝔼{|sₖ - ŝₖ|² | {ĥᵢ}}

  — the conditional mean squared error between transmitted and estimated data,
  given all channel estimates.

  Why it's unscalable: The matrix being inverted has dimension N|ℳₖ| × N|ℳₖ|,
  but it includes channel estimates ĥᵢ for all K UEs — requiring all K estimates
   at every AP in ℳₖ. Complexity grows with K.

  5.7 P-MMSE Combining — The Scalable Centralized Solution

  Key insight (from [30]): The interference at UE k is mainly generated by UEs
  served by partially the same APs. Distant UEs served by entirely different APs
   contribute negligible interference through the combining vector.

  Define the "partial overlap" set:

  𝒫ₖ = {i : DₖDᵢ ≠ 0ₗₙ}                                   — Eq. (22)

  Physical meaning: 𝒫ₖ contains all UEs that share at least one serving AP with
  UE k. These are the UEs whose interference is "visible" through UE k's
  combining operation.

  P-MMSE combining:

  vₖᴾ⁻ᴹᴹˢᴱ = pₖ (∑ᵢ∈𝒫ₖ pᵢDₖĥᵢĥᵢᴴDₖ + Z'ₖ)† Dₖĥₖ        — Eq. (23)

  with:

  Z'ₖ = Dₖ (∑ᵢ∈𝒫ₖ pᵢCᵢ + σ²ᵤₗIₗₙ) Dₖ                  — Eq. (24)

  Scalability analysis of |𝒫ₖ|:

  Under Assumption 1 (at most one UE per pilot per AP):

  - Each AP in ℳₖ serves at most τₚ UEs (one per pilot)
  - UE k's set includes itself plus UEs co-served at each of its APs
  - Minimum: |𝒫ₖ| = τₚ (when all APs in ℳₖ serve the exact same set of UEs)
  - Maximum: |𝒫ₖ| ≤ (τₚ - 1)|ℳₖ| + 1 (when APs serve entirely disjoint sets)
  - Both bounds are independent of K → P-MMSE is scalable ✓

  When does P-MMSE = MMSE? When UE k is served by all APs (ℳₖ = {1,...,L}), then
   Dₖ = Iₘ and 𝒫ₖ = {1,...,K} — the full set. P-MMSE reduces to MMSE.

  Complexity comparison (from Table II):

  ┌────────┬──────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────┐
  │ Scheme │    Channel Estimation    │     Combining Computation     │
  ├────────┼──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
  │ MMSE   │ (Nτₚ + N²) · K · |ℳₖ|    │ (N|ℳₖ|)³/3 + O(K·(N|ℳₖ|)²)    │
  ├────────┼──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
  │ P-MMSE │ (Nτₚ + N²) · |𝒫ₖ| · |ℳₖ| │ (N|ℳₖ|)³/3 + O(|𝒫ₖ|·(N|ℳₖ|)²) │
  ├────────┼──────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
  │ MR     │ (Nτₚ + N²) · |ℳₖ|        │ 0 (just use estimate)         │
  └────────┴──────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┘

  The critical difference: MMSE has factors of K (grows unboundedly) while
  P-MMSE has factors of |𝒫ₖ| (bounded).

  5.8 Distributed Combining — Proposition 2

  In distributed mode, AP l locally computes:

  ŝₖₗ = vₖₗᴴ Dₖₗ yₗᵘˡ                                     — Eq. (25)

  and sends only this scalar to the CPU. The CPU forms the final estimate:

  ŝₖ = ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ ŝₖₗ                                              — Eq. (26)

  Fronthaul per coherence block (per AP): τᵤ|𝒟ₗ| complex scalars ≤ τᵤτₚ →
  scalable ✓

  Proposition 2. An achievable UL SE for UE k is:

  SEₖ⁽ᵘˡ'²⁾ = (τᵤ/τ_c) · log₂(1 + SINRₖ⁽ᵘˡ'²⁾)                   — Eq. (27)

  where:

  SINRₖ⁽ᵘˡ'²⁾ = pₖ|𝔼{vₖᴴDₖhₖ}|² / (∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ pᵢ𝔼{|vₖᴴDₖhᵢ|²} - pₖ|𝔼{vₖᴴDₖhₖ}|² + 
  σ²ᵤₗ𝔼{‖Dₖvₖ‖²})    — Eq. (28)

  Critical difference from Proposition 1: The SINR uses expectations
  (statistical averages), not instantaneous values. This is the
  use-and-then-forget (UatF) bound [2, Th. 4.4].

  Why UatF? In distributed mode, the CPU doesn't know the channel estimates — it
   only receives the scalar summaries ŝₖₗ. It cannot perform instantaneous SINR
  computation. The UatF bound treats the channel uncertainty as additional
  noise, yielding a deterministic (non-random) SE expression.

  The log₂ is outside the expectation (compare with Eq. 17 where it's inside
  𝔼{...}):
  - Proposition 1: 𝔼{log₂(1 + SINR)} → averages over random SINR realizations
  - Proposition 2: log₂(1 + deterministic SINR) → a single number

  5.9 LP-MMSE Combining — The Scalable Distributed Solution

  Inspired by P-MMSE, the paper proposes Local Partial MMSE (LP-MMSE):

  vₖₗᴸᴾ⁻ᴹᴹˢᴱ = pₖ (∑ᵢ∈𝒟ₗ pᵢ(ĥᵢₗĥᵢₗᴴ + Cᵢₗ) + σ²Iₙ)⁻¹ ĥₖₗ    — Eq. (29)

  Key differences from L-MMSE:
  - L-MMSE sums over all K UEs → unscalable
  - LP-MMSE sums over only 𝒟ₗ (UEs served by AP l) → |𝒟ₗ| ≤ τₚ → scalable ✓

  Key difference from P-MMSE:
  - P-MMSE operates on the collective N|ℳₖ|-dimensional vector → requires N|ℳₖ|
  × N|ℳₖ| matrix inversion
  - LP-MMSE operates locally on N-dimensional vectors → requires only N × N
  matrix inversion → much cheaper

  Complexity from Table II:

  ┌─────────┬───────────────────────┬───────────────────┐
  │ Scheme  │ Matrix Inversion Size │ Per-UE Complexity │
  ├─────────┼───────────────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ MMSE    │ N|ℳₖ| × N|ℳₖ|         │ O((N|ℳₖ|)³)       │
  ├─────────┼───────────────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ P-MMSE  │ N|ℳₖ| × N|ℳₖ|         │ O((N|ℳₖ|)³)       │
  ├─────────┼───────────────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ LP-MMSE │ N × N                 │ O(N³ · |ℳₖ|)      │
  ├─────────┼───────────────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ MR      │ None                  │ O(N · |ℳₖ|)       │
  └─────────┴───────────────────────┴───────────────────┘

  For N = 4, |ℳₖ| = 10: P-MMSE inverts a 40×40 matrix; LP-MMSE inverts a 4×4
  matrix 10 times. LP-MMSE is ~100× cheaper.

  5.10 Closed-Form SE with MR (Corollary 2)

  When MR combining (vₖₗ = ĥₖₗ) is used, the expectations in Eq. (28) have
  closed-form expressions:

  𝔼{vₖᴴDₖhₖ} = pₖτₚ ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ tr(DₖₗRₖₗΨₜₖₗ⁻¹Rₖₗ)             — Eq. (30)

  𝔼{‖Dₖvₖ‖²} = pₖτₚ ∑ₗ₌₁ᴸ tr(DₖₗRₖₗΨₜₖₗ⁻¹Rₖₗ)             — Eq. (31)

  𝔼{|vₖᴴDₖhᵢ|²} = pₖτₚ ∑ₗ tr(DₖₗRᵢₗRₖₗΨₜₖₗ⁻¹Rₖₗ) + { pₖpᵢτₚ²|∑ₗ 
  tr(DₖₗRᵢₗΨₜₖₗ⁻¹Rₖₗ)|² if tᵢ=tₖ; 0 otherwise }    — Eq. (32)

  The pilot contamination term (second part of Eq. 32) is nonzero only when tᵢ =
   tₖ (UE i shares the pilot with UE k). This confirms that pilot contamination
  creates coherent interference that has a distinct mathematical structure from
  non-coherent interference.

  Setup: Continue with our 6-AP, 4-UE network. Focus on UE 4 with ℳ₄ = {AP 3, AP

  Step 1: Identify 𝒫₄ (partial overlap set)

  UE 4's APs are {3, 5, 6}. Which other UEs share these APs?

  ┌──────┬────────────────────┬─────────────────────┐
  │  AP  │     UEs served     │ Overlaps with UE 4? │
  ├──────┼────────────────────┼─────────────────────┤
  │ AP 3 │ {UE 4}             │ — (only UE 4)       │
  ├──────┼────────────────────┼─────────────────────┤
  │ AP 5 │ {UE 2, UE 3, UE 4} │ UE 2 and UE 3       │
  ├──────┼────────────────────┼─────────────────────┤
  │ AP 6 │ {UE 3, UE 4}       │ UE 3                │
  └──────┴────────────────────┴─────────────────────┘

  𝒫₄ = {UE 2, UE 3, UE 4}  →  |𝒫₄| = 3

  P-MMSE for UE 4 only needs channel estimates for 3 UEs, not all K. With K =
  10,000, it would still be 3 (or a small bounded number).

  Step 2: P-MMSE matrix construction (conceptual)

  The matrix to invert is N|ℳ₄| × N|ℳ₄| = 2×3 × 2×3 = 6 × 6:

  A = ∑ᵢ∈{2,3,4} pᵢ D₄ĥᵢĥᵢᴴD₄ + Z'₄

  where D₄ selects APs 3, 5, 6 (so the 6-dimensional vector only has components
  from these 3 APs).

  Step 3: Compare with MR

  With MR combining v₄ = D₄ĥ₄:
  - Simple: just use the channel estimate
  - But ignores interference from UE 2 (through AP 5) and UE 3 (through APs 5
  and 6)

  With P-MMSE:
  - The 6×6 matrix inversion accounts for interference from UEs 2 and 3
  - The resulting combining vector tilts away from the interferers' directions
  - Expected SE improvement: 2-3× over MR (consistent with paper's Fig. 5
  results)

  P-MMSE achieves 89% of unscalable MMSE. LP-MMSE matches unscalable L-MMSE with
   negligible loss. Both scalable schemes vastly outperform MR.

  Part C: Downlink Data Transmission — UL-DL Duality and Precoding

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  The DL problem: In UL, we can optimize each UE's combining vector
  independently (each UE's SINR only depends on its own combiner). In DL, every
  UE's SE depends on all precoders simultaneously — changing the precoder for UE
   1 affects the interference at UEs 2, 3, .... Joint optimization is required,
  which is inherently unscalable.

  The elegant solution — UL-DL duality: "Don't design DL precoders from scratch.
   Instead, take your good UL combiners and use them as DL precoders (with a
  normalization)." Proposition 4 proves that this achieves the same SINRs in DL
  as in UL (with appropriate power allocation).

  Analogy: Imagine you've perfected how to listen to each voice in a crowded
  room (UL combining). UL-DL duality says: if you reverse the direction —
  instead of listening, you speak to each person — you can use the same spatial
  "filters" to direct your voice, and achieve the same quality of communication.

  5.11 DL SE — Proposition 3

  Proposition 3. An achievable DL SE for UE k is:

  SEₖ⁽ᵈˡ⁾ = (τ_d/τ_c) · log₂(1 + SINRₖ⁽ᵈˡ⁾)                     — Eq. (34)

  SINRₖ⁽ᵈˡ⁾ = ρₖ|𝔼{hₖᴴDₖw̄ₖ}|² / (∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ ρᵢ𝔼{|hₖᴴDᵢw̄ᵢ|²} - ρₖ|𝔼{hₖᴴDₖw̄ₖ}|² + 
  σ²_dl)    — Eq. (35)

  where wᵢ = √ρᵢ w̄ᵢ with 𝔼{‖w̄ᵢ‖²} = 1 (unit-norm normalized precoder).

  Structure of Eq. (35):

  Numerator:   ρₖ |𝔼{hₖᴴ Dₖ w̄ₖ}|²        ← Average desired signal power
                                             (uses HARDENING bound — treats
                                              𝔼{hₖᴴDₖw̄ₖ} as deterministic gain)

  Denominator: Σᵢ ρᵢ 𝔼{|hₖᴴ Dᵢ w̄ᵢ|²}     ← Total average received power
              - ρₖ |𝔼{hₖᴴ Dₖ w̄ₖ}|²         ← Subtract deterministic desired
              + σ²_dl                         ← Noise

  Key challenge: The DL SE depends on all precoding vectors {w̄ᵢ : i=1,...,K}.
  Joint optimization is unscalable. We need a heuristic.

  5.12 UL-DL Duality — Proposition 4

  Proposition 4. Let {vᵢ : i=1,...,K} and {pᵢ} be the UL combining vectors and 
  transmit powers. If the normalized precoding vectors are selected as:

  w̄ᵢ = vᵢ / √(𝔼{vᵢᴴDᵢvᵢ})                              — Eq. (36)

  then there exists a DL power control policy {ρᵢ} with ∑ᵢρᵢ/σ²_dl = ∑ᵢpᵢ/σ²_ul 
  for which:

  SINRₖ⁽ᵈˡ⁾ = SINRₖ⁽ᵘˡ'²⁾   ∀k                                 — Eq. (37)

  What this means in practice:
  1. Design good UL combiners (e.g., P-MMSE or LP-MMSE) — these can be optimized
   per-UE
  2. Set DL precoders = normalized UL combiners (Eq. 36)
  3. The resulting DL SINRs match the UL SINRs (with properly chosen DL powers)
  4. If the UL scheme is scalable, the DL scheme is automatically scalable ✓

  The power allocation (from the Appendix):

  ρ = (σ²_dl/σ²_ul) · (Γ - Σ)⁻¹ · (Γ - Σᵀ) · p         — Eq. (49)

  where Γ and Σ are K×K matrices defined in Eqs. (45)-(46). This power
  allocation is unscalable (involves all K UEs), but the paper uses heuristic
  power control instead (Section V-D), which sacrifices exact duality but
  maintains scalability.

  Remark 3 (Hardening bound tightness): The DL SE in Proposition 3 uses the
  hardening bound, which can be loose when:
  - N is small (few antennas per AP)
  - MR precoding is used (large effective gain variations)

  The paper shows in Section VI that LP-MMSE achieves 90% of the genie-aided
  (perfect CSI) DL SE, while MR achieves only 60%. So the tightness concern is
  mainly an MR issue, not a fundamental limitation.

  5.13 Centralized vs. Distributed Precoding

  Centralized precoding:
  - CPU computes w̄ᵢ using UL estimates
  - Uses Eq. (36) with vᵢ = vᵢᴾ⁻ᴹᴹˢᴱ (or other centralized combiner)
  - CPU forms the AP's transmit signal xₗᵈˡ = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ √ρᵢ Dᵢₗ w̄ᵢₗ ςᵢ and sends it
  to AP l
  - Fronthaul: τₚN (pilot signals, UL) + τ_dN (DL signals) per coherence block

  Distributed precoding:
  - AP l computes w̄ᵢₗ locally using {ĥᵢₗ : i ∈ 𝒟ₗ}
  - Only data symbols {ςᵢ : i ∈ 𝒟ₗ} sent from CPU to AP l
  - Fronthaul: τ_d|𝒟ₗ| per coherence block → much less than centralized

  Two distributed precoding options:

  ┌─────────┬──────────────┬─────────────────────┬──────────────────────────┐
  │ Scheme  │   Formula    │    Interference     │        Hardening         │
  │         │              │     Suppression     │                          │
  ├─────────┼──────────────┼─────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ MR      │ wₖₗ ∝ ĥₖₗ    │ None                │ Poor (large gain         │
  │         │              │                     │ variance)                │
  ├─────────┼──────────────┼─────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤

  5.14 The LP-MMSE Hardening Effect — Figure 4

  The paper includes a remarkable illustration (Fig. 4) for the simplest case
  N=L=K=1:

  With perfect CSI, h ~ 𝒩_ℂ(0,1), ρ = σ²_dl = 1:

  MR precoding: w = h → effective gain = |h|² ~ Exponential distribution
  - Infinite tail — the gain can be arbitrarily large
  - Mean ≈ 1, but variance = 1 (high fluctuation)
  - The hardening bound is loose because the "deterministic" approximation
  𝔼{hᴴw} is a poor summary of a highly variable random quantity

  LP-MMSE precoding: w = h/(|h|² + 1/√(𝔼{|h|²/(|h|²+1)²}))
  - Bounded support — the gain lives in a compact interval
  - Much less variation around the mean
  - The hardening bound is tight because the effective gain is nearly
  deterministic

  This is a non-trivial finding: Even with a single antenna and single user,
  LP-MMSE creates a "hardening effect" that MR does not. In multi-AP scenarios,
  this translates to significantly higher SE because the capacity bound is
  tighter.

  Part D: Power Allocation

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  Power control answers: "How loud should each user talk (UL)?" and "How much
  energy should each AP dedicate to each user (DL)?" Optimal power control
  requires network-wide coordination — unscalable. The paper uses simple but
  effective heuristics.

  ---
  Layer 3: Technical Details

  UL Power Allocation:

  pᵢ = P  for i = 1,...,K                                       — Eq. (42)

  Every UE transmits at maximum power P. Reference [7] showed this provides good
   SE for both strong and weak UEs. No coordination needed → trivially scalable.

  DL Centralized Power Allocation:

  ρᵢ = ρ/τₚ                                                     — Eq. (implied)

  Equal power per UE. Since each AP serves at most τₚ UEs and the unit-norm
  precoder w̄ᵢ distributes power across APs, each AP's total transmit power is at
   most ρ.

  DL Distributed Power Allocation (from [33]):

  ρₖₗ = ρ · √βₖₗ / (∑ᵢ∈𝒟ₗ √βᵢₗ)   if k ∈ 𝒟ₗ;   0 otherwise    — Eq. (43)

  Physical meaning: AP l distributes its power budget ρ among served UEs
  proportionally to √βₖₗ. UEs with stronger channels get more power — this is a
  channel-aware heuristic that:
  - Allocates more power to nearby UEs (better channels → higher SE per Watt)
  - Guarantees non-zero power to all served UEs
  - Requires only local channel statistics → scalable ✓

  Guaranteed non-zero SE: Every UE has a Master AP that serves it. With Eq.
  (43), the Master AP allocates non-zero power → non-zero SE for every UE.

  Part E: Network Topology

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  The algorithms are described using a "star topology" (APs connected to one
  CPU), but the paper emphasizes that the CPU is a logical concept, not
  necessarily a physical box. The "CPU tasks" can be distributed across the
  network in multiple ways.

  ---
  Layer 3: Technical Details

  Three topology options discussed:

  Option 1: No physical CPU (fully distributed)
  - Each AP has a local processor (like a small base station)
  - The Master AP of UE k handles the "CPU tasks" for that UE (encoding DL data,
   decoding UL data)
  - Backhaul connects APs to the core network
  - This is closest to existing cellular infrastructure

  AP₁ ←──backhaul──→ Core Network
  AP₂ ←──backhaul──→ Core Network
  AP₃ ←──backhaul──→ Core Network
  (Master AP of UE k performs CPU tasks for UE k)

  Option 2: Edge-cloud processors
  - APs divided into disjoint groups
  - Each group connected to a physical edge-cloud processor via fronthaul
  - CPU tasks distributed across edge-cloud processors
  - This is the Cloud-RAN architecture

  AP₁ ─┐                AP₄ ─┐
  AP₂ ─┤── CPU₁         AP₅ ─┤── CPU₂
  AP₃ ─┘                AP₆ ─┘

  Option 3: Hybrid / Cloud-RAN variants
  - Various combinations possible [55], [56]
  - Computations can be distributed flexibly

  Key insight: The scalable algorithms only require local communication (between
   neighboring APs). They are "mostly transparent to the actual network
  topology" — making them compatible with any deployment scenario.

  Layer 4: Comprehensive Numerical Example (Bringing It All Together)

  Full walkthrough: UE 4 in our running example

  Given: UE 4 with ℳ₄ = {AP 3, AP 5, AP 6}, pilot t=1, N=2.

  UL Processing (Distributed LP-MMSE):

  At AP 3 (serves only UE 4 on its sole pilot):

  v₄,₃ᴸᴾ⁻ᴹᴹˢᴱ = p₄(p₄(ĥ₄,₃ĥ₄,₃ᴴ + C₄,₃) + σ²I₂)⁻¹ ĥ₄,₃

  Since 𝒟₃ = {UE 4} only, this is a single-user MMSE at AP 3 — pure matched
  filter with noise regularization.

  At AP 5 (serves UE 2, UE 3, UE 4):

  v₄,₅ᴸᴾ⁻ᴹᴹˢᴱ = p₄(∑ᵢ∈{2,3,4} pᵢ(ĥᵢ,₅ĥᵢ,₅ᴴ + Cᵢ,₅) + σ²I₂)⁻¹ ĥ₄,₅

  This 2×2 matrix inversion suppresses interference from UEs 2 and 3 at AP 5.

  At AP 6 (serves UE 3, UE 4):

  v₄,₆ᴸᴾ⁻ᴹᴹˢᴱ = p₄(∑ᵢ∈{3,4} pᵢ(ĥᵢ,₆ĥᵢ,₆ᴴ + Cᵢ,₆) + σ²I₂)⁻¹ ĥ₄,₆

  Suppresses interference from UE 3 at AP 6.

  Total UL complexity for UE 4:
  - 3 APs × one 2×2 matrix inversion = 3 × (2³/3 + ...) ≈ 3 × 8 = 24 complex
  multiplications
  - Independent of K ✓

  DL Precoding (via duality):

  Using Eq. (36): w̄₄ₗ = v₄ₗ / √(𝔼{v₄ₗᴴv₄ₗ}) for l ∈ {3, 5, 6}

  DL Power at AP 5 (using Eq. 43, 𝒟₅ = {UE 2, UE 3, UE 4}):

  ρ₄,₅ = ρ · √β₄,₅ / (√β₂,₅ + √β₃,₅ + √β₄,₅)

  With β₂,₅ = -99.8 dB, β₃,₅ = -87.3 dB, β₄,₅ = -98.4 dB:

  √β₂,₅ = √(1.05×10⁻¹⁰) = 1.02×10⁻⁵
  √β₃,₅ = √(1.86×10⁻⁹) = 4.31×10⁻⁵
  √β₄,₅ = √(1.45×10⁻¹⁰) = 1.20×10⁻⁵

  ρ₄,₅ = ρ × 1.20×10⁻⁵ / (1.02×10⁻⁵ + 4.31×10⁻⁵ + 1.20×10⁻⁵)
       = ρ × 1.20 / 6.53
       = 0.184ρ

  AP 5 allocates 18.4% of its power to UE 4, while UE 3 (much closer) gets
  66.0%. This makes physical sense — UE 3's strong channel converts power to SE
  more efficiently.

  Fronthaul summary for AP 5:

  ┌─────────────┬──────────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────┐

  ---
  Key Takeaways

  - Assumption 1 (at most one UE per pilot per AP) is the single most important
  design choice — it simultaneously ensures scalability, avoids worst-case pilot
   contamination, and bounds fronthaul requirements
  - The 3-step initial access algorithm is the first to jointly handle access,
  pilot assignment, and clustering while providing both guaranteed service
  (Master AP) and scalability — resolving open issues from [27], [28], [29],
  [33]
  - P-MMSE (centralized) and LP-MMSE (distributed) are the key algorithmic
  contributions: they approximate MMSE and L-MMSE by only considering UEs with
  overlapping AP clusters, reducing complexity from O(K) to O(τₚ|ℳₖ|) and O(τₚ)
  respectively
  - UL-DL duality (Proposition 4) eliminates the need for separate DL precoder
  design — the scalability of UL combining automatically transfers to DL
  precoding via the simple formula w̄ᵢ = vᵢ/‖vᵢ‖
  - LP-MMSE creates a channel hardening effect (Fig. 4) that makes the DL
  capacity bounds significantly tighter than MR, even with few antennas — this
  is a subtle but important advantage that goes beyond raw interference
  suppression

  Open Research Questions

  1. Can the 3-step algorithm be improved with machine learning? For example,
  using a graph neural network to predict optimal cluster assignments based on
  network topology and traffic patterns, rather than greedy distance-based
  decisions.
  2. What is the optimal cluster size |ℳₖ|? The paper uses a simple "accept if
  free slot" rule — is there an optimal stopping criterion that maximizes
  network-wide utility?
  3. How does P-MMSE perform with imperfect knowledge of 𝒫ₖ? In practice,
  detecting which UEs share APs requires inter-AP communication — what if this
  information is delayed or incomplete?
  4. Can the UL-DL duality be extended to account for per-AP power constraints
  (not just total power)? The current duality (Proposition 4) proves SINR
  matching with a total power constraint but the per-AP distributed power
  allocation (Eq. 43) is a separate heuristic.
  5. How does the LP-MMSE hardening effect scale with N? Fig. 4 shows N=1; does
  the advantage over MR grow or shrink as N increases? The Section VI results
  suggest it persists, but a theoretical characterization would be valuable.

  1. First τₚ = 10 UEs connect with distinct pilots (no contamination initially)
  2. Remaining 90 UEs connect one-by-one, each selecting a Master AP and being
  assigned a pilot

  This sequential procedure is realistic — in practice, UEs don't all appear
  simultaneously.

  Layer 4: Numerical Example (Reproducing One Data Point)

  Goal: Estimate the UL SE for a "typical" UE using LP-MMSE in the L=400, N=1
  setup.

  Simplified single-antenna analysis (N=1):

  With N=1, all vectors become scalars. The LP-MMSE combiner at AP l for UE k
  simplifies to:

  v₄ₗᴸᴾ⁻ᴹᴹˢᴱ = pₖ · ĥₖₗ / (∑ᵢ∈𝒟ₗ pᵢ(|ĥᵢₗ|² + Cᵢₗ) + σ²)

  Consider a "median" UE served by its 3 closest APs (ℳₖ = {AP_a, AP_b, AP_c})
  with:

  ┌───────────────┬──────────┬──────────────┬─────────────────────┐
  │      AP       │ Distance │ βₖₗ (linear) │ Pilot SNR (τₚpβ/σ²) │
  ├───────────────┼──────────┼──────────────┼─────────────────────┤
  │ AP_a (Master) │ 25 m     │ 5.0×10⁻⁹     │ 12,563 (41 dB)      │
  ├───────────────┼──────────┼──────────────┼─────────────────────┤
  │ AP_b          │ 60 m     │ 3.5×10⁻¹⁰    │ 879 (29 dB)         │
  ├───────────────┼──────────┼──────────────┼─────────────────────┤
  │ AP_c          │ 100 m    │ 6.3×10⁻¹¹    │ 158 (22 dB)         │
  └───────────────┴──────────┴──────────────┴─────────────────────┘

  Channel estimation quality (NMSE for each AP):

  For uncorrelated fading (N=1), with one co-pilot UE at distance ~200m from
  each AP:

  At AP_a: co-pilot UE has β_co,a ≈ 3.7×10⁻¹² (very weak at this AP)

  NMSE_a = (τₚp·β_co,a + σ²) / (τₚp·βₖ,a + τₚp·β_co,a + σ²)
         ≈ σ² / (τₚp·βₖ,a) = 1/(12,563) ≈ 0.008%

  At AP_b: NMSE_b ≈ 1/879 ≈ 0.11%
  At AP_c: NMSE_c ≈ 1/158 ≈ 0.63%

  Excellent estimation at all three APs.

  Approximate SINR computation:

  The desired signal power (with LP-MMSE, N=1, simplified):

  Signal ≈ pₖ · (∑ₗ∈ℳₖ βₖₗ · (1-NMSEₗ))²

  The dominant interference comes from co-pilot UEs (coherent interference) and
  nearby UEs on other pilots (non-coherent interference). For a typical scenario
   with K/τₚ = 10 UEs per pilot:

  SINR ≈ 10-20 dB (typical for median UE)

  SE = (190/200) · log₂(1 + SINR)

  For SINR = 15 dB (≈ 31.6 linear):
  SE = 0.95 × log₂(32.6) = 0.95 × 5.03 ≈ 4.8 bit/s/Hz

  For SINR = 20 dB (≈ 100 linear):
  SE = 0.95 × log₂(101) = 0.95 × 6.66 ≈ 6.3 bit/s/Hz

  Range: 4.8–6.3 bit/s/Hz — consistent with the median LP-MMSE SE of ~5.5 
  bit/s/Hz from Fig. 5(a).

  Throughput calculation:

  Throughput = SE × BW = 5.5 × 20 MHz = 110 Mbps per UE

  For 100 UEs with average SE = 5.5:
  Total network throughput = 5.5 × 20 × 100 = 11 Gbps

  This is a very competitive number for a network covering 4 km².

  Part B: Downlink Performance Evaluation

  ---
  Layer 1: Intuitive Overview

  What's different about the DL evaluation?

  The UL results established that scalable schemes match unscalable benchmarks.
  The DL analysis focuses on two deeper questions:

  1. Interference-limited behavior: When the system has plenty of transmit
  power, does adding more power help? Or is performance capped by interference?
  (Spoiler: it's interference-limited, and spatial interference management is
  crucial.)
  2. Bound tightness: The DL SE uses the "hardening bound" (Proposition 3). How
  close is this bound to the actual capacity? MR and LP-MMSE behave very
  differently here.

  ---
  Layer 2: Historical Context & Literature Linkage

  The DL bound tightness issue was first identified in:

  Chen & Björnson, IEEE Trans. Commun. 2018 [47]
  "Channel hardening and favorable propagation
   in cell-free massive MIMO with stochastic geometry"
  → Showed that channel hardening is WEAK in CF-mMIMO
  → Particularly with single-antenna APs (N=1)
  → The hardening bound may be very loose
          │
          ▼
  THIS PAPER:
  → Shows the tightness problem is SPECIFIC TO MR
  → LP-MMSE achieves 90% of genie-aided capacity
  → MR achieves only 60% → hardening bound is loose
  → The solution is to use LP-MMSE, not to find
     a tighter bound!

  This is a key conceptual insight: The literature worried that cell-free MIMO
  fundamentally lacks channel hardening (compared to co-located Massive MIMO).
  This paper shows the problem is not the channel physics but the choice of
  precoding scheme. LP-MMSE creates hardening through its regularization effect
  (recall Fig. 4).

  6.7 Bound Tightness — The Genie-Aided Comparison

  The paper computes the DL SE in a genie-aided scenario where UEs have perfect 
  CSI (they know exactly what hₖᴴDᵢw̄ᵢ equals in each realization). This removes
  the hardening approximation and provides the tightest possible capacity lower
  bound.

  Results (L=400, N=1):

  ┌─────────┬────────────────┬─────────────────┬───────────────────┐
  │ Scheme  │   Average SE   │ Genie-aided SE  │ Ratio (tightness) │
  ├─────────┼────────────────┼─────────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ P-MMSE  │ ≈ achieves 98% │ ~100% reference │ 98%               │
  ├─────────┼────────────────┼─────────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ LP-MMSE │ ≈ achieves 90% │ ~100% reference │ 90%               │
  ├─────────┼────────────────┼─────────────────┼───────────────────┤
  │ MR      │ ≈ achieves 60% │ ~100% reference │ 60%               │
  └─────────┴────────────────┴─────────────────┴───────────────────┘

  Physical explanation of the tightness gap:

  The hardening bound (Proposition 3) treats 𝔼{hₖᴴDₖw̄ₖ} as a deterministic
  effective channel gain. The gap between this and the true (random) effective
  gain determines the bound's tightness.

  With MR: The effective gain |hₖᴴwₖ| = |hₖᴴĥₖ| has an exponential-like
  distribution with high variance (recall Fig. 4). Treating its mean as
  deterministic ignores enormous fluctuations → loose bound (60%).

  With LP-MMSE: The regularization in LP-MMSE compresses the distribution of
  |hₖᴴwₖ| into a narrow range → the mean is a good approximation of any single
  realization → tight bound (90%).

  With P-MMSE: Centralized processing over multiple APs creates even more
  hardening (law of large numbers across APs) → very tight bound (98%).

  Implication: The concern from [47] that cell-free networks lack channel
  hardening is not fundamental — it's an artifact of using MR. Switching to
  LP-MMSE largely resolves it. This is a practically important finding because
  it means:
  - No need for DL pilot training to estimate effective channels (which would
  consume resources)
  - No need for alternative, more complex capacity bounds
  - Just use a better precoding scheme

  6.8 Interference-Limited Regime — Mathematical Characterization

  The system is interference-limited when the noise term σ²_dl in the SINR
  denominator is negligible compared to the interference terms. Let's verify:

  DL received signal power at a typical UE (median):

  Total received power = ∑ᵢ₌₁ᴷ ρᵢ 𝔼{|hₖᴴDᵢw̄ᵢ|²}

  For distributed LP-MMSE with ρ = 1W per AP, L = 400 APs:
  - Total network transmit power: 400 × 1W = 400W
  - Average received power per UE: ~400W × average path loss ≈ 400 × 10⁻¹⁰ =
  4×10⁻⁸ W = -44 dBm

  Noise power at 20 MHz:
  σ²_dl = -174 + 10log₁₀(20×10⁶) + 9 = -174 + 73 + 9 = -92 dBm

  SNR = -44 - (-92) = 48 dB

  The system has 48 dB of "total SNR" — but most of this received power is
  interference from other UEs' precoders, not the desired signal. The SINR is
  much lower (15-20 dB) because interference dominates.

  This is why centralized P-MMSE wins despite 40× less power:
  - It converts power efficiently into desired signal (good beamforming)
  - It minimizes interference leakage to other UEs
  - In an interference-limited regime, interference management > raw power

  Key Takeaways

  - LP-MMSE matches L-MMSE in UL with negligible loss — the scalability cost is
  essentially zero for distributed combining. P-MMSE achieves 89% of MMSE — a
  small price for scalability
  - Many single-antenna APs (L=400, N=1) provide better UL performance than
  fewer multi-antenna APs (L=100, N=4) due to higher macro-diversity, but
  multi-antenna APs close the gap in DL thanks to local interference suppression
  - The system is interference-limited in DL: centralized P-MMSE with 40× less
  total power outperforms distributed schemes — proving that spatial
  interference management dominates over raw transmit power in dense cell-free
  networks
  - Channel hardening is a scheme property, not a network property: MR achieves
  only 60% of genie-aided DL capacity (poor hardening), while LP-MMSE achieves
  90% and P-MMSE achieves 98%. The concern from [47] about weak hardening in
  CF-mMIMO is resolved by using proper precoding
  - The practical recommendation is clear: Use LP-MMSE for distributed operation
   (simpler infrastructure, good performance) or P-MMSE for centralized
  operation (best performance, more fronthaul). Never use MR if you can avoid it

  Open Research Questions

  1. How do the results change with larger K? The simulations use K=100. With
  K=1000 or K=10,000, does the interference landscape change qualitatively? Do
  the relative rankings of schemes shift?
  2. What about mixed-mode operation? Could some UEs use centralized processing
  (those needing high SE) while others use distributed (those with lower
  requirements)? This hybrid approach could optimize the throughput-fronthaul
  trade-off.
  3. How sensitive are the results to the path loss model? The paper uses one
  specific 3GPP-inspired model. Would mmWave channels (sparser, more LoS) or
  sub-THz channels favor different architectural choices?
  4. Can the DL power allocation be improved? The paper acknowledges that "equal
   power" (centralized) and "√β-proportional" (distributed) are heuristics. How
  much SE could be gained with scalable but more sophisticated power control?
  5. What about multi-antenna UEs? With N_UE > 1, UEs can perform local
  interference suppression — potentially reducing the need for centralized
  processing. How does this interact with the scalability framework?

  7.5 Critical Assessment — Strengths and Limitations

  Strengths

  1. Completeness: The paper addresses the entire chain — from initial access to
   SE computation — not just one piece in isolation. This makes it immediately
  usable as a system design reference.
  2. Reproducibility: All simulation code is publicly available at
  github.com/emilbjornson/scalable-cell-free. This is exceptional in the
  wireless communications literature and has contributed to its high impact.
  3. Intellectual honesty: The authors clearly state what they did NOT solve
  (power allocation) and that their schemes are heuristic (not provably
  optimal). This sets a clear research agenda.
  4. Bridging communities: Connecting CF-mMIMO to the older Network MIMO/DCC
  literature prevented the newer field from "reinventing the wheel" and brought
  mature techniques to bear on new problems.

  Limitations

  1. Single-layer network: The model assumes homogeneous APs. Real networks have
   heterogeneous infrastructure (macro cells, small cells, RIS). Extending DCC
  to heterogeneous settings adds significant complexity.
  2. Static channel model: The analysis assumes block fading with known
  statistics. High-mobility scenarios (vehicular, HSR) challenge the assumption
  that clusters and pilot assignments can be updated fast enough.
  3. Simplified power control: The heuristic power allocation may be
  significantly suboptimal. The gap is unquantified and could be large,
  especially for edge UEs.
  4. No multi-carrier treatment: Modern systems use OFDM with hundreds of
  subcarriers. The paper's narrowband model doesn't capture frequency-selective
  fading, which affects pilot design and fronthaul dimensioning.
  5. Synchronization assumption: Remark 2 acknowledges that perfect
  synchronization is infeasible but argues the model is "reasonable." For
  THz/mmWave frequencies with very short coherence times, this assumption
  becomes more questionable.

  - The DCC framework from Network MIMO is the mathematical engine that enables
  scalability. The paper's bridge between the CF-mMIMO and Network MIMO

  - P-MMSE and LP-MMSE are the practical workhorses — they achieve near-optimal

   overhead
  - The physical foundation of scalability is path loss: signal power
  concentrates at nearby APs, making it natural to limit each UE's serving
  cluster to a small set of APs
