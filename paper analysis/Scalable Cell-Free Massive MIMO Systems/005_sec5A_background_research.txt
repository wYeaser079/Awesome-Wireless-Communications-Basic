1. ASSUMPTION 1: ONE UE PER PILOT PER AP
=========================================

Assumption 1 states that each AP l serves at most one UE per pilot sequence
and uses all N antennas for those UEs, yielding |D_l| <= tau_p and
D_{il} = I_N if i in D_l, else 0_N.

(a) Why one UE per pilot avoids intra-AP pilot contamination:
    The MMSE channel estimate at AP l for UE k on pilot t is (Eq. 3):
      hat{h}_{kl} = sqrt(p_k tau_p) R_{kl} Psi_{tl}^{-1} y^{pilot}_{tl}
    where Psi_{tl} = sum_{i in S_t} tau_p p_i R_{il} + sigma^2 I_N.
    All UEs sharing pilot t contribute to Psi_{tl}. When AP l serves two UEs
    on the same pilot, both their MMSE estimates are derived from the same
    received signal y^{pilot}_{tl} and are therefore correlated. This
    correlation causes "coherent interference" that grows linearly with N,
    just like the desired signal power [22], [40], [41]. By restricting AP l
    to serve at most one UE per pilot, the AP never simultaneously precodes
    toward or combines from two UEs whose estimates are entangled. Interference
    from pilot-sharing UEs at *other* APs still exists but is attenuated by
    spatial filtering and the large-scale fading differential.

(b) Guarantee of scalability (connection to Lemma 1):
    Lemma 1 proves that if |D_l| is constant as K -> infinity for all l,
    then the first three scalability conditions in Definition 1 hold:
    channel estimation, signal processing, and fronthaul signaling all have
    finite complexity per AP. Since tau_p is a fixed system parameter
    (independent of K), the bound |D_l| <= tau_p is automatically constant
    as K -> infinity, thereby satisfying Lemma 1. Each AP computes at most
    tau_p MMSE estimates, tau_p combining/precoding vectors, and exchanges
    at most tau_p data streams over the fronthaul -- all independent of K.

(c) The coverage-versus-restriction trade-off:
    Limiting each AP to tau_p UEs means that APs in dense-UE areas may be
    unable to serve all nearby UEs. The three-step algorithm (Section V-A)
    resolves this by guaranteeing that every UE is served by at least its
    Master AP. Additional neighboring APs join the cluster only if they
    have capacity (i.e., an unused pilot slot). The trade-off is that some
    UEs in crowded regions may be served by fewer APs than in an unconstrained
    system, slightly reducing their macro-diversity gain. However, this is
    the price for provable scalability.


2. INITIAL ACCESS IN CELLULAR AND CELL-FREE NETWORKS
=====================================================

Step 1 of the algorithm requires the UE to measure the large-scale fading
coefficients beta_l = tr(R_{(K+1)l})/N from broadcast signals and then
contact its Master AP via a "standard random access procedure" [42]-[44].

(a) 5G NR initial access (3GPP TS 38.213, TS 38.211, TS 38.321):
    In 5G New Radio, the gNodeB periodically transmits Synchronization
    Signal / Physical Broadcast Channel (SS/PBCH) blocks. Each block contains:
    - Primary Synchronization Signal (PSS): a 127-length m-sequence that
      provides coarse time/frequency synchronization and identifies one of
      three cell-ID groups.
    - Secondary Synchronization Signal (SSS): a 127-length Gold sequence
      that refines synchronization and identifies the physical cell ID
      within the group, yielding 3 x 336 = 1008 total physical cell IDs.
    - PBCH: carries the Master Information Block (MIB) with system
      configuration parameters.
    Multiple SS/PBCH blocks are beamswept across angular directions within
    a 5 ms half-frame, enabling the UE to identify the best beam direction
    as well as the strongest cell/AP.

(b) Random Access Channel (RACH) procedure:
    After detecting the SS/PBCH block, the UE initiates the 4-step RACH:
    (i)   Msg1: UE transmits a preamble on the PRACH (selected from a set
          of Zadoff-Chu sequences) to the gNodeB.
    (ii)  Msg2: Random Access Response (RAR) from the gNodeB, containing
          timing advance correction and an uplink grant.
    (iii) Msg3: UE sends its identity (RRC connection request).
    (iv)  Msg4: Contention resolution from the gNodeB.
    5G NR also supports a 2-step RACH (combining Msg1+Msg3 and Msg2+Msg4)
    for reduced latency.

(c) Adaptation to cell-free networks:
    In this paper's framework, the UE measures beta_l from *all* nearby APs'
    broadcast signals (analogous to PSS/SSS) rather than selecting a single
    cell. The UE then contacts only the strongest AP (Master AP) via RACH.
    This is a minimal modification of the cellular procedure: the UE still
    performs standard cell search and random access, but the role of the
    "serving cell" is replaced by the Master AP. References [42]-[44] are:
    [42] Sesia, Toufik, Baker, "LTE - The UMTS Long Term Evolution,"
         2nd ed., Wiley, 2011.
    [43] Bjornson, Larsson, Marzetta, "Massive MIMO: Ten myths and one
         critical question," IEEE Commun. Mag., 2016.
    [44] Interdonato et al., "Ubiquitous cell-free massive MIMO
         communications," EURASIP J. Wireless Commun., 2019.


3. PILOT ASSIGNMENT IN CELL-FREE MASSIVE MIMO
===============================================

(a) The pilot assignment problem:
    With tau_p orthogonal pilots and K >> tau_p UEs, multiple UEs must share
    the same pilot. Pilot-sharing UEs contaminate each other's channel
    estimates, degrading SE. The combinatorial pilot assignment problem
    (mapping K UEs to tau_p pilots) is NP-hard in general [NSF PAR:10312911].

(b) Random pilot assignment:
    The simplest approach assigns pilots uniformly at random. This was used
    in [5] (Ngo et al., "Cell-free massive MIMO versus small cells," IEEE
    Trans. Wireless Commun., vol. 16, no. 3, 2017). It is trivially
    scalable and distributed but ignores channel geometry, causing severe
    pilot contamination when geographically close UEs share a pilot.

(c) Greedy pilot assignment:
    Ngo et al. [5] also proposed a greedy scheme: UEs are assigned pilots
    sequentially, choosing the pilot that maximizes the minimum per-UE rate.
    Bjornson et al. [2] (Massive MIMO Networks textbook, 2017) analyzed
    similar greedy schemes for co-located arrays. These centralized methods
    have complexity growing with K and are therefore unscalable.

(d) The criterion in this paper (Eq. 15):
    The Master AP assigns pilot tau = argmin_t tr(Psi_{tl}) to the new UE.
    Since Psi_{tl} = sum_{i in S_t} tau_p p_i R_{il} + sigma^2 I_N, the
    trace tr(Psi_{tl}) = sum_{i in S_t} tau_p p_i tr(R_{il}) + N sigma^2
    is the total pilot-domain received power on pilot t at AP l. Minimizing
    this selects the pilot with the least aggregate interference power as
    measured at the Master AP. This is a *local* decision -- the Master AP
    uses only its own statistics -- making it inherently distributed and
    scalable. Unlike network-wide greedy methods, it does not require
    knowledge of all K UEs' channels across all L APs.


4. THE MASTER AP CONCEPT
=========================

(a) Definition and selection (Eq. 14):
    The Master AP for a new UE (K+1) is the AP with index
      l* = argmax_l beta_l, where beta_l = tr(R_{(K+1)l})/N.
    The quantity beta_l is the average channel gain (averaged over N
    antennas), capturing path loss and shadowing. The AP with the largest
    beta_l has, on average, the strongest physical link to the UE.

(b) Role of the Master AP:
    The Master AP has three responsibilities:
    (i)   It is *guaranteed* to serve the UE -- this resolves the service-
          guarantee gap in [27] (Buzzi & D'Andrea, 2017) and [29] (Nayebi
          et al., 2017), where distributed clustering could leave some UEs
          unserved because no AP independently decided to serve them.
    (ii)  It assigns the pilot (Step 2) and coordinates the cluster by
          informing neighboring APs (Step 2-3).
    (iii) In the distributed implementation, it collects the soft estimates
          from the cooperating APs and forwards them to the CPU.

(c) The service-guarantee problem:
    In [27] and [29], each AP independently chose which UEs to serve based
    on channel quality thresholds. If a UE was weak to all nearby APs or
    if all APs had full schedules, no AP would elect to serve it. The
    Master AP concept eliminates this failure mode: every UE proactively
    claims one AP, and that AP must serve it. This is a UE-initiated
    mechanism, contrasting with the AP-initiated selection in [27], [29].


5. THE THREE-STEP CLUSTER FORMATION ALGORITHM
===============================================

(a) Step 1 -- UE appoints Master AP:
    The UE measures {beta_l} from broadcast synchronization signals and
    selects l* = argmax_l beta_l (Eq. 14). It then contacts l* via RACH.
    This step is fully UE-driven, requires no coordination between APs,
    and mirrors the standard cell-selection procedure in 5G NR.

(b) Step 2 -- Master AP assigns pilot and invites neighbors:
    The Master AP computes tau = argmin_t tr(Psi_{tl*}) (Eq. 15). It then
    informs a limited set of neighboring APs that it will serve UE (K+1)
    on pilot tau. "Limited set" is key: only nearby APs are contacted,
    keeping the signaling overhead bounded and independent of K.

(c) Step 3 -- Neighboring APs decide to join the cluster:
    Each notified AP independently decides whether to serve UE (K+1):
    - If the AP does not currently serve any UE on pilot tau, it joins.
    - If it does serve a UE on pilot tau, it joins only if UE (K+1) has a
      stronger channel (higher beta) than the currently served UE, in which
      case it switches.
    This greedy local rule maximizes per-pilot channel quality at each AP.

(d) Why this is distributed and scalable:
    Each step involves only local information: the UE measures its own
    channels (Step 1), the Master AP uses its local Psi matrix (Step 2),
    and each neighbor uses its local channel statistics (Step 3). No step
    requires global network knowledge. The per-AP computational and
    signaling costs are bounded by tau_p, satisfying Lemma 1.

(e) Comparison with prior approaches:
    - [28] (Buzzi & D'Andrea, PIMRC 2018) and [33] (Ngo et al., Asilomar
      2017): cluster UEs to APs based on large-scale fading thresholds but
      do *not* limit |D_l|. Hence |D_l| can grow unboundedly as K -> inf,
      violating scalability.
    - [27] (Buzzi & D'Andrea, 2017) and [29] (Nayebi et al., 2017):
      use AP-initiated selection that limits |D_l| but does *not* guarantee
      that every UE is served. A UE distant from all APs or in a crowded
      region may be left unserved.
    The three-step algorithm resolves both deficiencies simultaneously:
    the Master AP guarantee ensures universal service, while Assumption 1
    caps |D_l| <= tau_p to ensure scalability.
