───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. HIGH-LEVEL (LAYMAN) EXPLANATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Section II does TWO things:

(A) It precisely defines the cell-free massive MIMO set-up: who transmits
    what, when, and with what noise. There are three pieces of the clock
    to worry about:

        uplink training  → uplink data (ignored here) → downlink payload
        └── τ_p samples     (skipped)                    └── τ_c − τ_p samples
                                                           (the rest of the
                                                            coherence block)

    • During UPLINK TRAINING, every user simultaneously transmits a
      known "pilot" sequence to every AP. Each AP listens and estimates
      the channel between itself and every user using a standard
      statistical technique called Minimum Mean Square Error (MMSE)
      estimation. These estimates are NEVER shared between APs — every
      AP keeps its own.

    • During DOWNLINK PAYLOAD, every AP multiplies each user's data
      symbol by the *conjugate* of its local channel estimate — this
      is called "conjugate beamforming" or "matched-filter precoding".
      Intuition: when the real channel multiplies this conjugate on
      the way to the user, the two phases cancel, and the signal
      arrives "in phase". Summed over many APs, the phases still add
      coherently, so user k receives a strong clean version of q_k.

(B) It computes the DATA RATE (spectral efficiency, SE) that user k can
    achieve. Because the user does not know the exact effective
    channel (it only knows statistical averages — no downlink pilots!),
    one has to use a classical "use-and-forget" capacity bound from
    the massive-MIMO textbook. Think of it as:

         "Trust the average channel. Treat the deviation around
          the average as extra noise. Then apply Shannon."

    This produces a lower bound on SE that is a function of the power
    control coefficients {η_{m,k}}. The key technical result is
    Proposition 1, which gives this SE in a *clean closed form* — no
    integrals, no Monte-Carlo — so that Sections III–V can plug it into
    an optimization problem.

The big takeaways you should carry out of Section II are:

   1.  The precise definition of γ_{m,k} = mean-square of each MMSE
       channel estimate component. This quantity packages path-loss,
       shadowing, pilot length, and pilot-contamination all into one
       positive number.
   2.  The per-AP power constraint Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N.
   3.  The closed-form SE in Proposition 1 (Equation 15) — this is the
       mathematical object that the rest of the paper optimizes.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. DETAILED PHD-LEVEL EXPLANATION — PART A: SYSTEM MODEL
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─── 2.1  Geometry, numbers, and the coherence block ─────────────────────

  •  M APs, each with N antennas      → total of MN service antennas
  •  K single-antenna users
  •  Assumption: M ≫ K (the "massive" regime, otherwise channel
     hardening and favourable propagation do not kick in)
  •  TDD operation — uplink and downlink share the same frequency band,
     time-duplexed, so channel reciprocity holds inside each coherence
     block.
  •  Coherence interval length τ_c samples. By definition τ_c is the
     number of time-frequency samples over which the channel is
     approximately constant. If Doppler spread is B_d and delay spread
     is T_d, then τ_c ≈ 1 / (B_d · T_d) in samples.
  •  Out of these τ_c samples, τ_p < τ_c are reserved for uplink pilot
     training. The remaining τ_c − τ_p samples are used for downlink
     payload (the authors neglect uplink payload because the paper
     is downlink-only).

  Why do pilots live IN the coherence interval? Because the channel
  evolves with time-frequency, the pilots must be transmitted often
  enough that the estimate is still valid when the payload arrives.
  That is the whole massive-MIMO "coherence-interval budget" concept.

─── 2.2  Large-scale vs. small-scale fading ─────────────────────────────

The channel from AP m to user k is modelled as

                    𝐠_{m,k} = β_{m,k}^{1/2} · 𝐡_{m,k}          (2)

where

  •  β_{m,k} ∈ ℝ₊ is the large-scale fading = distance-dependent path
     loss × log-normal shadowing. It does NOT depend on which antenna
     at AP m we are looking at — all N antennas of AP m share the same
     β_{m,k} because their spatial separation is tiny compared to the
     distance to user k.
  •  𝐡_{m,k} ∈ ℂ^{N×1} is the small-scale fading vector. Every entry
     is i.i.d. 𝒞𝒩(0, 1) — Rayleigh, uncorrelated across antennas.

Consequence:
      𝐠_{m,k} ~ 𝒞𝒩(𝟎_N, β_{m,k} · 𝐈_N)

so each component is circularly symmetric complex Gaussian with
variance β_{m,k}, and components are independent. This is the "IID
Rayleigh" assumption, standard in cell-free literature.

  The factor β_{m,k} will be modelled numerically in Sec VI as a
  three-slope path loss (near, intermediate, far field) times a
  log-normal shadow term. For now the math only uses β_{m,k}.

─── 2.3  Uplink training: the received pilot signal at AP m ─────────────

Let 𝛗_k ∈ ℂ^{τ_p × 1} be the pilot vector transmitted by user k.
It is normalized so that ‖𝛗_k‖² = 𝛗_k^H 𝛗_k = 1. This normalization
is important — it means "the total energy of the pilot over τ_p
symbols is 1 unit". The complex conjugate is denoted 𝛗_k^H (since
𝛗_k is a column vector, 𝛗_k^H is a row vector).

All K users transmit their τ_p-length pilot SIMULTANEOUSLY. Each of
the N antennas of AP m observes a superposition. Stacking the N
antenna observations into rows gives an N × τ_p matrix:

    𝐘_{p,m} = √(τ_p ρ_p) · Σ_{k=1}^{K}  𝐠_{m,k} · 𝛗_k^H  +  𝐖_{p,m}     (1)

Dimensional check:
  •  𝐠_{m,k}  is N × 1
  •  𝛗_k^H   is 1 × τ_p
  •  their outer product 𝐠_{m,k} 𝛗_k^H is N × τ_p   ✓
  •  𝐖_{p,m} is the additive Gaussian noise matrix, also N × τ_p, with
     i.i.d. 𝒞𝒩(0,1) entries.
  •  ρ_p is the NORMALIZED uplink training SNR per pilot symbol — it
     is "normalized" because the 𝒞𝒩(0,1) noise has been absorbed into
     the definition. The factor √(τ_p ρ_p) reflects the effective
     energy per pilot symbol (processing gain τ_p from matched filter).

Interpretation. If only user k were transmitting, the AP would see
√(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k} 𝛗_k^H on every row. With K users, everything is
linearly superimposed.

─── 2.4  Pilot despreading at AP m: the de-facto matched filter ──────

To isolate user k's pilot contribution, AP m multiplies 𝐘_{p,m} on the
right by 𝛗_k (NOT 𝛗_k^H — that would be redundant because the pilots
live in a sequence space, not a frequency-signal space). Since each
AP stores its own pilots, this is a fully local operation. Result:

  𝐲_{p,m,k} = 𝐘_{p,m} 𝛗_k

             = √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k} 𝛗_k^H 𝛗_k

                 + √(τ_p ρ_p) Σ_{k'≠k} 𝐠_{m,k'} 𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k

                 + 𝐖_{p,m} 𝛗_k

             = √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k}

                 + √(τ_p ρ_p) Σ_{k'≠k} 𝐠_{m,k'} (𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k)

                 + 𝐰̃_{p,m,k}                                                 (3)

where 𝐰̃_{p,m,k} ≝ 𝐖_{p,m} 𝛗_k is an N × 1 vector. Since 𝛗_k is a
deterministic unit vector, 𝐰̃_{p,m,k} is 𝒞𝒩(𝟎, 𝐈_N).

The three terms in (3) have a crucial interpretation:

   term 1:  the clean pilot of user k, scaled by √(τ_p ρ_p). Good.
   term 2:  **pilot contamination** — contributions from OTHER users
            k'≠k whose pilots are not orthogonal to 𝛗_k. The coupling
            coefficient 𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k is nonzero because, in CF-mMIMO,
            M+K is too large for the pilot book to be fully orthogonal.
            If the pilots were fully orthogonal (τ_p ≥ K), this term
            would vanish.
   term 3:  post-despread noise, still 𝒞𝒩(𝟎, 𝐈_N).

─── 2.5  MMSE channel estimate ──────────────────────────────────────────

Given 𝐲_{p,m,k}, AP m computes the MMSE estimate of 𝐠_{m,k}. For jointly
Gaussian vectors, MMSE is just a linear least-squares projection:

  ĝ_{m,k} = 𝔼{𝐠_{m,k} · 𝐲_{p,m,k}^H} · (𝔼{𝐲_{p,m,k} 𝐲_{p,m,k}^H})^{-1} · 𝐲_{p,m,k}

Let us unpack both expectations. We need the fact that 𝐠_{m,k} and
𝐠_{m,k'} are independent for k ≠ k' (different users → different
channels).

   𝔼{𝐠_{m,k} 𝐲_{p,m,k}^H}
     = 𝔼{ 𝐠_{m,k} · [ √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k}
                      + √(τ_p ρ_p) Σ_{k'≠k} 𝐠_{m,k'} (𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'})
                      + 𝐰̃_{p,m,k} ]^H }
     = √(τ_p ρ_p) · 𝔼{𝐠_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^H}    ← only this survives
     = √(τ_p ρ_p) · β_{m,k} · 𝐈_N.

   𝔼{𝐲_{p,m,k} 𝐲_{p,m,k}^H}
     = τ_p ρ_p · Σ_{k'=1}^{K} β_{m,k'} · |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|² · 𝐈_N
       + 𝐈_N
     = ( τ_p ρ_p Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|² + 1 ) · 𝐈_N.

So both matrices are scalars times 𝐈_N, and inverting the second is
trivial. Substituting:

                        √(τ_p ρ_p) β_{m,k}
     ĝ_{m,k}  = ────────────────────────────────────────── · 𝐲_{p,m,k}
                τ_p ρ_p Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|² + 1                (4)

This is Equation (4) of the paper. Two observations:

  (i)  ĝ_{m,k} is a *scalar multiple* of the despread pilot
       𝐲_{p,m,k}. That is why all N components of ĝ_{m,k} share the
       same statistics — they are IID scalings of IID Gaussians.
  (ii) The denominator includes the pilot-contamination interference.
       The more other users share a non-orthogonal pilot with user k,
       the more the denominator inflates, and the more the estimate is
       BIASED towards that pilot-cluster subspace rather than the pure
       channel. This is exactly pilot contamination.

─── 2.6  Second-order statistics of the estimate: γ_{m,k} ────────────────

Define

     γ_{m,k}  ≝  𝔼{ |[ĝ_{m,k}]_n|² }     (6, inside Eq. (5) of paper)

which is the mean square of any single component of the N-dimensional
estimate ĝ_{m,k} (they all have the same by symmetry). A short
calculation — take the squared magnitude of the scalar multiplier in
(4), multiply by 𝔼{|[𝐲_{p,m,k}]_n|²}, simplify — gives:

                    τ_p ρ_p  β_{m,k}²
     γ_{m,k}  = ─────────────────────────────────────────                  (5)
                 τ_p ρ_p Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|² + 1

Equation (5) of the paper. This γ_{m,k} is THE central parameter of the
entire paper — it compresses large-scale fading, pilot length, pilot
power, pilot contamination, and receiver noise into a single scalar.
Every power-allocation and every SE formula below will be written as a
function of {γ_{m,k}}.

Sanity check:
   (a) If there is no pilot contamination (|𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k| = 0 for all
       k'≠k), then γ_{m,k} = τ_p ρ_p β_{m,k}² / (τ_p ρ_p β_{m,k} + 1).
       For large τ_p ρ_p this tends to β_{m,k}. i.e., the estimator
       recovers the true variance — perfect CSI limit.
   (b) With heavy pilot contamination (Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|²
       ≫ β_{m,k}), γ_{m,k} drops below β_{m,k} — the estimator is hurt.
   (c) Setting τ_p = K with orthonormal pilots 𝛗_k recovers the
       "ideal orthogonal pilot" limit.

Estimation error: by the MMSE orthogonality principle, the error
𝐠̃_{m,k} ≝ 𝐠_{m,k} − ĝ_{m,k} is independent of ĝ_{m,k}, and has entries
𝒞𝒩(0, β_{m,k} − γ_{m,k}). (Notice γ_{m,k} ≤ β_{m,k}, always.)

─── 2.7  Downlink transmit signal and conjugate beamforming ─────────────

During the payload phase, AP m transmits the N × 1 vector

     𝐱_m  =  √ρ_d  Σ_{k=1}^K  √η_{m,k} · ĝ_{m,k}^*  ·  q_k                (6)

The symbols:
   •  q_k is the unit-power data symbol for user k:  𝔼{|q_k|²} = 1.
   •  ρ_d is the maximum downlink transmit SNR, normalized by noise
      power N₀. "Normalized" again means the 𝒞𝒩(0,1) receiver noise.
   •  η_{m,k} ≥ 0 is the POWER CONTROL COEFFICIENT at AP m for user k.
      These are the variables we will optimize in Section IV.
   •  ĝ_{m,k}^* is the *element-wise complex conjugate* of the local
      channel estimate to user k. This is what makes it "conjugate
      beamforming": in the inner product 𝐠_{m,k}^T · ĝ_{m,k}^*,
      the phase of 𝐠_{m,k} will be cancelled by the conjugate of
      ĝ_{m,k} if the estimate is close to the true channel.

Why conjugate and not ĝ itself? Because the channel from AP to user
is the transpose/conjugate viewpoint: if the uplink channel is
𝐠_{m,k}, reciprocity gives downlink = 𝐠_{m,k}^T. Hence the matched
filter for user k's downlink signal uses ĝ_{m,k}^* so that
𝐠_{m,k}^T · ĝ_{m,k}^* ≈ ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² (a positive real number, up to
estimation error).

Expected transmit power at AP m:

     𝔼{‖𝐱_m‖²}  =  ρ_d Σ_k η_{m,k} · 𝔼{‖ĝ_{m,k}‖²}
                =  ρ_d Σ_k η_{m,k} · (N · γ_{m,k})
                =  ρ_d · N · Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}.                           (7)

We require this to be ≤ ρ_d (i.e. AP m has a unit normalized-power
budget). Dividing by ρ_d · N:

     Σ_{k=1}^{K}  η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}   ≤   1 / N,    m = 1, …, M               (8)

This is the per-AP power constraint. Observe that N has entered the
upper bound — more antennas per AP means LESS power per antenna is
allowed, so the TOTAL AP power stays fixed. This is the "fair" way
to compare different values of N.

─── 2.8  Received signal at user k ──────────────────────────────────────

User k receives the superposition over all M APs:

     r_k = Σ_{m=1}^M 𝐠_{m,k}^T · 𝐱_m  +  w_k
         = √ρ_d Σ_{m=1}^M √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* · q_k
             + √ρ_d Σ_{k'≠k} Σ_{m=1}^M √η_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k'}^* · q_{k'}
             + w_k                                                          (9)

where w_k ~ 𝒞𝒩(0, 1) is additive noise at the user. Note that the
downlink channel from AP m to user k is 𝐠_{m,k}^T (a row vector), so
𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐱_m is a scalar.

3. DETAILED PHD-LEVEL EXPLANATION — PART B: SPECTRAL EFFICIENCY
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─── 3.1  The "use-and-then-forget" capacity bound ───────────────────────

User k has no downlink pilots — there is no way to measure the
instantaneous effective channel Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*. All
user k knows are channel STATISTICS (the β's and γ's). So for
detection, the user treats the MEAN of the effective channel as the
true gain and lumps the deviation into a Gaussian-equivalent "effective
noise." This is the standard technique in all massive-MIMO SE papers
(Marzetta-Larsson-Yang-Ngo textbook [7], Ch. 2).

Rewrite (9) by adding and subtracting √ρ_d · 𝔼{Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T
ĝ_{m,k}^*} · q_k:

     r_k =  DS_k · q_k        (desired signal, deterministic gain)
          + BU_k · q_k        (beamforming uncertainty)
          + Σ_{k'≠k} UI_{k,k'} · q_{k'}   (inter-user interference)
          + w_k               (thermal noise)                              (10)

where, with √ρ_d folded in,

     DS_k  ≝ √ρ_d · 𝔼{ Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* }                  (12)

     BU_k  ≝ √ρ_d · ( Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*
                       - 𝔼{ Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* } )          (13)

     UI_{k,k'} ≝ √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k'}^*                (14)

Now the key lemma (see e.g. Marzetta et al. textbook [7, Sec 2.3]):
conditional on the true channel, if the "self-noise" BU_k + Σ_{k'≠k}
UI_{k,k'} q_{k'} + w_k is uncorrelated with q_k (true here by linearity
of expectation and independence of q_k, q_{k'}, ĝ's), then
treating it as independent Gaussian noise gives an ACHIEVABLE lower
bound on the true capacity:

     SE_k  ≥  (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂( 1 + SINR_k )   [bit/s/Hz]              (11)

with

                            |DS_k|²
     SINR_k  = ───────────────────────────────────────────
               𝔼{|BU_k|²} + Σ_{k'≠k} 𝔼{|UI_{k,k'}|²} + 1

The prefactor (1 − τ_p/τ_c) accounts for the τ_p pilot samples "lost"
per coherence block (only (τ_c−τ_p)/τ_c of the block carries payload).
The "+1" at the end of the denominator is the normalised thermal
noise variance w_k.

The bound is called "use-and-then-forget" because the receiver
"uses" the channel to receive the signal but "forgets" everything
about the instantaneous realization when decoding — only means are
kept. The looseness of this bound decreases as M grows, because
channel hardening makes BU_k small compared to DS_k.

─── 3.2  Proposition 1 — the closed-form SE ──────────────────────────────

After careful expectation of (12)–(14) using the Gaussianity of the
channels and the MMSE estimates (see Appendix A), the bound in (11)
becomes an *explicit, closed-form* expression. Define

     𝛈_k  ≝ [η_{1,k}, η_{2,k}, …, η_{M,k}]^T   ∈ ℝ₊^M            (vector of
                                                                  power-ctrl
                                                                  coeffs. of
                                                                  user k)

     𝛄_{k',k}  ≝ |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k| · [ γ_{1,k'} · √(β_{1,k}/β_{1,k'}),
                                      γ_{2,k'} · √(β_{2,k}/β_{2,k'}),
                                      ⋯,
                                      γ_{M,k'} · √(β_{M,k}/β_{M,k'}) ]^T
                                                                    ∈ ℝ^M

                 (Eq. just before (15) in the paper;  𝛄_{k,k} = 𝛄_k with
                  components [𝛄_{k,k}]_m = γ_{m,k})

     𝐃_{k',k}  ≝ diag(  √(β_{1,k} γ_{1,k'}),
                         √(β_{2,k} γ_{2,k'}),
                         …,
                         √(β_{M,k} γ_{M,k'}) )   ∈ ℝ^{M×M}
                                                                 (diagonal
                                                                  matrix)

Then Proposition 1 states that the achievable SE of user k is

     SE_k({η_{m,k}}) = (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂(  1  +

                       ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|²
         ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
         ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|² + ρ_d N Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖² + 1
                                                                          )     (15)

where the inner sum Σ_{k'} at the second denominator term runs from
k' = 1 to K (including k' = k — that sum is NOT minus {k}).

Interpretation of each piece:

  (numerator)   ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|²
      This is the *coherent array gain*. The N² (not N) is the
      signature of "coherent" combining across N antennas within
      each AP + across M APs. Note N² × (something independent of
      N) — THIS is the massive-MIMO power gain that makes simple
      conjugate beamforming work.

  (denom, 1st term)  ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²
      This is the *pilot-contamination-induced inter-user
      interference*. It scales as N², like the useful signal —
      meaning that pilot contamination is the ONE interference
      that does NOT average out with more antennas. This is the
      famous "pilot contamination curse" of massive MIMO.
      Note that 𝛄_{k,k'} is zero if user k and user k' use fully
      orthogonal pilots (|𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k| = 0).

  (denom, 2nd term)  ρ_d N Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖²
      This is the *non-coherent interference* (beamforming
      uncertainty + cross-user interference). It scales linearly
      in N, so it is dominated by the N² useful signal as N grows
      — this is precisely the array gain that makes conjugate
      beamforming effective.

  (denom, 3rd term)  1
      Thermal noise at the user.

  (prefactor)  (1 − τ_p/τ_c)
      The penalty for spending τ_p samples on pilots.

Special cases (sanity checks the authors highlight):

  • N = 1 (single-antenna APs): (15) reduces exactly to Eq. (24) of
    the Ngo 2017 TWC paper [6], the canonical single-antenna
    CF-mMIMO SE.
  • M = 1, N → M' (colocated case with M' antennas at ONE AP): (15)
    reduces to Table 3.2 of the Marzetta-Larsson-Yang-Ngo textbook [7].

─── 3.3  Sum spectral efficiency ────────────────────────────────────────

     S_e({η_{m,k}})  =  Σ_{k=1}^{K}  SE_k({η_{m,k}})                        (16)

This is what enters the backhaul term B · S_e · P_{bt,m} in Section III.
The paper will maximize energy efficiency with respect to {η_{m,k}}
under the constraint SE_k ≥ S_{o,k} ∀k and Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N
∀m.

─── 3.4  Why "closed form" matters ──────────────────────────────────────

Before [6, 7] and this paper, typical CF-mMIMO / distributed MIMO SE
formulas required Monte-Carlo evaluation (sample the random channels,
average). The closed-form (15) depends only on deterministic
quantities {β_{m,k}, γ_{m,k}, η_{m,k}, τ_p, τ_c, ρ_d}. This means:

  • power control {η_{m,k}} can be OPTIMIZED without running any
    channel simulation inside the loop,
  • the large-scale fading {β_{m,k}} (and therefore {γ_{m,k}}) varies
    on a much slower time scale than small-scale fading, so the
    optimization needs to be re-done only once per ~100–1000
    coherence blocks.

This is what makes the SCA-SOCP optimization of Section IV
computationally feasible.

• MMSE channel estimation for massive MIMO with pilot contamination
  was fully worked out by Hoydis-ten Brink-Debbah (IEEE JSAC 2013) in
  their classical paper on the deterministic equivalents approach.
  The single-cell IID case used here is a direct application of
  Kay, "Fundamentals of Statistical Signal Processing, Vol. 1:
  Estimation Theory," Prentice Hall 1993, chapter on Bayesian
  Gaussian estimation.

• The "use-and-then-forget" capacity bound comes from Medard (IEEE
  TIT 2000) "The effect upon channel capacity in wireless
  communications of perfect and imperfect knowledge of the channel"
  — the mutual-information lower bound with worst-case Gaussian
  noise replacing the true non-Gaussian effective noise. Marzetta
  popularized its use for massive MIMO starting with his 2010 paper,
  and the textbook [7] Ch. 2.3 is the cleanest modern treatment.

• Conjugate / matched-filter beamforming is the oldest linear
  precoder in wireless (it goes back to radar detection theory in
  the 1940s). Its renaissance for massive MIMO is due to the fact
  that, under channel hardening, ‖𝐠‖²/N → β almost surely, so
  matched filtering is asymptotically optimal in the number of
  antennas. Marzetta 2010 established this formally.

• The pilot-contamination phenomenon in cellular massive MIMO was
  identified and named by Marzetta (2010). The cell-free massive
  MIMO papers [6, 8, 9] extended the analysis. A clean treatment of
  pilot contamination in the cell-free downlink using MMSE estimation
  is given in the original Ngo-2017 TWC paper [6]; Section II of the
  present paper is essentially a multi-antenna generalization.

5. MATHEMATICAL RECAP IN UNICODE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Equations (1)-(16) restated cleanly for the terminal:

  (1)  𝐘_{p,m} = √(τ_p ρ_p) Σ_{k=1}^{K} 𝐠_{m,k} 𝛗_k^H + 𝐖_{p,m}

  (2)  𝐠_{m,k} = √β_{m,k} · 𝐡_{m,k},   𝐡_{m,k} ~ 𝒞𝒩(𝟎_N, 𝐈_N)

  (3)  𝐲_{p,m,k} = 𝐘_{p,m} 𝛗_k
             = √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k}
             + √(τ_p ρ_p) Σ_{k'≠k} 𝐠_{m,k'} (𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k)
             + 𝐰̃_{p,m,k}

                                          √(τ_p ρ_p) β_{m,k}
  (4)  ĝ_{m,k}  = ─────────────────────────────────────────────── · 𝐲_{p,m,k}
                     τ_p ρ_p Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|² + 1

                      τ_p ρ_p β_{m,k}²
  (5)  γ_{m,k}  = ─────────────────────────────────────────
                     τ_p ρ_p Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|² + 1

  (6)  𝐱_m  = √ρ_d Σ_{k=1}^{K} √η_{m,k} · ĝ_{m,k}^* · q_k

  (7)  𝔼{‖𝐱_m‖²} = ρ_d · N · Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}

  (8)  Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,  m = 1, …, M

  (9)  r_k = √ρ_d Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* q_k
           + √ρ_d Σ_{k'≠k} Σ_m √η_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k'}^* q_{k'}  + w_k

  (10) r_k = DS_k q_k + BU_k q_k + Σ_{k'≠k} UI_{k,k'} q_{k'} + w_k

  (11) SE_k = (1 − τ_p/τ_c) log₂(1 + |DS_k|² / (𝔼{|BU_k|²}+Σ_{k'≠k}𝔼{|UI_{k,k'}|²}+1))

  (12) DS_k       ≝ √ρ_d · 𝔼{ Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* }
  (13) BU_k       ≝ √ρ_d · (Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* − 𝔼{…})
  (14) UI_{k,k'}  ≝ √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k'}^*

  (15) SE_k({η_{m,k}}) = (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂( 1 +
                       ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|²
         ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
         ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|² + ρ_d N Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖² + 1
                                                                            )

  (16) S_e({η_{m,k}}) = Σ_{k=1}^{K} SE_k({η_{m,k}})


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. NUMERICAL WORKED EXAMPLE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Let us compute γ_{m,k}, per-AP power, and SE by hand for a tiny
scenario: **M = 2 APs, K = 2 users, N = 2 antennas per AP,
τ_p = τ_c = 200** (we will neglect the prefactor for simplicity,
τ_p/τ_c = 0.005 small), τ_c = 200 (so 1-τ_p/τ_c ≈ 0.995).

Large-scale fading (in linear scale):

           β_{1,1} = 0.1   β_{1,2} = 0.01
           β_{2,1} = 0.01  β_{2,2} = 0.1
   (i.e. AP 1 is close to user 1, AP 2 is close to user 2; the classic
    "diagonal" topology.)

Assume orthogonal pilots, so 𝛗_1^H 𝛗_2 = 0, and ρ_p = 1, ρ_d = 1.

Step 1 — compute γ_{m,k} from (5):

   γ_{1,1} = τ_p ρ_p β_{1,1}² / ( τ_p ρ_p β_{1,1} + 1 )
           = 200 · 1 · 0.01 / (200 · 0.1 + 1)
           = 2 / 21
           ≈ 0.0952

   γ_{1,2} = 200 · 0.0001 / (200 · 0.01 + 1)  = 0.02 / 3  ≈ 0.00667

   γ_{2,1} = 200 · 0.0001 / (200 · 0.01 + 1)  = 0.02 / 3  ≈ 0.00667

   γ_{2,2} = 200 · 0.01 / (200 · 0.1 + 1)     = 2 / 21    ≈ 0.0952

Step 2 — pick power control coefficients. Full-power equal allocation:

   η_{1,k} = η_{2,k} = 1/(N Σ_k γ_{m,k})
           = 1 / (2 · (0.0952 + 0.00667)) = 1/0.2038 ≈ 4.908
   (for each AP, equal across users)

   But for a cleaner number, let us instead split equally between the
   two users at each AP: η_{m,1} = η_{m,2}. Then (8) says
        η_{m,1}·γ_{m,1} + η_{m,2}·γ_{m,2} ≤ 1/N = 0.5

        AP 1:  η·(0.0952 + 0.00667) ≤ 0.5 ⇒ η ≤ 4.908
        AP 2:  η·(0.00667 + 0.0952) ≤ 0.5 ⇒ η ≤ 4.908

   Pick η_{m,1} = η_{m,2} = 4.908, so both constraints are active.

Step 3 — compute |𝛄_{1,1}^T 𝛈_1|² (numerator of SINR for user 1):

   𝛄_{1,1} = [γ_{1,1}, γ_{2,1}]^T = [0.0952, 0.00667]^T
   𝛈_1 = [η_{1,1}, η_{2,1}]^T = [√4.908, √4.908]^T ≈ [2.215, 2.215]^T

   Wait — 𝛈_k in Proposition 1 holds √η, not η. Let me re-check.
   Looking at (6): 𝐱_m = √ρ_d Σ_k √η_{m,k} ĝ_{m,k}^* q_k.
   The power control coefficient η_{m,k} enters with a square root.
   In Proposition 1's notation, 𝛈_k = [η_{1,k}, …, η_{M,k}]^T is the
   vector of √-ROOTS? Let us re-read Eq. (15) and the paragraph just
   before.

   [Paper quote: "𝛈_k ≝ [η_{1,k}, …, η_{M,k}]^T … power control
    coefficients associated with user k"]

   So 𝛈_k is the η's themselves. But then the numerator
   ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|² has units (linear-power-coeff × √-power?).
   Looking at the proof in Appendix A, one can see that the authors
   collapse the √η_{m,k} back into the c_{m,k} = √η_{m,k} in Sec IV.
   So in the SE formula as written, the symbol η_{m,k} in Eq. (15)
   actually is √η_{m,k}! The authors are reusing notation — this is
   a minor inconsistency in the paper. In Sec IV they fix it by
   defining c_{m,k} = √η_{m,k} explicitly.

   Therefore, in the formula (15), we should read η_{m,k} as the
   SQUARE ROOT of the power coefficient. Let us use that:

   Redefine  𝛈_1 := [√4.908, √4.908]^T ≈ [2.215, 2.215]^T.

   Numerator for user 1:
     |𝛄_{1,1}^T 𝛈_1|² = |0.0952 · 2.215 + 0.00667 · 2.215|²
                      = |0.2108 + 0.01478|²
                      = |0.2256|²
                      ≈ 0.05090

   With N² = 4 and ρ_d = 1:  ρ_d N² · 0.05090 = 0.2036

Step 4 — denominator terms (still for user 1):

  • Pilot-contamination term Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|² :
    Since pilots are orthogonal, 𝛄_{1,2} = 0 (all components), so
    this sum is 0.

  • Non-coherent term Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',1} 𝛈_{k'}‖² :
    𝐃_{k',1} = diag( √(β_{1,1} γ_{1,k'}), √(β_{2,1} γ_{2,k'}) )
    For k' = 1: 𝐃_{1,1} = diag(√(0.1·0.0952), √(0.01·0.00667))
                        = diag(√0.00952, √0.0000667)
                        = diag(0.09757, 0.008165)
        ‖𝐃_{1,1} 𝛈_1‖² = (0.09757·2.215)² + (0.008165·2.215)²
                        = (0.2161)² + (0.01809)²
                        ≈ 0.04670 + 0.000327
                        ≈ 0.04703
    For k' = 2: 𝐃_{2,1} = diag(√(0.1·0.00667), √(0.01·0.0952))
                        = diag(√0.000667, √0.000952)
                        = diag(0.02583, 0.03086)
        ‖𝐃_{2,1} 𝛈_2‖² = (0.02583·2.215)² + (0.03086·2.215)²
                        = (0.05723)² + (0.06836)²
                        ≈ 0.003275 + 0.004673
                        ≈ 0.007948
    Sum ≈ 0.04703 + 0.00795 ≈ 0.05498
    With ρ_d N = 2:  ρ_d N · 0.05498 ≈ 0.10995

  • Thermal noise:  1

Step 5 — SINR_1 and SE_1:

     SINR_1 = 0.2036 / (0 + 0.10995 + 1) = 0.2036 / 1.10995 ≈ 0.1834

     SE_1 = (1 - 0.005) · log₂(1 + 0.1834)
          ≈ 0.995 · log₂(1.1834)
          ≈ 0.995 · 0.2429
          ≈ 0.2417 bit/s/Hz

The SINR is very low because the example is tiny (small M, N) and the
desired user has only 0.1 average path gain. With M = 100, N = 4 this
number would be orders of magnitude larger due to the coherent N²
scaling of the useful signal.

By symmetry SE_2 = SE_1 ≈ 0.2417 bit/s/Hz. Sum SE:
     S_e = 2 · 0.2417 ≈ 0.4834 bit/s/Hz.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7. SUMMARY OF SECTION II
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Setup. M APs (each N antennas), K single-antenna users, TDD with
  coherence τ_c and pilot length τ_p < τ_c. IID Rayleigh small-scale
  fading, deterministic large-scale fading β_{m,k}.

• Uplink training (Eq. 1). Users transmit τ_p-symbol pilots 𝛗_k
  simultaneously; AP m receives a superposition corrupted by noise.

• Despreading + MMSE estimation (Eqs. 3, 4). AP m despreads by 𝛗_k
  and applies an MMSE scalar to get ĝ_{m,k}. All components share
  the same per-component variance γ_{m,k} (Eq. 5). γ_{m,k} is the
  single scalar that captures path-loss, pilot power, pilot length,
  and pilot contamination. The residual error is independent and
  has variance β_{m,k} − γ_{m,k}.

• Conjugate beamforming (Eq. 6). AP m transmits ĝ_{m,k}^* · q_k for
  each user, scaled by local power-control coefficients η_{m,k}.
  Per-AP total transmit power (Eq. 7) gives the constraint
  Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N (Eq. 8).

• Received signal at user k (Eq. 9). Superposition of all APs; three
  ingredients: desired signal, inter-user interference, thermal noise.

• "Use-and-forget" SE bound (Eq. 10-11). User k knows only channel
  statistics, so it treats 𝔼{effective channel} as the known gain
  and lumps everything else into Gaussian "effective noise." Result:
  an achievable SE lower bound that is tight in the massive-MIMO limit.

• Proposition 1 (Eq. 15). The SE bound is written as an explicit
  closed form in {γ_{m,k}}, {β_{m,k}}, and the (√)power-control
  vectors {𝛈_k}. Key structural features:

      • numerator ∝ ρ_d N² · (coherent combining term)²
      • pilot-contamination term ∝ ρ_d N² (does NOT vanish with N!)
      • non-coherent interference term ∝ ρ_d N (washes out as N →∞)

  Reduces to Ngo 2017 TWC (single-antenna CF) when N = 1 and to
  colocated massive MIMO (ref [7, Table 3.2]) when M = 1.

• Why closed form matters. No Monte-Carlo needed inside the
  optimization loop. The subsequent power control (Sec IV) can
  operate purely on deterministic β's and γ's, which change slowly.

• Small notational subtlety. Proposition 1 uses 𝛈_k to denote the
  vector of *power-control coefficients* but the formula internally
  needs the square root √η_{m,k}. In Section IV the paper introduces
  c_{m,k} ≝ √η_{m,k} to avoid confusion.
