───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. HIGH-LEVEL (LAYMAN) EXPLANATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Sections II–V have given us everything we need:
  •  a closed-form data-rate expression (Sec II, Prop 1),
  •  a realistic power-consumption model with backhaul (Sec III),
  •  an SCA-SOCP power-allocation algorithm (Sec IV, Algorithm 1),
  •  two AP-selection schemes (Sec V, Algorithms 2 and 3).

Section VI's job is to run all of this on a REALISTIC simulated
cell-free deployment and answer four practical questions:

   Q1. Does Algorithm 1 actually converge, and how fast?
   Q2. How much does power control help total energy efficiency,
       compared to naively "use full power equally"?
   Q3. How much does AP selection help, and which scheme is better?
       How many APs are actually needed per user?
   Q4. What is the optimal number of antennas per AP (N), for a
       fixed total service-antenna count MN? And is cell-free
       massive MIMO really more energy-efficient than a single
       colocated massive-MIMO base station with the same MN?

The answers — which this section then pays off with six well-chosen
figures — are:

   A1. Algorithm 1 converges in ~10 iterations and empirically
       matches the global optimum found by a branch-and-bound
       method on small problems. So you can safely trust its
       solutions as "effectively optimal" in practice.

   A2. Power control (Algorithm 1) improves EE by a factor of about
       2.6 to 3× compared to "no power control." This is a huge
       free win that requires zero hardware change.

   A3. AP selection further improves EE significantly, especially
       at large M or at high backhaul cost P_bt. The received-
       power-based scheme (Algorithm 2) is marginally better than
       the LSF-based scheme (Algorithm 3), but the gap is small —
       practical deployments can confidently use Algorithm 3, which
       is cheaper. And each user ends up needing only about
       10–20 APs out of 80–200, i.e. 5–20% of the total.

   A4. There is an OPTIMAL number of antennas per AP N*, which
       depends on the deployment density and the backhaul cost
       P_bt. When backhaul is cheap and the area is small, you
       want MANY APs with FEWER antennas each (N* small). When
       backhaul is expensive or the area is large, you want FEWER
       APs with MORE antennas each (N* large). And — critically —
       cell-free massive MIMO beats colocated massive MIMO in EE
       by a factor of 7.4× (at MN = 128) to 2.2× (at MN = 256)
       when each user has a 1 bit/s/Hz quality-of-service
       requirement. This is the "order-of-magnitude" headline that
       appeared in the abstract.

Together, these results validate the whole theoretical edifice of
Sections II–V and deliver the punchline:

    "Cell-free massive MIMO is not just a blackboard concept — it
     is practically GREEN, provided you run the power-control +
     AP-selection algorithms presented in this paper. Skipping
     either step throws away most of the benefit."

2. PHD-LEVEL EXPLANATION — PART A: PARAMETERS AND SETUP
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─── 2.1  Geometry: the wrap-around square ────────────────────────────

APs and users are placed UNIFORMLY AT RANDOM inside a square of side
D kilometres, giving a D × D km² deployment area. The square is
"wrapped around at the edges to avoid boundary effects", which
means the simulation places identical copies of the square on every
side of the original (a 3 × 3 torus) and uses the SHORTEST wrapped
distance for computing path losses. This is the standard technique
for simulating an infinite-grid deployment with only a finite amount
of computation, pioneered by Marzetta's massive-MIMO textbook [7].

Without wrap-around, an AP at the corner of the square would have
half as many neighbours as an AP in the centre, and it would both
experience AND cause less interference. Users near the edges would
likewise be "cornered". Wrap-around restores translation invariance
so that every AP and every user is statistically equivalent.

Two deployment sizes are explored in the figures:
   •  D = 1 km (dense deployment, e.g. a stadium or downtown
      district)
   •  D = 2 km (sparser deployment, e.g. a residential neighbourhood)

─── 2.2  Pilot assignment: random from a pool of τ_p ─────────────────

For simplicity the paper uses RANDOM pilot assignment: each user
independently picks one of τ_p orthogonal length-τ_p pilot sequences
uniformly at random. If K ≤ τ_p (e.g. K = 20 users and τ_p = 20),
every user gets a DIFFERENT pilot and there is NO pilot contamination.
If K > τ_p (e.g. K = 40 users and τ_p = 20), some users MUST share
pilots, and the expected number of "collision partners" per user is

      K/τ_p − 1 ≈ 2 − 1 = 1 collision partner

so each user has ~one pilot-contamination interferer. This is a
modest amount of contamination; heavier contamination requires
smarter pilot-assignment schemes (covered in follow-up papers).

The random assignment is pessimistic compared to smart assignment
(e.g., Ngo 2017 TWC [6] Sec. III uses a greedy assignment that
minimizes max inter-user interference), so the numerical results
here represent a lower bound on what CF-mMIMO can achieve. The
paper uses random assignment for simplicity and to keep the focus
on EE rather than on pilot-assignment algorithms.

─── 2.3  Large-scale fading: path loss × shadow fading (Eqs. 46–47) ─

The large-scale fading β_{m,k} is modelled as

     β_{m,k}  =  PL_{m,k}  ·  z_{m,k}                                 (46)

with z_{m,k} = 10^(σ_sh · n_{m,k} / 10), n_{m,k} ~ 𝒩(0, 1), i.e.
log-normal shadowing with standard deviation σ_sh dB. The paper
uses σ_sh = 8 dB — the standard value for outdoor propagation used
by Marzetta and across the cellular industry.

PL_{m,k} is a THREE-SLOPE PATH LOSS model from Tang-Sun-Gong 2001
(ref [24] in the paper), originally developed for NLOS street
microcells in dense urban areas with low-antenna base stations:

              ⎧ −L − 35 · log₁₀(d_{m,k}),           d_{m,k} > d_1
   PL_{m,k} = ⎨ −L − 15 · log₁₀(d_1) − 20 · log₁₀(d_{m,k}),  d_0 < d_{m,k} ≤ d_1
              ⎩ −L − 15 · log₁₀(d_1) − 20 · log₁₀(d_0),     d_{m,k} ≤ d_0        (47)

where d_{m,k} is the distance between AP m and user k in metres, and

   d_0 = 10 m          (below this, path loss is CAPPED at a constant)
   d_1 = 50 m          (breakpoint between "near" and "far" regime)
   L   = 140.7 dB      (absorbs carrier frequency, AP/user heights,
                        reference distance; corresponds to about
                        1.9 GHz with 15 m base-station antenna and
                        1.65 m user antenna — typical urban macro)

The three regimes have clear physical meanings:

  •  Very close (d ≤ d_0 = 10 m):
     The path loss is FROZEN at a constant −L − 15 log_{10}(d_1) −
     20 log_{10}(d_0) ≈ −140.7 − 15·1.699 − 20·1.0 ≈ −186.2 dB.
     (This includes the 10 m ground-wave cap and prevents absurd
     "infinite gain" artefacts when a user sits right next to an AP.)

  •  Near regime (10 m < d ≤ 50 m):
     PL slope is −20 log₁₀(d) relative to the 10 m reference —
     i.e. FREE SPACE PROPAGATION (path-loss exponent 2). This is
     the typical very-near-AP behaviour.

  •  Far regime (d > 50 m):
     PL slope is −35 log₁₀(d) — i.e. path-loss exponent 3.5.
     This is an AGGRESSIVE decay exponent representative of dense
     urban NLOS propagation, where buildings severely attenuate the
     signal.

The split at d_1 = 50 m into "free-space" and "NLOS" regimes captures
the transition from "you are close enough to the AP to enjoy
line-of-sight-like propagation" to "you are in the NLOS shadow of
buildings". The three-slope model is a widely-used 3GPP-compatible
simplification of the full 3D ray-tracing propagation measurements
from [24].

─── 2.4  Power-consumption parameters (Table I of the paper) ───────

   Parameter                                          Value
   ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   PA efficiency  ν_m                                 0.4
   Per-antenna circuit  P_{tc,m}                      0.2 W
   Fixed backhaul per AP  P_{0,m}                    0.825 W
   Traffic-dep backhaul  P_{bt,m}                    0.25 W/(Gbit/s)

These values are taken from Björnson-2015 [11] and Zuo-2016 [14],
which in turn calibrated to the EARTH project measurements of
Auer-2011. They are representative of small-cell / femtocell-grade
AP hardware in the 2015–2017 era. Newer power amplifiers (envelope-
tracking, GaN) can hit ν_m = 0.5 and beyond, which would make
CF-mMIMO even more energy-efficient than Fig. 2–6 suggest.

─── 2.5  Other constants ───────────────────────────────────────────

   System bandwidth           B           = 20 MHz
   Noise figure (at receiver) NF          = 9 dB
   Normalized DL transmit SNR ρ_d         = 1 W    (see caveat below)
   Normalized UL pilot  SNR   ρ_p         = 0.2 W  (20% of downlink)
   Coherence interval         τ_c         = 200 samples

The thermal noise power at 20 MHz bandwidth with NF = 9 dB is
approximately

   N_0  =  k T B · 10^(NF/10)  ≈  (1.38 × 10^{−23}) · (290) · (2 × 10^7)
                                       · 10^0.9
         ≈  6.4 × 10^{−14}  W     (~ −132 dBm)

Caveat on "ρ_d = 1 W": in the paper's notation ρ_d is the
NORMALIZED downlink SNR (dimensionless, normalized by N_0). So
"ρ_d = 1" really means "the AP can transmit at SNR level 1 in units
of noise power", which corresponds to an actual transmit power of
roughly N_0 × 1 ≈ 6.4 × 10^{−14} W. In the simulations, a
dimensional transmit power of ~200 mW is implicitly recovered by
scaling; the numerical EE figures (which are the reported Mbit/J)
should be interpreted in that calibrated sense.

─── 3.1  Fig. 1 — Convergence of Algorithm 1 ───────────────────────

SETUP: N = 1 (single-antenna APs), D = 1 km, τ_p = 20, arbitrary
large-scale-fading realization. Several (M, K) combinations are
shown:
   •  (M = 8, K = 2)    — a TINY case used to benchmark against BRB
   •  (M = 100, K = 20)
   •  (M = 100, K = 40)
   •  (M = 200, K = 40)

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS: EE (in Mbit/Joule) as a function of the SCA
iteration index, from iteration 0 to iteration ~15. The curves
rise monotonically from the initial feasible point (solved by the
initialization SOCP in step 1 of Algorithm 1) and plateau.

KEY OBSERVATIONS:

  (i)  Monotone increase. The EE at iteration n+1 is ALWAYS ≥ the
       EE at iteration n (for all 4 curves). This confirms the
       convergence analysis of Sec IV-D: the SCA bounds are tight at
       the tangent point, so the previous iterate is always
       feasible for the next SOCP, and the objective cannot
       decrease.

  (ii) Fast convergence. All curves essentially plateau after
       ~10 iterations. The marginal improvement beyond iteration
       10 is < 1%, which is well below any engineering-relevant
       precision.

  (iii) BRB benchmark (only for M = 8, K = 2). The paper modifies
       the BRB code from Tervo-Tran-Juntti 2015 [25] and runs it on
       the tiny (8, 2) case, finding the GLOBAL optimum as an
       asymptote. Algorithm 1 converges to EXACTLY the BRB optimum.
       So in the toy case, SCA IS globally optimal — not just a
       KKT point. The paper extrapolates (cautiously) that SCA is
       near-globally-optimal on larger instances as well.

  (iv) Larger networks converge similarly fast. Going from (100,
       20) to (200, 40) does NOT increase the number of iterations
       needed. The convergence of SCA depends mostly on the shape
       of the problem (degree of non-convexity), not on its size.

CONCLUSION FROM FIG. 1: it is SAFE to fix N_I = 10 iterations and
ε_tol = 0.01 for the rest of the numerical study. This is what the
remaining figures use.

─── 3.2  Fig. 2 — Impact of power control (vs "no power control") ──

SETUP: N = 1, D = 1 km, τ_p = 20, M varies from 20 to 100 on the
x-axis. Two curves per K value: (K = 20) and (K = 40).

WHAT IS "NO POWER CONTROL"? Each AP uses FULL TRANSMIT POWER (the
per-AP power constraint holds with equality) and distributes it
EQUALLY among the K users:

    η_{m,k}  =  ( N · Σ_{k'=1}^K γ_{m,k'} )^{-1}    for all k
             (independent of k)

This way Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} = 1/N is exactly satisfied. The target
S_o,k for the optimized Algorithm-1 curve is set to match the
spectral efficiency that "no power control" achieves, so that the
comparison is fair (both deliver the same per-user QoS).

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS: Average EE versus the number of APs M, for two
user-count values (K = 20 and K = 40), for both power-control
methods. Averages are taken over many large-scale-fading
realizations.

KEY OBSERVATIONS:

  (i)  Power control WINS by a LARGE margin. At M = 100:
          K = 20:  EE(Alg-1) ≈ 11   Mbit/J
                    EE(no PC) ≈ 3.8 Mbit/J
                    → improvement factor ≈ 2.9×
          K = 40:  EE(Alg-1) ≈ 10   Mbit/J
                    EE(no PC) ≈ 3.9 Mbit/J
                    → improvement factor ≈ 2.6×
       So Algorithm 1 roughly TRIPLES energy efficiency at zero
       hardware cost. This is the single biggest "free win" of the
       whole paper.

  (ii) Why does EE increase with M for the optimized curve? Because
       a larger number of APs provides more spatial diversity and
       array gain, which raises S_e faster than the backhaul cost
       can drag it down (at least for M up to ~100). This is the
       classical "more APs help" massive-MIMO intuition.

  (iii) Why does EE DECREASE slightly with K (from K=20 to K=40)?
       Because more users means more pilot contamination (random
       pilot assignment, and K > τ_p = 20 when K = 40 forces
       collisions), and also more backhaul traffic — both of which
       hurt EE. The effect is moderate: EE drops from ~11 to ~10
       Mbit/J.

  (iv) The "no power control" curve is roughly flat in M, because
       adding APs under equal-power allocation just replicates the
       same waste of transmit power across more APs; no intelligent
       reallocation happens. The benefit from Algorithm 1 is that
       it aggressively SHUTS DOWN some η_{m,k} entries when they
       are inefficient.

CONCLUSION FROM FIG. 2: the remaining figures in the paper always
use power control (Algorithm 1). Also: when a problem is infeasible
(S_o,k cannot be achieved with any power allocation), the paper
sets EE = 0 — the curves always plot finite positive values because
the simulation samples only feasible realizations, or averages in
infeasible EE = 0 events.

─── 3.3  Fig. 3 — Impact of AP selection ────────────────────────────

SETUP: M = 100, K = 40, N = 1, D = 1 km, τ_p = 40 (enough pilots
for all K users to have an orthogonal pilot, so NO pilot
contamination in this figure), S_o,k = 1 bit/s/Hz for all users.
Selection threshold ρ = 95%. The x-axis is the traffic-dependent
backhaul power P_{bt,m} (assumed equal for all m, i.e. P_{bt}),
ranging from 0.25 to 2 W/(Gbit/s).

THREE CURVES:
   (a) "no AP selection" (Algorithm 1 alone, baseline)
   (b) "largest-large-scale-based selection" (Algorithm 3)
   (c) "received-power-based selection" (Algorithm 2)

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS: Average EE in Mbit/J versus P_bt.

KEY OBSERVATIONS:

  (i)  The baseline (no selection) EE DECREASES STEEPLY with P_bt:
       at P_bt = 0.25 it is high (say 10 Mbit/J), but at P_bt = 2
       it has crashed to ~3 Mbit/J. This is the "backhaul is the
       elephant" effect — as backhaul becomes more expensive, the
       denominator of E_e blows up and EE collapses.

  (ii) Both AP-selection curves are almost FLAT in P_bt. They
       start at roughly the same value as the baseline at P_bt =
       0.25 and remain within a few % of that value as P_bt grows
       to 2. The algorithms effectively SHIELD EE from increases
       in backhaul cost by keeping only the necessary APs active.
       This is the main argument for using AP selection.

  (iii) Both AP-selection schemes are close: Received-power-based
       is slightly better than largest-LSF-based (as expected,
       since the former uses more information about interference
       and power allocation). But the gap is SMALL — typically
       within 5–10%. So Algorithm 3 is an excellent practical
       choice because it is cheaper.

  (iv) Gains are LARGEST at high P_bt. At P_bt = 2, AP selection
       doubles EE compared to the baseline. At P_bt = 0.25, the
       gain is smaller (~20%) because the baseline already works
       well. The lesson: AP selection is essential in deployments
       with expensive backhaul (e.g. wireless backhaul, distant
       APs), and optional but still helpful elsewhere.

─── 3.4  Fig. 4 — How many APs per user are actually chosen? ──────

SETUP: K = 40, N = 1, τ_p = 40, S_o,k = 1, ρ = 95%. D ∈ {1, 2} km.
M on the x-axis, ranging from 80 to 200.

TWO CURVES per D (so four curves total): received-power-based and
largest-LSF-based.

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS: the AVERAGE NUMBER OF APs SELECTED per user,
as a function of total M. If it scaled as M (e.g. always 50% of M),
the curves would be straight lines through the origin; if it were
a constant, the curves would be horizontal.

KEY OBSERVATIONS:

  (i)  The average |A_k| is ROUGHLY CONSTANT in M for D = 1 km:
       at M = 80 it is ~10 APs, at M = 200 it is ~15 APs. So the
       number of ACTIVE associations per user scales SUB-LINEARLY
       with M. This is a crucial scalability observation: even as
       you densify the network, the backhaul load per user stays
       roughly constant. Without selection, it would scale linearly.

  (ii) D = 2 km (sparser deployment) requires MORE APs per user
       than D = 1 km (denser): at M = 200, D = 2 km selects ~30
       APs per user while D = 1 km selects only ~15. Why? Because
       in a sparser deployment, the β's decay more slowly across
       APs (there are fewer very-close APs to dominate), so you
       need more of them to reach the 95% cumulative threshold.

  (iii) Received-power-based selects SLIGHTLY FEWER APs than
       largest-LSF-based (by ~10%). The reason is that the
       received-power criterion "sees" how well the power-control
       algorithm is allocating η — some of the APs with strong β
       are actually being given very small η because they serve
       many users, so they contribute less than an LSF-only ranking
       would suggest. Received-power-based correctly drops them.

CONCLUSION FROM FIG. 4: the paper concludes "only about 10%–20%
of the total number of APs really participate in serving a given
user. These results imply that ... it is enough for each user to
choose a small number of APs around it, which enables the PRACTICAL
IMPLEMENTATION of cell-free massive MIMO." This is essentially the
motto of user-centric cell-free massive MIMO that blossomed in
2018 and afterwards. In the remaining numerical results, the paper
uses received-power-based selection with ρ = 95%.

─── 3.5  Fig. 5 — Effect of N (antennas per AP), fixed MN = 256 ───

SETUP: K = 40, τ_p = 40, total service antennas MN = 256. N varies
on the x-axis from 1 to ~150, so M decreases correspondingly from
256 down to ~2. Received-power-based selection with ρ = 95%.

Three curves for three (D, P_{bt,m}, S_{o,k}) combinations:
   •  D = 1 km, P_{bt,m} = 0.25 W/(Gbit/s), S_{o,k} = 1  (best case)
   •  D = 2 km, P_{bt,m} = 0.5  W/(Gbit/s), S_{o,k} = 1  (medium)
   •  D = 2 km, P_{bt,m} = 0.25 W/(Gbit/s), S_{o,k} = 2  (harder QoS)

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS: The EE is NOT MONOTONE in N — it first
INCREASES (as N grows from 1), reaches a MAXIMUM at some optimal
N*, and then DECREASES (as N grows beyond N*). The curves look
like inverted-U / concave functions, with distinct peaks.

THE INTUITION behind the U-shape is the classical CF-mMIMO trade-off:

  •  Small N (many APs). You have M = MN/N = 256/N APs, so M is
     large. This gives:
        + High macro-diversity: every user is close to some AP.
        + High spectral efficiency (the direct-signal N² gain per
          AP is small but the number of APs is huge).
        − High per-AP backhaul: Σ_m P_{0,m} = 256/N × 0.825 W.
          For N = 1, this is 211 W — a massive fixed cost.
        − High per-AP circuit power: unchanged.
     Net effect: backhaul costs dominate EE.

  •  Large N (few APs). You have few APs, each with many antennas.
        + Low per-AP backhaul: Σ_m P_{0,m} = 256/N × 0.825 W is small.
          For N = 64, it is only 3.3 W.
        + Large array gain per AP: N² coherent combining within
          each AP.
        − Low macro-diversity: users near "gaps" between APs suffer.
          SE decreases because users are farther from the nearest
          AP on average.
     Net effect: lower SE but much lower power → EE could go
     either way.

OPTIMAL N*: the sweet spot between these two effects. It depends
on the specific deployment:

  (i)  D = 1 km, P_bt = 0.25, S_o = 1 (dense + cheap backhaul):
       N* is small (~4), because backhaul is cheap so you can
       afford many APs. Peak EE ≈ 25 Mbit/J.

  (ii) D = 2 km, P_bt = 0.5, S_o = 1 (sparse + expensive backhaul):
       N* shifts HIGHER (~16), because backhaul is expensive so
       you save money by using fewer APs. Peak EE ≈ 15 Mbit/J
       — lower than (i) because the underlying conditions are worse.

  (iii) D = 2 km, P_bt = 0.25, S_o = 2 (sparse + cheap backhaul +
       harder QoS): N* is even HIGHER (~32), because the user
       is asking for twice the data rate of (ii), which requires
       more array gain per AP. Peak EE ≈ 5 Mbit/J — much lower
       because the QoS is harder to meet.

GENERAL RULE-OF-THUMB from the paper:
   •  at LOW P_bt, LOW S_o, SMALL D  →  small N* (many APs, few
      antennas per AP).
   •  at HIGH P_bt, HIGH S_o, LARGE D →  large N* (few APs, many
      antennas per AP).

CONCLUSION FROM FIG. 5: there is a design principle for choosing N
given the deployment parameters. The paper does NOT derive a
closed-form N* expression, but Fig. 5 lets an engineer eyeball the
right range for any given (D, P_bt, S_o) triple.

─── 3.6  Fig. 6 — Cell-Free vs Colocated Massive MIMO ──────────────

SETUP: K = 20, τ_p = 40 (no pilot contamination), D = 1 km. Two
values of MN (= 128 and 256). x-axis: SUM SPECTRAL EFFICIENCY TARGET
Σ_{k=1}^K S_{o,k} in bit/s/Hz. y-axis: average EE in Mbit/J.

COLOCATED MASSIVE MIMO baseline: M = 1, N ∈ {128, 256}. One huge
BS with a massive antenna array.

CELL-FREE MASSIVE MIMO: received-power-based selection with the
best-for-that-column N* as selected from Fig. 5. For MN = 128
and D = 1 km, N* = 4 → M = 32; for MN = 256 and D = 1 km, N* = 4
→ M = 64 (approximately).

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS: EE vs. sum rate target. As the sum target
increases, both CF and colocated curves rise (you're getting more
bits per joule), reach a peak, and fall (once the target becomes
too hard for the available power — SE saturates and EE drops).

KEY OBSERVATIONS:

  (i)  CELL-FREE MASSIVE MIMO IS SIGNIFICANTLY BETTER, for BOTH
       MN values. The CF curve is uniformly above the colocated
       curve across the entire range of sum-rate targets.

  (ii) Gain quantified: with Σ_k S_{o,k} = 20 bit/s/Hz (1 bit/s/Hz
       per user), cell-free massive MIMO delivers:
          MN = 128:  CF ≈ 20 Mbit/J, colocated ≈ 2.7 Mbit/J
                    → CF factor ≈ 7.4×
          MN = 256:  CF ≈ 22 Mbit/J, colocated ≈ 10 Mbit/J
                    → CF factor ≈ 2.2×
       So at MODERATE antenna counts (MN = 128), CF-mMIMO is
       7.4× MORE ENERGY-EFFICIENT than a single colocated massive-
       MIMO BS. This is the order-of-magnitude headline from the
       abstract.

  (iii) The gap SHRINKS as MN grows (from 7.4× at MN = 128 to
       2.2× at MN = 256). Why? At very large antenna counts, the
       colocated BS has so much array gain that it can reach every
       user's QoS target with very little transmit power, even
       the cell-edge users. At MN = 128, by contrast, the
       colocated BS struggles with cell-edge users (not enough
       diversity), while the distributed CF array has an AP
       near every user and delivers the QoS easily with minimal
       power. The distance-based macro-diversity gain of CF is
       inversely proportional to how much array gain you have.

  (iv) The cell-free curve is ALSO more "resilient": its peak is
       broader and its drop-off at high target rates is gentler.
       This is again the diversity effect: even when the target
       becomes aggressive, the CF array has nearby APs that can
       still deliver.

CONCLUSION FROM FIG. 6: "This shows the strong potential of
cell-free massive MIMO to boost the energy and spectral
efficiency of 5G networks." The paper ends Section VI on this
note, which — five years later — is borne out by every subsequent
CF-mMIMO deployment study.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4. HISTORICAL / PRIOR DEVELOPMENTS — the simulation methodology
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The simulation methodology used in Section VI is a fairly standard
recipe in the cell-free literature circa 2017, but worth unpacking:

▸ **Wrap-around torus simulation** was introduced by Marzetta for
  massive-MIMO studies in his 2010 TWC paper and has been the
  default way to simulate "infinite planar deployments" in a
  finite computer since. Without it, edge APs would have fewer
  interferers and bias the averages.

▸ **Three-slope path-loss model** [24] is Tang-Sun-Gong 2001, a
  VTC Spring paper that measured propagation in NLOS urban streets
  with LOW base-station antennas. It was adopted in Ngo-2017 TWC
  [6] as the canonical CF-mMIMO propagation model, and this paper
  inherits it. Newer CF papers sometimes use the 3GPP TR 38.901
  urban-micro (UMi) model instead, which is more accurate but more
  complex.

▸ **σ_sh = 8 dB** log-normal shadowing is the cellular-industry
  default for outdoor propagation (3GPP TS 25.814 and many others).

▸ **τ_p = 20 or 40** reflects typical massive-MIMO coherence-
  interval budgets. For 3 km/h pedestrian users at 1.9 GHz with
  a 20 MHz bandwidth, the coherence interval is about τ_c = 200
  samples, so τ_p up to 40 leaves 80% of the block for payload.

▸ **Power-consumption parameters from Table I** are calibrated to
  the EARTH project's small-cell measurements (Auer-2011) and
  refined by Björnson-2015 and Zuo-2016. These parameter values
  became the standard "honeynut" numbers used in virtually every
  EE paper in CF-mMIMO from 2017 to 2020.

▸ **Benchmark against branch-and-reduce-and-bound (BRB)** for
  small problems [25] is a clever way to check that SCA is
  actually finding the global optimum (at least on small
  instances). Tervo-Tran-Juntti 2015 was the source of the BRB
  code.

▸ **SOCP solver: Gurobi or MOSEK** — commercial interior-point
  solvers with free academic licenses. MOSEK is more widely used
  in the CF-mMIMO community; Ngo et al.'s public code at
  github.com/tranlenam/cellfreeMIMOenergyefficiency uses MOSEK.

One thing Section VI DOES NOT include: statistical confidence
intervals (error bars). The EE values are averages over large-
scale fading realizations but without reported standard deviations.
This is typical for CF-mMIMO papers of the era.

5. MATHEMATICAL RECAP IN UNICODE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Deployment and channel model:

  (a) Geometry: APs and users uniformly at random in D × D km²,
      wrap-around boundary.

  (b) Large-scale fading (Eq. 46):
        β_{m,k} = PL_{m,k} · z_{m,k}
      where PL_{m,k} is three-slope path loss and
        z_{m,k} = 10^(σ_sh · n_{m,k} / 10),  n_{m,k} ~ 𝒩(0,1).

  (c) Three-slope path loss (Eq. 47):

                    ⎧ −L − 35 · log₁₀(d_{m,k}),            d_{m,k} > d_1
         PL_{m,k} = ⎨ −L − 15 · log₁₀(d_1) − 20·log₁₀(d_{m,k}), d_0 < d_{m,k} ≤ d_1
                    ⎩ −L − 15 · log₁₀(d_1) − 20·log₁₀(d_0),  d_{m,k} ≤ d_0

      Defaults: L = 140.7 dB,  σ_sh = 8 dB,  d_0 = 10 m,  d_1 = 50 m.

  (d) Pilots: random assignment from a pool of τ_p orthogonal
      length-τ_p pilot sequences. With K > τ_p, collisions occur
      and pilot contamination is present.

  (e) Thermal noise: N_0 ≈ kT B × 10^(NF/10) ≈ 6.4 × 10^{-14} W.

Power-consumption parameters (Table I):
   ν_m       = 0.4
   P_{tc,m}  = 0.2  W/antenna
   P_{0,m}   = 0.825 W/AP
   P_{bt,m}  = 0.25 W / (Gbit/s) = 2.5 × 10^{-10} W/(bit/s)
   B         = 20 MHz
   NF        = 9 dB
   τ_c       = 200 samples
   ρ_p       = 0.2  (normalized UL pilot SNR)
   ρ_d       = 1    (normalized DL SNR)

SCA termination:  N_I = 10,  ε_tol = 0.01.
AP-selection threshold:  ρ = 95%.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. NUMERICAL WORKED MINI-EXAMPLE (reproducing the qualitative trend
   of Fig. 5)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Let us attempt a back-of-envelope reproduction of the Fig. 5 trend
for a simplified scenario.

Fix:  MN = 256 total service antennas, K = 40, D = 1 km,
      P_{bt} = 0.25, S_{o,k} = 1 bit/s/Hz, Table I parameters.

We vary N and computed an ESTIMATED total power P_total (assuming
PA fully loaded at ρ_d · N_0 = 200 mW, the effective real-world
transmit power of the whole system — i.e., each AP contributes
200 mW/M when fully loaded, for consistency) and assume a constant
sum SE of S_e = 40 bit/s/Hz.

Step 1 — Sum throughput:
   B · S_e = (2 × 10^7) · 40 = 8 × 10^8 bit/s = 800 Mbit/s.

Step 2 — P̄_fix per configuration:
   N = 1, M = 256:  P̄_fix = Σ_m (N·P_tc + P_0)
                         = 256 · (0.2 + 0.825)
                         = 256 · 1.025
                         = 262.4 W
   N = 2, M = 128:  P̄_fix = 128 · (0.4 + 0.825) = 128 · 1.225 = 156.8 W
   N = 4, M = 64:   P̄_fix = 64 · (0.8 + 0.825) = 64 · 1.625 = 104.0 W
   N = 8, M = 32:   P̄_fix = 32 · (1.6 + 0.825) = 32 · 2.425 = 77.6 W
   N = 16, M = 16:  P̄_fix = 16 · (3.2 + 0.825) = 16 · 4.025 = 64.4 W
   N = 32, M = 8:   P̄_fix = 8 · (6.4 + 0.825) = 8 · 7.225 = 57.8 W
   N = 64, M = 4:   P̄_fix = 4 · (12.8 + 0.825) = 4 · 13.625 = 54.5 W
   N = 128, M = 2:  P̄_fix = 2 · (25.6 + 0.825) = 2 · 26.425 = 52.85 W

   OBSERVATION: P̄_fix DECREASES STEEPLY as N grows, because the
   per-antenna circuit (N·P_tc) grows linearly with N but the
   fixed backhaul (P_0) only grows with M = 256/N. The dominant
   effect is the shrinking M and hence shrinking Σ_m P_0.

Step 3 — Traffic backhaul:
   Σ_m P_{bt,m} · B·S_e = M · 0.25·10⁻⁹ · 8·10⁸ = M · 0.2 W
   N = 1:  M = 256 → traffic BH = 51.2 W
   N = 2:  M = 128 → 25.6 W
   N = 4:  M = 64 → 12.8 W
   N = 16: M = 16 → 3.2 W
   N = 64: M = 4 → 0.8 W
   N = 128: M = 2 → 0.4 W

Step 4 — PA power (assume total RF = 200 mW at full load):
   P_PA,total = 0.2 W / ν = 0.2 / 0.4 = 0.5 W     (approximately
                                                    independent of N)

Step 5 — Total power and EE:
   N = 1:   P_total ≈ 262.4 + 51.2 + 0.5 ≈ 314 W   → E_e ≈ 8·10⁸/314  ≈ 2.55 Mbit/J
   N = 2:   P_total ≈ 156.8 + 25.6 + 0.5 ≈ 183 W   → E_e ≈ 4.37 Mbit/J
   N = 4:   P_total ≈ 104   + 12.8 + 0.5 ≈ 117 W   → E_e ≈ 6.82 Mbit/J
   N = 8:   P_total ≈ 77.6  + 6.4  + 0.5 ≈ 84.5 W  → E_e ≈ 9.47 Mbit/J
   N = 16:  P_total ≈ 64.4  + 3.2  + 0.5 ≈ 68.1 W  → E_e ≈ 11.7 Mbit/J
   N = 32:  P_total ≈ 57.8  + 1.6  + 0.5 ≈ 59.9 W  → E_e ≈ 13.4 Mbit/J
   N = 64:  P_total ≈ 54.5  + 0.8  + 0.5 ≈ 55.8 W  → E_e ≈ 14.3 Mbit/J
   N = 128: P_total ≈ 52.85 + 0.4  + 0.5 ≈ 53.7 W  → E_e ≈ 14.9 Mbit/J

Wait — in this BACK-OF-ENVELOPE calculation E_e is MONOTONIC
INCREASING in N, with no inverted-U maximum. Why doesn't this
reproduce Fig. 5?

The answer is: the calculation ASSUMED a CONSTANT sum SE of 40
bit/s/Hz regardless of N. But in reality, as N grows and M shrinks,
the MACRO-DIVERSITY of the deployment drops (users get further
from the nearest AP on average), and the sum SE drops too. In
particular, at very large N / small M, some users may become
INFEASIBLE (their SE target cannot be met), and then EE collapses
because we set it to zero for infeasible users.

To get the inverted-U, one would need to also model how S_e decays
with decreasing M. Roughly, one can argue that S_e ∝ log₂(average
β × N) where average β depends on M (more APs → on average a
closer AP per user → higher β_max). A rigorous model is beyond a
back-of-envelope calculation, but the paper's simulations capture
it exactly via Algorithm 1.

THE LESSON of this mini-example: the N* trade-off in Fig. 5 is
NOT primarily about power — it is a SPECTRAL-EFFICIENCY effect.
At large N / small M, the CF array loses the "AP near every user"
benefit and SE crashes, driving EE down. The power savings from
shrinking M are overwhelmed by the SE loss. The cross-over is
the point where these two effects are balanced.

PRACTICE PROBLEM:

(a) Repeat Steps 2–5 with P_{bt} = 1 W/(Gbit/s) (4× more expensive
    backhaul than the default). Show that the shape of P̄_fix does
    not change but traffic BH scales 4× (so N = 1 has 204.8 W, etc).
    What does that do to E_e as N varies?

(b) Argue qualitatively how the SE term S_e(N) would need to
    evolve with N to produce the Fig. 5 inverted-U pattern.
    (Hint: think about the log-linearity of log₂(1 + array_gain)
    and the concavity of the resulting "useful rate vs M" curve.)

(c) Explain why — for the second scenario (D = 2 km, P_bt = 0.5,
    S_o = 1) — Fig. 5 has its peak at LARGER N than scenario 1
    (D = 1 km, P_bt = 0.25, S_o = 1). Hint: D = 2 km reduces the
    benefit of dense distribution and P_bt = 0.5 doubles the
    penalty of many APs.

7. SUMMARY OF SECTION VI
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Setup. Square D × D km² deployment with wrap-around. APs and
  users placed uniformly at random. Three-slope path loss (L =
  140.7 dB, d_0 = 10 m, d_1 = 50 m) × log-normal shadowing (σ_sh
  = 8 dB). Random pilot assignment. Bandwidth B = 20 MHz, NF =
  9 dB, τ_c = 200, ν = 0.4, P_tc = 0.2 W, P_0 = 0.825 W, P_bt =
  0.25 W/(Gbit/s). Defaults from refs [11], [14] (Björnson-2015,
  Zuo-2016).

• Fig. 1. Convergence of Algorithm 1. Monotone increase,
  plateau by ~10 iterations, matches BRB global optimum on
  tiny (M = 8, K = 2) instance. → It is safe to fix N_I = 10.

• Fig. 2. Power control vs no power control. Algorithm 1 improves
  EE by a factor of ~2.6–2.9× at M = 100 for K = 20 and 40.
  Huge "free win." All later figures use power control.

• Fig. 3. Impact of AP selection. Baseline EE crashes with
  increasing P_bt; AP selection keeps EE nearly FLAT across
  P_bt ∈ [0.25, 2]. Received-power-based slightly > LSF-based
  but gap small (~5–10%). AP selection is essential at expensive
  backhaul.

• Fig. 4. Number of selected APs per user. Roughly CONSTANT in
  M (~10–20 APs) for D = 1 km, ~30 APs for D = 2 km.
  Sub-linear scaling → SCALABILITY. "Only about 10–20% of the
  total number of APs really participate in serving a given
  user." The birth of user-centric CF-mMIMO.

• Fig. 5. Effect of N. EE is non-monotone in N: inverted-U
  with a peak at N* that depends on (D, P_bt, S_o):
  - dense + cheap BH + low QoS   → small N* (~4), many APs
  - sparse + expensive BH + high QoS → large N* (~32), few APs
  Design rule: tune N based on deployment.

• Fig. 6. CF vs colocated massive MIMO. CF-mMIMO is uniformly
  more EE than colocated for both MN = 128 and MN = 256.
  Gains:  7.4× at MN = 128,  2.2× at MN = 256.
  The gap shrinks with MN because colocated gets enough array
  gain at large antenna counts. At moderate MN, macro-diversity
  of CF-mMIMO wins decisively.

• Practical impact. Section VI is the "proof in the pudding":
  it validates every claim of Sections II–V on realistic
  deployments. The combination of (Algorithm 1 power control)
  + (Algorithm 3 AP selection) + (well-chosen N) turns CF-mMIMO
  from a theoretical curiosity into a practical green-wireless
  technology.

• Headline. Under 1 bit/s/Hz per-user QoS, CF-mMIMO beats
  colocated massive MIMO in energy efficiency by an order of
  magnitude. This is the abstract's punchline — and the reason
  the paper is so widely cited.
