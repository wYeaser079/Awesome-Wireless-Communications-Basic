───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. HIGH-LEVEL (LAYMAN) EXPLANATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The paper is essentially done by the end of Section VI. Section VII
is a one-paragraph summary of what was shown and what it means.
Appendix A then backs up the central closed-form result
(Proposition 1, Eq. 15) with a careful computation of the three
statistical moments that define the "use-and-forget" capacity bound:

   •  DS_k        = expected desired-signal gain at user k
   •  E[|BU_k|²]  = variance of the beamforming-uncertainty term
   •  E[|UI_{kk'}|²] = variance of the inter-user interference
                       caused by user k' upon user k

Plugging these three into the Shannon-style SE expression (Eq. 11),
you land squarely at the closed-form expression (Eq. 15) — the
cornerstone of the entire paper, because every optimization in
Sections IV and V treats (15) as a deterministic function of the
power-control coefficients.

The mathematical tools used in Appendix A are ENTIRELY ELEMENTARY
— independence of MMSE estimate and estimation error, fourth
moments of complex Gaussian vectors, and the identity that makes
matched-filter combining look coherent. But the bookkeeping is
tricky, and several cancellations produce the simple final
formula. The appendix is about half a page of algebra for a
lifetime of optimization work.

In layman terms, Appendix A answers:

   "I said in Section II that the per-user spectral efficiency has
    a clean closed form. How do I actually PROVE that, starting
    from the channel model?"

And Section VII answers:

   "Now that we have seen everything work, what's the practical
    bottom line for building cell-free massive MIMO networks?"


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. SECTION VII — CONCLUSION (full content and elaboration)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─── 2.1  Literal content of the conclusion ─────────────────────────────

The paper's conclusion (one paragraph, Eq. 1–6 of Sec VII) states
essentially the following claims:

   (a) We investigated the total energy efficiency of a cell-free
       massive MIMO system WITH MULTIPLE-ANTENNA APs, taking into
       account four realistic effects:
         (i) channel-estimation errors,
         (ii) power control optimized for EE,
         (iii) non-orthogonality of pilot sequences (pilot
              contamination),
         (iv) backhaul power consumption.

   (b) We proposed TWO AP-selection schemes (received-power-based
       and largest-large-scale-fading-based) to reduce the backhaul
       power consumption, which is the dominant term at large M.

   (c) We showed that our power-allocation algorithm together with
       the AP-selection schemes can improve EE significantly.

   (d) For a given user, only a small number of APs really
       participate in serving it — on the order of 10-20% of M.

   (e) With a 1 bit/s/Hz per-user QoS requirement, cell-free
       massive MIMO can improve EE by AN ORDER OF MAGNITUDE
       compared to colocated massive MIMO.

─── 2.2  What each claim really says (the subtext) ─────────────────────

Let us elaborate each of these five claims to extract the deeper
implication that the paper is making.

(a) MULTIPLE ANTENNAS PER AP ARE A FIRST-CLASS CITIZEN.

    Before this paper, the CF-mMIMO literature (Ngo 2017 TWC,
    Nayebi 2015, Interdonato 2016) treated APs as having only a
    SINGLE antenna. The present paper is the first to derive an
    EXACT closed-form SE for the multi-antenna case. Multi-antenna
    APs matter because:
       •  they amortize the per-AP fixed backhaul across more
          antennas, reducing the fixed-cost-per-antenna (which
          is what the EE optimizer cares about);
       •  they deliver a coherent N² array gain within each AP,
          which has no analog in the single-antenna case;
       •  they provide a degree of freedom — N — that the network
          designer can tune to balance macro-diversity against
          backhaul cost (as shown in Fig. 5 of Section VI).
    A single AP with multiple antennas is the RIGHT abstraction
    layer between a true distributed antenna system (one antenna
    per AP) and a colocated massive-MIMO base station (one AP
    with many antennas).

(b) BACKHAUL IS THE ELEPHANT AND AP SELECTION IS THE CURE.

    Without the AP-selection schemes of Sec V, the backhaul term
    would crush EE at large M. Sections III and V framed this
    observation mathematically; Section VI quantified it (Fig. 3:
    EE with no selection collapses at high P_bt; EE with selection
    is nearly constant). The conclusion now frames it as a DESIGN
    PRINCIPLE: any serious CF-mMIMO deployment must include an
    AP-selection layer, otherwise backhaul will eat the benefits.

    Importantly, the authors propose TWO schemes of different
    complexity, allowing the system designer to choose between
    them. This is good practice for a paper aimed at practitioners.

(c) POWER CONTROL IS "FREE."

    The EE-maximizing power control of Algorithm 1 requires NO
    hardware changes — only a software update that runs once per
    large-scale-fading realization (~1 s). Yet it improves EE by a
    factor of 2.6-3×. In any deployment-optimization meeting, this
    is the single most persuasive argument for turning on
    Algorithm 1 by default.

(d) "ONLY A SMALL NUMBER OF APs PARTICIPATE" = SCALABILITY.

    This is the claim that launched the user-centric CF-mMIMO
    paradigm. In a subsequent literature (Buzzi-D'Andrea 2017,
    Björnson-Sanguinetti 2020, Interdonato 2019), this observation
    became formalized as a SCALABILITY property: a CF-mMIMO
    deployment is "scalable" if the per-user computational,
    backhaul, and CSI overhead does not grow with the TOTAL
    system size, only with the LOCAL cluster size. The present
    paper is one of the first to empirically document this
    scalability via the constant |A_k| ~ 10-20 APs regardless of
    M (Fig. 4). The 2020 Björnson-Sanguinetti paper you have
    already studied formalizes scalability as an exact
    mathematical property.

(e) ORDER-OF-MAGNITUDE EE IMPROVEMENT OVER COLOCATED MASSIVE MIMO.

    This is the marketing-friendly headline: "cell-free is 10×
    more green than 4G+ massive MIMO, under a 1 bit/s/Hz QoS
    requirement, at MN = 128 service antennas." The claim
    dissolves somewhat at larger MN (2.2× at MN = 256) because
    colocated eventually gets enough array gain, but at moderate
    antenna counts — the regime of interest for early 6G
    deployments — CF-mMIMO wins decisively. This is THE reason
    the paper is widely cited.

─── 2.3  What Section VII does NOT discuss (limitations) ───────────────

The conclusion is intentionally brief, and several important
limitations of the paper are NOT flagged. A PhD student should
be aware of them:

  •  Uplink not analyzed. The whole paper is downlink-only. The
     uplink has its own power-consumption model (cheaper PAs on
     the user side, but more ADCs at the AP side) and its own
     SE formula. A follow-up paper (Bashar-Cumanan-Burr-Ngo 2017)
     extends the EE analysis to the uplink.

  •  No CSI-sharing cost. The paper assumes each AP estimates its
     own channels locally and uses them for conjugate beamforming
     — "distributed" processing. If instead the CPU were to
     perform centralized precoding (e.g. MMSE), CSI would have
     to be transmitted over the backhaul, adding a CSI-overhead
     term to P_bh,m that the paper does not model. The 2019
     Björnson-Sanguinetti MMSE paper (which you have studied)
     addresses exactly this centralized-processing regime.

  •  Fronthaul vs backhaul distinction ignored. The paper calls
     the link between AP and CPU a "backhaul", but in C-RAN
     terminology it is more accurately a "fronthaul" (carrying
     baseband or quantized baseband data). The difference
     matters for the traffic-dependent term — a fronthaul
     carrying CPRI-style baseband samples is MUCH more expensive
     per bit/s than a backhaul carrying user data. The paper's
     P_bt,m is an abstraction that can absorb either case,
     but the numerical values of Table I correspond closer to
     a backhaul than a fronthaul.

  •  Only conjugate beamforming. The paper does NOT consider
     zero-forcing (ZF) or MMSE precoding, which would give
     higher SE at the cost of more centralized processing. The
     choice is conservative — conjugate gives the lowest bound
     on SE, so the EE numbers are a lower bound as well. ZF
     cell-free was analyzed in Nayebi 2015 [8]; MMSE cell-free
     was analyzed in Björnson-Sanguinetti 2019.

  •  Pilot contamination is only partially analyzed. The paper
     uses random pilot assignment; more sophisticated schemes
     (graph-coloring, location-aware) would reduce pilot
     contamination and boost EE. This was an open question
     in 2017 and has been addressed in several follow-up papers.

  •  No user mobility. The large-scale fading is treated as
     static during each run. Real users move, and the sets A_k
     must be re-computed over time; the dynamics are not
     analyzed. This is the motivation for the ML-based AP
     selection papers of 2022-2024.

Knowing these limitations is how you can plan a follow-up paper of
your own — they are the natural entry points for subsequent
research.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3. APPENDIX A — PROOF OF PROPOSITION 1
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─── 3.1  What we need to compute ────────────────────────────────────────

Recall the use-and-forget SE bound (Eq. 11):

                          |DS_k|²
  SE_k = (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂(1 + ─────────────────────────────────)
                         E{|BU_k|²} + Σ_{k'≠k} E{|UI_{kk'}|²} + 1

with

   DS_k      ≝ √ρ_d · E{ Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* }           (12)

   BU_k      ≝ √ρ_d · ( Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*
                       − E{Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*} )       (13)

   UI_{kk'}  ≝ √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k'}^*              (14)

We will compute |DS_k|², E[|BU_k|²], and E[|UI_{kk'}|²] in that
order, then substitute into (11) to obtain (15).

Throughout the proof we use the following facts:

  F1. MMSE orthogonality: ĝ_{m,k} and 𝐠̃_{m,k} ≝ 𝐠_{m,k} − ĝ_{m,k}
      are STATISTICALLY INDEPENDENT, not just uncorrelated. This
      is because jointly Gaussian + uncorrelated implies
      independent.

  F2. ĝ_{m,k} has entries 𝒞𝒩(0, γ_{m,k}), and 𝐠̃_{m,k} has entries
      𝒞𝒩(0, β_{m,k} − γ_{m,k}). All entries independent within
      each vector.

  F3. For any m ≠ m' and any k, k': 𝐠_{m,k}, ĝ_{m,k}, 𝐠_{m',k'},
      ĝ_{m',k'} involve different AP indices and hence different
      pilot noise realizations. In particular the channels across
      different APs are independent (because the underlying
      physical channels are modelled as independent across AP
      locations).

  F4. Cross AP-index terms have zero mean once you take the
      expectation E{·}, so they VANISH when you compute the second
      moment of a sum across APs.

  F5. Complex Gaussian fourth moments: if 𝐱 ~ 𝒞𝒩(𝟎, σ² 𝐈_N), then
         E{‖𝐱‖²}  = N σ²
         E{‖𝐱‖^4} = N(N+1) σ^4
      (This is a special case of the Wishart/Gamma fourth-moment
      identity.)

  F6. The identity ĝ_{m,k} = b_{m,k} · y_{p,m,k}, where

         b_{m,k}  ≝  √(τ_p ρ_p) · β_{m,k}
                   ─────────────────────────────────────────────── (52)
                   τ_p ρ_p Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|² + 1

      and y_{p,m,k} is the despread received pilot from Eq. (3).

─── 3.2  Computing DS_k  (Eq. 48 of the paper) ────────────────────────

Because ĝ_{m,k} ⊥ 𝐠̃_{m,k} (fact F1), and because 𝐠_{m,k} =
ĝ_{m,k} + 𝐠̃_{m,k}, we have

  DS_k = √ρ_d · E{ Σ_m √η_{m,k} (ĝ_{m,k} + 𝐠̃_{m,k})^T ĝ_{m,k}^* }
       = √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k} · E{ ĝ_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* }
                                     + E{ 𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* }
       = √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k} · (E{ ‖ĝ_{m,k}‖² } + 0)
       = √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k} · N γ_{m,k}
       = √ρ_d · N · Σ_m √η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}.                                    (48)

Two observations:

  (i)  The cross term E{𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*} vanishes because 𝐠̃ is
       independent of ĝ and has zero mean. This is the clean
       consequence of orthogonality + Gaussianity.

  (ii) The AP index m appears inside √η_{m,k} and γ_{m,k} only;
       the N comes out as a factor. So |DS_k|² scales like:

         |DS_k|² = ρ_d · N² · (Σ_m √η_{m,k} γ_{m,k})²

       where the sum inside the parenthesis is exactly
       𝛄_{k,k}^T · [√η_{1,k}, …, √η_{M,k}]^T, which is the paper's
       compact form 𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k when the symbol η_k is taken to
       mean the vector of SQUARE ROOTS. This is the notational
       subtlety I flagged back in Section II.

─── 3.3  Computing E[|BU_k|²]  (Eqs. 49–50 of the paper) ─────────────

From (13),

  BU_k = √ρ_d · ( Σ_m √η_{m,k} (X_{m} − E{X_{m}}) )

where X_m ≝ 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*. Then

  E{|BU_k|²}
   = ρ_d · E{ |Σ_m √η_{m,k} (X_m − E{X_m})|² }

Expand the magnitude squared:
   = ρ_d · Σ_m Σ_{m'} √η_{m,k} √η_{m',k} · E{(X_m − E{X_m})·(X_{m'} − E{X_{m'}})^*}

The expectation is zero UNLESS m = m', because X_m and X_{m'}
involve different APs with independent channels (fact F3). So all
m ≠ m' terms vanish:

   = ρ_d · Σ_m η_{m,k} · Var(X_m).                                           (49)

(The paper writes this as Eq. (49).)

We now compute Var(X_m) = E{|X_m|²} − |E{X_m}|². Use X_m =
(ĝ_{m,k} + 𝐠̃_{m,k})^T ĝ_{m,k}^* = ĝ_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* +
𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*:

  E{X_m}   = E{‖ĝ_{m,k}‖²} + 0 = N γ_{m,k}

  |X_m|²  = |‖ĝ_{m,k}‖²|² + |𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*|²
            + 2 Re( ‖ĝ_{m,k}‖² · (𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*)^* )
          = ‖ĝ_{m,k}‖^4 + |𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*|² + 2 Re( ‖ĝ‖² 𝐠̃^T ĝ* )

Take expectations. Since ĝ and 𝐠̃ are independent and 𝐠̃ has zero
mean, the cross term 2 Re(‖ĝ‖² E[𝐠̃^T ĝ*]) = 0. Hence

  E{|X_m|²} = E{‖ĝ_{m,k}‖^4} + E{|𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*|²}.

Compute each:

  (a) E{‖ĝ_{m,k}‖^4} = N(N+1) γ_{m,k}^2  (Gaussian fourth moment, F5)

  (b) E{|𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*|²}
      = Σ_n E{ |[𝐠̃_{m,k}]_n|² · |[ĝ_{m,k}]_n|² }
        (components n ≠ n' vanish by independence across entries)
      = Σ_n ( E{|[𝐠̃_{m,k}]_n|²} ) · ( E{|[ĝ_{m,k}]_n|²} )
        (because 𝐠̃ and ĝ are independent random vectors)
      = N · (β_{m,k} − γ_{m,k}) · γ_{m,k}
      = N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k} − N γ_{m,k}^2.

So

  E{|X_m|²} = N(N+1) γ_{m,k}^2 + N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k} − N γ_{m,k}^2
            = N² γ_{m,k}^2 + N γ_{m,k}^2 + N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k} − N γ_{m,k}^2
            = N² γ_{m,k}^2 + N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}.

And

  Var(X_m) = E{|X_m|²} − |E{X_m}|²
           = N² γ_{m,k}^2 + N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k} − N² γ_{m,k}^2
           = N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}.

A clean result! The N² γ² from the fourth moment cancels exactly
with the square of the mean N² γ², leaving only the N β γ
contribution.

Substituting back into (49):

  E{|BU_k|²} = ρ_d N · Σ_m η_{m,k} β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}.                          (50)

This is Eq. (50) of the paper. Intuitively, E{|BU_k|²} is
proportional to N (not N²), because the variance of a sum of N IID
complex Gaussians grows linearly with N. This is the reason the BU
term is "washed out" by channel hardening as N grows large — the
desired signal grows as N², the uncertainty only as N.

─── 3.4  Computing E[|UI_{kk'}|²]  (Eqs. 51–55 of the paper) ──────────

This is the most involved of the three computations because of
pilot contamination. Let us unfold it carefully.

From (14) and the identity ĝ_{m,k'} = b_{m,k'} · y_{p,m,k'}
(fact F6), we can write

  UI_{kk'}  =  √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k'} · b_{m,k'} · 𝐠_{m,k}^T · y_{p,m,k'}^*

Now substitute the expansion of y_{p,m,k'} from Eq. (3) (with k
replaced by k'):

  y_{p,m,k'}  =  √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k'}
               + √(τ_p ρ_p) Σ_{k''≠k'} 𝐠_{m,k''} (𝛗_{k''}^H 𝛗_{k'})
               + 𝐰̃_{p,m,k'}.

Taking the conjugate transpose (element-wise) and multiplying by
𝐠_{m,k}^T:

  𝐠_{m,k}^T · y_{p,m,k'}^*
   = √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐠_{m,k'}^*
     + √(τ_p ρ_p) Σ_{k''≠k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐠_{m,k''}^* (𝛗_{k''}^H 𝛗_{k'})^*
     + 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐰̃_{p,m,k'}^*.

Two cases for the middle sum:

  •  k'' = k:  the coefficient is √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐠_{m,k}^*
             = √(τ_p ρ_p) ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² · (𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'})^*
    (PILOT CONTAMINATION CONTRIBUTION — survives expectation)
  •  k'' ≠ k, k'': the coefficient is √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐠_{m,k''}^*
    (non-contaminating — zero mean, behaves like noise)

Note that the first term √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐠_{m,k'}^* is itself
also zero mean (since k ≠ k' by definition of UI_{kk'}), so it
behaves like noise too.

So we can split:

  𝐠_{m,k}^T y_{p,m,k'}^*
   = √(τ_p ρ_p) ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² · (𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'})^*        [pilot contamination]
     + "other terms with zero mean"                   [non-contaminating]

And accordingly:

  UI_{kk'}
   = √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k'} · b_{m,k'}
     · [ √(τ_p ρ_p) ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² (𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'})^*
         + other zero-mean terms ].

Taking |·|² and expectation:

  E{|UI_{kk'}|²}
   = ρ_d · τ_p ρ_p · E{ |Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² (𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'})^*|² }
   + ρ_d · τ_p ρ_p · [other-terms contribution]
   + ρ_d · [noise contribution]                                             (51)

The paper writes this as the three terms in Eq. (51) of the paper:

   E{|UI_k|²}
    = ρ_d · τ_p ρ_p · |𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'}|² · E{ |Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖²|² }
    + ρ_d · τ_p ρ_p · E{ |Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T (other sum)|² }
    + ρ_d · E{ |Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐰̃_{p,m,k'}^*|² }.

Let's compute each piece.

(i) The pilot-contamination term (first line of Eq. 51):

    We need E{|Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖²|²}.

    Expand as Σ_m Σ_{m'} √η_{m,k'}√η_{m',k'} b_{m,k'} b_{m',k'}
    E{‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² ‖𝐠_{m',k}‖²}.

    The diagonal (m = m') terms contribute:
       E{‖𝐠_{m,k}‖^4} = N(N+1) β_{m,k}^2.

    The off-diagonal (m ≠ m') terms:
       E{‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² ‖𝐠_{m',k}‖²} = E{‖𝐠_{m,k}‖²} · E{‖𝐠_{m',k}‖²}
                                  = N β_{m,k} · N β_{m',k}
                                  = N² β_{m,k} β_{m',k}
       (independent channels across APs, fact F3).

    Summing:
       diag:  Σ_m η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'}^2 · N(N+1) β_{m,k}^2
       off-diag: Σ_m Σ_{m'≠m} √(η_{m,k'} η_{m',k'}) b_{m,k'} b_{m',k'}
                   · N² β_{m,k} β_{m',k}

    Together (using the identity Σ_m Σ_{m'} = diag + off-diag):
       = N² (Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} β_{m,k})²
         + N Σ_m η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'}^2 β_{m,k}^2.

    (The N(N+1) − N² = N extra term gives the residual.)

    Multiplying by the factor ρ_d · τ_p ρ_p · |𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'}|²:
       (pilot-contamination contribution)
       =  ρ_d τ_p ρ_p |𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'}|² · N² · (Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} β_{m,k})²
        + ρ_d τ_p ρ_p |𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'}|² · N · Σ_m η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'}^2 β_{m,k}^2

    Substituting b_{m,k'} from (52) and using γ_{m,k'} = √(τ_p ρ_p)·β_{m,k'}·b_{m,k'}
    (from Eq. 5), one can verify that the first term simplifies to:

       ρ_d N² · (Σ_m √η_{m,k'} · γ_{m,k'} β_{m,k}/β_{m,k'})^2 · |𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'}|²

    which matches the first term in the pilot contamination
    quadratic form of Eq. (15), after noting that

       [𝛄_{k,k'}]_m = |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k| · γ_{m,k'} · √(β_{m,k}/β_{m,k'}).

    So the pilot-contamination term in the SINR denominator is
    captured.

(ii) The non-contaminating cross-term (second line of Eq. 51):

    This is the contribution from the sum over k'' ≠ k', k of
    𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐠_{m,k''}^*. Each such term has zero mean and variance
    that depends on the product of ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² and ‖𝐠_{m,k''}‖².
    The paper computes it explicitly using the formula (Eq. 54)

       E{|Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T Σ_{k''≠k'} 𝐠_{m,k''}^* · ...|²}
        = N · Σ_m Σ_{k''≠k'} η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'}^2 β_{m,k} β_{m,k''}

    which after multiplication by ρ_d τ_p ρ_p becomes

       ρ_d τ_p ρ_p · N · Σ_m η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'}^2 β_{m,k}
                        · (Σ_{k''≠k'} β_{m,k''})

(iii) The noise cross-term (third line of Eq. 51):

     E{|Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐰̃_{p,m,k'}^*|²}

     Using the independence of 𝐰̃ (white) and 𝐠, this simplifies
     to
         Σ_m η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'}^2 · N β_{m,k}.

     Multiplying by ρ_d gives
         ρ_d N · Σ_m η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'}^2 β_{m,k}.

(iv) Combining (i), (ii), (iii), (iv) and grouping terms by powers
    of N, one ends up with the clean form

     E{|UI_{kk'}|²}
       = ρ_d N² · |𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'}|² · (Σ_m √η_{m,k'} γ_{m,k'} √(β_{m,k}/β_{m,k'}))^2
         + ρ_d N · Σ_m η_{m,k'} γ_{m,k'} β_{m,k}                                (55)

    which is Eq. (55) of the paper — precisely the form needed to
    produce (15).

    Observe the two terms:
      •  N² term with |𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'}|² — the pilot-contamination
         interference, which DOES NOT VANISH with more antennas
         and is zero only if the pilots of users k and k' are
         orthogonal. This becomes Σ_{k'≠k}|𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|² in (15).
      •  N term — the non-coherent interference, which washes out
         relative to the N² desired signal as N grows. This becomes
         Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖² in (15).

─── 3.5  Assembling Proposition 1 (the final step) ─────────────────────

Substituting (48), (50), and (55) into (11):

   |DS_k|² = ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|²

   E{|BU_k|²} = ρ_d N · Σ_m η_{m,k} β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
              = ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k,k} 𝛈_k‖²
                          (where [𝐃_{k,k}]_{m,m} = √(β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}))

   E{|UI_{kk'}|²} = ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²
                  + ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖²
                                 (for k' ≠ k)

Summing E{|BU_k|²} + Σ_{k'≠k} E{|UI_{kk'}|²} gives

   ρ_d N² · Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²
   + ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k,k} 𝛈_k‖²
   + ρ_d N · Σ_{k'≠k} ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖²

   = ρ_d N² · Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²
   + ρ_d N · Σ_{k'=1}^K ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖²

which absorbs the BU term into the full sum over k' (note that
the BU term is exactly the k' = k instance of the non-coherent
interference sum — they have the same form). Plugging into (11)
with the "+1" for thermal noise yields exactly (15):

  SE_k({η_{m,k}})
    = (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂( 1 +
                            ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|² /
         ( ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²
         + ρ_d N Σ_{k'=1}^K ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖² + 1) ).                      □

Q.E.D. The entire closed-form SE of Section II is now rigorously
derived.

─── 3.6  Why this is a beautiful proof ─────────────────────────────────

Despite its bookkeeping burden, the proof has several elegant
features:

  (i)  The DS_k computation is a one-liner by orthogonality of
       ĝ and 𝐠̃. The expected gain is just N·γ_{m,k} per AP,
       weighted by √η_{m,k}. This is the simplest part.

  (ii) The BU_k variance involves a surprising cancellation: the
       N² γ² from the fourth moment of ‖ĝ‖² cancels exactly with
       (E{‖ĝ‖²})² = N² γ², leaving only the linear-in-N residual
       N β γ. This is the reason channel hardening exists — the
       variance of the channel norm grows as O(N) while its mean
       grows as O(N), so the relative uncertainty |BU|/|DS| ~
       1/√N vanishes in the large-N limit.

  (iii) The UI_{kk'} calculation exposes pilot contamination
       cleanly: the N² term survives if and only if 𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'}
       ≠ 0, and its magnitude is proportional to the CORRELATED
       part of 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐠_{m,k'}^* — i.e., to ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² scaled
       by the pilot inner product. This is where "pilot contamination"
       as a phrase gets its precise mathematical meaning.

  (iv) All three contributions (DS_k, BU_k, UI_{kk'}) end up
       sharing the SAME matrices (𝛄_{k,k'} and 𝐃_{k',k}), which
       is why the final SE expression (15) has such a compact
       quadratic-form look. The γ_{m,k'} β_{m,k}/β_{m,k'} factor
       that appears inside 𝛄_{k,k'} is the "effective gain" of the
       contaminated component.

4. HISTORICAL / PRIOR DEVELOPMENTS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The proof technique used in Appendix A is entirely standard in
the massive-MIMO capacity literature. Key historical references:

▸ 1960 — Raiffa-Schlaifer and Kailath develop the algebra of
  MMSE estimation for jointly Gaussian vectors. The orthogonality
  principle (F1) dates from this era.

▸ 2000 — Médard, "The effect upon channel capacity in wireless
  communications of perfect and imperfect knowledge of the
  channel," IEEE TIT. The "use-and-then-forget" bound.

▸ 2010 — Marzetta, "Noncooperative cellular wireless with
  unlimited numbers of base station antennas," IEEE TWC. First
  application of the bound to massive MIMO; first formal
  identification of pilot contamination as the N²-scaling
  interference term.

▸ 2013 — Hoydis, ten Brink, Debbah, "Massive MIMO in the UL/DL
  of cellular networks: How many antennas do we need?" IEEE
  JSAC. Extends the use-and-forget analysis with deterministic-
  equivalent tools from random matrix theory, giving exact
  asymptotic expressions.

▸ 2016 — Marzetta-Larsson-Yang-Ngo, "Fundamentals of Massive
  MIMO," Cambridge Univ. Press (ref [7] in Ngo paper). Chapter 2
  contains the canonical derivation of the use-and-forget bound
  and its application to colocated massive MIMO. Appendix A of
  the Ngo paper essentially generalizes this to the distributed
  (cell-free) setting with multi-antenna APs.

▸ Mar 2017 — Ngo, Ashikhmin, Yang, Larsson, Marzetta, "Cell-free
  massive MIMO versus small cells," IEEE TWC (ref [6]). Proof
  of the N = 1 case of Appendix A.

▸ Feb 2017 — THIS paper. Generalizes to N ≥ 1, adding the
  multiplicative N² and N scaling of DS, BU, UI computations.

▸ 2019 — Björnson, Sanguinetti, "Making cell-free massive MIMO
  competitive with MMSE processing and centralized
  implementation," IEEE TWC. You have studied this paper. The
  appendix uses a closely-related proof for the MMSE decoder
  case.

The main technical "trick" that makes the proof work in cell-free
is the INDEPENDENCE OF CHANNELS ACROSS APs (fact F3). Without
this, the cross-AP terms would not vanish and the final expression
would be much messier. In real deployments, channels are not
perfectly independent — neighboring APs may see correlated
shadowing, for example. Post-2018 papers have explored relaxing
this assumption, with varying degrees of success.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5. MATHEMATICAL RECAP IN UNICODE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Three central moments:

  (i)  |DS_k|²  =  ρ_d · N² · (Σ_m √η_{m,k} γ_{m,k})²
              =  ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|²

       (where 𝛈_k is the vector of √η_{m,k} values)

  (ii) E{|BU_k|²}  =  ρ_d · N · Σ_m η_{m,k} β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
                  =  ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k,k} 𝛈_k‖²

  (iii) E{|UI_{kk'}|²}
       =  ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²   (pilot contamination)
        + ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖²   (non-coherent crossterm)

Definitions:

  𝛄_{k,k}    vector with entries γ_{m,k} for m = 1,…,M.

  𝛄_{k,k'}   vector with entries |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k| · γ_{m,k'}
             · √(β_{m,k}/β_{m,k'}) for m = 1,…,M.

  𝐃_{k',k}   diagonal matrix with diagonal [𝐃_{k',k}]_{m,m}
             = √(β_{m,k} γ_{m,k'}) for m = 1,…,M.

Final closed-form SE (plugging into (11)):

  SE_k = (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂(  1  +
         ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|² /
         ( ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²
         + ρ_d N Σ_{k'=1}^K ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖² + 1 )  )     (15)

Proof-technical identities used:

  E{‖𝐱‖²}  = N σ²        for 𝐱 ~ 𝒞𝒩(𝟎, σ² 𝐈_N)
  E{‖𝐱‖^4} = N(N+1) σ^4   (the Wishart/Gamma fourth-moment identity)
  Var(‖𝐱‖²) = N σ^4

  ĝ, 𝐠̃ jointly Gaussian + MMSE-orthogonal ⇒ independent.
  Channels across different APs are independent (deployment model).

─── 5.1  Sanity checks ────────────────────────────────────────────────

(A) Single-antenna APs (N = 1): the factor N² becomes 1 and N
    becomes 1, so (15) reduces to Eq. (24) of Ngo-2017-TWC [6] as
    claimed.

(B) Colocated massive MIMO (M = 1, rename N → M): the sums over
    m become single terms, and 𝐃, 𝛄 become scalars. The formula
    reduces to the colocated massive-MIMO SE of Marzetta-Larsson-
    Yang-Ngo textbook Table 3.2 [7].

(C) No pilot contamination (pilots fully orthogonal, |𝛗_k^H 𝛗_{k'}|
    = δ_{k,k'}): the Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|² term vanishes.
    Only the non-coherent interference Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖²
    remains.

(D) Noise-dominated limit (ρ_d → 0): SE → 0 linearly in ρ_d as
    expected.

(E) Interference-dominated limit (ρ_d → ∞, pilot contamination
    present): SE → (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂(1 + (|𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|² /
    Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²)), a FINITE constant. This is
    the famous "pilot contamination ceiling" first identified by
    Marzetta 2010.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. NUMERICAL WORKED EXAMPLE — SANITY CHECK OF THE PROOF
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Let us verify the BU-variance computation for a minimal case:
N = 2, β_{m,k} = 1, γ_{m,k} = 0.5 (i.e. half the channel power is
captured by the estimate, half goes into estimation error).

Step 1 — E{‖ĝ‖²} = N γ = 2 · 0.5 = 1.
Step 2 — E{‖ĝ‖^4}:
  Using N(N+1) γ² = 2·3·0.25 = 1.5.
Step 3 — Var(‖ĝ‖²) = 1.5 − 1² = 0.5.

Step 4 — E{|𝐠̃^T ĝ^*|²}:
  = N · (β − γ) · γ = 2 · 0.5 · 0.5 = 0.5.

Step 5 — Var(X_m) = Var(‖ĝ‖²) + E{|𝐠̃^T ĝ^*|²}
                 = 0.5 + 0.5 = 1.

Step 6 — Direct formula: N β γ = 2 · 1 · 0.5 = 1. ✓ Matches.

Now let us also verify the DS scaling. Same parameters:
|DS|² = ρ_d N² (Σ_m √η_{m,k} γ_{m,k})²

For M = 1 AP and η_{1,k} = 1, ρ_d = 1:
|DS|² = 1 · 4 · (√1 · 0.5)² = 4 · 0.25 = 1.
Directly from definition: DS = √ρ_d · √η · E{‖ĝ‖²} = 1 · 1 · N γ
                             = 1 · 2 · 0.5 = 1.
|DS|² = 1. ✓

The proof arithmetic checks out.

    the fact that |[𝐱]_n|² ~ Exp(1/σ²). Verify the result N σ^4.

(b) Show that for independent 𝒞𝒩(0, σ_a²) and 𝒞𝒩(0, σ_b²) scalars,
    E{|a b^*|²} = σ_a² σ_b². Extend this to vector inner products.

(c) Compute, step by step, the value of E{|UI_{kk'}|²} for a toy
    configuration: M = 2, N = 1, τ_p = 2, orthogonal pilots,
    β = 1, γ = 0.5, η = 1. You should get a clean number like
    the non-coherent part of (55) without the N² pilot-contamination
    piece.

(d) Re-derive the cancellation that causes the N² γ² term of
    E{‖ĝ‖^4} to disappear in Var(X_m). Explain in words why this
    cancellation is the mathematical reason channel hardening
    exists.

7. FULL-PAPER SYNTHESIS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Having walked through all seven sections and the appendix, let us
zoom out and describe the paper as a coherent intellectual
artifact.

─── 7.1  The intellectual arc of the paper ──────────────────────────────

   Section I    —  Why we care: cell-edge users are crushed in
                   conventional cellular networks; network MIMO
                   cooperation helps but is heavy; cell-free
                   massive MIMO is the scalable incarnation of
                   network MIMO; BUT its backhaul-power cost has
                   not been honestly modeled. We will fill that
                   gap and do the EE analysis.

   Section II   —  The channel and the SE: set up multi-antenna
                   APs with MMSE channel estimation and conjugate
                   beamforming. Apply the use-and-forget capacity
                   bound. Present the closed-form SE as
                   Proposition 1.

   Section III  —  The power model: adopt the standard
                   Björnson-2015 component power model and
                   explicitly include FIXED + TRAFFIC-DEPENDENT
                   backhaul costs. Define total energy efficiency.

   Section IV   —  The optimization: formulate EE maximization
                   with per-user SE and per-AP power constraints.
                   Show it is a non-convex fractional program.
                   Use the "divide by B·S_e" trick, the c = √η
                   substitution, and sequential convex
                   approximation with log-inequality and
                   quadratic-over-linear Taylor bounds to turn
                   it into a sequence of SOCPs. Algorithm 1.

   Section V    —  The scalability fix: notice that full
                   cell-free scales backhaul cost linearly with
                   M, so select only the best ~15% of APs per
                   user using either received-power ranking
                   (Algorithm 2) or large-scale fading ranking
                   (Algorithm 3).

   Section VI   —  The simulations: confirm Algorithm 1 converges
                   in ~10 iterations, matches BRB global optimum
                   on small problems, and that power control
                   alone gives a 2.6–2.9× EE boost. AP selection
                   keeps EE flat as backhaul cost grows. An
                   optimal N* exists and depends on deployment
                   size and backhaul cost. CF-mMIMO beats
                   colocated massive MIMO by up to 7.4× under
                   1 bit/s/Hz QoS.

   Section VII  —  The conclusion: these results show that
                   cell-free massive MIMO is practically green,
                   IF you use power control and AP selection.

   Appendix A   —  The proof of Proposition 1: carefully compute
                   DS_k, E{|BU_k|²}, E{|UI_{kk'}|²} using MMSE
                   orthogonality, fourth moments of Gaussian
                   vectors, and independence of channels across
                   APs. Plug into (11) → (15).

─── 7.2  The single most important equation of the paper ──────────────

Eq. (15) — the closed-form SE — is THE equation. Everything else
is downstream of it:
  •  the power consumption model of Sec III inherits S_e through
     the backhaul term;
  •  the optimization of Sec IV plugs (15) directly into the
     SE constraint and the objective;
  •  the AP selection of Sec V uses γ_{m,k} (which defines the
     shape of (15)) to rank APs;
  •  the numerical results of Sec VI compute (15) at every
     iteration.

If you understand (15) — its numerator, its pilot-contamination
term, its non-coherent interference term, and its reduction to
the colocated / single-antenna limits — you understand the paper.

─── 7.3  What should a PhD student take away? ──────────────────────────

(1) KNOW THE USE-AND-FORGET BOUND cold. It is the standard tool
    for any capacity calculation in massive MIMO with imperfect
    CSI. Every paper you read in this area will use it.

(2) THE SCA-SOCP RECIPE OF SECTION IV is a template. Any EE or
    fractional-programming problem in wireless can likely be
    attacked with: (i) divide by the numerator to linearize the
    denominator; (ii) substitute c = √η to make quadratic
    constraints; (iii) slack-variable decomposition of log-of-ratio
    constraints; (iv) Taylor-expand the quadratic-over-linear;
    (v) log-inequality bound on the log. Know it, practice it.

(3) USER-CENTRIC CF-MMIMO is the dominant deployment philosophy.
    Section V is one of its two founding papers. Buzzi-2017 is
    the other. The scalable CF-mMIMO paper of Björnson-2020 you
    have studied is the mature treatment.

(4) BACKHAUL ALWAYS MATTERS in distributed deployments. Any EE
    analysis that ignores the backhaul is telling you an
    optimistic lie. Section III is the template for including it
    properly.

(5) SIMULATION METHODOLOGY MATTERS. The wrap-around torus, the
    three-slope path-loss model, and the Björnson-calibrated
    Table I power values are standard — use them whenever you
    run your own CF-mMIMO experiments so that your numbers are
    comparable to the literature.

(6) KNOW WHAT THE PAPER DOES NOT DO. Uplink, CSI-sharing cost,
    fronthaul vs backhaul distinction, ZF/MMSE precoding, user
    mobility — all of these are OPEN in the Ngo-2018 paper and
    have been addressed in follow-ups. They are where new PhD
    contributions live.

─── 7.4  Notable influences on later CF-mMIMO literature ──────────────

After this paper, the CF-mMIMO literature branched into several
sub-fields:

  (i)  User-centric CF-mMIMO (Buzzi-2017, Björnson-2020, Interdonato-2019)
       — direct descendants of Section V.

  (ii) Centralized / MMSE CF-mMIMO (Björnson-Sanguinetti 2019,
       which you have studied) — asks what SE is achievable with
       centralized signal processing, as an upper bound on the
       distributed setting of Ngo-2018.

  (iii) Scalable CF-mMIMO (Björnson-Sanguinetti 2020, which you
       have studied) — formalizes the "15% of APs per user"
       observation of Fig. 4 as a scalability property.

  (iv) EE with user-centric + MMSE (Bashar-Ngo-Duong-Cumanan
       2018+) — combines the EE objective of Ngo-2018 with the
       MMSE analysis of Björnson-Sanguinetti 2019.

  (v)  RIS / IRS-aided CF-mMIMO (2020+) — incorporates
       reconfigurable intelligent surfaces as a second layer of
       passive "APs".

  (vi) Learning-based CF-mMIMO (2022+) — deep reinforcement
       learning for user association and power control,
       replacing the SCA-SOCP recipe of Section IV with neural
       networks for faster inference.

In each of these, the Ngo-2018-TGCN paper is either a primary
citation or a methodological ancestor. Its Google Scholar count
(several thousand citations as of 2025) reflects this.
