───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. HIGH-LEVEL / LAYMAN EXPLANATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Section II told us how many bits per second per hertz cell-free massive
MIMO can squeeze out of the spectrum. Section III now asks the
complementary question:

    "How many JOULES per second does this same system burn to deliver
     those bits?"

You could naively answer "just the transmit power" and be tragically
wrong — modern wireless systems spend FAR more energy on hardware and
signalling overhead than they do radiating RF out of the antennas.
A concrete example from the 2014-era cellular literature: a 20-W-output
macro base station typically draws 800–1300 W from the wall socket.
Only about 1–2% of its total electricity bill actually leaves the
antenna as radio waves. The rest is:

   •  heat lost in the power amplifier (PA efficiency ≈ 30–50%),
   •  DC power burned by the hardware chains — mixers, ADCs/DACs,
      oscillators, filters, synthesizers,
   •  baseband digital processing (FFTs, channel estimation, decoding),
   •  backhaul transceivers that ship data from the BS to the core
      network through fiber or microwave,
   •  air-conditioning, power supplies, losses, monitoring, …

Any honest energy-efficiency paper therefore needs a COMPONENT-LEVEL
power-consumption model that charges every Watt to its source.
Björnson, Sanguinetti, Hoydis, Debbah (IEEE TWC 2015, ref [11]) wrote
THE canonical such model for colocated massive MIMO and gave closed-
form expressions for the optimal number of antennas, the optimal
number of users, etc. That paper is the direct parent of Section III
here.

In the cell-free case, however, there is a BIG new term that the
colocated model does not capture: the backhaul from each AP to the
central CPU. Unlike a colocated base station with ONE backhaul, a
cell-free system has M backhaul links, one per AP, and the total
backhaul energy grows roughly linearly with M. Worse, the traffic-
dependent part of the backhaul also grows linearly with the sum data
rate S_e. So if you are not careful, adding more APs simultaneously
raises S_e (good) AND raises total power (bad), and the "bit per joule"
ratio may actually DECREASE even though the bit/s increases.

Section III models all of this cleanly. The result is:

   •  Equation (17): P_total = (total AP hardware power) + (total
                                backhaul power)
   •  Equation (18): per-AP hardware power = PA-draw for transmit
                     power + per-antenna fixed circuit power
   •  Equation (19): per-AP backhaul power = fixed backhaul part +
                     traffic-dependent backhaul part ∝ data rate
   •  Equation (20): a clean all-in-one expression for P_total as a
                     function of the power-control coefficients η_{m,k}
                     and the sum SE S_e
   •  Equation (21): total energy efficiency E_e = B · S_e / P_total,
                     measured in bit per joule.

With E_e written explicitly in terms of the variables we already
control (the power-control coefficients), Section IV can turn the
"maximize EE" mission into an actual optimization problem.

The practical headline the reader must carry forward:

    "In cell-free massive MIMO, the backhaul is the elephant in the
     room. A realistic EE analysis without it is not a realistic EE
     analysis at all."


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. PHD-LEVEL DETAILED EXPLANATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─── 2.1  The decomposition of total power (Eq. 17) ─────────────────────

The authors partition the total network power consumption into two
parts:

         P_total  =  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_m  +  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bh,m}         (17)

  •  P_m         = power at the m-th AP due to the amplifier and
                   the circuit components;
  •  P_{bh,m}    = power consumed by the backhaul link between the
                   m-th AP and the central processing unit (CPU).

Notice what is NOT modeled separately: the CPU itself. The paper
ignores the CPU power as a constant add-on (it would appear only in a
single "+P_CPU" term, which does not depend on the power-allocation
variables and therefore does not change the shape of the optimization
problem). The reference [11] treats CPU/coding/decoding explicitly,
but for the cell-free context its contribution is dominated by the
AP + backhaul terms, so the authors absorb it into a "background"
constant or neglect it.

Similarly, the paper ignores any power-allocation-dependent baseband
processing cost (FFTs, channel estimation, matched filtering) at the
APs. Those costs DO scale with the number of antennas N (and, to a
lesser extent, with K), but they are all bundled into the per-antenna
circuit term N·P_{tc,m} below. This is a mildly coarse abstraction
that is standard in the massive-MIMO EE literature.

─── 2.2  The AP hardware power P_m (Eq. 18) ──────────────────────────

The AP hardware draw is the classical "PA + circuit" decomposition:

            P_m  =  (1/ν_m) · ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
                     ───────────────────────────────────────────────
                                (PA-draw at AP m, in Watt)

                    + N · P_{tc,m}                                      (18)
                     ─────────────
                      (circuit draw,
                       in Watt)

The first term is the DC power the POWER AMPLIFIER (PA) must pull from
the battery in order to radiate the transmit signal.

Let us unpack it step by step:

  (i) In Section II, the expected transmit RF power at AP m, in
      NORMALIZED units (normalized by noise power N₀), was Eq. (7):

           𝔼{‖𝐱_m‖²} = ρ_d · N · Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}

      Multiplying by N₀ converts the normalized power to actual Watts:

           P_RF,m = ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}  [W]

      Here ρ_d is the MAXIMUM normalized transmit SNR (dimensionless),
      N₀ is the thermal noise power (in Watts), and the sum Σ_k η_{m,k}
      γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N is the fraction of the AP's maximum normalized
      power that is actually being used (≤ 1/N).

  (ii) The PA does not convert all the DC power it pulls from the
       battery into RF. Only a fraction ν_m ∈ (0, 1] actually comes
       out as RF; the rest is heat. Hence the DC draw is

           P_PA,m = P_RF,m / ν_m

       This is where (1/ν_m) in (18) comes from. Typical values:
           •  ν_m ≈ 0.3–0.4 for a class-A/AB PA in commodity base
              stations (default in this paper: ν_m = 0.4).
           •  ν_m ≈ 0.5–0.6 for a well-designed class-B/class-F PA.
           •  ν_m ≈ 0.8–0.9 only for envelope-tracking PAs in
              research prototypes.
       For cell-free massive MIMO with many small APs, 0.3–0.4 is the
       realistic default.

  (iii) The factor N inside the sum is NOT accidental — it comes from
        the N antennas per AP. But observe the clever counter-balance:
        the per-AP power constraint (Eq. 8) says Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
        ≤ 1/N, so

            P_RF,m  ≤  ρ_d · N₀ · N · (1/N)  =  ρ_d · N₀

        Therefore the PA's worst-case DC draw per AP is ρ_d·N₀/ν_m,
        independent of N. Changing the number of antennas per AP does
        not change the total PA power at full load — this is how the
        paper enforces a "fair comparison" across different N values.

The second term in Eq. (18), N · P_{tc,m}, is the "transceiver-chain"
or circuit power per AP. P_{tc,m} is the DC power consumed by ONE RF
chain (oscillator, mixer, LNA, ADC/DAC, filter, etc.) to operate one
antenna. This cost is paid EVEN IF the antenna is not radiating any
signal — it is the cost of having the hardware switched on. For
multiple-antenna APs, the circuit power scales linearly with N because
each antenna needs its own chain.

In the paper's Table I, P_{tc,m} = 0.2 W per antenna. A 4-antenna AP
therefore continuously burns 0.8 W in hardware, whatever the data
traffic happens to be.

Formal summary:

    P_m  =  (1/ν_m) · ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}    [PA-draw]
         +  N · P_{tc,m}                                      [fixed
                                                                circuit
                                                                draw]

─── 2.3  Backhaul power P_{bh,m} (Eq. 19) ─────────────────────────────

This is the term that does NOT exist in the classical colocated
massive-MIMO EE model of [11], and it is the physical reason this
whole paper was written:

    P_{bh,m}  =  P_{0,m}  +  B · S_e · P_{bt,m}                        (19)

Three ingredients:

  •  P_{0,m} — the "fixed" backhaul power (traffic-independent). This
     is the power consumed by the backhaul transceiver just being
     switched on: fiber transceivers, media converters, switching,
     etc. It depends on the DISTANCE between AP m and the CPU, on
     the link technology (fiber vs. copper vs. microwave), and on
     the topology (star, daisy-chain, ring, …). It does NOT depend
     on how much data is flowing through the link. In the paper's
     default Table I, P_{0,m} = 0.825 W per AP.

  •  P_{bt,m} — the "traffic-dependent" or "data-dependent" backhaul
     power per unit data rate. Units: watts per (bit/s), or
     equivalently joules per bit. The more bits you pipe from the
     CPU to the AP, the more electricity the backhaul transceivers
     and associated DSP burn. In the paper's Table I, P_{bt,m} =
     0.25 W / (Gbit/s) = 2.5 · 10^{-10} W / (bit/s).

  •  B · S_e — the total data rate (in bit/s) carried by the
     backhaul. Here B is the system bandwidth (Hz) and S_e is the
     sum spectral efficiency in bit/s/Hz (Eq. 16). For the default
     B = 20 MHz and a sum SE of, say, 20 bit/s/Hz, that is 400 Mbit/s.

Why is the traffic-dependent term proportional to B·S_e? Because in a
cell-free TDD system the backhaul carries the SAME data symbols
{q_1,…,q_K} to every AP (since every AP serves every user with the
conjugate-beamforming weight). The downlink information rate delivered
to the end users equals B · S_e bit/s, and every AP must receive ALL
of that data from the CPU. Hence the per-AP backhaul data rate is also
B · S_e, and multiplying by the per-bit/s cost P_{bt,m} gives the
data-dependent power.

(Later in Section V, the authors point out that if AP m only serves
a SUBSET of the users, it only needs the sum rate of THOSE users on
its backhaul link, which is strictly smaller than B·S_e. This is
the argument behind AP selection.)

One subtlety acknowledged in Footnote 1 of the paper: the backhaul
also carries synchronization signals, power-control coefficients,
and large-scale-fading updates. These are transmitted ONCE per
large-scale-fading realization (i.e., roughly every second), and the
information rate is vanishingly small compared to the user-data rate.
So their power is neglected.

─── 2.4  The all-in-one expression for P_total (Eq. 20) ────────────────

Plugging (18) and (19) into (17):

    P_total =   ρ_d · N₀ · Σ_{m=1}^{M} (1/ν_m) · N · Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
                  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                          (total PA power across all APs — depends on η)

             + Σ_{m=1}^{M} (N · P_{tc,m} + P_{0,m})
                  ──────────────────────────────────
                  (fixed constant = circuit draw + fixed backhaul)

             + B · Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bt,m} · S_e({η_{m,k}})               (20)
                 ──────────────────────────────────────
                 (traffic-dependent backhaul, depends on η through S_e)

It is useful to define the "background fixed" power

    P̄_fix  ≝  Σ_{m=1}^{M} ( N · P_{tc,m}  +  P_{0,m} )

which the paper introduces right after (23) to simplify notation. This
is the power the network burns no matter what the users are doing —
the baseline floor. With this shorthand,

    P_total  =  ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
              + P̄_fix
              + B · (Σ_m P_{bt,m}) · S_e({η_{m,k}})

Note the two "η-dependent" pieces: the first one grows with the power
you put on air; the last one grows with the sum spectral efficiency
you deliver. Both go up when you increase η. EE therefore faces a
genuine trade-off — more η means both more bits AND more joules, and
which effect wins depends on the numerical values of the coefficients.

─── 2.5  The total energy efficiency E_e (Eq. 21) ─────────────────────

Finally:

         E_e   =   B · S_e / P_total                                   (21)

This is the "bit per joule" figure of merit the rest of the paper
maximizes.

Units sanity check:
  numerator:  [Hz] · [bit/s/Hz] = [bit/s]
  denominator: [W] = [J/s]
  ratio:      [bit/s] / [J/s] = [bit/J]   ✓

Note the structure of (21): it is a RATIO of two functions of the
power-control coefficients η, both of which are NON-CONVEX in η
(S_e is a sum of log terms, and P_total has those same log terms in
the backhaul piece). That is why Section IV needs SCA — you cannot
directly throw (21) into a standard convex solver.

Ratio objectives of this form are called "fractional programs." Two
very famous tools exist for them:

  (a) Dinkelbach's transform — introduces a parameter λ and
      iteratively solves max {numerator − λ · denominator}, updating
      λ until numerator = λ · denominator.
  (b) Charnes–Cooper transformation — applicable when the numerator
      and denominator are linear (which they are not here).

The Ngo et al. paper uses a hybrid: they first transform (21) by
DIVIDING numerator and denominator by B·S_e (a positive quantity)
to absorb the traffic-dependent backhaul into the fixed part, and
then apply SCA + SOCP to the transformed problem. We will see this
in Section IV.

─── 2.6  An early preview of the "divide and conquer" trick ───────────

Because the paper refers to this trick in Section IV but introduces
it right at the end of Section III, it is worth spelling out here so
you can follow Section IV later.

Divide the numerator and denominator of (21) by B·S_e (and note that
B·S_e is strictly positive whenever any user actually gets some rate):

                           1
   E_e  =  ─────────────────────────────────────
           P̄_fix / (B S_e)
         + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} / (B S_e)
         + Σ_m P_{bt,m}

Observe that the LAST term in the denominator, Σ_m P_{bt,m}, is now
CONSTANT — it does NOT depend on η any more, because we divided the
backhaul term B·S_e·Σ_m P_{bt,m} by B·S_e. So E_e is a monotonically
decreasing function of the (still-η-dependent) quantity

        Ẽ  ≝  P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}

DIVIDED by B·S_e. In other words,

        E_e  monotone in   B·S_e / Ẽ

Therefore, maximizing E_e is EQUIVALENT to maximizing the fractional
objective B·S_e / Ẽ, where Ẽ has KNOCKED OUT the non-linear (log)
backhaul dependence and is simply an AFFINE function of the
"quadratic power" variables (after the c_{m,k} ← √η_{m,k}
substitution). This is why Section IV can reformulate the problem
as a sequence of SOCPs.

This "quotient-by-B·S_e" simplification is the single most important
analytic trick of the whole paper, and it is enabled precisely by
the specific shape of the backhaul-power model (19) — linear in S_e.
If the backhaul power were, say, logarithmic in S_e (which is the
case for information-theoretically optimal backhaul quantizers), the
trick would not work and a much harder non-convex optimization would
be needed.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3. HISTORICAL / PRIOR DEVELOPMENTS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The component-level EE power consumption modelling tradition goes
back about 15 years. A brief timeline:

▸ 2011 — Auer, Giannini, Desset, Gódor, Skillermark, et al., "How
  much energy is needed to run a wireless network?" IEEE Wireless
  Commun. Mag., Oct 2011. This is the EARTH project landmark paper
  that introduced the "linear" power model for macro/micro/pico/femto
  base stations:

     P_{total}  =  N_{TRX} · ( P_0 + ΔP · P_{out}/P_{max} )

  where N_{TRX} is the number of transceiver chains, P_0 is the
  idle/no-load power, ΔP is the slope, P_{out} is the transmit
  output, and P_{max} is the maximum. This paper measured empirical
  data on commercial base stations and gave numerical values. It is
  THE foundation of every EE paper afterwards, including Ngo et al.

▸ 2011 — Tombaz, Västberg, Zander, "Energy- and cost-efficient
  ultra-high-capacity wireless access," IEEE Wireless Commun. Mag.
  (ref [13] in the Ngo paper). Extended EARTH to include backhaul
  with a model very close to P_{bh} = P_0 + P_{bt}·R.

▸ 2013 — Björnson, Jorswieck, Debbah, Ottersten, "Designing multi-
  user MIMO for energy efficiency: When is massive MIMO the answer?"
  arXiv:1310.3843. Introduced the rigorous problem formulation:
  "given a component power model, choose the number of antennas,
  number of users, and transmit power that maximize bit/J under a
  gross-rate constraint."

▸ 2015 — **Björnson, Sanguinetti, Hoydis, Debbah, "Optimal design
  of energy-efficient multi-user MIMO systems: Is massive MIMO the
  answer?" IEEE TWC, June 2015** (ref [11] in the Ngo paper).
  THIS is the canonical reference. It refined the 2013 workshop
  paper into a full TWC paper with closed-form optimal M, K, P,
  using a component-level model of the form

      P_total = P_fix + P_PA + P_cir(M, K) + P_coding + P_decoding
              + P_BH + P_SP

  where each piece has its own coefficient measured from industry
  data (Auer 2011). This is the DIRECT parent of Eqs. (17)-(20)
  in the Ngo paper. Observations from [11] that carry over here:

     •  circuit power is linear in both M (number of antennas) and
        K (number of users);
     •  power amplifier draw is (1/ν) × transmit power;
     •  backhaul is linear in data rate plus a fixed offset;
     •  there is an OPTIMAL operating point — too few antennas
        waste the array gain, too many antennas drown in circuit
        power. This is the famous "U-shaped" EE curve.

  The 2015 paper won the 2018 IEEE Marconi Prize Paper Award in
  Wireless Communications, confirming its canonical status.

▸ 2016 — Zuo, Zhang, Yuen, Jiang, Luo, "Energy efficient downlink
  transmission for multi-cell massive DAS with pilot contamination,"
  IEEE TVT, Apr 2016 (ref [14] in the Ngo paper). Adapted the
  Björnson-2015 model to DAS (distributed antenna systems), which
  is mathematically closest to cell-free. This is where the
  specific parameter values (P_{tc}, P_0, P_{bt}, ν) adopted in
  Table I of the Ngo paper are taken from.

▸ 2016 — Dai and Yu, "Energy efficiency of downlink transmission
  strategies for cloud radio access networks," IEEE JSAC, Apr 2016
  (ref [15] in the Ngo paper). Applied the EE optimization to
  C-RAN, explicitly separating fronthaul and backhaul power. C-RAN
  is the architectural twin of cell-free, so this work was another
  close cousin.

▸ Feb 2017 — **This paper (Ngo et al.)**. First to apply the
  Björnson-2015 component model to cell-free massive MIMO and
  couple it with the closed-form SE of Section II, producing a
  tractable optimization problem over power-control coefficients.

▸ 2017 — Bashar, Cumanan, Burr, Matthaiou, Ngo, "Energy efficiency
  of the cell-free massive MIMO uplink with optimal uniform
  quantization," IEEE TGCN, Dec 2017. Extended the EE analysis to
  the uplink with quantization.

▸ 2018–present — A flood of CF-mMIMO EE papers: user-centric CF EE
  (Nguyen, Duong, Ngo 2017); EE with low-resolution ADCs; EE under
  fronthaul constraints; EE with RIS aiding; EE with user-centric
  clustering (the scalable CF paper you already studied). All of
  them either cite or directly build on Eqs. (17)–(21) of Ngo 2018.

So Section III is not a new power model — it is the *application*
of the by-then-standard component power model ([11], [13]–[15]) to
the specific architecture of cell-free massive MIMO, with the
critical addition of EXPLICIT backhaul terms that are the Achilles'
heel of CF-mMIMO.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4. MATHEMATICAL RECAP IN UNICODE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Variables and constants:

   M          number of APs
   N          antennas per AP
   K          number of users
   ν_m        PA efficiency of AP m, ν_m ∈ (0,1]
   ρ_d        maximum normalized DL transmit SNR
   N₀         thermal noise power, W
   γ_{m,k}    MMSE per-component variance (Sec. II, Eq. 5)
   η_{m,k}    power control coefficient (≥ 0)
   P_{tc,m}   per-antenna circuit/hardware power, W
   P_{0,m}    fixed backhaul power of AP m, W
   P_{bt,m}   traffic-dep. backhaul power, W / (bit/s)
   B          system bandwidth, Hz
   S_e        sum spectral efficiency, bit/s/Hz  (from Eq. 16)

Equation (17): total power decomposition
   P_total  =  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_m  +  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bh,m}

Equation (18): AP hardware power
   P_m  =  (1/ν_m) · ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
       +  N · P_{tc,m}

Equation (19): backhaul power
   P_{bh,m}  =  P_{0,m}  +  B · S_e · P_{bt,m}

Equation (20): all-in-one
   P_total  =  ρ_d · N₀ · Σ_{m=1}^{M} (1/ν_m) · N · Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
             + Σ_{m=1}^{M} (N · P_{tc,m} + P_{0,m})
             + B · Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bt,m} · S_e({η_{m,k}})

Shorthand:
   P̄_fix  ≝  Σ_{m=1}^{M} ( N · P_{tc,m}  +  P_{0,m} )

so that
   P_total  =  ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
             + P̄_fix
             + B · (Σ_m P_{bt,m}) · S_e({η_{m,k}})

Equation (21): total energy efficiency
   E_e  =  B · S_e({η_{m,k}})  /  P_total({η_{m,k}})      [bit / Joule]

Equivalent "divide-by-S_e" form (used in Section IV):
   E_e  ∝  B · S_e({η_{m,k}}) / [ P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ]
   (modulo a constant additive Σ_m P_{bt,m} in the denominator, which
    does not depend on η and so can be absorbed into the problem)


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5. NUMERICAL WORKED EXAMPLE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Let us use the default Table I parameters of the paper and work out
P_total and E_e by hand for a mid-size configuration.

  • M        = 100 APs
  • N        = 4 antennas per AP  (total 400 service antennas)
  • K        = 20 users
  • ν_m      = 0.4  (PA efficiency)
  • P_{tc,m} = 0.2 W   per antenna
  • P_{0,m}  = 0.825 W per AP     (fixed backhaul)
  • P_{bt,m} = 0.25 W/(Gbit/s) = 2.5 × 10⁻¹⁰ W/(bit/s)
  • B        = 20 MHz = 2 × 10⁷ Hz
  • ρ_d      = 1 W (in normalized units)
  • N₀       ≈ 4 × 10⁻¹³ W  (at 9 dB NF, B = 20 MHz)
  • sum SE   S_e = 20 bit/s/Hz  (i.e. 1 bit/s/Hz per user)

Assume every AP operates at full transmit budget, i.e.,
Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} = 1/N = 0.25 for all m.

─── Step 1. PA-draw part of each P_m ──────────────────────────────────

Per-AP:
   P_PA,m = (1/ν_m) · ρ_d · N₀ · N · (1/N)
          = (1/0.4) · 1 · (4 × 10⁻¹³) · 1
          ≈ 1 × 10⁻¹² W

This is tiny because ρ_d and N₀ are normalized to noise — that is
not the "real" transmit-power cost. Let us recompute using a REAL
transmit power: in the paper, ρ_d = 1 is a *normalized* SNR, not
1 W. The actual transmit budget is ρ_d · N₀ ≈ 4 × 10⁻¹³ W — i.e.
the setting "ρ_d = 1" just means "the AP can transmit at 1 dB
above noise floor," which is unphysically small. The paper's
Table I fixes the PA-draw numbers by scaling things differently —
in practice, the numerical experiments in Section VI use a
transmit budget of a few hundred mW per AP, not 4×10⁻¹³ W.

To get meaningful numbers, let us instead interpret "ρ_d · N₀" as
"max RF output per AP" = 200 mW = 0.2 W (a reasonable small-cell
AP output). Then:

   P_PA,m = (1/0.4) · 0.2 · (sum η γ) / (1/N)       ← normalize
          ≤ (1/0.4) · 0.2  =  0.5 W (at full load)

─── Step 2. Circuit part of each P_m ──────────────────────────────────

Per-AP circuit = N · P_{tc,m} = 4 · 0.2 = 0.8 W.

So per-AP hardware P_m (at full PA load):
   P_m = 0.5 + 0.8 = 1.3 W

Across M = 100 APs:
   Σ_m P_m = 130 W

─── Step 3. Fixed backhaul Σ_m P_{0,m} ─────────────────────────────────

Σ_m P_{0,m} = 100 · 0.825 = 82.5 W

─── Step 4. Sum throughput B·S_e ──────────────────────────────────────

B · S_e = 2 × 10⁷ · 20 = 4 × 10⁸ bit/s = 400 Mbit/s

─── Step 5. Traffic-dep. backhaul ─────────────────────────────────────

Σ_m P_{bt,m} · B · S_e
   = 100 · (2.5 × 10⁻¹⁰) · (4 × 10⁸)
   = 100 · 0.1
   = 10 W

─── Step 6. Total power ──────────────────────────────────────────────

P_total = Σ_m P_m + Σ_m P_{0,m} + traffic backhaul
        = 130 + 82.5 + 10
        = 222.5 W

─── Step 7. Energy efficiency ────────────────────────────────────────

E_e = (4 × 10⁸) / 222.5
    ≈ 1.798 × 10⁶ bit/J
    ≈ 1.80 Mbit/J

─── Observations ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. **Circuit + fixed backhaul dominate.** Out of 222.5 W, the PA only
   accounts for 50 W (22.5%), the per-antenna circuit power accounts
   for 80 W (36%), and the fixed backhaul accounts for another 82.5 W
   (37%). The traffic-dependent backhaul is only 10 W (4.5%) at
   S_e = 20 bit/s/Hz. This is the typical "fixed-cost-dominated"
   regime of small/micro base stations.

2. **Increasing N helps the data rate but inflates circuit power.**
   Going from N = 1 to N = 4 at fixed total antennas MN = 400 means
   M drops from 400 to 100, so:
     • Σ_m N·P_{tc,m} = 400·0.2 = 80 W (unchanged!)
     • Σ_m P_{0,m}    = 100·0.825 = 82.5 W  vs.  400·0.825 = 330 W
     • Traffic BH     = 100·P_{bt}·B·S_e  vs.  400·P_{bt}·B·S_e
   So going from M=400 to M=100 SLASHES the fixed backhaul from
   330 W to 82.5 W (–247.5 W!) and the traffic backhaul from 40 W
   to 10 W (–30 W), at the cost of some macro-diversity loss. This
   is exactly the "number-of-antennas-per-AP trade-off" that the
   paper will visualize in Fig. 5 of Section VI.

3. **What if we ignored backhaul?** Removing P_{0,m} and P_{bt,m}
   from P_total gives P_total = 130 W and E_e = 4×10⁸/130 ≈ 3.08
   Mbit/J — an overestimate by 70 %. This is exactly why an EE
   analysis without backhaul is misleading.

─── PRACTICE PROBLEM ──────────────────────────────────────────────────

Repeat the seven-step calculation for M = 200 APs, N = 2 antennas per
AP, K = 40 users, ρ_d·N₀ (real transmit power) = 0.2 W at full load,
ν_m = 0.4, P_{tc,m} = 0.2 W, P_{0,m} = 0.825 W, P_{bt,m} = 0.25 W/(Gbit/s),
B = 20 MHz, sum SE S_e = 40 bit/s/Hz (i.e. 1 bit/s/Hz per user).

(a) Σ_m P_m (hint: per-AP PA = 0.5 W, circuit = 0.4 W)
(b) fixed backhaul Σ_m P_{0,m}
(c) traffic-dep backhaul Σ_m P_{bt,m}·B·S_e
(d) P_total
(e) E_e in Mbit/J
(f) Fraction of P_total attributable to backhaul

[Expected answers:
   (a) per-AP = 0.9 W × 200 = 180 W
   (b) 200 × 0.825 = 165 W
   (c) 200 × 2.5·10⁻¹⁰ × (2·10⁷ · 40)
       = 200 × 2.5·10⁻¹⁰ × 8·10⁸
       = 200 × 0.2
       = 40 W
   (d) 180 + 165 + 40 = 385 W
   (e) B·S_e = 2·10⁷·40 = 8·10⁸ bit/s
       E_e = 8·10⁸ / 385 ≈ 2.08 Mbit/J
   (f) Backhaul = 165 + 40 = 205 W out of 385 ⇒ 53% !
       More than half of the power is backhaul — the cell-free
       "elephant in the room." ]

Notice that doubling M from 100 to 200 (while halving N from 4 to 2
so that MN stays at 400) REDUCED circuit power a bit (80→80, same)
but DOUBLED the fixed backhaul from 82.5 to 165 W. EE actually did
not change much because we also doubled B·S_e. Still, the backhaul
share jumped from ~37% (M=100) to ~53% (M=200). This is the
*quantitative* meaning of "backhaul kills EE at large M" that the
paper keeps emphasizing.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. SUMMARY OF SECTION III
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Component-level power model. Section III adopts the now-standard
  component-level power model of [11], [13]–[15] and specializes it
  to cell-free massive MIMO. Total power has two parts:
     P_total = Σ_m (P_m)         +  Σ_m (P_{bh,m})
             AP hardware             backhaul
  (Eq. 17).

• AP hardware power (Eq. 18):
     P_m = (1/ν_m) · ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}   [PA draw]
         + N · P_{tc,m}                                     [circuit]
  Two insights:
     (i) per-AP PA draw is bounded by ρ_d·N₀/ν_m regardless of N
         (because of the Σ η γ ≤ 1/N per-AP power constraint);
     (ii) circuit power scales linearly with N and is paid even
          when no data is transmitted.

• Backhaul power (Eq. 19):
     P_{bh,m} = P_{0,m} + B · S_e · P_{bt,m}
  Fixed traffic-independent part + traffic-dependent part linear
  in sum data rate. This is the NEW term (vs. colocated MIMO)
  and the reason cell-free EE requires careful analysis.

• All-in-one (Eq. 20): P_total = η-dependent PA + fixed (N·P_{tc}+
  P_{0}) + η-dependent traffic backhaul (∝ S_e).

• Total energy efficiency (Eq. 21):
     E_e = B · S_e / P_total       [bit / J]
  This is the objective maximized in Section IV.

• The "elephant in the room." Fixed backhaul Σ_m P_{0,m} scales
  linearly with M. Traffic-dependent backhaul also scales linearly
  with M (through Σ_m P_{bt,m}). For large M the backhaul is THE
  dominant power consumer, and naive deployment (more APs = better)
  becomes counter-productive in joules/bit terms. This motivates
  the AP-selection schemes of Section V.

• "Divide by B·S_e" trick. Dividing numerator and denominator of
  (21) by B·S_e removes the backhaul non-linearity and leaves an
  equivalent fractional objective whose denominator is AFFINE in
  the quadratic power variables. This is the analytic key that
  will make Section IV's SCA-SOCP reformulation possible.

• Default Table I parameters (used throughout the paper):
     ν_m       = 0.4
     P_{tc,m}  = 0.2 W per antenna
     P_{0,m}   = 0.825 W per AP
     P_{bt,m}  = 0.25 W / (Gbit/s)
     B         = 20 MHz
     noise figure = 9 dB  →  N₀ ≈ 4·10⁻¹³ W
     τ_c       = 200 samples
