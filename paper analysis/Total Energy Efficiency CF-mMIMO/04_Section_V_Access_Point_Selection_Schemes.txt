───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. HIGH-LEVEL (LAYMAN) EXPLANATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Section IV gave us an algorithm that takes a fixed cell-free massive
MIMO topology (M APs, K users, all APs serving all users) and squeezes
maximum energy efficiency out of it by choosing the best power-control
coefficients {η_{m,k}}. But the algorithm does not change the
TOPOLOGY — it still assumes that EVERY AP serves EVERY user. This
assumption is what Section III called the "elephant in the room":
it inflates backhaul power linearly with M because every AP must
receive every user's data from the CPU, whether or not that AP is
actually close enough to contribute useful signal.

Section V attacks the topology. The author's two observations are:

  (i)  For a given user k, there are many APs that are located VERY
       FAR AWAY. Their large-scale fading β_{m,k} is essentially zero,
       so they contribute almost nothing to user k's desired signal.
       Forcing them to participate (allocate nonzero η, send data
       over backhaul) wastes power for no benefit.

  (ii) If AP m only serves a SUBSET U_m of the users, then its
       backhaul only needs to carry the data of THOSE users. Since
       the traffic-dependent backhaul power is proportional to the
       sum of the rates actually being served, reducing U_m shrinks
       the second backhaul term proportionally.

Combining the two: for each user k, let only a small GROUP A_k of
nearby APs serve it. Equivalently, AP m only serves users in the set
U_m = { k : m ∈ A_k }. How small can A_k be without hurting the
desired signal too much? Numerically, the paper finds that only about
10–20% of the APs per user are enough to deliver 95% of the received-
signal power.

Section V proposes two GROUPING heuristics:

  (A) Received-power-based selection (Algorithm 2): rank APs by how
      much useful power they actually contribute to user k given the
      CURRENT optimal power-control solution, and keep the top few
      that cumulatively deliver ≥ ρ% (typically ρ = 95%). This
      requires running Algorithm 1 ONCE on the full system to get the
      baseline power-control coefficients, so it is more accurate but
      more expensive.

  (B) Largest-large-scale-fading-based selection (Algorithm 3): rank
      APs simply by β_{m,k} (the deterministic large-scale fading
      coefficient) and keep the top few whose β's sum to ≥ ρ% of the
      total. This does NOT require running Algorithm 1 first — it
      uses only the slowly-varying large-scale fading that is
      anyway needed to compute γ_{m,k}. Much cheaper, almost as good.

After choosing the sets A_k, the optimization problem becomes a
MODIFIED version of (P) where η_{m,k} is FORCED to zero if k ∉ U_m,
and the backhaul term sums only over the users actually served by
each AP. The paper shows that, up to an upper-bound approximation,
this modified problem can be solved by the SAME Algorithm 1 from
Section IV — you just zero out the "inactive" coefficients.

The practical headline from Section V is:

    "For each user, you only need to listen to the closest ~15% of
     APs. The rest can be shut off, which slashes backhaul power
     and nearly doubles EE without appreciably hurting SE."

This is the PRECURSOR to the modern "user-centric cell-free massive
MIMO" paradigm (Buzzi–D'Andrea 2017, Björnson–Sanguinetti 2020
scalable CF, etc.) that has since become the dominant way to deploy
CF-mMIMO. Ngo-2018-TGCN is one of the earliest papers to formalize
the idea.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. DETAILED PHD-LEVEL EXPLANATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─── 2.1  The motivation — re-read the EE denominator (Eq. 39) ───────────

Section V opens by reconsidering the EE expression (21) after the
trick "divide numerator and denominator by B·S_e" that we already
used in Section IV:

                                         1
    E_e   =   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────    (39)
              [P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}]
              ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                                    B · S_e
            + Σ_m P_{bt,m}

(Actually this is the reciprocal of (39) — the paper writes E_e
directly in (39), but the structure is the same.)

Look at the second term of the denominator of 1/E_e: Σ_m P_{bt,m}.
This is the TRAFFIC-DEPENDENT BACKHAUL POWER per unit throughput.
It is a SUM over ALL M APs, whether the AP actively contributes or
not. Even if AP m is 500 meters from user k, it still pays the full
backhaul cost P_{bt,m} to receive user k's data symbol at the CPU.

Observation: the paper points out that this "elephant" term scales
linearly with M. Doubling the number of APs doubles Σ_m P_{bt,m}.
For small M and small ρ_d (low-power regime), P_{bt,m} is dwarfed by
the PA draw and nobody cares. But for large M and moderate-to-high
ρ_d, Σ_m P_{bt,m} becomes the DOMINANT denominator term, and the
algorithm of Section IV is powerless to reduce it — because
Algorithm 1 only tunes {η_{m,k}} and {η_{m,k}} does NOT appear in
Σ_m P_{bt,m}. You could set ALL η's to zero and the traffic-backhaul
power would still be B·Σ_m P_{bt,m}·S_e, which goes down only if
S_e goes down (i.e., if you stop serving users) — which defeats the
purpose of the system.

Conclusion: to reduce Σ_m P_{bt,m}, you must either reduce M (fewer
physical APs) or more cleverly reduce the SET of users each AP
actually serves, so that the backhaul cost is only proportional to
the sum rate of the ACTIVE service relationships, not to the
hypothetical M·K full-mesh.

─── 2.2  The per-AP backhaul modification (Eq. 40) ─────────────────────

Let U_m ⊆ {1,…,K} be the set of users served by AP m. Then the
backhaul between AP m and the CPU only needs to carry the data
symbols of those users — whose sum rate is Σ_{k∈U_m} SE_k, not
Σ_k SE_k. The backhaul power expression (19) therefore becomes

     P_{bh,m}  =  P_{0,m}  +  B · P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k∈U_m} SE_k            (40)

Compared to the original (19):

   (original) P_{bh,m} = P_{0,m} + B · P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k=1}^K SE_k

The fixed part P_{0,m} does NOT shrink — the backhaul transceiver
still has to be powered on — but the traffic-dependent part shrinks
whenever U_m ⊊ {1,…,K}. If U_m = {1,…,K} for all m, (40) reduces to
(19) — full cell-free, no AP selection.

─── 2.3  The two observations that justify AP selection ───────────────

The authors list two guiding principles (the paper's bullet points
at the start of Section V):

  (O1) "If the mth AP serves only some users, then it needs to send
       only the data corresponding to these users. As a result, the
       backhaul power consumption depends only on the spectral
       efficiencies of these users."

  (O2) "For a given user, there are many APs which are located very
       far away. These APs will not add significantly to the overall
       spatial diversity gains. This implies that not all APs really
       participate in serving this user."

(O1) is a power argument (backhaul), (O2) is a signal-processing
argument (diversity). Together they imply that each user k should be
served by a SMALL set A_k ⊂ {1,…,M} of nearby APs, and the dual set
U_m = { k : m ∈ A_k } defines which users AP m actually serves.

The rest of Section V is about HOW to choose A_k.

─── 2.4  Scheme A: Received-power-based selection  (Section V-A) ─────────

This scheme uses the optimal power-control coefficients {η^*_{m,k}}
returned by Algorithm 1 (Sec IV) to compute how much desired-signal
power each AP actually delivers to each user.

From the proof of Proposition 1 (Appendix A, Eq. (48) of the paper),
the expected desired-signal power DS_k can be written as

     DS_k  =  √ρ_d · E[ Σ_m √η_{m,k} g_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* ]

The magnitude squared of DS_k, using E[g_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*] = N γ_{m,k},
gives the total USEFUL power of user k at the receiver (before the
N² coherent combining and after the initial expectation):

     |DS_k|²  =  ρ_d · N² · ( Σ_m √η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} )²  ≡  ρ_d N² · (...)

But the paper makes a clever simplification: it writes the TOTAL
received desired-signal power across APs as

     DS_k  =  ρ_d · N · Σ_{m=1}^{M}  γ_{m,k}  η_{m,k}                  (41)

where now we use η_{m,k} (not √η_{m,k}) because (41) is recast in
terms of the INSTANTANEOUS-average received power, not the amplitude
in the SE expression. (The paper's (41) is a high-level summary, not
a fully rigorous expression — the rigor lives in Eq. (15). But the
point is that you can identify, AP by AP, how much of the total
received-power budget each AP contributes.)

Specifically, the fraction of the desired-signal power that AP m
contributes to user k is:

     p(m, k)  ≝  γ_{m,k} · η_{m,k}  /  Σ_{m'=1}^{M} γ_{m',k} · η_{m',k}   (42)

This is a probability-like quantity: p(m,k) ≥ 0, and
Σ_m p(m,k) = 1 by construction. Think of it as the PERCENTAGE OF
DESIRED-SIGNAL POWER that AP m is responsible for, given that user k
receives SUM p(·, k) across all APs.

The algorithm picks A_k to be the set of APs that cumulatively
deliver at least ρ% of the desired-signal power, with the SMALLEST
cardinality. Formally:

   1.  Sort p(m, k) in descending order:
       p(π_k(1), k) ≥ p(π_k(2), k) ≥ ... ≥ p(π_k(M), k)
       where π_k is a permutation of {1,...,M}.

   2.  Find the smallest integer M_k such that
       Σ_{i=1}^{M_k} p(π_k(i), k)  ≥  ρ/100.

   3.  Set A_k = { π_k(1), π_k(2), ..., π_k(M_k) }.

With ρ = 95 (paper's default), you keep the top-M_k APs that jointly
deliver 95% of the desired-signal power. The remaining 5% is dropped
to save backhaul.

After A_1, ..., A_K are chosen, we derive U_m = { k : m ∈ A_k },
i.e., U_m is the set of users who have AP m in their serving set.

─── 2.5  The modified optimization problem (P2)  (Eq. 43) ───────────────

With the U_m sets fixed, the EE-maximization problem becomes

                                 B · S_e({η_{m,k}})
              max   ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
   (P2) :  {η_{m,k}}   P̄_fix
                     + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
                     + B · Σ_{m=1}^M P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k∈U_m} SE_k         (43)
              s.t.   SE_k ≥ S_{o,k}
                     Σ_{k∈U_m} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,  m=1,…,M
                     η_{m,k} ≥ 0, for k ∈ U_m, m = 1,…,M
                     η_{m,k} = 0, for k ∉ U_m, m = 1,…,M.

Comparing with the original (P) (Eq. 22):

   •  The per-AP power constraint now sums only over k ∈ U_m. APs
      that don't serve user k will have η_{m,k} = 0, so this is
      EQUIVALENT to restricting the summation to U_m.
   •  A new explicit equality constraint η_{m,k} = 0 for k ∉ U_m
      is added.
   •  The backhaul term in the objective denominator now uses (40)
      instead of (19), summing only over k ∈ U_m per AP.

The per-AP power budget is UNCHANGED at 1/N. This means that an AP
that serves fewer users can concentrate its entire transmit power on
those users, which actually helps SE.

─── 2.6  Trick: absorb the zero-forcing into modified γ's  (Eq. 44) ─────

The equality constraints η_{m,k} = 0 for k ∉ U_m are not nice for an
SOCP. The paper does a clever rewrite: define a new modified γ:

     γ̂_{m,k}  =  γ_{m,k}    if  k ∈ U_m
              =  0           if  k ∉ U_m

Under this definition, the power-control coefficient η_{m,k} for
k ∉ U_m NEVER APPEARS in the optimization (because γ̂_{m,k} = 0
multiplies it everywhere) — it is effectively projected out. Then
(43) can be written in the SAME form as (P) but with γ_{m,k}
replaced by γ̂_{m,k}:

                                        B · S_e
            max      ──────────────────────────────────────────────
   (P2):  {η_{m,k}}  P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ̂_{m,k}
                            + B · Σ_m P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k∈U_m} SE_k       (44)
            s.t.    SE_k ≥ S_{o,k}
                    Σ_k η_{m,k} γ̂_{m,k}  ≤  1/N,   m = 1,…,M
                    η_{m,k} ≥ 0, ∀ m, k.

Now the only remaining structural difference from (P) is the backhaul
term Σ_m P_{bt,m} Σ_{k∈U_m} SE_k instead of Σ_m P_{bt,m} Σ_k SE_k.

─── 2.7  The upper-bound approximation ───────────────────────────────────

The new backhaul term in (44) involves discrete sets U_m, which makes
the problem combinatorial and hard to solve directly. The paper
side-steps this by USING A SIMPLE UPPER BOUND:

    Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k∈U_m} SE_k   ≤   Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k=1}^{K} SE_k
                                              =   Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bt,m} · S_e

(since U_m ⊆ {1,…,K}, the inner sum is bounded by the full sum.)

With this upper bound, the backhaul term in the denominator of (44)
becomes Σ_m P_{bt,m} · S_e — exactly the same as in the ORIGINAL
problem (P)/(P1). But now γ̂_{m,k} has been substituted for γ_{m,k},
and the coefficients η_{m,k} for k ∉ U_m are effectively zero.

So the approximate EE maximization problem with AP selection is:

    max       B · S_e / [ P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ̂_{m,k}
                          + B · Σ_m P_{bt,m} · S_e ]

    s.t.      SE_k({η_{m,k}, γ̂}) ≥ S_{o,k}
              Σ_k η_{m,k} γ̂_{m,k}  ≤  1/N
              η_{m,k} ≥ 0.

This is EXACTLY the form solvable by Algorithm 1 — just replace the
original γ's by the selection-modified γ̂'s. No new solver is needed.

Why is the upper-bound approximation OK? Two reasons:

  (i)  The bound is tight when U_m = {1,…,K} (no selection). As more
       APs are pruned, the bound becomes looser, but the OBJECTIVE
       of the original (exact) problem is LARGER than what the bound
       suggests — so the approximated problem is PESSIMISTIC about
       EE and the true EE gain from AP selection is UNDERESTIMATED.
       That is a desirable direction for the approximation.

  (ii) Empirically, the authors verify in Section VI that the EE
       produced by this approximation is very close to the exact EE
       that would be obtained by directly optimizing (43) (they
       can check this with BRB on small problems).

─── 2.8  Algorithm 2: Received-power-based selection (Sec V-A) ─────────

Put it all together:

  Algorithm 2 (Received-power-based selection)

  Step 1. Choose ρ% (e.g. ρ = 95).
          Run Algorithm 1 on the FULL (no-selection) problem (P) to
          obtain the optimal power-control coefficients {η^*_{m,k}}.
          Compute the fractions p(m, k) from (42).

  Step 2. For each user k = 1,…,K:
             •  Sort p(m, k) in descending order.
             •  Initialize A_k ← { the top AP }.
             •  Walk down the sorted list, adding each next AP to A_k,
                until Σ_{m ∈ A_k} p(m, k) ≥ ρ/100.
             •  Done: A_k is now the minimal serving set for user k.

  Step 3. Derive U_m = { k : m ∈ A_k } for each AP m. Define
          γ̂_{m,k} = γ_{m,k} if k ∈ U_m and 0 otherwise.

  Step 4. Run Algorithm 1 AGAIN with γ̂ in place of γ. The result
          is the new {η^**_{m,k}} optimized for AP-selected operation.

Notes:
  •  Algorithm 2 requires TWO runs of Algorithm 1 (one to get the
     baseline {η^*}, one with γ̂) — roughly double the computational
     cost of Algorithm 1 alone.
  •  The {A_k}, {U_m} sets are recomputed whenever the large-scale
     fading {β_{m,k}} changes (i.e., slowly).
  •  The quality of Algorithm 2 depends on the choice of ρ. The
     paper uses ρ = 95, which typically selects 10–20% of the M APs.
     Smaller ρ → fewer APs but some useful signal is lost;
     larger ρ → more APs but less backhaul savings.

─── 2.9  Algorithm 3: Largest-large-scale-fading-based selection (Sec V-B) ─

The second scheme avoids the first run of Algorithm 1 by ranking APs
directly on their LARGE-SCALE FADING β_{m,k}. The intuition: if
AP m has a very large β_{m,k}, it is physically close to user k and
can contribute strongly regardless of η_{m,k}. Conversely, if β_{m,k}
is very small (far AP), no amount of power control will rescue it.

The criterion mirrors (42) but replaces p(m,k) with β_{m,k}:

     ┌── sorted in descending order ──┐
     β̃_{1,k}  ≥  β̃_{2,k}  ≥  …  ≥  β̃_{M,k}    (the sorted β's for user k)

Define A_k as the smallest set of APs satisfying:

     Σ_{m=1}^{M_{0,k}}  β̃_{m,k}  /  Σ_{m=1}^{M} β_{m,k}  ≥  ρ/100       (45)

i.e., the cumulative fraction of the sum of the largest M_{0,k} β's
must reach at least ρ%. The paper writes it as

          M_{0,k}    β̃_{m,k}
           Σ         ─────────   ≥   ρ%
         m = 1      Σ_{m=1}^{M} β_{m,k}

where M_{0,k} ≤ M is to be determined.

The rest of the algorithm is identical to Algorithm 2 from Step 3
onwards.

  Algorithm 3 (Largest-LSF-based selection)

  Step 1. Using criterion (45), each user k chooses a group of
          M_{0,k} serving APs corresponding to the M_{0,k} largest
          β_{m,k}. Determine the dual sets U_m.

  Step 2. Define γ̂_{m,k} = γ_{m,k} if k ∈ U_m and 0 otherwise.

  Step 3. Run Algorithm 1 once with γ̂ to find the optimal power-
          control coefficients {η^*_{m,k}}.

Advantages over Algorithm 2:

  •  Only ONE run of Algorithm 1, not two. Roughly half the compute.
  •  The set A_k depends only on {β_{m,k}}, which is LONG-TERM-STATIONARY
     information that the CPU already has (it is needed for computing
     γ_{m,k}). No power allocation is required first.
  •  The sets A_k are simpler to explain: "choose the closest APs."

Disadvantage: A_k is chosen without regard for power control or
interference. So the selected set can be slightly sub-optimal
compared to Algorithm 2. However, Section VI shows that the
EE gap between Algorithms 2 and 3 is SMALL in practice.

─── 2.10  Complexity and practical implications ───────────────────────

  •  Algorithm 2: 2× run of Algorithm 1, plus a K-sort (each of M
     numbers). Dominant cost is the two SOCP-SCA iterations —
     O(N_I × (MK)^3.5) each. For M = 100, K = 40 and N_I = 10
     iterations, this is roughly ~seconds on a standard laptop.

  •  Algorithm 3: 1× run of Algorithm 1 plus a K-sort. Half the time.

  •  Both schemes REDUCE the effective dimensionality of the
     optimization variable c ∈ ℝ^{MK} to a smaller set (the nonzero
     entries). For typical ρ = 95%, only ~15% of η_{m,k} are nonzero,
     so the effective MK is ~0.15·MK. Since the per-iteration SOCP
     cost is polynomial in the dimension, AP selection also SPEEDS UP
     the solver by a large factor.

  •  The "sets U_m" change only when the large-scale fading changes
     (i.e., on a ~second timescale in typical outdoor deployments).
     So Algorithms 2 and 3 are run infrequently. In between, the APs
     just apply the stored power-control coefficients.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3. HISTORICAL / PRIOR DEVELOPMENTS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Section V is the birthplace — together with the roughly concurrent
Buzzi–D'Andrea 2017 paper — of what is now called "user-centric
cell-free massive MIMO." A brief timeline:

▸ 2010 — Heath, Wu, Kwon, Soong, "Multiuser MIMO in distributed
  antenna systems with out-of-cell interference," IEEE TSP. Early
  treatment of AP subset selection in distributed antenna systems
  (DAS), based on SINR/throughput maximization.

▸ 2013 — Ngo, Larsson, Marzetta, "Energy and spectral efficiency
  of very large multiuser MIMO systems," IEEE TCom. Showed that,
  in colocated massive MIMO, AP selection is moot because there is
  only ONE AP. The paper is often cited for the "everything-on"
  nature of colocated massive MIMO.

▸ 2015 — Dai, Yu (ref [15] in Ngo paper), "Energy efficiency of
  downlink transmission strategies for cloud radio access networks."
  Explicitly formulates JOINT user association and power control for
  energy efficiency in C-RAN. The algorithmic approach (decomposing
  association + power-control via an MILP-like step and a convex-
  optimization step) is a precursor of the Ngo V/IV combination.

▸ Jan 2017 — Ngo, Ashikhmin, Yang, Larsson, Marzetta, IEEE TWC (ref
  [6] in Ngo paper): original cell-free paper. Does NOT use AP
  selection; all M APs serve all K users. So this Section V is the
  first formal AP selection in the CF-mMIMO literature.

▸ Feb 2017 — **Ngo-Tran-Duong-Matthaiou-Larsson (this paper,
  arXiv:1702.07601).** First explicit AP selection in CF-mMIMO,
  justified by energy-efficiency arguments.

▸ Dec 2017 — **Buzzi, D'Andrea, "Cell-free massive MIMO: User-
  centric approach," IEEE WCL**. This is the canonical "user-centric
  CF-mMIMO" paper. It proposes that each user k is served by only a
  subset of APs (called the user-centric CF approach). The selection
  criterion is similar in spirit to Algorithm 3 here — based on
  large-scale fading — but targeted at SE maximization with a
  different algorithmic framework. Ngo-2018-TGCN and Buzzi-2017-WCL
  are the two most-cited papers that launched user-centric CF-mMIMO.

▸ 2018–2019 — A wave of user-centric CF papers: Bashar-Cumanan-Burr
  (EE with user-centric clustering), Interdonato-Björnson-Ngo-Frenger-
  Larsson (scalable distributed precoding), Attarifar-Abbasfar-
  Lozano (modulation-constrained user-centric CF), etc. All cite
  Ngo-2018-TGCN and Buzzi-2017-WCL as precursors.

▸ 2020 — **Björnson, Sanguinetti, "Scalable Cell-Free Massive MIMO
  Systems," IEEE TCom**. Formalizes "scalability" as a property of
  CF-mMIMO deployments and proves that any CF-mMIMO variant where
  each AP serves all users (like the original [6]) is NOT scalable.
  Proposes Dynamic Cooperation Clustering (DCC) based on pilot
  assignment — a more sophisticated cousin of the Ngo-Section-V
  heuristics. You have already studied this paper (memory).

▸ 2021–present — A lot of ML-based AP selection (deep reinforcement
  learning, graph neural networks) to handle user mobility. All
  inherit the basic idea that each user should be served by a
  small subset of nearby APs, which Section V pioneered.

So Section V is historically important as one of the first two
papers to formalize user-centric AP selection in cell-free massive
MIMO. Its AP-selection heuristics (received-power-based and
largest-LSF-based) remain standard baselines that newer papers
compare against.

4. MATHEMATICAL RECAP IN UNICODE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Notation:
   A_k   ⊆ {1,…,M}      set of APs that serve user k
   U_m   ⊆ {1,…,K}      set of users served by AP m
                         (note: m ∈ A_k  ⇔  k ∈ U_m)
   ρ     ∈ (0, 100]      threshold percent (paper default 95)
   γ̂_{m,k}              modified γ:  γ̂_{m,k} = γ_{m,k} if k ∈ U_m, 0 otherwise

Modified per-AP backhaul power  (40):
   P_{bh,m}  =  P_{0,m}  +  B · P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k ∈ U_m} SE_k

Received-power fraction at AP m from user k  (42):
   p(m, k)  =  γ_{m,k} · η_{m,k}  /  Σ_{m'=1}^{M} γ_{m',k} · η_{m',k}

Received-power-based selection criterion:
   find smallest M_k such that
   Σ_{i=1}^{M_k} p(π_k(i), k)  ≥  ρ/100,
   where π_k sorts p(·, k) in descending order.
   Set A_k = {π_k(1),…,π_k(M_k)}.

Largest-LSF-based selection criterion  (45):
   find smallest M_{0,k} such that
   Σ_{i=1}^{M_{0,k}} β̃_{i,k}  /  Σ_{m=1}^{M} β_{m,k}  ≥  ρ/100,
   where β̃ sorts β_{·,k} in descending order.

Modified optimization problem (P2), after γ̂-substitution (44):
   max       B · S_e({η_{m,k}; γ̂})   /   [ P̄_fix
                                + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ̂_{m,k}
                                + B · (Σ_m P_{bt,m}) · S_e ]
   s.t.      SE_k({η_{m,k}; γ̂}) ≥ S_{o,k}
             Σ_k η_{m,k} γ̂_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,   m = 1,…,M
             η_{m,k} ≥ 0.

Backhaul upper-bound approximation:
   Σ_m P_{bt,m} Σ_{k ∈ U_m} SE_k  ≤  Σ_m P_{bt,m} · S_e

After approximation, (44) reduces to the form of (P1) in Section IV
with γ̂ in place of γ. Algorithm 1 solves it directly.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5. NUMERICAL WORKED EXAMPLE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Let us walk through Algorithm 3 on a moderate-size toy with M = 10
APs, K = 3 users, ρ = 95%.

─── Setup ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Large-scale fading (in linear scale):

        β_{m,1}  β_{m,2}  β_{m,3}
   m=1   0.50     0.003    0.002
   m=2   0.30     0.005    0.001
   m=3   0.20     0.010    0.005
   m=4   0.050    0.40     0.003
   m=5   0.020    0.30     0.006
   m=6   0.010    0.20     0.010
   m=7   0.002    0.05     0.40
   m=8   0.003    0.02     0.30
   m=9   0.005    0.01     0.20
   m=10  0.001    0.003    0.10

The topology is three "clusters" of strong APs for the three users.
User 1's strong APs are m = 1, 2, 3; user 2's are m = 4, 5, 6;
user 3's are m = 7, 8, 9, 10.

─── Step 1: sum of β's per user ────────────────────────────────────────

   Σ_m β_{m,1}  =  0.50 + 0.30 + 0.20 + 0.050 + 0.020 + 0.010
                   + 0.002 + 0.003 + 0.005 + 0.001  =  1.091
   Σ_m β_{m,2}  =  0.003 + 0.005 + 0.010 + 0.40 + 0.30 + 0.20
                   + 0.05 + 0.02 + 0.01 + 0.003  =  1.001
   Σ_m β_{m,3}  =  0.002 + 0.001 + 0.005 + 0.003 + 0.006 + 0.010
                   + 0.40 + 0.30 + 0.20 + 0.10  =  1.027

─── Step 2: choose A_k with ρ = 95% ────────────────────────────────────

User 1:
   Sort descending: 0.50, 0.30, 0.20, 0.050, 0.020, 0.010, 0.005, 0.003,
                    0.002, 0.001.
   Cumulative:      0.50, 0.80, 1.00, 1.05, 1.07, 1.08, 1.085, 1.088,
                    1.090, 1.091.
   Target:          0.95 · 1.091  =  1.036.
   Smallest M_{0,1}:  first index where cumulative ≥ 1.036, which is
                     i=4 (cumulative 1.05 after adding AP m=4 with β=0.05).
   So A_1 = {1, 2, 3, 4}, four APs out of ten.

User 2:
   Sort descending: 0.40, 0.30, 0.20, 0.05, 0.02, 0.01, 0.010, 0.005,
                    0.003, 0.003.
   Cumulative:      0.40, 0.70, 0.90, 0.95, 0.97, 0.98, 0.99, 0.995,
                    0.998, 1.001.
   Target:          0.95 · 1.001  =  0.951.
   Smallest M_{0,2}: i=4 (cumulative 0.95) FAILS, i=5 (cumulative 0.97)
                    PASSES.
   Wait — 0.95 < 0.951. So we need i=5.
   Actually, 0.95 ≈ 0.951, barely under the target. Let us pick i=5:
   cumulative = 0.97, safely above 0.951. So M_{0,2} = 5.
   A_2 = {4, 5, 6, 7, 3} — i.e. the 5 APs corresponding to the 5
   largest β_{m,2}. Looking at the unsorted list, the top 5 are
   AP 4 (0.40), AP 5 (0.30), AP 6 (0.20), AP 7 (0.05), AP 3 (0.01).
   So A_2 = {3, 4, 5, 6, 7}.

User 3:
   Sort descending: 0.40, 0.30, 0.20, 0.10, 0.010, 0.006, 0.005, 0.003,
                    0.002, 0.001.
   Cumulative:      0.40, 0.70, 0.90, 1.00, 1.010, 1.016, 1.021, 1.024,
                    1.026, 1.027.
   Target:          0.95 · 1.027  =  0.976.
   Smallest M_{0,3}: i=4 (cumulative 1.00) PASSES (since 1.00 ≥ 0.976).
   So A_3 = {7, 8, 9, 10}.

─── Step 3: build U_m ──────────────────────────────────────────────────

   m=1:  U_1 = {1}         (in A_1 only)
   m=2:  U_2 = {1}
   m=3:  U_3 = {1, 2}      (in A_1 and A_2)
   m=4:  U_4 = {1, 2}
   m=5:  U_5 = {2}
   m=6:  U_6 = {2}
   m=7:  U_7 = {2, 3}      (in A_2 and A_3)
   m=8:  U_8 = {3}
   m=9:  U_9 = {3}
   m=10: U_10 = {3}

─── Step 4: count active associations ─────────────────────────────────

Without AP selection: M × K = 10 × 3 = 30 (m, k) pairs.

With selection: count |A_1| + |A_2| + |A_3| = 4 + 5 + 4 = 13.

That is a reduction of (30 − 13)/30 ≈ 57% in the number of active
power-control coefficients — and therefore ~57% reduction in the
traffic-dependent backhaul power. In a larger system (M = 100 APs,
ρ = 95), the paper reports that typically only 10–20% of APs per
user are active, i.e. a reduction of 80–90%!

─── Step 5: backhaul savings estimate ──────────────────────────────────

Suppose B = 20 MHz, S_e = 3 bit/s/Hz (one per user), Pbt,m = 0.25
W/(Gbit/s) for all m, so Σ_m Pbt,m = 2.5 W/(Gbit/s).

Without selection:
   traffic BH = B · S_e · Σ_m Pbt,m = 0.06 Gbit/s · 2.5 W/(Gbit/s)
             = 0.15 W.

With selection (upper bound approximation):
   approximately the same 0.15 W as above (because the upper
   bound replaces U_m by {1,...,K}).

But the ACTUAL traffic backhaul, computed AP-by-AP:
   m=1:  Pbt · B · SE_1                  = 0.25·0.02·1 = 0.005 W
   m=2:  0.005 W
   m=3:  Pbt · B · (SE_1 + SE_2)         = 0.25·0.02·2 = 0.010 W
   m=4:  0.010 W
   m=5:  Pbt · B · SE_2                  = 0.005 W
   m=6:  0.005 W
   m=7:  Pbt · B · (SE_2 + SE_3)         = 0.010 W
   m=8:  0.005 W
   m=9:  0.005 W
   m=10: 0.005 W
   Total = 0.065 W.

So actual BH is 0.065 W, whereas the upper-bound used in (44) is
0.15 W. The approximation is conservative, as predicted. The true
EE is HIGHER than what Algorithm 3 reports — i.e., Algorithm 3
underestimates the benefit of selection. In practice this is fine
because the approximation is still much better than doing no
selection at all, AND it keeps the SOCP form simple.

─── PRACTICE PROBLEM ────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Same β matrix as above, but now set ρ = 80% (instead of 95%).

(a) Recompute |A_k| for each user.
(b) What is the total number of active (m, k) associations?
(c) Compute the actual traffic-dependent backhaul power under the
    assumption SE_k = 1 bit/s/Hz, B = 20 MHz, P_{bt,m} = 0.25 W /
    (Gbit/s).
(d) What fraction of the "no selection" traffic BH remains?

[Sketch of expected answers:
 (a) Probably |A_1| = |A_2| = |A_3| = 3 (the three strongest APs
     for each user).
 (b) 9 active pairs.
 (c) Every active AP serves exactly 1 user, so BH = 9 · 0.25 ·
     0.02 · 1 = 0.045 W.
 (d) 0.045 / 0.15 = 30% of the no-selection BH. A 70% saving,
     at the cost of some signal loss because ρ = 80% < 95%. ]


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. SUMMARY OF SECTION V
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Motivation. Section IV's Algorithm 1 only tunes power-control
  coefficients — it cannot reduce the Σ_m P_{bt,m} backhaul term,
  because that term depends only on M (number of APs) and S_e
  (sum rate), not on η. For large M the backhaul dominates the
  total power and the only way to reduce it is to REDUCE the
  number of active (m,k) associations.

• Two guiding observations.
     (O1) If AP m only serves users in U_m, its backhaul only
          carries those users' rates → backhaul becomes
          P_{bh,m} = P_{0,m} + B · P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k∈U_m} SE_k.
     (O2) For any given user k, far-away APs contribute negligible
          desired-signal power anyway, so dropping them barely
          hurts SE.
  Together, they motivate a user-centric serving set A_k for
  each user.

• Scheme A: Received-power-based selection (Algorithm 2).
     – Run Algorithm 1 once to get baseline η^*.
     – Compute received-power fraction p(m,k) from γ and η^*.
     – Sort p(·, k) descending, pick the top-M_k APs cumulatively
       ≥ ρ% (ρ = 95 by default). Set A_k = those.
     – Derive U_m = {k : m ∈ A_k}.
     – Re-run Algorithm 1 with γ̂_{m,k} = γ_{m,k}·1_{k∈U_m}.
  Advantage: more accurate because it uses the actual optimal η.
  Disadvantage: two Algorithm-1 runs.

• Scheme B: Largest-large-scale-fading-based selection (Algorithm 3).
     – Rank APs by β_{m,k} directly (no power control needed).
     – Pick the top M_{0,k} APs cumulatively ≥ ρ% of Σ_m β_{m,k}.
     – Derive U_m and γ̂.
     – Run Algorithm 1 once with γ̂.
  Advantage: only one run, uses long-term-stationary information.
  Disadvantage: slightly sub-optimal (does not see η).

• Backhaul upper-bound approximation. The selection-modified
  objective has Σ_m P_{bt,m} Σ_{k ∈ U_m} SE_k, which is combinatorial.
  The paper upper-bounds it by Σ_m P_{bt,m} · S_e, which recovers
  the same form as the original (P)/(P1) — so Algorithm 1 can be
  reused without modification, just with γ̂ in place of γ. The
  bound is pessimistic about EE and tight when U_m = {1,…,K}.

• Practical implications.
     – With ρ = 95%, only ~10–20% of the M APs per user are active.
     – Backhaul power is slashed by roughly 80–90%.
     – Per-SOCP dimensionality is reduced to ~15% of the full MK,
       speeding up the solver.
     – The sets A_k, U_m change only when β changes (~second scale),
       so selection is run infrequently.

• Historical significance. Together with Buzzi-D'Andrea 2017
  (IEEE WCL), Section V pioneered the "user-centric cell-free
  massive MIMO" paradigm that is now the dominant CF-mMIMO
  deployment philosophy. Every scalable CF-mMIMO paper since
  (including Björnson-Sanguinetti 2020) is a descendant of these
  ideas.

• The one-line takeaway.
     "Each user should be served by the ~15% of nearest APs.
      Everybody wins: lower backhaul power, smaller optimization,
      same spectral efficiency — and cell-free massive MIMO
      finally becomes scalable."
