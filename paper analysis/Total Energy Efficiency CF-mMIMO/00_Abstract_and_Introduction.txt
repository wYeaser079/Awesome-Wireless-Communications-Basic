───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. HIGH-LEVEL / LAYMAN EXPLANATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Imagine a football stadium filled with spectators (users) who are all trying
to watch a live-stream on their phones simultaneously. In the classical
cellular model, you place a few very tall towers at the corners of the
stadium. The people sitting in the middle of the stadium get a strong signal,
but the poor fans near the fence — right between two towers — get terrible
reception because the two towers' signals interfere with each other and
neither is close enough.

"Cell-free massive MIMO" (CF-mMIMO) turns this picture inside-out. Instead
of 3 or 4 huge towers, you scatter HUNDREDS of tiny antennas (each one is
called an "access point" or AP) all over the stadium — on every light-post,
every railing, every seat-row. Every access point cooperates with every other
access point through a wired backhaul that connects them to one central
processing unit (CPU). All of them *simultaneously* transmit to every user at
the same time, using the same frequency band, and they beam-shape their
signals so that the waves add up *constructively* exactly at the user's
phone and *destructively* everywhere else. The word "cell-free" means there
are no more cells and no more cell edges — every user is effectively at the
"center" because antennas are always nearby.

But there is a catch nobody wants to talk about: running all those little
antennas costs electricity, and more importantly, the wired backhaul
connecting them all back to the CPU also costs a lot of power (fiber
transceivers, baseband processors, etc.). Even if the system provides a
blazing-fast data rate, if every megabit cost you a whole joule of power,
the system would be a green disaster. So a natural question is:

    "Is cell-free massive MIMO actually ENERGY EFFICIENT,
     once you honestly include the backhaul power cost?"

This paper answers that question. It does four concrete things:

(a) It derives a clean closed-form formula for the per-user data rate
    (spectral efficiency, SE) when every AP has MULTIPLE antennas (the
    earlier Ngo-2017 paper only treated single-antenna APs).

(b) It writes down a realistic power model that counts EVERY power source:
    (i) the radio-frequency amplifier, (ii) hardware (mixers, filters,
    converters) per antenna, (iii) a fixed backhaul cost per AP, and
    (iv) a "traffic-dependent" backhaul cost that grows with the data
    rate you push down the backhaul.

(c) It formulates the energy-efficiency maximization as an optimization
    problem (with a minimum-rate guarantee per user and a per-AP power
    budget), shows it is NON-convex, then uses Sequential Convex
    Approximation (SCA) to turn it into a sequence of Second-Order Cone
    Programs (SOCPs) that modern solvers (Gurobi, MOSEK) can handle.

(d) It proposes two *AP-selection* heuristics so that each user is served by
    only a small subset of nearby APs, not by all M APs. This slashes the
    backhaul power and — perhaps counter-intuitively — almost does not hurt
    the spectral efficiency, because the faraway APs were barely
    contributing anyway.

Headline result (from the numerical section): with a 1 bit/s/Hz quality-of-
service (QoS) constraint per user, CF-mMIMO beats conventional colocated
massive MIMO in energy efficiency BY AN ORDER OF MAGNITUDE (roughly 7.4×
when MN = 128 and 2.2× when MN = 256 service antennas). That is what made
this paper an instant staple of the CF-mMIMO energy-efficiency literature.

2. DETAILED PHD-LEVEL TECHNICAL EXPLANATION OF THE INTRODUCTION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Introduction (Section I) plays three roles:

   (i)   It frames the *problem* in its historical context (network MIMO,
         coordinated multi-point, distributed antenna systems).
   (ii)  It reviews the *state-of-the-art* of cell-free massive MIMO as
         of early 2017, identifying the gap the paper will fill.
   (iii) It enumerates the paper's *contributions* and the *organization*
         of the remaining sections.

Let us unpack each of these carefully, adding context the authors assume
you already know.

─── 2.1. The pain point: inter-cell interference in cellular networks ─────

The opening paragraph reminds us that conventional cellular networks are
INTERFERENCE-LIMITED, not noise-limited. If Alice is sitting at the edge of
cell 𝑗 she hears a "desired" signal 𝑃ᴅ from BS-𝑗 and "interfering" signals
𝑃ᵢ from BS-𝑖, 𝑖 ≠ 𝑗. Her cell-edge SINR is roughly

          SINR_edge  ≈  𝑃ᴅ / ( 𝑃ᵢ + 𝜎² )

and at the edge 𝑃ᴅ ≈ 𝑃ᵢ, so SINR_edge is of order 0 dB — terrible compared
to the ≈20 dB that a cell-center user might enjoy. This creates the infamous
"5-percentile / cell-edge problem": the bottom 5% of users have two orders
of magnitude worse throughput than the median user.

─── 2.2. How did we try to fix this? Network MIMO ─────────────────────────

The accepted remedy, dating back to the mid-2000s, is to let neighbouring
BSs *cooperate* instead of fighting each other. The same concept appeared
under several names:

  •  Network MIMO  (Karakayali-Foschini-Valenzuela 2006, ref [1])
  •  Coordinated Multi-Point transmission / reception  (CoMP) — this is
     the 3GPP LTE-Advanced terminology introduced in Rel-11 (~2012).
  •  Distributed Antenna Systems (DAS)  — used in the cellular industry
     since the 1990s for in-building coverage.

In all three the idea is the same: the BSs share channel state information
(CSI) and possibly user data over a high-capacity backhaul, then they jointly
design the downlink precoder so that the (formerly interfering) signals
become coherent contributions to every user's desired signal.

There is, however, a famous negative result that the paper flags:
Lozano-Heath-Andrews 2013 [2] proved that with full BS cooperation and
a finite-density deployment, the ergodic per-user capacity is upper-bounded
by a FINITE constant even as the transmit power goes to infinity. The reason
is out-of-cluster interference plus pilot contamination. This means
"full-cooperation network MIMO" is not the silver bullet people once hoped
for — BUT it still gives large, practically useful gains compared to no
cooperation at all. Hence the continued interest ([3]–[5]).

The practical enemies of network MIMO are:
   (a) huge backhaul overhead (you must ship precoded symbols + CSI
       between every pair of cooperating BSs),
   (b) very high deployment cost (fiber everywhere),
   (c) a sufficiently fast & capable central processing unit (CPU).

─── 2.3. Cell-free massive MIMO: the new embodiment ──────────────────────

Cell-free massive MIMO was introduced as a *practical* and *scalable*
flavour of network MIMO in Ngo, Ashikhmin, Yang, Larsson, Marzetta 2017 [6]
("Cell-free Massive MIMO versus Small Cells", IEEE TWC). Its defining
features are:

  •  A very large number M of APs, each with few antennas (originally one).
  •  All APs are connected via a backhaul to one or more CPUs.
  •  All APs serve all K single-antenna users on the SAME time-frequency
     resource (universal frequency reuse, no hand-overs, no cells).
  •  CSI is acquired by TDD uplink pilots; channel reciprocity gives the
     downlink CSI for free. This is the classical massive-MIMO trick that
     makes CSI overhead grow as O(K) instead of O(M·K).
  •  Each AP applies LOCAL precoding using the CSI it estimated for
     itself (a "distributed" scheme). Matched-filter / conjugate beam-
     forming is the simplest such choice and is what the paper uses.

The marriage of "distributed antennas" with "massive MIMO TDD" is exactly
what makes cell-free massive MIMO interesting. From massive MIMO it
inherits:
   •  channel-hardening (|g|² concentrates around 𝔼{|g|²}) and
   •  favorable propagation (channels to different users become
      asymptotically orthogonal),
so simple matched filtering becomes near-optimal with a large number of
service antennas.

From distributed antennas it inherits:
   •  macro-diversity — every user sees at least one AP with line of
      sight,
   •  much smaller average path loss,
   •  spatially uniform quality of service (no cell-edge users).

Reference [6] had already shown that a single-antenna-per-AP CF-mMIMO
system beats a small-cell deployment of the same total number of antennas,
especially in the low-outage regime.

─── 2.4. The research gap the paper is filling ───────────────────────────

As of Feb-2017 the literature on CF-mMIMO was thin:

  •  [6] originally studied conjugate beamforming, single-antenna APs,
     orthogonal or non-orthogonal pilots.
  •  [8] (Nayebi-Ashikhmin-Marzetta-Yang, Asilomar 2015) studied
     zero-forcing, but under the simplifying assumption that all pilots
     were mutually orthogonal — i.e. no pilot contamination.
  •  [9] (Interdonato-Ngo-Larsson-Frenger, GLOBECOM 2016) studied
     downlink training with beamformed pilots, showing that beamformed
     pilots substantially improve performance.
  •  [10] (Huang-Burr 2016) proposed compute-and-forward to reduce the
     backhaul load.

Everyone above assumed single-antenna APs. But APs can of course be
equipped with multiple antennas, which trades the number of APs for the
number of antennas per AP, and which affects the array gain, the
macro-diversity, and very critically the backhaul power cost. Furthermore,
while colocated massive MIMO had already been shown to be energy-efficient
(Björnson-Sanguinetti-Hoydis-Debbah 2015 [11], the canonical reference),
NOBODY had honestly quantified the EE of cell-free massive MIMO, because
nobody had included the backhaul cost in the power model. This paper is
therefore:

   (a) the first CF-mMIMO paper with multi-antenna APs and a closed-form
       SE expression;
   (b) the first CF-mMIMO paper with a rigorous EE analysis that includes
       backhaul power;
   (c) the first to propose AP-selection schemes that were explicitly
       justified by EE considerations rather than only SE or complexity.

─── 2.5. The four claimed contributions, in a little more depth ─────────

▸ Contribution 1 — Closed-form SE expression. The authors derive an
  exact (not asymptotic!) closed-form for the achievable downlink SE
  of user k with arbitrary K, arbitrary M, arbitrary N ≥ 1, arbitrary
  pilot sequences (even non-orthogonal, so pilot contamination is built
  in). This generalizes two known results:

     - Setting N = 1 recovers ref. [6, Eq. (24)]: the original CF-mMIMO
       with single-antenna APs.
     - Setting M = 1 and renaming N → M (so you lump all antennas onto
       ONE AP) recovers the colocated massive-MIMO SE of [7, Table 3.2].

  The derivation relies on the classical "use-and-then-forget" bound
  that is standard in the massive-MIMO capacity literature (Marzetta,
  Larsson, Yang, Ngo textbook 2016, ref. [7]): because of channel
  hardening, the user can treat 𝔼{𝐠ᵀ ĝ*} as the "known" channel gain
  and lump the deviation into a Gaussian-equivalent "effective noise".

▸ Contribution 2 — Optimal power control for EE. The problem

       maximize  Ee  s.t.  per-user SE ≥ S_o,k
                             per-AP power ≤ 1/N

  is a fractional program with a non-convex numerator and a non-convex
  set of SE constraints. The authors:

     (i) change variables c_mk = √η_mk to kill the square-roots,
    (ii) use Charnes–Cooper / linear-fractional transformation ideas to
         turn the objective into a linear function,
   (iii) approximate the SE constraint from below by first-order Taylor
         expansion + an ln(x) ≤ x − 1 log-inequality,
    (iv) reformulate everything as a Second-Order Cone Program (SOCP),
         which is then solved iteratively by the SCA method. Each
         iteration is a standard SOCP (polynomial time), and the sequence
         of objectives is monotonically non-decreasing and bounded
         above, hence convergent.

▸ Contribution 3 — Two AP-selection schemes. If M is very large the
  second term in the denominator of Ee — the backhaul power, which is
  proportional to M · Se — dominates and crushes the EE. Two fixes:

     (a) Received-power-based selection: for each user k, rank all M APs
         by the fraction of the useful received desired-signal power
         they contribute, then keep only the top few that together
         contribute ≥ ρ% (they use ρ = 95%) of the total.

     (b) Largest-large-scale-fading-based selection: for each user k,
         rank APs by β_mk (the large-scale fading coefficient, essentially
         path loss × shadow fading) and keep only the top few that
         cumulatively contribute ≥ ρ% of the total large-scale fading.

  Scheme (b) is simpler because it does not require solving Algorithm 1
  first. Numerically they give almost the same EE, and they cut the
  number of serving APs per user from M down to roughly 10–20% of M.

▸ Contribution 4 — Quantitative CF vs. colocated comparison. With their
  power-control + AP-selection in place, the paper shows that under a
  uniform per-user QoS requirement CF-mMIMO trounces colocated mMIMO
  in EE, with the margin growing at lower MN.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3. HISTORICAL / PRIOR DEVELOPMENTS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

To really understand WHY this paper matters, you need a timeline of how
the community arrived at "cell-free massive MIMO + energy efficiency."

▸ 1987 — Wyner's circular/linear cellular model (the "Wyner array") is
  the first analytically tractable multi-cell model with BS cooperation.
  Not practical, but it seeded the idea that joint processing could boost
  capacity.

▸ 1990s–2000s — Distributed Antenna Systems (DAS) deployed as passive
  in-building coverage solutions. No joint processing, pure coverage.

▸ 2006 — Karakayali, Foschini, Valenzuela publish the seminal "Network
  Coordination for Spectrally Efficient Communications" paper [1]. This
  is the reference for the term "Network MIMO." They propose that BSs
  share user data and CSI over a high-speed backhaul and jointly precode.

▸ 2010 — Björnson-Zakhour-Gesbert-Ottersten [3] give the multicell
  precoding rate region with instantaneous and statistical CSI, and
  distributed coordination strategies. This is the beginning of the
  "limited/imperfect CSI sharing" thread.

▸ 2010 — Marzetta's landmark paper "Noncooperative Cellular Wireless with
  Unlimited Numbers of Base Station Antennas" (IEEE TWC, Nov. 2010, not
  in this paper's reference list but canonical) launches *massive* MIMO.
  Massive MIMO says: cooperation is hard, but if you put a huge number
  of antennas at one base station, channel hardening + favorable
  propagation let simple linear processing do almost everything you
  hoped joint processing would do, at a small fraction of the
  cooperation cost.

▸ 2011–2014 — 3GPP LTE-Advanced standardizes CoMP (JT-CoMP, DPS, CS/CB)
  in Release 11. Real deployment is severely limited by backhaul
  latency and capacity.

▸ 2013 — Lozano-Heath-Andrews "Fundamental Limits of Cooperation" [2]
  shows that even with unlimited cooperation the per-user rate is
  bounded by a finite constant (due to out-of-cluster interference),
  dashing hopes of "cooperation → infinite rate."

▸ 2014 — 3GPP LTE Rel-12/13 begin exploring ultra-dense networks and
  C-RAN (Cloud-RAN) to realize distributed antennas with a central
  baseband processing pool. This is the industrial parallel of
  cell-free thinking.

▸ 2015 — Björnson, Sanguinetti, Hoydis, Debbah publish "Optimal Design
  of Energy-Efficient Multi-User MIMO Systems: Is Massive MIMO the
  Answer?" (IEEE TWC, [11]). This is the canonical EE paper for
  colocated massive MIMO. It introduces the now-standard power
  consumption model that the Ngo et al. paper under study extends:
  total power = amplifier + per-antenna circuit + per-UE baseband +
  coding/decoding + backhaul, with the last term scaling linearly
  with the sum rate. This is THE reference model for EE analyses.

▸ 2015 — Nayebi, Ashikhmin, Marzetta, Yang introduce the CF-mMIMO
  terminology at Asilomar [8], analyzing zero-forcing under orthogonal
  pilots.

▸ 2016 — Cell-free papers multiply: [9] beamformed downlink pilots,
  [10] compute-and-forward backhaul compression.

▸ Jan 2017 — Ngo, Ashikhmin, Yang, Larsson, Marzetta publish the
  reference cell-free paper "Cell-Free Massive MIMO versus Small
  Cells" in IEEE TWC [6]. Single-antenna APs, conjugate beamforming,
  MMSE channel estimation with pilot contamination. This paper sets
  up the canonical CF-mMIMO framework.

▸ Feb 2017 — The paper under study (arXiv:1702.07601) extends [6]
  to multi-antenna APs and performs the first rigorous EE analysis
  with a backhaul power term. Published in IEEE TGCN Mar 2018.

▸ 2018–present — A flood of follow-up work: user-centric CF
  (Buzzi-D'Andrea 2017), scalable CF (Björnson-Sanguinetti 2020),
  fronthaul-limited CF, RIS-aided CF, CF with LSFD, etc. The
  energy-efficiency narrative of the 2017-Ngo paper is repeatedly
  cited as the first to warn that backhaul cost can flip the
  CF-mMIMO EE advantage if not carefully managed.

The *single* most important prior reference for this paper is [11]
(Björnson et al. 2015), because it supplies the power consumption
model; and [6] (Ngo et al. 2017 TWC), because it supplies the system
model that is being generalized.

4. MATHEMATICAL PREVIEW OF THE INTRODUCTION
   (full formal math appears in later sections; here we expose the scaffolding)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Introduction does not contain numbered equations. But to prepare you
for Section II, here are the key objects the Introduction implicitly
introduces, written in clean Unicode.

▸ Number of APs          :  M
▸ Number of users        :  K  (assumed M ≫ K, i.e. massive regime)
▸ Antennas per AP        :  N  (≥ 1 — the new generalization)
▸ Total service antennas :  MN
▸ Coherence interval     :  τ_c     (samples)
▸ Pilot length           :  τ_p < τ_c    (samples)
▸ Downlink payload       :  τ_c − τ_p    (samples)
▸ Pilot sequence of user k :  𝛗_k ∈ ℂ^{τ_p}, with ‖𝛗_k‖ = 1
▸ Channel of AP m to user k :
        𝐠_{m,k} = √β_{m,k} · 𝐡_{m,k},     𝐡_{m,k} ~ 𝒞𝒩(𝟎_N, 𝐈_N)
   where β_{m,k} is the deterministic large-scale fading (path-loss ×
   shadow fading).

▸ MMSE estimate of 𝐠_{m,k} :
        ĝ_{m,k} = α_{m,k} · 𝐲_{p,m}^{(k)}
   where 𝐲_{p,m}^{(k)} = 𝐘_{p,m} · 𝛗_k^{*}  is the despread received
   pilot, and α_{m,k} is the MMSE scalar. The mean square of each
   component is denoted γ_{m,k} (see Sec II).

▸ Conjugate-beamforming downlink transmit signal from AP m :
        𝐱_m = √ρ_d · Σ_{k=1}^{K} √η_{m,k} · ĝ_{m,k}^{*} · q_k

  where q_k is the unit-energy data symbol of user k, ρ_d is the
  maximum normalized transmit SNR, and η_{m,k} ≥ 0 are the power
  control coefficients that we will later OPTIMIZE for EE.

▸ Per-AP power budget :
        Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} · γ_{m,k}  ≤  1 / N,     m = 1, …, M

▸ Total downlink power consumption (anticipating Sec III-A) :
        P_total  =  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_m  +  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bh,m}
  with
        P_m    =  (1/ν_m) · ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}  +  N · P_{tc,m}
        P_{bh,m} = P_{0,m}  +  B · S_e · P_{bt,m}

  Here:
   • ν_m     ∈ (0, 1] is the PA efficiency of AP m
   • P_{tc,m} is per-antenna circuit power
   • P_{0,m}  is fixed backhaul power per AP
   • P_{bt,m} is traffic-dependent backhaul power (W per bit/s)
   • B       is system bandwidth, S_e is sum spectral efficiency

▸ Total energy efficiency (the object being maximized in Sec IV) :

                    B · S_e ({η_{m,k}})
        E_e   =  ─────────────────────────────       [bit / Joule]
                    P_total ({η_{m,k}})

All of these symbols become explicit equations in Section II and III.
The whole paper is a story about how to compute E_e in closed form and
then maximize it.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5. NUMERICAL WORKED EXAMPLE (to ground the story)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Let's pick a small, hand-computable configuration that illustrates why
the backhaul cost can be the dominant piece of the EE denominator.

Take:
   • M          = 100 APs
   • N          = 1  antenna per AP (single-antenna case)
   • K          = 20 users
   • Bandwidth  : B   = 20 MHz   (so 20 × 10⁶ Hz)
   • Per-user SE target : S_o,k = 1 bit/s/Hz   → S_e = K · 1 = 20 bit/s/Hz
   • ν_m        = 0.4  (PA efficiency, 40%)
   • P_{tc,m}   = 0.2 W per antenna
   • P_{0,m}    = 0.825 W per AP (fixed backhaul)
   • P_{bt,m}   = 0.25 W / (Gbit/s) = 0.25 × 10⁻⁹ W / (bit/s)
   • ρ_d        = 1 W (normalized transmit power)
   • N_0        = thermal noise power at 9 dB noise figure and
                   B = 20 MHz ≈ 4 × 10⁻¹³ W  (we approximate −134 dBW)

Step 1 — Sum throughput :
   R_sum = B · S_e = 2 × 10⁷ · 20 = 4 × 10⁸ bit/s = 400 Mbit/s = 0.4 Gbit/s

Step 2 — PA + circuit power  (assume power control uses full budget) :
   Per AP PA draw = (ρ_d · N · 1/ν_m) · [Σ_k η_{m,k}γ_{m,k}]
                 ≈ (1 · 1 / 0.4) · (1/N)
                 = 2.5 · 1   = 2.5 W
   Plus per-antenna circuit: N · P_{tc,m} = 1 · 0.2 = 0.2 W
   So   P_m ≈ 2.7 W   per AP
   Total over M=100 APs:  Σ_m P_m ≈ 270 W

Step 3 — Backhaul power :
   Fixed part:   Σ_m P_{0,m}            = 100 · 0.825 = 82.5 W
   Traffic part: B · S_e · Σ_m P_{bt,m} = 4 × 10⁸ · 100 · 2.5 × 10⁻¹⁰
                                        = 4 × 10⁸ · 2.5 × 10⁻⁸
                                        = 10 W
   Total backhaul ≈ 82.5 + 10 = 92.5 W

Step 4 — Total power :
   P_total ≈ 270 + 92.5 = 362.5 W

Step 5 — Energy efficiency :
   E_e = R_sum / P_total = 4 × 10⁸ / 362.5
       ≈ 1.10 × 10⁶  bit/Joule
       ≈ 1.10  Mbit/Joule

Observation: the backhaul accounts for roughly 92.5 / 362.5 ≈ 25% of
the total power already at M = 100, and more importantly the FIXED
backhaul part (82.5 W) alone is non-trivial. Now imagine M grows to 500:

   Fixed backhaul:  500 · 0.825 ≈ 412.5 W  (↑ 5×)
   Circuit / PA:    500 · 2.7  ≈ 1350 W   (↑ 5×)
   Traffic-dep. backhaul:   4 × 10⁸ · 500 · 2.5 × 10⁻¹⁰ ≈ 50 W
   Total:           ≈ 1812.5 W

The new EE would be ≈ 4 × 10⁸ / 1.8 × 10³ ≈ 0.22 Mbit/J — EE collapsed to
one fifth! That is precisely why the paper screams "backhaul matters"
and then introduces AP selection. With AP selection, only ~10–20% of
the 500 APs per user are actually active, so the traffic-dependent
backhaul and a large chunk of the PA power goes away.

─── PRACTICE PROBLEM ────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Repeat the five-step calculation above for:

   M = 200,  K = 40,  N = 4 antennas per AP,
   S_o,k = 2 bit/s/Hz (so S_e = 80 bit/s/Hz),
   other parameters as in Table I of the paper
   (ν_m = 0.4, P_{tc,m} = 0.2 W, P_{0,m} = 0.825 W, P_{bt,m} = 0.25 W/(Gbit/s),
    B = 20 MHz, ρ_d = 1 W).

(a) Compute R_sum.
(b) Compute Σ_m P_m (remember N enters both the PA and the circuit term).
(c) Compute the fixed + traffic-dependent backhaul power.
(d) Compute E_e in Mbit/Joule.

[Expected sanity check answers:
   (a) R_sum = 1.6 × 10⁹ bit/s = 1.6 Gbit/s
   (b) per-AP PA ≈ 2.5 W, per-AP circuit = 4·0.2 = 0.8 W,
       per-AP total ≈ 3.3 W, Σ_m P_m ≈ 660 W
   (c) fixed BH = 200·0.825 = 165 W,
       traffic BH = 1.6 × 10⁹ · 200 · 2.5 × 10⁻¹⁰ = 80 W,
       total BH = 245 W
   (d) P_total ≈ 905 W, E_e ≈ 1.77 Mbit/J ]

Notice that increasing N (from 1 → 4) and K (from 20 → 40) raised E_e
despite higher total power, because R_sum grew faster. But the fixed
backhaul part is identical whether or not users are actually active —
this is the term that AP selection is trying to kill.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. SUMMARY OF SECTION I
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Problem context. Conventional cellular networks are interference-limited
  at the cell-edge. Network-MIMO / CoMP / DAS try to fix this by having
  base stations cooperate, at the cost of heavy backhaul. Fundamental
  limits [2] show that even full cooperation saturates, but the gains
  are still practically large.

• Cell-free massive MIMO. Introduced in [6] as a practical flavour of
  network MIMO. Huge number of APs (single-antenna in prior work),
  TDD channel reciprocity, conjugate beamforming with local CSI, no
  cells, no hand-overs. Inherits macro-diversity (from DAS) and
  channel hardening + favorable propagation (from massive MIMO).

• The gap. Prior CF-mMIMO work assumed single-antenna APs and did NOT
  analyze energy efficiency with a realistic backhaul power model.
  Since CF-mMIMO needs many more backhaul links than colocated
  massive MIMO, it was a priori unclear whether CF-mMIMO is actually
  green.

• This paper's contributions.
    1. Closed-form downlink SE for multi-antenna APs with arbitrary
       pilots; reduces to [6] for N=1 and to colocated massive MIMO [7]
       for M=1.
    2. Realistic power-consumption model including fixed and
       traffic-dependent backhaul power (extended from [11]).
    3. Optimal EE-maximizing power control under per-user SE and
       per-AP power constraints, approximated by a sequence of SOCPs
       via SCA.
    4. Two AP-selection schemes (received-power-based and
       largest-large-scale-fading-based) to slash backhaul power.
    5. Quantitative comparison with colocated massive MIMO showing
       CF-mMIMO's clear EE advantage under per-user QoS.

• Paper organization. Sec II (system model + SE), Sec III (power
  model + EE), Sec IV (power control optimization), Sec V (AP
  selection), Sec VI (numerical results), Sec VII (conclusion).

• Headline number. Under 1 bit/s/Hz per-user QoS, CF-mMIMO improves
  EE by ≈7.4× (MN = 128) and ≈2.2× (MN = 256) vs. colocated
  massive MIMO — roughly an order of magnitude at moderate service-
  antenna counts. This establishes CF-mMIMO as a genuinely green
  candidate for 5G/6G distributed MIMO deployments.
