───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. HIGH-LEVEL (LAYMAN) EXPLANATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We now know two things precisely:

  •  The spectral efficiency S_e of cell-free massive MIMO as a function
     of the power-control coefficients {η_{m,k}}  (Section II).
  •  The total power consumption P_total as a function of those same
     coefficients  (Section III).

Their ratio

     E_e  =  (B · S_e) / P_total                                       [bit/J]

is the number we want to MAXIMISE. So Section IV asks:

     "Which choice of {η_{m,k}} makes E_e as large as possible,
      while still guaranteeing a minimum data rate S_{o,k} for
      every user and respecting the per-AP transmit-power budget?"

Why is this hard?

     (i)    It is a RATIO of two complicated functions of η — so-called
            "fractional programming." Ratios of sums of logs are nasty.
     (ii)   The SPECTRAL EFFICIENCY is a log of an SINR, and the SINR is
            a ratio of quadratic forms in η — so the SE constraint
            SE_k ≥ S_{o,k} is itself a non-convex inequality.
     (iii)  Even the OBJECTIVE is non-convex (it is a log in the
            numerator over a linear-plus-log in the denominator).

Standard convex solvers like Gurobi or MOSEK cannot touch problems like
this directly. The paper therefore uses a three-step recipe borrowed
from the convex-optimization toolbox:

  STEP A — Transform the ratio into a non-fractional form. The trick
           is the "divide by B·S_e" preview from Section III: the
           resulting denominator is now AFFINE in the new variable
           c_{m,k} ≝ √η_{m,k}. So the fractional objective becomes
           "(sum of SE terms) / (affine function of c)". That is the
           structure Charnes-Cooper-style transformations like best.

  STEP B — Approximate the remaining non-convexities by CONVEX LOWER
           BOUNDS. The non-convex pieces are:
              (1) the numerator of the SE (a quadratic in c),
              (2) the log in log₂(1 + SINR).
           Both get replaced with tractable convex bounds:
              (1) uses a first-order Taylor expansion of a quadratic-
                  over-linear function,
              (2) uses the logarithmic inequality  ln(x) ≥ 1 − 1/x.

  STEP C — Solve the resulting approximation as a **Second-Order Cone
           Program** (SOCP), which modern solvers can handle quickly.
           Iterate: each iteration gives a new, better point; feed it
           back into the Taylor expansion of the next iteration.

The loop over STEPs B–C is called **Sequential Convex Approximation
(SCA)**. It is the standard work-horse of wireless optimization
whenever you face a non-convex problem with a nice "difference of
convex" or "quadratic-over-linear" structure. The paper shows:

  (a)  The sequence of objectives is MONOTONICALLY NON-DECREASING
       (each iteration improves E_e, never worsens it).
  (b)  The sequence is upper-bounded (total power has a floor
       P̄_fix), so it converges.
  (c)  Empirically it converges in ~10 iterations — fast.

The deliverable of Section IV is therefore ALGORITHM 1: a sequence
of SOCPs that the computer can solve in polynomial time per
iteration. Sections V and VI plug this algorithm into a full
cell-free simulation.

The bottom-line intellectual point of Section IV is:

    "Fractional, non-convex-looking EE maximization problems in
     wireless communications almost always admit an SCA-SOCP
     reformulation. The cell-free case is no exception."

2. DETAILED PHD-LEVEL EXPLANATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─── 2.1  The original problem (P)  (Eq. 22) ─────────────────────────────

Given the per-user SE formula (15) and the total-power expression (20),
the paper writes down the energy-efficiency maximization problem as

                                     E_e({η_{m,k}})
     (P) :     max   ─────────────────────────────
             {η_{m,k}}                                                  (22)

               s.t.    SE_k({η_{m,k}})  ≥  S_{o,k},  k = 1,…,K      [QoS]

                       Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}  ≤  1/N,  m = 1,…,M [per-AP]

                       η_{m,k}  ≥  0,  k = 1,…,K,  m = 1,…,M.

  •  S_{o,k} is the minimum spectral efficiency that user k must
     receive, expressed in bit/s/Hz. This is the quality-of-service
     (QoS) requirement — it guarantees nobody is starved for rate
     in the quest to maximize the network-wide "bits per joule."
  •  The per-AP constraint is the original (8) from Section II.
  •  The non-negativity η_{m,k} ≥ 0 is trivial but necessary —
     power-control coefficients are variances, not negative numbers.

Where does this come from?
  •  It is the CF-mMIMO analogue of the classical colocated massive-
     MIMO EE problem of [11], except that now {η_{m,k}} has M·K free
     variables (one per AP-user pair) rather than just K (one per
     user). M is typically hundreds, so the problem has MK = tens of
     thousands of variables. This is why the algorithm must scale
     gracefully with problem size.

Why is it non-convex?

  (a) The objective E_e is a ratio of non-convex functions of η.
      Even without the constraints, the numerator S_e is a SUM of
      log-of-SINR terms, each of which is non-concave in η. And
      dividing by P_total — which is itself a complicated function
      of η — destroys any concavity that might have existed.

  (b) The per-user SE constraint SE_k ≥ S_{o,k} is non-convex.
      Expand it:
          log₂(1 + num(η)/den(η))  ≥  S_{o,k} τ_c / (τ_c − τ_p)
      ⇔   num(η)/den(η)  ≥  2^{…} − 1
      ⇔   num(η)  ≥  (2^{…} − 1) · den(η).
      Both num and den are quadratic forms in √η, and the inequality
      is "quadratic ≥ quadratic" which is non-convex in general.

  (c) The per-AP power constraint IS convex (it is a linear
      inequality in η directly), so that one is fine.

─── 2.2  First reformulation: absorb the backhaul into the denominator ─

Plug (20) into E_e and write E_e explicitly:

                          B · S_e({η_{m,k}})
     E_e  =  ─────────────────────────────────────────
             P̄_fix                                          ← constant
            + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}   ← linear in η
            + B · Σ_m P_{bt,m} · S_e({η_{m,k}})             ← ∝ S_e

The paper then makes its key observation:

    "Dividing the numerator and denominator of the objective by
     B · S_e, we can see that the objective INCREASES as

            B · S_e({η_{m,k}})
       ─────────────────────────────────────────────
       P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}

     increases."

The reasoning is:
   After dividing by B·S_e, the denominator becomes

      (P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}) / (B·S_e)
         +  Σ_m P_{bt,m}             ← CONSTANT (does not depend on η)

   Since 1/E_e = A/(B·S_e) + (constant), and B·S_e > 0, E_e is a
   strictly decreasing function of A/(B·S_e). Minimizing A/(B·S_e)
   is the same as maximizing (B·S_e)/A. Thus maximizing E_e is
   equivalent to maximizing (B·S_e)/A where

      A(η)  =  P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}

   is an AFFINE function of η — not log-affine. This is the only
   place where the linear-in-S_e structure of the backhaul power
   (Eq. 19) is used; without it, the transformation would fail.

The equivalent problem is therefore

                                   B · S_e({η_{m,k}})
     (P1) :   max   ──────────────────────────────────────────           (24a)
             {η_{m,k}}                                                  ←
             P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}        (24b)

               s.t.   SE_k({η_{m,k}}) ≥ S_{o,k},   k = 1,…,K            (24c)

                       Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,  m = 1,…,M            (24d)

                       η_{m,k} ≥ 0,  ∀ m,k.

Comparing with the original (P), the only difference is that the
traffic-dependent backhaul has been cleanly REMOVED from the
denominator — it is now absorbed into a constant that doesn't matter
for the argmax. This is essentially a one-line Charnes-Cooper-like
rearrangement, enabled by the backhaul model being linear in S_e.

─── 2.3  Substitution c_{m,k} = √η_{m,k}  (Eq. 25, 26) ──────────────────

The SE formula (15) contains TERMS LIKE √η_{m,k} inside quadratic
forms. To get a cleaner algebra, the paper substitutes

     c_{m,k}  ≝  √η_{m,k}  ≥  0,
     c_k      ≝  [c_{1,k}, c_{2,k}, …, c_{M,k}]^T  ∈ ℝ^M,
     c        ≝  [c_1^T, c_2^T, …, c_K^T]^T   ∈ ℝ^{MK}.

Under this substitution, the numerator in (15) becomes
|𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|² (purely quadratic in c), the pilot-contamination
term becomes |𝛄_{k,k'}^T c_{k'}|², and the per-AP power constraint
Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N becomes

     Σ_{k=1}^{K} c_{m,k}² · γ_{m,k}  ≤  1/N,   m = 1,…,M        (25c)

which is a quadratic — convex — constraint in c. The PA-draw term
in the denominator of (24b) similarly becomes

     Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k}

which is a positive weighted sum of squares — also convex in c.

Therefore, in the new variable c the problem becomes

                                       B · S_e(c)
     (P1)  :   max   ──────────────────────────────────────────         (25a)
                c
                      P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k}  (25b)

               s.t.  SE_k(c) ≥ S_{o,k},  k = 1,…,K                       (25c,paper)

                     Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,  m = 1,…,M              (25d,paper)

                     c_{m,k} ≥ 0,  ∀ m, k.

where

                            1 − τ_p/τ_c
     SE_k(c)  =  ───────  log₂ (  1  +

                    ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|²
       ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────   )  (26)
       ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T c_{k'}|² + ρ_d N Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',k} c_{k'}‖² + 1

The denominator (25b) is now **affine in the squared c-variables** —
equivalently a positive-definite quadratic in c. Good. But the SE
constraint is still the awful "log of ratio of quadratics ≥ constant"
creature.

─── 2.4  The per-user SE constraint as an SOC constraint  (Eq. 27) ─────

The crucial observation: although SE_k ≥ S_{o,k} is non-convex in
general, it happens to define a SECOND-ORDER CONE constraint in c
if we stare at it carefully. Rewrite

     SE_k(c)  ≥  S_{o,k}
   ⇔  log₂(1 + (num/den))  ≥  S_{o,k} · τ_c/(τ_c − τ_p)
   ⇔  1 + num/den  ≥  2^{ S_{o,k} τ_c/(τ_c − τ_p) }
   ⇔  num ≥ (2^{Ŝ_{o,k}} − 1) · den
   ⇔  ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|²
           ≥ (2^{Ŝ_{o,k}} − 1) · ( ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T c_{k'}|²
                                  + ρ_d N Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',k} c_{k'}‖² + 1 )

where Ŝ_{o,k} ≝ S_{o,k} · τ_c / (τ_c − τ_p).

Now the key inequality: since c_{m,k} ≥ 0 and all entries of 𝛄_{k,k}
are non-negative, we have 𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k ≥ 0 — i.e. the inner product
is a non-negative REAL scalar. Therefore |𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|² = (𝛄_{k,k}^T
c_k)², and we can take the square root on both sides without losing
information:

       𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k  ≥  √(2^{Ŝ_{o,k}}−1)  ·

         √(  Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T c_{k'}|²
           + (1/N) Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',k} c_{k'}‖²
           + 1/(N² ρ_d)  )                                          (27)

where we have pulled out ρ_d N² as an overall scale factor. (The
paper writes out (27) with the same algebra; I have rearranged to
clarify the SOC structure.)

Observation: the left-hand side is LINEAR in c. The right-hand side
is the Euclidean norm of a vector whose entries are linear in c.
So (27) has the form

     linear ≥ constant · ‖affine vector‖

which is exactly the definition of a second-order cone (SOC)
constraint (also called Lorentz cone, or "ice-cream cone"):

     Cone : { (t, v) ∈ ℝ × ℝ^n : t ≥ ‖v‖ }.

SOC constraints are convex, SOCP solvers handle them efficiently in
polynomial time, and EVERY modern solver (Gurobi, MOSEK, ECOS) has a
dedicated SOCP backend. This is why the paper puts so much effort
into getting the SE constraint into SOC form — once it is in SOC form,
it is free to use.

Note the subtle assumption: c_{m,k} ≥ 0. Since c_{m,k} = √η_{m,k} and
η_{m,k} ≥ 0, this is automatic. If we had allowed complex precoders,
we would lose the non-negativity and the SOC trick would not apply
directly — one would need a more general semidefinite programming
(SDP) relaxation. Conjugate beamforming with real power-control
coefficients is therefore the sweet spot that makes the optimization
tractable.

─── 2.5  Second reformulation: (P̂1) with slack variables  (Eq. 28) ───

The objective (25a)/(25b) is still a FRACTION. Convex solvers prefer
LINEAR objectives. So the paper introduces slack variables t_k
(one per user) and t_0 (one scalar for the denominator), and rewrites
(P1) as

                                    B · Σ_{k=1}^K t_k
                                     ─────────────────
     (P̂1)  :    max         t_0                                         (28a)
                c,t,t_0
                               s.t.  SE_k(c) ≥ t_k,   k = 1,…,K          (28b)

                     P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k}  ≤  t_0  (28c)

                                   (25c), (25d), (27)                   (28d)

where t ≝ [t_1, …, t_K]^T. Intuition:

  •  t_k is a PROXY for SE_k (we want SE_k ≥ t_k, and at the optimum
     this will hold with equality).
  •  t_0 is a PROXY for the denominator (we want denominator ≤ t_0,
     again with equality at the optimum).
  •  The resulting objective Σ t_k / t_0 is still a ratio but now a
     ratio of two LINEAR functions of (t, t_0) — it is a LINEAR
     FRACTIONAL function, which is the classical form that the
     Charnes-Cooper transformation handles beautifully.

"Charnes-Cooper" recipe for max (cT x + α)/(dT x + β) with dT x + β > 0
over a convex set:
   Let y = x/(dT x + β)  and  z = 1/(dT x + β). Then the problem
   becomes max (cT y + α z) s.t. dT y + β z = 1, (y/z) in the original
   set, z > 0 — a LINEAR program!

The paper does NOT invoke Charnes-Cooper by name, but the reasoning
behind the (P̂1) reformulation is identical. Once (P̂1) is in this
form, the remaining difficulty is the non-convex constraint (28b),
because SE_k is a log of a RATIO of quadratics (convex over convex,
hence non-convex in general). The rest of Section IV is about
approximating (28b) using SCA.

─── 2.6  The SCA step — slack variables u_k  (Eqs. 29–33) ───────────

The troublesome constraint is (28b): SE_k(c) ≥ t_k. Expanding via
(26), this reads

    (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂( 1 + num_k(c) / den_k(c) )  ≥  t_k

where num_k is a quadratic in c (equal to ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|²)
and den_k is also a quadratic (the rest of the denominator of the
SINR plus 1).

The paper introduces a slack variable u_k and DECOMPOSES (28b) into
TWO constraints:

     1 + num_k(c)/den_k(c)   ≥   u_k                                    (29a)

             log₂(u_k)           ≥   t_k · (τ_c − τ_p)/τ_c ≝ t_k·ε     (29b)

The constraint (29a) captures the SINR lower bound, and (29b) applies
the log-of-linear step on top. Notice that (29a) and (29b) together
are equivalent to (28b) IF AND ONLY IF both hold with equality at the
optimum (which they do, because u_k is a free slack variable).

Step 1: convex bound for (29a).

Rewrite (29a) as

           num_k(c) + den_k(c)  ≥  u_k · den_k(c)

which is equivalent to

    f(c, u_k) ≝ (num_k(c) + den_k(c)) / u_k  ≥  den_k(c)                (30)

rewriting once more with the paper's exact expression:

            ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|² + [ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} + ρ_d N Σ_{k'}‖·‖² + 1]
                                                                            (31)
    f(c,u_k) = ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                                           u_k

Here f(c, u_k) is a **quadratic-over-linear function** — a classical
convex function when the numerator is a positive definite quadratic
in c and u_k > 0. (Proof: take the Schur complement, or apply the
perspective-function definition of quadratic-over-linear. Both
arguments are in Boyd-Vandenberghe "Convex Optimization" Ch. 3.)

A convex function is ALWAYS UPPER-BOUNDED BY its first-order Taylor
expansion around any point (cⁿ, uⁿ_k):

    f(c, u_k)  ≥  f(cⁿ, uⁿ_k) + ∇_c f(cⁿ, uⁿ_k)^T (c − cⁿ)
                 + (∂f/∂u_k)(cⁿ, uⁿ_k) · (u_k − uⁿ_k)

Wait — that is the LOWER bound for a convex function, which is
exactly what we want here. (A convex function lies ABOVE any tangent
line.) Writing F(c, u_k; cⁿ, uⁿ_k) for this tangent lower bound, (30)
is replaced by

     F(c, u_k; cⁿ, uⁿ_k)  ≥  den_k(c)                                   (32)

This is a restrictive replacement (strictly tighter than the original
inequality — any c feasible for (32) is also feasible for (30) but
not vice versa). As the SCA iteration proceeds, the tangent point
(cⁿ, uⁿ_k) is updated to the current iterate, so the approximation
improves round by round.

The explicit form of the Taylor expansion (Eq. 33 of the paper):

     F(c, u_k; cⁿ, uⁿ_k)

           = f(cⁿ, uⁿ_k)

             + (2 ρ_d / uⁿ_k) · Σ_{k'=1}^{K} (cⁿ_{k'})^T
                                              · [ N² 𝛄_{k,k'} 𝛄_{k,k'}^T
                                                  + N 𝐃_{k',k}² ] · (c_{k'} − cⁿ_{k'})

             − (f(cⁿ, uⁿ_k) / uⁿ_k) · (u_k − uⁿ_k)                      (33)

where the middle line uses the matrix identity
∇_x (x^T A x) = (A + A^T)x = 2A x for symmetric A.

Note that the k'=k term in the sum captures the useful signal
𝛄_{k,k}, while the k' ≠ k terms capture the pilot-contamination
contribution. The 𝐃_{k',k}² matrices capture the non-coherent
interference from the denominator of the SINR. All of these appear
together because inside f(c, u_k), both "good" num_k and "bad"
den_k pieces are present in the numerator of the quadratic-over-linear.

Right-hand side den_k(c) in (30)/(32): a QUADRATIC in c (sum of
squares). The constraint (32) therefore has the form

      (affine in c, u_k)  ≥  (quadratic in c)

which is an SOC-representable constraint (after some algebra
detailed in Boyd-Vandenberghe App. B). The paper says "Note that (32)
is SOC representable [20]" — that is ref [20] = Boyd-Vandenberghe.

Step 2: convex bound for (29b), the log constraint.

Now we have to deal with

     log₂(u_k)  ≥  ε · t_k,    where  ε = (τ_c−τ_p)/τ_c.

Here u_k, t_k are both scalar variables. The function log₂(u_k) is
concave in u_k, so the constraint "log₂(u_k) ≥ linear" is CONVEX —
nothing is broken. In principle we could leave it alone. But the
authors want the whole program to be a SOCP (for speed and solver
availability), and log constraints need "exponential cone" support
which — in 2017 — was not yet implemented efficiently in Gurobi and
MOSEK. So they replace log₂(u_k) with a convex LOWER BOUND that is
ITSELF SOC-representable.

The key inequality used is the classical logarithm inequality

                ln(x)  ≥  1  −  1/x,   for all x > 0                   (‡)

with equality at x = 1. This is a one-variable convex bound that is
widely used in wireless SCA papers (for example, in Shen-Yu 2018
"Fractional Programming Part II" it appears as a standard building
block).

Apply (‡) to x = u_k / uⁿ_k and convert to log₂:

     log₂(u_k / uⁿ_k)  =  log₂(u_k) − log₂(uⁿ_k)
                        =  ln(u_k / uⁿ_k) · log₂(e)

and by (‡) with x = u_k/uⁿ_k,

     ln(u_k / uⁿ_k)  ≥  1 − uⁿ_k/u_k.

Therefore

     log₂(u_k)  ≥  log₂(uⁿ_k) + log₂(e) · (1 − uⁿ_k/u_k)               (34)

This is a linear-fractional lower bound in u_k. It is TIGHT at
u_k = uⁿ_k and has the same first derivative at that point, which is
exactly the SCA-"proper convex bound" condition (see Scutari-
Facchinei-Lampariello 2016 [18] or Beck-Ben-Tal-Tetruashvili 2010
[17]).

Replacing log₂(u_k) with (34) in (29b) yields the convex bound

     log₂(uⁿ_k) + log₂(e) · (1 − uⁿ_k/u_k)  ≥  ε · t_k                 (35)

Rearranging: log₂(e)·uⁿ_k/u_k  ≤  log₂(uⁿ_k) + log₂(e) − ε·t_k, i.e.

     u_k · (log₂(uⁿ_k) + log₂(e) − ε·t_k)  ≥  log₂(e) · uⁿ_k.

This is a LINEAR-times-LINEAR hyperbolic constraint of the form
x · y ≥ constant with x, y affine in variables. Such constraints
are SOC-representable via

      x · y ≥ z²   ⇔   (x + y)/2 ≥ ‖((x−y)/2, z)‖,

which is a standard rotated-cone representation. So (35) is SOC
representable — the authors say "this constraint can be reformulated
as an SOC constraint [12]" where [12] is Alizadeh-Goldfarb "Second-
Order Cone Programming" 2003.

─── 2.7  The full SOCP at iteration n+1 (Eqs. 36, 37) ────────────────

After substituting the two convex bounds (32) and (35) into (28b),
the problem at iteration n+1 of the SCA loop becomes

                                 K
                         B · Σ_{k=1}^K t_k
                         ───────────────────
     (P̂1, n+1) :  max          t_0                                      (36a)
                  c,t,u
                                 s.t.  (25c), (25d), (27), (28c), (32), (35)   (36b)

This is the OPTIMIZATION PROBLEM SOLVED AT EACH ITERATION. By a
perspective transformation (treating 1/t_0 as a new variable) it is
converted to an honest SOCP in Eq. (37) of the paper. The perspective
transformation is simply:

     Σ t_k / t_0   →    maximize w subject to w · t_0 ≤ Σ t_k

but since we already have separate variables t_k, t_0, the only
thing needed is to rewrite the linear-fractional objective Σ t_k / t_0
as a linear objective over a slightly larger variable set using the
Charnes-Cooper substitution (ỹ = t/t_0, z̃ = 1/t_0). The resulting
program is a vanilla SOCP; the paper lists all the constraints (37b)–
(37g) but the structural content is the same as (36).

Remark: The authors note that (P̂1) could ALSO be solved to GLOBAL
OPTIMALITY by a monotonic optimization method called "branch-and-
reduce-and-bound" (BRB) [16]. They use it ONLY for small problems
(M = 8, K = 2) as a sanity check, because BRB has exponential
complexity in the problem dimension and is therefore hopeless for
M = 100–200. SCA, in contrast, is polynomial per iteration and
empirically converges to the BRB optimum in ~10 iterations.

─── 2.8  Algorithm 1 — the SCA loop ──────────────────────────────────

The final iterative algorithm is:

  Algorithm 1 (SCA for (28))

  1. Initialization:
        n ← 1
        choose feasible initial point (c¹, u¹) by solving a
          FEASIBILITY SOCP (find any c with SE_k ≥ S_{o,k}
          and Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N)
        set tolerance ε_tol, max iterations N_I

  2. Iteration n:
        solve the SOCP (37) with tangent point (cⁿ, uⁿ)
        let (c⁺, t⁺, u⁺) be its solution

  3. Convergence check:
        if  |Σ_k t⁺_k − Σ_k tⁿ_k|  <  ε_tol  or  n = N_I:
            STOP
        else:
            go to Step 4.

  4. Update:
        (cⁿ⁺¹, uⁿ⁺¹) ← (c⁺, u⁺)
        n ← n + 1
        go to Step 2.

With ε_tol = 0.01 and N_I = 10 (the paper's defaults), Algorithm 1
converges in at most 10 iterations per large-scale fading
realization.

Remark 1 (initialization): For a given spectral efficiency target
{S_{o,k}}, the feasible set of (P1) — i.e., SE_k ≥ S_{o,k} and the
per-AP power constraints — is CONVEX (it is the intersection of SOC
sets with a non-negative orthant). So finding an initial feasible
point is itself an SOCP feasibility problem, which solvers handle
directly. If infeasible (the target rates cannot be met by any
power allocation), the authors simply set E_e = 0 and move on.

─── 2.9  Convergence analysis ──────────────────────────────────────────

The convergence is a standard SCA argument (see Beck-Ben-Tal-
Tetruashvili 2010 [17] or Scutari-Facchinei-Lampariello 2016 [18]):

  (i)   Monotone improvement. The convex bounds F(·) and (34) are
        TIGHT at the tangent point (cⁿ, uⁿ): they equal the original
        non-convex functions at (cⁿ, uⁿ) and have the same first
        derivatives there. Therefore, (cⁿ, uⁿ) is feasible for the
        (n+1)-th approximation, and by optimization the (n+1)-th
        iterate must be at least as good as (cⁿ, uⁿ). Hence the
        sequence of objective values is non-decreasing.

  (ii)  Upper bound. The objective (24a), E_e, is upper bounded
        because P_total ≥ P̄_fix > 0 and S_e is upper-bounded by a
        constant for any feasible η (finite power → finite rate).
        Therefore the sequence of E_e values is bounded.

  (iii) Convergence. A monotonically non-decreasing, bounded-above
        sequence converges. Algorithm 1 therefore produces a sequence
        of E_e values that converges.

NOTE: SCA converges to a KKT point of the original non-convex
problem, NOT necessarily to the global optimum. The paper verifies
empirically, by comparing with BRB on small problems, that SCA
indeed finds the global optimum in most cases for CF-mMIMO of
practical sizes (Fig. 1, M = 8, K = 2).

─── 2.10  Computational complexity ──────────────────────────────────────

Each iteration of Algorithm 1 solves an SOCP with:

  •  Variables: c ∈ ℝ^{MK}_+, t ∈ ℝ^K, u ∈ ℝ^K, t_0 ∈ ℝ.
     Total dimension: MK + 2K + 1.
  •  Constraints: K SOC constraints from (27), K SOC constraints
     from (32), K constraints from (35), 1 per-AP quadratic ×M =
     M quadratic constraints from (25d), 1 linear constraint
     (28c). So O(K + M) constraints.

The interior-point complexity of an SOCP with n variables and m
cone constraints is O((n + m)^{3.5}) — polynomial. For M = 100,
K = 40 (a typical CF-mMIMO deployment), n = 4000 + 80 + 1 and
the solver handles it in seconds on a laptop. This is why SCA is
scalable while BRB (exponential) is not.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3. HISTORICAL / PRIOR DEVELOPMENTS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Section IV draws on four different threads of the optimization
literature. A brief history:

▸ 1962 — **Charnes, Cooper**, "Programming with linear fractional
  functionals," Naval Res. Logistics Quarterly. The ORIGINAL
  transformation for linear-fractional programs. Introduces the
  variable change that turns (c^T x + α)/(d^T x + β) into a linear
  program.

▸ 1967 — **Dinkelbach**, "On nonlinear fractional programming,"
  Management Science. The parametric algorithm: max f/g ↔ find λ
  s.t. max (f − λ g) = 0. This is the other classical tool for
  non-linear fractional programming, used heavily in EE papers.

▸ 1978 — **Hoyer**, Isermann, "Successive approximation algorithms"
  — seeds of the SCA idea for non-convex programming.

▸ 1994 — Nesterov-Nemirovski "Interior-Point Polynomial Methods in
  Convex Programming," SIAM. Establishes polynomial-time interior-
  point methods for SOCP and SDP. This is the mathematical backbone
  that makes all subsequent SCA-SOCP work practical.

▸ 2003 — **Alizadeh, Goldfarb**, "Second-order cone programming,"
  Math. Programming (ref [12] in Ngo paper). THE reference for
  SOCP — formulations, duality, interior-point algorithms,
  applications. This is the paper you cite whenever you say
  "this is SOC representable."

▸ 2004 — **Boyd, Vandenberghe**, "Convex Optimization," Cambridge
  University Press (ref [20] in the Ngo paper). The bible of
  practical convex optimization. Ch. 2–3 give the definitions of
  cones and convex functions, Ch. 4 gives the SOC examples used
  in the Ngo SCA derivation, App. B lists the SOC representations
  of all standard convex functions.

▸ 2010 — **Beck, Ben-Tal, Tetruashvili**, "A sequential parametric
  convex approximation method with applications to nonconvex truss
  topology design problems," J. Glob. Optim. (ref [17] in Ngo
  paper). The seminal SCA convergence-analysis paper. Establishes
  the conditions under which SCA converges to a KKT point of the
  original non-convex problem.

▸ 2012 — **Tran, Hanif, Tolli, Juntti**, "Fast converging algorithm
  for weighted sum rate maximization in multicell MISO downlink,"
  IEEE SPL (ref [19] in Ngo paper). The first paper to apply SCA
  + SOCP to weighted-sum-rate maximization in wireless. The
  methodology used in Ngo-TGCN-2018 is a direct generalization of
  this work (and Tran is a co-author of both!).

▸ 2015 — **Tervo, Tran, Juntti**, "Optimal energy-efficient transmit
  beamforming for multi-user MISO downlink," IEEE TSP (ref [25] in
  Ngo paper). Applies the same SCA-SOCP methodology to EE
  maximization in colocated MIMO with BRB as a global-optimization
  sanity check. The code from this paper is what Ngo et al. MODIFY
  to build their BRB benchmark for the cell-free case.

▸ 2016 — **Scutari, Facchinei, Lampariello**, "Parallel and
  distributed methods for constrained nonconvex optimization—part
  I: Theory," IEEE TSP (ref [18] in Ngo paper). A unifying
  convergence theory for SCA, including sufficient conditions on
  the "surrogate" convex functions that make the iteration a
  proper descent / ascent method.

▸ 2017 — **Ngo, Tran, Duong, Matthaiou, Larsson** (this paper).
  Brings the whole machinery (SCA + SOCP + BRB benchmark) into
  cell-free massive MIMO.

▸ 2018 — **Shen, Yu**, "Fractional Programming for Communication
  Systems—Parts I & II," IEEE JSAC. Survey + new technique
  ("quadratic transform") for EE-like fractional programs, which
  became the standard methodology after 2018. Ngo-2017-TGCN is
  one of the last papers in the pre-Shen-Yu era that uses the
  classical SCA + Taylor approach. Modern papers often use Shen-
  Yu's quadratic transform instead, which is slightly simpler but
  ultimately equivalent.

▸ Industrial solvers used: **Gurobi** [22] (commercial, free
  academic license) and **MOSEK** [23] (commercial, also free
  academic license). Both have native SOCP support and solve
  tens of thousands of variables in seconds. The paper's code
  repository (github.com/tranlenam/cellfreeMIMOenergyefficiency)
  shows that the authors call MOSEK via YALMIP from MATLAB.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4. MATHEMATICAL RECAP IN UNICODE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Original problem  (P):
   max_{η}     B · S_e({η_{m,k}}) / P_total({η_{m,k}})
   s.t.        SE_k ≥ S_{o,k},  ∀ k
               Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,  ∀ m
               η_{m,k} ≥ 0,  ∀ m,k.

Equivalent (P1) after "divide by B·S_e":
   max_{η}     B · S_e / [P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}]
   s.t.        same constraints,  where  P̄_fix = Σ_m (N P_{tc,m} + P_{0,m}).

Substitution:  c_{m,k} ≝ √η_{m,k},  c_k ≝ [c_{1,k},…,c_{M,k}]^T ∈ ℝ^M,
                c ≝ [c_1^T,…,c_K^T]^T ∈ ℝ^{MK}_+.

Per-AP power:  Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,  ∀ m    (convex quadratic)

SE in c :
   SE_k(c) = (1−τ_p/τ_c) · log₂(1 +
             ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|² /
             ( ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k}|𝛄_{k,k'}^T c_{k'}|² + ρ_d N Σ_{k'}‖𝐃_{k',k} c_{k'}‖² + 1 ))

SE constraint as SOC  (27):
   𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k ≥ √(2^{Ŝ_{o,k}}−1) · ‖v_k(c)‖
   where v_k(c) is an affine function of c and Ŝ_{o,k}=S_{o,k}τ_c/(τ_c−τ_p).

Linear-fractional reformulation  (P̂1):
   max_{c,t,t_0}   B · Σ_k t_k / t_0
   s.t.            SE_k(c) ≥ t_k,  k = 1,…,K                 ← non-convex
                   P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k} ≤ t_0
                   per-AP power,   SE-SOC (27),   c ≥ 0.

Slack-variable decomposition of the SE constraint:
   1 + num_k(c)/den_k(c)  ≥  u_k                                  (29a)
   log₂(u_k)              ≥  ε t_k,   ε ≝ (τ_c−τ_p)/τ_c           (29b)

Convex lower bound for (29a) via Taylor of f(c,u_k) = (num_k+den_k)/u_k:
   F(c,u_k; cⁿ,uⁿ_k) ≥ den_k(c)                                    (32)
   where
   F(c,u_k; cⁿ,uⁿ_k)
     = f(cⁿ,uⁿ_k)
       + (2ρ_d/uⁿ_k) · Σ_{k'=1}^K (cⁿ_{k'})^T
                                · [N²𝛄_{k,k'}𝛄_{k,k'}^T + N 𝐃_{k',k}²]
                                · (c_{k'} − cⁿ_{k'})
       − (f(cⁿ,uⁿ_k)/uⁿ_k) · (u_k − uⁿ_k).                          (33)

Convex lower bound for log₂(u_k) via ln(x) ≥ 1 − 1/x:
   log₂(u_k) ≥ log₂(uⁿ_k) + log₂(e) · (1 − uⁿ_k/u_k)              (34)

Convex approximation constraint at iteration n+1:
   log₂(uⁿ_k) + log₂(e)·(1 − uⁿ_k/u_k) ≥ ε t_k                    (35)
   (rotated-cone / hyperbolic constraint, SOC representable)

Algorithm 1 iteration: solve SOCP (37), update (cⁿ,uⁿ), repeat.
Convergence: objective values form a non-decreasing, bounded-above
sequence → converges (typically within 10 iterations).


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5. NUMERICAL WORKED MINI-EXAMPLE OF ONE SCA ITERATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Let us simulate ONE iteration of Algorithm 1 on a toy cell-free
instance.

Configuration (same as the Sec. II worked example):

   M = 2 APs, K = 2 users, N = 1,   τ_p = τ_c = 10  (drop the prefactor),
   orthogonal pilots, ρ_d = 1, ρ_p = 1.

   Large-scale fading:
        β_{1,1} = 0.1,  β_{1,2} = 0.01,
        β_{2,1} = 0.01, β_{2,2} = 0.1.

   From Eq. (5):
        γ_{1,1} = γ_{2,2} = 10·0.01/(10·0.1+1) = 0.1/2 = 0.05
        γ_{1,2} = γ_{2,1} = 10·0.0001/(10·0.01+1) = 0.001/1.1 ≈ 0.000909.

   QoS target S_{o,1} = S_{o,2} = 0.1 bit/s/Hz, so
        ε = 1,  Ŝ_{o,k} = 0.1 → 2^{0.1} − 1 ≈ 0.0718.

   Per-AP power constraint: c_{m,1}² γ_{m,1} + c_{m,2}² γ_{m,2} ≤ 1
   (since N = 1).

   Power model: set P̄_fix = 10 (arbitrary units), ν_m = 0.4,
   N₀ = 1, B = 1, P_{bt,m} = 0 (ignore traffic BH),
   Σ_m (1/ν_m) = 5.

Step 0 — Initial feasible point. Use equal power at each AP:
   c_{1,1}² = c_{1,2}² = 1/(2·γ_{1,1}) = 10 (waits, need to check γ)

Actually, let us use c_{m,k}² = 1/(N · (γ_{m,1} + γ_{m,2})).
   AP 1: 1/(0.05+0.000909) ≈ 1/0.0509 ≈ 19.64
         so c_{1,1}² = c_{1,2}² = 19.64/2 ≈ 9.82
         c_{1,1} = c_{1,2} ≈ 3.133
   Check (25d):  9.82·0.05 + 9.82·0.000909 ≈ 0.491 + 0.00893 ≈ 0.500 ≤ 1 ✓
   Same for AP 2 by symmetry.

   So c⁰ = [c¹⁰, c²⁰] with c¹⁰ = c²⁰ = [3.133, 3.133]^T
   (stacked: c⁰ = [3.133, 3.133, 3.133, 3.133]^T).

Step 1 — Compute num_1(c⁰), den_1(c⁰), u_1⁰.
   𝛄_{1,1} = [γ_{1,1}, γ_{2,1}]^T = [0.05, 0.000909]^T
   𝛄_{1,2} = 0 (orthogonal pilots)
   𝐃_{1,1} = diag(√(β_{1,1}γ_{1,1}), √(β_{2,1}γ_{2,1}))
            = diag(√0.005, √0.00000909)
            ≈ diag(0.0707, 0.00302)
   𝐃_{2,1} = diag(√(β_{1,1}γ_{1,2}), √(β_{2,1}γ_{2,2}))
            = diag(√0.000909, √0.0005)
            ≈ diag(0.0302, 0.02236)

   num_1(c⁰) = ρ_d N² |𝛄_{1,1}^T c_1⁰|²
             = 1 · 1 · |0.05·3.133 + 0.000909·3.133|²
             = |0.1567 + 0.00285|²
             = (0.1595)²
             ≈ 0.02545

   den_1(c⁰) = ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠1}|𝛄_{1,k'}^T c_{k'}⁰|²
               + ρ_d N Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',1} c_{k'}⁰‖² + 1
             = 0                            ← orthogonal pilots
             + 1·1· ( (0.0707·3.133)² + (0.00302·3.133)² )
                  + ( (0.0302·3.133)² + (0.02236·3.133)² )
                  + 1
             = ((0.2216)² + (0.00947)²) + ((0.0946)² + (0.0700)²) + 1
             ≈ (0.04910 + 0.0000897) + (0.00895 + 0.00490) + 1
             ≈ 0.04919 + 0.01385 + 1
             ≈ 1.063.

   SINR_1(c⁰) = 0.02545 / 1.063 ≈ 0.02394
   log₂(1 + 0.02394) ≈ 0.0342 bit/s/Hz.

   So the initial feasible point actually FAILS the QoS target 0.1
   bit/s/Hz for user 1. In a real run one would either lower S_{o,k}
   or increase transmit power. For pedagogical purposes let us pretend
   the target is 0.03 bit/s/Hz instead, so the point is feasible.

   Set u_1⁰ = 1 + num_1/den_1 ≈ 1 + 0.02394 = 1.02394.

Step 2 — Compute the tangent point functions f(c⁰, u_1⁰) and
gradients. This is mechanical but tedious; the SOCP solver does it
automatically once you hand it the closed-form (33).

Step 3 — Build the SOCP (37) with the above Taylor-coefficient
matrices, hand it to MOSEK, and read off the new iterate c¹, u¹.

Step 4 — Check if Σ_k t_k has moved by less than ε_tol = 0.01; if
not, go back to Step 1 with n ← 1.

After roughly 5–10 iterations (for a toy problem with MK = 4
variables, one typically converges in 3–5), the iterate (cⁿ, uⁿ)
will stabilize at a KKT point of (P̂1), and the corresponding E_e
is the answer.

─── The "numerical lesson" ──────────────────────────────────────────

Even for the smallest case M = 2, K = 2 the algebra is heavy —
which is exactly why the paper reaches for a solver. The point of
the worked example is NOT to compute the final answer by hand, but
to see the STRUCTURE of what each iteration builds:

  •  {γ_{m,k}}, {𝐃_{k',k}}, {𝛄_{k,k'}} are pre-computed once from
     the large-scale fading.
  •  At iteration n, the current iterate (cⁿ, uⁿ) plugs into (33)
     and (35) to produce a CONVEX, SOC-representable approximation
     of (28b).
  •  The solver returns a new iterate c⁺, u⁺ which strictly improves
     the objective (or stays the same if you are at a KKT point).
  •  Stop when the improvement is smaller than ε_tol.

─── PRACTICE PROBLEM ────────────────────────────────────────────────

(a) Show that f(c, u) = x^T A x / u with A positive semidefinite and
    u > 0 is JOINTLY convex in (x, u). Hint: use the Schur complement
    definition of a positive-semidefinite matrix, or the perspective
    function theorem from Boyd-Vandenberghe Ch. 3.

(b) Derive inequality (34) from ln(x) ≥ 1 − 1/x by substituting
    x = u/uⁿ and noting log₂(u) = log₂(uⁿ) + log₂(e)·ln(u/uⁿ).

(c) For the toy example above with S_{o,k} = 0.03 bit/s/Hz,
    compute 2^{Ŝ_{o,k}} − 1 with Ŝ_{o,k} = 0.03 · (10/10) = 0.03,
    so 2^{0.03} − 1 ≈ 0.0210. Write out inequality (27) for
    user 1 with c_1 = [c_{1,1}, c_{2,1}]^T as the only variable
    (fix c_2 arbitrarily). Verify that it is a linear (LHS)
    vs. norm (RHS) inequality — i.e., SOC.

(d) Look up the MATLAB code at
    github.com/tranlenam/cellfreeMIMOenergyefficiency and identify
    the files that implement (i) the feasibility initialization,
    (ii) the SCA loop, (iii) the SOCP handoff to MOSEK. Map the
    lines of code back to Eqs. (25)–(37). This is the single best
    way to grasp how the paper is actually run.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. SUMMARY OF SECTION IV
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Problem (P). Maximize E_e = B·S_e/P_total over power-control
  coefficients η_{m,k} subject to per-user QoS (SE_k ≥ S_{o,k})
  and per-AP power (Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N). Non-convex
  because (i) the objective is a ratio of two non-convex
  functions, and (ii) the SE constraint is a log of a ratio of
  quadratic forms.

• Reformulation (P1). Divide numerator and denominator by B·S_e.
  The traffic-dependent backhaul, being linear in S_e, turns into
  a constant that does not affect the argmax. The new fractional
  objective has an AFFINE denominator in the "squared power"
  variables c_{m,k}² = η_{m,k}, which is the structure SOCP likes.

• Substitution c_{m,k} = √η_{m,k} puts the per-AP power constraint
  Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N into quadratic (convex) form and
  makes the SE numerator a perfect square, which enables the
  SOC-representation trick (27) for the SE constraint.

• SOC representation of the SE constraint (27). Because c ≥ 0 and
  all coefficients are real non-negative, 𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k ≥ 0 and the
  SINR-lower-bound constraint admits a clean SOC form: linear-LHS
  ≥ norm-of-vector-affine-in-c-RHS. All the hard work in Sec. IV
  boils down to getting (28b) into this form.

• Linear-fractional reformulation (P̂1). Introduce slack t_k for
  SE_k and t_0 for the denominator. The objective becomes a
  linear-fractional Σ t_k / t_0, treatable by Charnes-Cooper /
  perspective transformations and converting to an honest LP-
  style SOCP objective.

• SCA approximation of the remaining non-convex constraint
  (28b, i.e. SE_k ≥ t_k) in two steps:
     (a) decouple using slack u_k via (29a)+(29b);
     (b) convex bound (29a) by Taylor-expanding the quadratic-over-
         linear f(c, u_k) around the current iterate (cⁿ, uⁿ_k),
         giving (32)–(33) — SOC representable;
     (c) convex bound log₂(u_k) below using ln(x) ≥ 1 − 1/x,
         giving (34)–(35) — also SOC representable.

• Algorithm 1. Iterate: solve (37) with current tangent point,
  update (cⁿ,uⁿ), stop when |Σ_k t_k| stops improving (ε_tol)
  or after N_I = 10 iterations.

• Convergence. Objective is non-decreasing (because the convex
  bounds are TIGHT at the tangent point, so the previous iterate
  is feasible for the new SOCP), and upper-bounded (because E_e
  is bounded for finite total power). Therefore the algorithm
  converges (numerically, within ~10 iterations). SCA converges
  to a KKT point in general; empirically on small CF-mMIMO it
  matches the BRB global optimum.

• Computational complexity. Per-iteration SOCP with MK + 2K + 1
  variables and O(M + K) cones. Interior-point complexity is
  polynomial. Tractable for M ≈ 100–200, K ≈ 20–40.

• Intellectual take-away. EE maximization in cell-free massive
  MIMO is a prototypical SCA-SOCP problem. Mastering this recipe —
  (i) absorb the fractional objective into an equivalent non-
  fractional form; (ii) substitute variables to make the power
  constraint quadratic; (iii) decouple log-of-ratio constraints
  by slack variables; (iv) Taylor-expand and log-inequality-bound
  the remaining non-convexities — gives you a blueprint for
  optimizing virtually any EE-like objective in wireless
  communications. The same recipe (with minor variations) is
  used in every subsequent CF-mMIMO EE paper.
