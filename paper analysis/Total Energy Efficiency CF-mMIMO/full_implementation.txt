  ---
  1 · High-Level (Layman) Explanation

  Think of a football stadium full of fans streaming video. The old way was to
  put 3–4 huge towers at the corners — people near a tower are fine, but fans at
   the edges hear two towers fighting each other. Cell-free massive MIMO 
  (CF-mMIMO) scatters hundreds of tiny antennas ("access points," APs) all over
  the stadium. Every AP cooperates with every other AP via a wired backhaul to a
   central processing unit (CPU), and they all transmit simultaneously on the
  same frequency, beam-shaping their signals so the waves add up exactly at each
   user's phone. "Cell-free" = no cells, no cell-edges, every user is "in the
  middle."

  The catch: running all those antennas costs energy, and the wired backhaul 
  connecting them costs a lot of energy too (fiber transceivers, baseband,
  etc.). So the natural question — and the subject of the paper — is:

  ▎ "Is cell-free massive MIMO actually energy-efficient once you honestly 
  ▎ include backhaul power?"

  The paper answers yes, but only if you are clever, and it delivers four
  concrete things:

  1. A closed-form expression for the downlink spectral efficiency (SE) with
  multi-antenna APs (earlier CF-mMIMO papers only handled single-antenna APs).
  2. A realistic power model counting every source of consumption: power
  amplifier, per-antenna hardware (mixers, filters, converters), fixed backhaul
  cost per AP, and traffic-dependent backhaul cost growing with the data rate.
  3. An optimal EE-maximizing power-control algorithm with per-user SE and
  per-AP power constraints. The problem is non-convex → they use Sequential 
  Convex Approximation (SCA) to turn it into a sequence of second-order cone 
  programs (SOCPs).
  4. Two AP-selection schemes (received-power-based and
  largest-large-scale-fading-based) so each user is served by only a small
  subset of nearby APs, drastically cutting backhaul cost.

  Headline result: under a 1 bit/s/Hz per-user QoS requirement, CF-mMIMO beats
  colocated massive MIMO in EE by ≈7.4× (MN = 128) and ≈2.2× (MN = 256) —
  roughly an order of magnitude at moderate service-antenna counts.

  ---
  2 · PhD-Level Technical Explanation

  2.1 Inter-cell interference — the pain point

  Cellular networks are interference-limited, not noise-limited. At the cell
  edge, desired and interfering powers are comparable, so SINR_edge ≈ P_D/(P_I +
   σ²) ≈ 0 dB, while a cell-center user enjoys ~20 dB. Result: the 5-percentile
  user has ~100× worse throughput than the median. This is the "cell-edge
  problem."

  2.2 The classical remedy — Network MIMO

  Since the mid-2000s, the community has let neighboring BSs cooperate instead
  of fighting:
  - Network MIMO (Karakayali-Foschini-Valenzuela 2006, ref [1])
  - CoMP (3GPP LTE-A Rel-11 industrial version)
  - Distributed Antenna Systems (DAS) (passive in-building coverage since the
  1990s)

  They all share CSI + user data over a backhaul and jointly precode so that
  formerly interfering signals become coherent contributions to every user's
  desired signal.

  A key negative result the authors cite: Lozano-Heath-Andrews 2013 (ref [2])
  proved that even with full cooperation, per-user capacity saturates at a
  finite constant as transmit power → ∞ (out-of-cluster interference + pilot
  contamination). So network MIMO is not a silver bullet, but it still gives
  large practical gains.

  Its real-world enemies are (a) huge backhaul overhead, (b) very high
  deployment cost, (c) a capable CPU.

  2.3 Cell-free massive MIMO — the new embodiment

  Introduced in Ngo-Ashikhmin-Yang-Larsson-Marzetta IEEE TWC 2017 (ref [6]).
  Defining features:
  - Large number of APs, each with few antennas (originally one).
  - All APs connected to one or more CPUs via backhaul.
  - All APs serve all K single-antenna users on the same time-frequency resource
   — universal reuse, no handovers, no cells.
  - CSI via TDD uplink pilots → reciprocity gives downlink CSI (classic
  massive-MIMO trick, CSI overhead is O(K) not O(MK)).
  - Each AP does local precoding with its own CSI (distributed). The simplest
  choice, used here, is conjugate beamforming.

  From massive MIMO it inherits channel hardening (|g|² concentrates around
  𝔼{|g|²}) and favorable propagation (channels of different users become
  asymptotically orthogonal), so simple matched filtering is near-optimal.
  From DAS it inherits macro-diversity, low average path loss, and uniformly 
  good QoS (no cell-edge users).

  2.4 The research gap

  As of Feb 2017, CF-mMIMO literature was thin:
  - [6] — original paper, single-antenna APs, conjugate beamforming, pilot
  contamination.
  - [8] Nayebi et al. — zero-forcing, assumed orthogonal pilots.
  - [9] Interdonato et al. — beamformed downlink pilots.
  - [10] Huang-Burr — compute-and-forward to compress backhaul.

  Everyone assumed single-antenna APs, and nobody had rigorously analyzed 
  CF-mMIMO energy efficiency with a realistic backhaul power term. Colocated
  massive MIMO EE was already well understood via
  Björnson-Sanguinetti-Hoydis-Debbah 2015 (ref [11]), but CF-mMIMO has many more
   backhaul links, so a priori it was unclear whether CF-mMIMO is actually
  green.

  2.5 The four contributions in depth

  Contribution 1 — Closed-form SE. An exact (not asymptotic) closed-form
  downlink SE for arbitrary M, K, N ≥ 1, and arbitrary pilot sequences
  (non-orthogonal → pilot contamination baked in). It reduces to:
  - ref [6, Eq. (24)] when N = 1 (original single-antenna CF-mMIMO), and
  - colocated massive-MIMO SE of [7, Table 3.2] when M = 1 and N → M.

  The derivation uses the classical "use-and-then-forget" bound from the
  massive-MIMO capacity literature: thanks to channel hardening, the user can
  treat 𝔼{𝐠ᵀĝ*} as the "known" channel and absorb the deviation into
  Gaussian-equivalent effective noise.

  Contribution 2 — Optimal EE power control. Problem: max Ee s.t. per-user SE ≥ 
  S_{o,k}, per-AP power ≤ 1/N. This is a fractional program with non-convex
  numerator and non-convex SE constraints. The recipe:
  1. Substitute c_{mk} = √η_{mk} to remove square-roots.
  2. Apply Charnes-Cooper / linear-fractional transformation to linearize the
  objective.
  3. Convex-bound the SE constraint via first-order Taylor expansion + the
  inequality ln(x) ≥ 1 − 1/x (which gives log₂(u) ≥ log₂(uⁿ) + log₂(e)(1 − 
  uⁿ/u)).
  4. Reformulate as an SOCP; solve iteratively (Algorithm 1). The objective
  sequence is non-decreasing and bounded → converges (empirically in ~10
  iterations).

  Contribution 3 — Two AP-selection schemes. At large M, the B · S_e · Σ_m 
  P_{bt,m} backhaul term dominates and crushes EE. Fix: each user is served by
  only a subset of nearby APs.
  - (a) Received-power-based: rank APs by fraction of desired-signal power
  contributed, keep top APs contributing ≥ ρ% (they use ρ = 95%).
  - (b) Largest-large-scale-fading-based: rank by β_{mk} (path loss × shadow
  fading), keep top APs contributing ≥ ρ% of total β.

  Scheme (b) is cheaper (no Algorithm 1 needed upfront). Numerically they give
  nearly identical EE, and only ~10–20% of APs per user are actually needed.

  Contribution 4 — CF vs. Colocated comparison. With power control + AP
  selection, CF-mMIMO dramatically outperforms colocated massive MIMO in EE
  under uniform per-user QoS.

  4 · Mathematical Preview (Scaffolding for Sec II–III)

  Although Section I has no numbered equations, it implicitly introduces all the
   key objects.

  M                  : number of APs
  K                  : number of users           (M ≫ K)
  N                  : antennas per AP           (≥ 1, the new generalization)
  MN                 : total service antennas
  τ_c                : coherence interval (samples)
  τ_p < τ_c          : pilot length (samples)
  τ_c − τ_p          : downlink payload length
  𝛗_k ∈ ℂ^{τ_p}      : pilot of user k, ‖𝛗_k‖ = 1

  Channel (Rayleigh + large-scale fading):
  𝐠_{m,k} = √β_{m,k} · 𝐡_{m,k},    𝐡_{m,k} ~ 𝒞𝒩(𝟎_N, 𝐈_N)
  where β_{m,k} is deterministic (path-loss × log-normal shadowing).

  MMSE channel estimate (from despread pilot 𝐲_{p,m}^{(k)} = 𝐘_{p,m} · 𝛗_k*):
  ĝ_{m,k} = α_{m,k} · 𝐲_{p,m}^{(k)},   per-component mean-square = γ_{m,k}

  Conjugate beamforming transmit signal at AP m:
  𝐱_m = √ρ_d · Σ_{k=1}^{K} √η_{m,k} · ĝ_{m,k}* · q_k

  Per-AP power constraint:
  Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} · γ_{m,k}  ≤  1/N,   m = 1, …, M

  Total downlink power consumption (from Sec III-A):
  P_total  =  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_m  +  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bh,m}
  P_m       =  (1/ν_m) · ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_k η_{m,k}γ_{m,k}  +  N · P_{tc,m}
  P_{bh,m}  =  P_{0,m}  +  B · S_e · P_{bt,m}

  Total energy efficiency (the objective):
             B · S_e({η_{m,k}})
  E_e   =  ─────────────────────────        [bit / Joule]
             P_total({η_{m,k}})

  Every symbol becomes an explicit numbered equation in Sec II–III.

  5 · Numerical Worked Example

  Let's ground the abstract power-model story with a hand calculation showing
  why backhaul is scary.

  Configuration:
  M = 100 APs,   N = 1,   K = 20,   B = 20 MHz,
  S_{o,k} = 1 bit/s/Hz  →  S_e = 20 bit/s/Hz,
  ν_m = 0.4,  P_{tc,m} = 0.2 W,  P_{0,m} = 0.825 W,
  P_{bt,m} = 0.25 W/(Gbit/s) = 2.5 × 10⁻¹⁰ W/(bit/s),
  ρ_d = 1 W,   N₀ ≈ 4 × 10⁻¹³ W  (@ NF = 9 dB, B = 20 MHz)

  Step 1 — Sum throughput:
  R_sum = B · S_e = 2×10⁷ · 20 = 4 × 10⁸ bit/s = 400 Mbit/s

  Step 2 — PA + circuit power per AP (full-budget assumption):
  P_m ≈ (1/0.4) · 1 · (1/N) + N·0.2 = 2.5 + 0.2 = 2.7 W
  Over M = 100:  Σ_m P_m ≈ 270 W

  Step 3 — Backhaul:
  - Fixed: 100 · 0.825 = 82.5 W
  - Traffic-dependent: 4×10⁸ · 100 · 2.5×10⁻¹⁰ = 10 W
  - Total BH: 92.5 W

  Step 4 — Total:
  P_total = 270 + 92.5 = 362.5 W

  Step 5 — EE:
  E_e = 4×10⁸ / 362.5 ≈ 1.10 × 10⁶ bit/J ≈ 1.10 Mbit/Joule

  The scary scaling: push M → 500 (same K = 20). Fixed backhaul → 412.5 W,
  PA+circuit → 1350 W, traffic BH → 50 W ⇒ P_total ≈ 1812.5 W ⇒ E_e ≈ 0.22 
  Mbit/J — a 5× collapse. This is precisely why the paper introduces AP
  selection: if only ~15% of APs actively serve each user, most of the 412.5 W
  fixed backhaul and traffic-dep. backhaul evaporates.

  Expected answers (sanity check):
  - (a) R_sum = 1.6 Gbit/s
  - (b) per-AP PA ≈ 2.5 W, circuit = 4·0.2 = 0.8 W, per-AP ≈ 3.3 W → Σ P_m ≈ 660
   W
  - (c) fixed BH = 165 W, traffic BH = 1.6×10⁹ · 200 · 2.5×10⁻¹⁰ = 80 W → total
  BH = 245 W
  - (d) P_total ≈ 905 W, E_e ≈ 1.77 Mbit/J

  Notice that going from (M=100, N=1, K=20) to (M=200, N=4, K=40) raised EE
  despite higher absolute power, because R_sum grew faster. But the fixed
  backhaul part is paid whether or not users are active — that is the term AP
  selection is trying to kill.

  ---
  6 · Summary of Section I

  - Problem context. Cellular networks are interference-limited at the edge.
  Network-MIMO / CoMP / DAS try to fix this via BS cooperation, at the cost of
  heavy backhaul. Fundamental limits [2] show even full cooperation saturates,
  but gains are still practically large.
  - Cell-free massive MIMO (introduced in [6]): huge number of APs
  (single-antenna in prior work), TDD reciprocity, conjugate beamforming with
  local CSI, no cells. Inherits macro-diversity + channel hardening + favorable
  propagation.
  - The gap. Prior CF-mMIMO work assumed single-antenna APs and did not analyze
  EE with a realistic backhaul model. Because CF needs many more backhaul links
  than colocated massive MIMO, it was unclear whether CF is actually green.
  - This paper's contributions:
    a. Closed-form downlink SE for multi-antenna APs with arbitrary pilots;
  reduces to [6] for N = 1 and colocated massive MIMO [7] for M = 1.
    b. Realistic power model including fixed and traffic-dependent backhaul
  (extending [11]).
    c. EE-maximizing power control under per-user SE + per-AP power constraints,
   approximated by a sequence of SOCPs via SCA.
    d. Two AP-selection schemes (received-power-based, largest-LSF-based) to
  slash backhaul power.
    e. Quantitative CF vs. colocated massive MIMO comparison showing CF's clear
  EE advantage under per-user QoS.
  - Headline. Under 1 bit/s/Hz per-user QoS, CF-mMIMO improves EE by ≈7.4× (MN =
   128) and ≈2.2× (MN = 256) vs. colocated massive MIMO — roughly an order of
  magnitude at moderate service-antenna counts. This established CF-mMIMO as a
  genuinely green candidate for 5G/6G distributed MIMO deployments.

  ---
  1 · High-Level (Layman) Explanation

  Section II does two things:

  (A) System Model. It defines the cell-free massive MIMO set-up — who transmits
   what, when, and with what noise. Each coherence block has three phases:

  uplink training  →  uplink data (ignored here)  →  downlink payload
  └── τ_p samples     (skipped)                     └── τ_c − τ_p samples

  - During uplink training every user simultaneously transmits a known "pilot"
  sequence. Each AP listens and uses MMSE (Minimum Mean-Square Error) estimation
   to estimate its channel to every user. Estimates are never shared between APs
   — each AP keeps its own (distributed processing).
  - During downlink payload every AP multiplies each user's data symbol by the
  complex conjugate of its local channel estimate — this is conjugate 
  beamforming (matched-filter precoding). Intuition: when the real channel
  multiplies this conjugate on the way to the user, the two phases cancel and
  the signal lands "in phase". Summed over many APs the phases add coherently →
  user k receives a strong clean version of q_k.

  (B) Spectral Efficiency. It derives the per-user downlink data rate. Since
  users have no downlink pilots, they cannot know the exact instantaneous
  effective channel. So the paper applies the classical "use-and-then-forget"
  capacity bound from the Marzetta–Larsson–Yang–Ngo massive-MIMO textbook:

  ▎ "Trust the average channel. Treat the deviation around the average as extra 
  ▎ noise. Then apply Shannon."

  The key technical result is Proposition 1, which gives the SE in a clean 
  closed form — no integrals, no Monte-Carlo — so Sections III–V can plug it
  straight into optimization problems.

  Big takeaways:
  1. The central parameter γ_{m,k} = mean square of each MMSE estimate component
   packages path-loss, shadowing, pilot length, pilot power, and pilot
  contamination into one scalar.
  2. The per-AP power constraint Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N.
  3. The closed-form SE in Proposition 1 (Eq. 15) — the object the rest of the
  paper optimizes.

  2 · PhD-Level Explanation — PART A: System Model

  2.1 Geometry, numbers, coherence block

  - M APs, each with N antennas → total MN service antennas
  - K single-antenna users
  - M ≫ K (massive regime — required for channel hardening + favorable
  propagation)
  - TDD operation → reciprocity holds within each coherence block
  - Coherence interval τ_c samples (≈ 1 / (B_d · T_d) with Doppler B_d and delay
   T_d)
  - τ_p < τ_c reserved for uplink pilots; τ_c − τ_p for downlink payload (uplink
   payload neglected)

  2.2 Large-scale vs. small-scale fading

  𝐠_{m,k} = √β_{m,k} · 𝐡_{m,k}                               (Eq. 2)
  - β_{m,k} ∈ ℝ₊: large-scale fading = path loss × log-normal shadowing. Does
  not depend on antenna index — all N antennas of AP m are spatially collocated,
   so they share β.
  - 𝐡_{m,k} ∈ ℂ^{N×1}: small-scale (Rayleigh) fading, entries IID 𝒞𝒩(0,1).

  Consequence: 𝐠_{m,k} ~ 𝒞𝒩(𝟎_N, β_{m,k}·𝐈_N).

  2.3 Uplink training received pilot

  Let 𝛗_k ∈ ℂ^{τ_p} be user k's pilot, normalized ‖𝛗_k‖² = 1. All K users
  transmit simultaneously. AP m observes a superposition:

  𝐘_{p,m} = √(τ_p ρ_p) · Σ_{k=1}^{K} 𝐠_{m,k} · 𝛗_k^H  +  𝐖_{p,m}           (Eq.
  1)

  Dimensions: 𝐠_{m,k} is N×1, 𝛗_k^H is 1×τ_p → outer product is N×τ_p ✓. 𝐖_{p,m}
   is N×τ_p noise with IID 𝒞𝒩(0,1) entries. ρ_p is the normalized uplink pilot
  SNR. The factor √(τ_p ρ_p) represents processing gain — a matched filter over
  τ_p samples boosts SNR by τ_p.

  2.4 Despreading at AP m

  AP m multiplies 𝐘_{p,m} on the right by 𝛗_k:

  𝐲_{p,m,k} = 𝐘_{p,m} 𝛗_k
           = √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k}                              ← clean pilot of
  user k
           + √(τ_p ρ_p) Σ_{k'≠k} 𝐠_{m,k'} (𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k)     ← PILOT
  CONTAMINATION
           + 𝐰̃_{p,m,k}                                        ← 𝒞𝒩(𝟎, 𝐈_N) noise
                                                                        (Eq. 3)

  The pilot contamination term is nonzero whenever two users' pilots are not
  orthogonal. Since τ_p < K is common in cell-free deployments, full
  orthogonality is impossible and this term is the villain of the story.

  2.5 MMSE channel estimate

  For jointly Gaussian vectors, MMSE is a linear projection. Computing the two
  relevant expectations:

  𝔼{𝐠_{m,k} 𝐲_{p,m,k}^H} = √(τ_p ρ_p) · β_{m,k} · 𝐈_N

  𝔼{𝐲_{p,m,k} 𝐲_{p,m,k}^H} = (τ_p ρ_p · Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|² + 1) ·
  𝐈_N

  Inverting gives:

                     √(τ_p ρ_p) β_{m,k}
  ĝ_{m,k}  = ─────────────────────────────────────────── · 𝐲_{p,m,k}     (Eq. 4)
              τ_p ρ_p Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|² + 1

  Two important observations:
  1. ĝ_{m,k} is a scalar multiple of 𝐲_{p,m,k} — all N components share the same
   per-component statistics.
  2. The denominator includes the pilot-contamination interference — more
  non-orthogonal pilots → larger denominator → estimate biased towards
  pilot-cluster subspace.

  2.6 The central parameter γ_{m,k}

  Define γ_{m,k} ≝ 𝔼{|[ĝ_{m,k}]_n|²} — mean square of any component of the
  estimate. A short calculation gives:

                  τ_p ρ_p β_{m,k}²
  γ_{m,k}  = ─────────────────────────────────────                         (Eq.
  5)
              τ_p ρ_p Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k|² + 1

  This scalar γ_{m,k} is the most important object of the paper. It compresses
  path loss, shadowing, pilot length, pilot power, pilot contamination, and
  receiver noise into one positive number. Every power-allocation and SE formula
   below is written as a function of {γ_{m,k}}.

  Sanity checks:
  - No pilot contamination: γ_{m,k} = τ_p ρ_p β_{m,k}² / (τ_p ρ_p β_{m,k} + 1) →
   β_{m,k} as τ_p ρ_p → ∞ (perfect CSI limit).
  - Heavy contamination: γ_{m,k} drops below β_{m,k} — the estimator is hurt.

  Orthogonality principle: the error 𝐠̃_{m,k} ≝ 𝐠_{m,k} − ĝ_{m,k} is independent
  of ĝ_{m,k} and has entries 𝒞𝒩(0, β_{m,k} − γ_{m,k}). Note γ_{m,k} ≤ β_{m,k}
  always.

  2.7 Conjugate beamforming transmit signal

  𝐱_m = √ρ_d · Σ_{k=1}^{K} √η_{m,k} · ĝ_{m,k}^* · q_k                    (Eq. 6)

  where q_k (unit-power symbol), ρ_d (max normalized DL SNR), η_{m,k} ≥ 0 (power
   control coeffs — the optimization variables), and ĝ_{m,k}^* (element-wise
  conjugate of local estimate).

  Why conjugate? Because downlink channel is 𝐠_{m,k}^T (row vector, by
  reciprocity). The matched filter needs ĝ_{m,k}^* so that 𝐠_{m,k}^T · ĝ_{m,k}^*
   ≈ ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² (a positive real number up to estimation error).

  Expected transmit power at AP m:
  𝔼{‖𝐱_m‖²} = ρ_d Σ_k η_{m,k} · 𝔼{‖ĝ_{m,k}‖²}
           = ρ_d Σ_k η_{m,k} · N γ_{m,k}
           = ρ_d · N · Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}                              (Eq. 7)

  Constraining 𝔼{‖𝐱_m‖²} ≤ ρ_d yields the per-AP constraint:
  Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,    m = 1, …, M                    (Eq. 8)

  Notice the 1/N on the right: more antennas per AP → less power per antenna is
  allowed, so total AP power stays fixed regardless of N. This is the "fair
  comparison" across different N values.

  2.8 Received signal at user k

  r_k = √ρ_d Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* · q_k
  (desired)
      + √ρ_d Σ_{k'≠k} Σ_m √η_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k'}^* · q_{k'}       (interf)
      + w_k,       w_k ~ 𝒞𝒩(0,1)                                        (noise)
                                                                         (Eq. 9)

  ---
  3 · PhD-Level Explanation — PART B: Spectral Efficiency

  3.1 "Use-and-then-forget" capacity bound

  User k has no downlink pilots — no way to measure the instantaneous effective
  channel Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*. All it knows are channel statistics
  (β's and γ's). So for detection, the user treats the mean of the effective
  channel as the true gain and lumps the deviation into Gaussian-equivalent
  "effective noise."

  Add-and-subtract the expectation in Eq. 9:

  r_k = DS_k · q_k             (desired signal, deterministic mean gain)
      + BU_k · q_k             (beamforming uncertainty, zero-mean)
      + Σ_{k'≠k} UI_{k,k'} · q_{k'}    (inter-user interference)
      + w_k                     (thermal noise)                      (Eq. 10)

  with
  DS_k      ≝ √ρ_d · 𝔼{Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*}               (Eq. 12)
  BU_k      ≝ √ρ_d · (Σ_m √η_{m,k} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^* − 𝔼{…})        (Eq. 13)
  UI_{k,k'} ≝ √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k'} 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k'}^*                (Eq. 14)

  The Medard (IEEE TIT 2000) / Marzetta worst-case-uncorrelated-noise argument
  says that if the self-noise BU_k·q_k + Σ_{k'≠k} UI_{k,k'}·q_{k'} + w_k is
  uncorrelated with q_k (it is, by linearity of expectation and independence of
  data symbols and channel estimates), then treating it as independent Gaussian 
  noise gives an achievable lower bound on capacity:

                                           |DS_k|²
  SE_k ≥ (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂(1 + ─────────────────────────────────── )   (Eq.
  11)
                                  𝔼{|BU_k|²} + Σ_{k'≠k} 𝔼{|UI_{k,k'}|²} + 1

  The prefactor (1 − τ_p/τ_c) accounts for the τ_p pilot samples "lost" per
  coherence block. The "+1" is the normalized thermal noise. The bound is called
   "use-and-forget" because the receiver uses the channel to receive signals but
   forgets everything about the instantaneous realization when decoding — only
  means are kept. The looseness of this bound shrinks as M grows, because
  channel hardening makes BU_k small relative to DS_k.

  3.2 Proposition 1 — the closed-form SE

  After careful expectation of (12)–(14) using the Gaussianity of channels and
  MMSE estimates (Appendix A), (11) becomes an explicit closed form. Define:

  𝛈_k  ≝ [η_{1,k}, η_{2,k}, …, η_{M,k}]^T   ∈ ℝ₊^M

  𝛄_{k',k} ≝ |𝛗_{k'}^H 𝛗_k| · [γ_{1,k'}·√(β_{1,k}/β_{1,k'}),
                                γ_{2,k'}·√(β_{2,k}/β_{2,k'}),
                                ⋯,
                                γ_{M,k'}·√(β_{M,k}/β_{M,k'})]^T  ∈ ℝ^M
         (with [𝛄_{k,k}]_m = γ_{m,k})

  𝐃_{k',k} ≝ diag(√(β_{1,k} γ_{1,k'}), …, √(β_{M,k} γ_{M,k'}))  ∈ ℝ^{M×M}

  Proposition 1:

  SE_k({η_{m,k}}) = (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂(  1  +

                     ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|²
       ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────   )   (Eq.
  15)
       ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|² + ρ_d N Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖² +
  1

  Interpretation of each piece:

  ┌───────────┬──────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │   Term    │    Expression    │                   Role                   │
  ├───────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤

  ├───────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Denom 1   │ `ρ_d N² ·        │ 𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}                        │

  │           │ ρ_d N · Σ_{k'}   │ Non-coherent interference (BU +          │

  Special cases the authors highlight:
  - N = 1 (single-antenna APs): (15) reduces exactly to Eq. (24) of the Ngo 2017

  - M = 1, N → M' (one colocated AP with M' antennas): (15) reduces to Table 3.2
   of the Marzetta textbook [7].

  3.3 Sum spectral efficiency

  S_e({η_{m,k}}) = Σ_{k=1}^{K} SE_k({η_{m,k}})                            (Eq.
  16)
  This is what enters the backhaul term B · S_e · P_{bt,m} in Section III.

  3.4 Why "closed form" matters

  Before this paper, CF-mMIMO SE formulas required Monte-Carlo (sample channels,
   average). The closed-form (15) depends only on deterministic quantities
  {β_{m,k}, γ_{m,k}, η_{m,k}, τ_p, τ_c, ρ_d}. Therefore:
  - Power control {η_{m,k}} can be optimized without running any channel
  simulation inside the loop.
  - β's and γ's vary on a much slower time scale than small-scale fading →
  optimization is re-run only once per ~100–1000 coherence blocks.

  This is what makes the SCA-SOCP optimization of Section IV computationally
  feasible.

  3.5 A notational subtlety

  Looking at the proof in Appendix A carefully, the η_{m,k} appearing inside
  |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|² in Eq. (15) really should be read as √η_{m,k} — i.e. the
  paper reuses symbols. Section IV fixes this by explicitly defining c_{m,k} ≝ 
  √η_{m,k}. Keep this in mind when verifying Eq. (15) by hand.

  4 · Historical Context

  - MMSE estimation for massive MIMO with pilot contamination: worked out fully
  by Hoydis–ten Brink–Debbah (IEEE JSAC 2013) via deterministic equivalents. The
   single-cell IID case used here is a direct application of Kay, Fundamentals 
  of Statistical Signal Processing, Vol. 1: Estimation Theory (1993, Ch. 10–12).
  - Use-and-then-forget bound: originates in Médard (IEEE TIT 2000) "The effect
  upon channel capacity in wireless communications of perfect and imperfect
  knowledge of the channel" — worst-case Gaussian noise replacing the true
  non-Gaussian effective noise. Marzetta popularized it for massive MIMO
  starting with his 2010 IEEE TWC paper. The cleanest modern treatment is
  Marzetta–Larsson–Yang–Ngo textbook Ch. 2.3.
  - Conjugate / matched-filter beamforming: goes back to radar detection theory
  (1940s). Massive-MIMO renaissance is due to channel hardening: ‖𝐠‖²/N → β
  almost surely, so matched filtering is asymptotically optimal in the number of
   antennas.
  - Pilot contamination: identified and named by Marzetta (2010). The CF-mMIMO
  literature [6, 8, 9] extended the analysis. The clean treatment of CF pilot
  contamination with MMSE is in Ngo 2017 TWC [6]; Section II here is its
  multi-antenna generalization.

  5 · Numerical Worked Example

  Setup: M = 2, K = 2, N = 2, τ_p = τ_c = 200 (prefactor ≈ 0.995), orthogonal
  pilots, ρ_p = ρ_d = 1. Large-scale fading:
  β_{1,1} = 0.1,  β_{1,2} = 0.01
  β_{2,1} = 0.01, β_{2,2} = 0.1
  (AP 1 close to user 1, AP 2 close to user 2.)

  Step 1 — γ_{m,k} from Eq. 5:
  γ_{1,1} = 200·(0.1)² / (200·0.1 + 1) = 2/21 ≈ 0.0952
  γ_{1,2} = 200·(0.01)² / (200·0.01 + 1) = 0.02/3 ≈ 0.00667
  γ_{2,1} = 0.00667 (by symmetry)
  γ_{2,2} = 0.0952

  Step 2 — equal-split power control. Per-AP constraint: η·(γ_{m,1} + γ_{m,2}) ≤
   1/N = 0.5, so η ≤ 0.5 / 0.1019 ≈ 4.908. Use equality: η_{m,k} = 4.908 ∀ m, k.

  In Eq. 15 we use the √ of η (see §3.5): 𝛈_k = [√4.908, √4.908]^T ≈ [2.215, 
  2.215]^T.

  Step 3 — numerator (user 1):
  𝛄_{1,1} = [0.0952, 0.00667]^T
  |𝛄_{1,1}^T 𝛈_1|² = |0.0952·2.215 + 0.00667·2.215|²
                  = |0.2108 + 0.01478|² = 0.05090
  ρ_d N² · 0.05090 = 4 · 0.05090 = 0.2036

  Step 4 — denominator:
  - Pilot-contamination term: 0 (orthogonal pilots ⇒ 𝛄_{1,2} = 𝟎).
  - Non-coherent term:
  𝐃_{1,1} = diag(√(0.1·0.0952), √(0.01·0.00667)) = diag(0.09757, 0.008165)
  ‖𝐃_{1,1} 𝛈_1‖² = (0.2161)² + (0.01809)² ≈ 0.04703
  𝐃_{2,1} = diag(√(0.1·0.00667), √(0.01·0.0952)) = diag(0.02583, 0.03086)
  ‖𝐃_{2,1} 𝛈_2‖² ≈ 0.003275 + 0.004673 ≈ 0.00795
  Sum ≈ 0.05498  →  ρ_d N · 0.05498 ≈ 0.10995
  - Thermal noise: 1

  Step 5 — SINR and SE:
  SINR_1 = 0.2036 / (0 + 0.10995 + 1) ≈ 0.1834
  SE_1   = 0.995 · log₂(1.1834) ≈ 0.995 · 0.2429 ≈ 0.2417 bit/s/Hz
  By symmetry SE_2 ≈ 0.2417, sum SE ≈ 0.4834 bit/s/Hz. Low because of the tiny
  scale — real M = 100, N = 4 would give orders of magnitude higher thanks to
  the N² coherent gain.

  6 · Summary of Section II

  - Setup. M APs (N antennas each), K single-antenna users, TDD, coherence τ_c,
  pilot length τ_p < τ_c, IID Rayleigh small-scale fading + deterministic
  large-scale β_{m,k}.
  - Uplink training (Eq. 1): users simultaneously transmit τ_p-sample pilots
  𝛗_k; AP m receives a superposition corrupted by AWGN.
  - MMSE estimation (Eqs. 3–5): AP m despreads by 𝛗k and applies the MMSE scalar
   to get ĝ{m,k}. All components of ĝ_{m,k} share the same variance γ_{m,k} —
  the single scalar encapsulating path-loss × pilot × contamination. Estimation
  error is independent with variance β_{m,k} − γ_{m,k}.
  - Conjugate beamforming (Eq. 6): 𝐱_m = √ρ_d Σ_k √η_{m,k} ĝ_{m,k}^* q_k. Per-AP
   average power ρ_d N Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} gives the constraint Σ_k η_{m,k} 
  γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N (Eq. 8).
  - Received signal (Eq. 9): superposition of desired, inter-user interference,
  and AWGN.
  - Use-and-forget SE bound (Eqs. 10–11): user treats 𝔼{effective channel} as
  known gain, lumps everything else into Gaussian noise. Achievable SE lower
  bound, tight in the massive-MIMO limit.
  - Proposition 1 (Eq. 15): explicit closed-form SE in {γ_{m,k}, β_{m,k}, 𝛈_k, 
  τ_p, τ_c, ρ_d}. Key structure:
    - numerator ∝ ρ_d N² (coherent combining)
    - pilot-contamination term ∝ ρ_d N² (does not vanish with N)
    - non-coherent interference ∝ ρ_d N (washes out as N → ∞)

  Reduces to Ngo 2017 TWC for N = 1 and to colocated massive MIMO for M = 1.
  - Why closed form matters: no Monte-Carlo inside optimization; β's and γ's
  change slowly, so the optimization (Sec IV) runs once per hundreds of
  coherence blocks.
  - Subtlety: Eq. 15 internally needs √η_{m,k}; Section IV fixes this via
  c_{m,k} ≝ √η_{m,k}.

  1 · High-Level (Layman) Explanation

  Section II told us how many bits/s/Hz CF-mMIMO can squeeze out of the
  spectrum. Section III asks the complementary question:

  ▎ "How many joules per second does this same system burn to deliver those 
  ▎ bits?"

  You could naively answer "just the transmit power" and be tragically wrong —
  modern wireless systems spend far more energy on hardware and signaling 
  overhead than on radiating RF. A concrete example: a 20-W-output macro BS
  typically draws 800–1300 W from the wall socket. Only 1–2 % of its electricity
   bill actually leaves the antenna as radio waves. The rest is:

  - Heat lost in the power amplifier (PA efficiency ≈ 30–50 %)
  - DC power burned by hardware chains (mixers, ADCs/DACs, oscillators, filters)
  - Baseband digital processing (FFTs, channel estimation, decoding)
  - Backhaul transceivers shipping data between BS and core network
  - Air-conditioning, power supplies, losses, monitoring, …

  Any honest EE paper therefore needs a component-level power-consumption model.
   Björnson–Sanguinetti–Hoydis–Debbah IEEE TWC 2015 [11] is the canonical parent
   model for colocated massive MIMO, and Section III is essentially its
  specialization to cell-free with one critical addition: explicit backhaul 
  power for each of the M access-point links.

  Why is backhaul new? A colocated BS has one backhaul. A cell-free system has M
   of them. So:
  - Total backhaul energy grows roughly linearly in M.
  - Traffic-dependent backhaul also grows linearly with the sum rate S_e.

  Adding more APs simultaneously raises S_e (good) and P_total (bad). If you are
   not careful, "bit per joule" can actually decrease even though "bit per
  second" increases. Section III models all of this cleanly and produces a
  closed-form P_total that Section IV can optimize.

  Headline to carry forward: In cell-free massive MIMO, the backhaul is the 
  elephant in the room. A realistic EE analysis without it is not a realistic EE
   analysis at all.

  2 · PhD-Level Detailed Explanation

  2.1 Total power decomposition (Eq. 17)

  P_total = Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_m  +  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bh,m}                     (17)

  - P_m = power at AP m due to amplifier + circuit (transceiver chains, signal
  processing)
  - P_{bh,m} = power consumed by the backhaul link from AP m to the CPU

  What is NOT modeled separately: CPU power itself — it would be a constant
  "+P_CPU" additive term that does not depend on the power-allocation variables
  and therefore does not change the optimization. Reference [11] treats it
  explicitly, but here it is absorbed into the background constant. Also,
  baseband processing cost (FFTs, channel estimation, matched filtering) is not
  modeled separately — it is bundled into the per-antenna circuit term
  N·P_{tc,m} below.

  2.2 AP hardware power (Eq. 18)

  P_m = (1/ν_m) · ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}    [PA draw]
      + N · P_{tc,m}                                            [circuit]
  (18)

  Unpacking the PA-draw term step by step:

  (i) From Eq. (7) of Section II, the expected normalized transmit power at AP m
   is 𝔼{‖𝐱_m‖²} = ρ_d · N · Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}. Multiplying by N₀ converts
  normalized → Watts:
  P_RF,m = ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}   [W]

  (ii) The PA does not convert all DC power into RF — only a fraction ν_m ∈ (0, 
  1] comes out as RF, the rest is heat. Hence DC draw = P_RF,m / ν_m. That is
  where (1/ν_m) comes from. Typical values:
  - ν_m ≈ 0.3–0.4 for class-A/AB PAs in commodity BSs (paper's default: 0.4)
  - ν_m ≈ 0.5–0.6 for class-B/F PAs
  - ν_m ≈ 0.8–0.9 only for envelope-tracking PAs in research prototypes

  (iii) The N factor is cleverly counter-balanced by the per-AP power constraint
   Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N. Therefore:
  P_RF,m ≤ ρ_d · N₀ · N · (1/N) = ρ_d · N₀
  The worst-case per-AP PA draw is ρ_d·N₀/ν_m, independent of N. Increasing
  antennas per AP does not change total PA power at full load — this enforces a
  fair comparison across different N values.

  The circuit term N · P_{tc,m} is the DC power consumed by the transceiver
  chains (one per antenna): oscillator, mixer, LNA, ADC/DAC, filter. This cost
  is paid even if the antenna is not radiating anything — it is the idle/on
  cost. Scales linearly with N. Paper default: P_{tc,m} = 0.2 W/antenna → a
  4-antenna AP burns 0.8 W continuously regardless of traffic.

  2.3 Backhaul power (Eq. 19) — the new term

  This is the term that does not exist in the classical colocated MIMO EE model
  and is the physical reason the paper was written:

  P_{bh,m} = P_{0,m}  +  B · S_e · P_{bt,m}                              (19)

  Three ingredients:

  - P_{0,m} — fixed, traffic-independent backhaul power. The power consumed by
  the backhaul transceiver simply being switched on: fiber transceivers, media
  converters, switching. Depends on the distance AP-to-CPU, the link technology
  (fiber/copper/microwave), and topology (star/daisy-chain/ring). Does not 
  depend on traffic. Paper default: 0.825 W/AP.
  - P_{bt,m} — traffic-dependent backhaul power per unit rate. Units: W /
  (bit/s), or equivalently J/bit. Paper default: 0.25 W / (Gbit/s) = 2.5 × 10⁻¹⁰
   W / (bit/s).
  - B · S_e — total data rate (bit/s) carried by the backhaul. Every AP serves
  every user with conjugate beamforming, so every AP's backhaul must receive all
   K users' data symbols. Per-AP backhaul data rate is therefore B · S_e, and
  multiplying by P_{bt,m} gives the traffic-dependent power.

  Later in Section V the authors note that if AP m only serves a subset of
  users, its backhaul only needs to carry the sum rate of those users — which is
   strictly smaller than B·S_e. That is the argument behind AP selection.

  Footnote 1 of the paper acknowledges that the backhaul also carries sync
  signals, power-control coefficients, and large-scale-fading updates. These are
   transmitted only once per large-scale-fading realization (~1 s), so their
  rate is negligible compared to user data → neglected.

  2.4 All-in-one expression (Eq. 20)

  Plugging (18) and (19) into (17):

  P_total = ρ_d · N₀ · Σ_m (1/ν_m) · N · Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
                     └── η-dependent PA power, summed over APs ──┘

          + Σ_m (N · P_{tc,m} + P_{0,m})
            └── constant: circuit + fixed backhaul ──┘

          + B · Σ_m P_{bt,m} · S_e({η_{m,k}})                            (20)
            └── η-dependent traffic backhaul (via S_e) ──┘

  Introduce the "background fixed" shorthand:
  P̄_fix ≝ Σ_{m=1}^{M} (N · P_{tc,m} + P_{0,m})
  This is the power the network burns no matter what the users are doing — the
  baseline floor.

  Key observation — the EE trade-off: two pieces of P_total depend on η:
  1. The first one grows with the RF power you put on air.
  2. The last one grows with the sum spectral efficiency you deliver.

  Both go up when you increase η. More η ⇒ more bits and more joules — which
  effect wins depends on the coefficients. That is the EE optimization tension.

  2.5 Total energy efficiency (Eq. 21)

  E_e = B · S_e / P_total                                                (21)

  Units check:
  numerator:   [Hz] · [bit/s/Hz] = [bit/s]
  denominator: [W] = [J/s]
  ratio:       [bit/s] / [J/s] = [bit/J]   ✓

  E_e is a ratio of two functions of η, both of which are non-convex (S_e is a
  sum of logs, and P_total contains those same logs through the backhaul piece).
   This is a fractional program, and the two classical tools are:

  (a) Dinkelbach's transform — introduce parameter λ and iteratively solve max 
  {numerator − λ · denominator}, update λ until numerator = λ·denominator.

  (b) Charnes–Cooper transformation — applicable only when numerator and
  denominator are linear (they are not here).

  The paper uses a hybrid: it first transforms (21) by dividing numerator and 
  denominator by B·S_e (a positive quantity) to absorb the traffic-dependent
  backhaul into the fixed part, then applies SCA + SOCP to the transformed
  problem.

  2.6 The "divide-by-B·S_e" trick (preview of Sec IV)

  Because the paper relies on this trick in Section IV but introduces it here at
   the end of Section III, it is worth spelling out now.

  Divide numerator and denominator of (21) by B·S_e (strictly positive whenever
  any user actually gets rate):

                         1
  E_e  =  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
          P̄_fix / (B·S_e)
        + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} / (B·S_e)
        + Σ_m P_{bt,m}

  The last term Σ_m P_{bt,m} is now constant — no more η-dependence, because we
  just divided the backhaul term B·S_e·Σ_m P_{bt,m} by B·S_e. Therefore E_e is a
   monotonically increasing function of

                      B · S_e
         ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
         P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}

  i.e., maximizing E_e is equivalent to maximizing this new fractional objective
   whose denominator is affine in the "quadratic power" variables (after c_{m,k}
   ≝ √η_{m,k}). That is why Section IV can reformulate the problem as a sequence
   of SOCPs.

  This quotient trick is the single most important analytic manoeuvre in the
  whole paper, and it is enabled precisely by the linear-in-S_e shape of the
  backhaul power model (19). If backhaul power were, say, logarithmic in S_e (as
   for information-theoretically optimal backhaul quantizers), the trick would
  not work and a much harder non-convex problem would remain.

  4 · Mathematical Recap (Unicode)

  Variables & constants:
  M      number of APs               ν_m        PA efficiency of AP m ∈ (0,1]
  N      antennas per AP              ρ_d        max normalized DL SNR
  K      number of users              N₀         thermal noise power, W
  γ_{m,k}  MMSE per-component var.    η_{m,k}    power-control coeff. (≥ 0)
  P_{tc,m} per-antenna circuit, W     P_{0,m}    fixed backhaul, W
  P_{bt,m} traffic-dep backhaul, W/(bit/s)
  B      system bandwidth, Hz          S_e        sum SE (bit/s/Hz)

  Eq. (17) — total power decomposition:
  P_total = Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_m  +  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bh,m}

  Eq. (18) — AP hardware power:
  P_m = (1/ν_m) · ρ_d · N₀ · N · Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}  +  N · P_{tc,m}

  Eq. (19) — backhaul power:
  P_{bh,m} = P_{0,m}  +  B · S_e · P_{bt,m}

  Eq. (20) — all-in-one:
  P_total  =  ρ_d · N₀ · Σ_m (1/ν_m) · N · Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
            + Σ_m (N · P_{tc,m} + P_{0,m})
            + B · Σ_m P_{bt,m} · S_e({η_{m,k}})

  Shorthand:
  P̄_fix ≝ Σ_m (N · P_{tc,m} + P_{0,m})

  P_total = ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
          + P̄_fix
          + B · (Σ_m P_{bt,m}) · S_e({η_{m,k}})

  Eq. (21) — total energy efficiency:
  E_e = B · S_e({η_{m,k}}) / P_total({η_{m,k}})    [bit / Joule]

  Equivalent "divide-by-B·S_e" form (used in Sec IV):
  E_e  ∝  B · S_e / [ P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ]
  (modulo an additive constant Σ_m P_{bt,m} that does not depend on η)

  5 · Numerical Worked Example

  Setup — mid-size CF-mMIMO using Table I parameters:
  M = 100,  N = 4,  K = 20
  ν_m = 0.4,  P_{tc,m} = 0.2 W,  P_{0,m} = 0.825 W
  P_{bt,m} = 0.25 W/(Gbit/s) = 2.5×10⁻¹⁰ W/(bit/s)
  B = 20 MHz,  sum SE S_e = 20 bit/s/Hz (= 1 bit/s/Hz per user)
  Per-AP max transmit output = 0.2 W at full load (≈ what Sec VI uses)
  Assume every AP at full budget:  Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} = 1/N = 0.25

  Step 1 — Per-AP PA draw:
  P_PA,m = (1/0.4) · 0.2 W = 0.5 W   (at full load)

  Step 2 — Per-AP circuit:
  N · P_{tc,m} = 4 · 0.2 = 0.8 W

  Step 3 — Per-AP hardware & total over M APs:
  P_m = 0.5 + 0.8 = 1.3 W
  Σ_m P_m = 100 · 1.3 = 130 W

  Step 4 — Fixed backhaul:
  Σ_m P_{0,m} = 100 · 0.825 = 82.5 W

  Step 5 — Sum throughput:
  B · S_e = 2×10⁷ · 20 = 4×10⁸ bit/s = 400 Mbit/s

  Step 6 — Traffic-dep. backhaul:
  Σ_m P_{bt,m} · B·S_e = 100 · 2.5×10⁻¹⁰ · 4×10⁸ = 10 W

  Step 7 — Total power and EE:
  P_total = 130 + 82.5 + 10 = 222.5 W
  E_e = 4×10⁸ / 222.5 ≈ 1.80 Mbit/J

  Power breakdown:

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────┬───────┬────────┐
  │               Component                │ Watts │ Share  │
  ├────────────────────────────────────────┼───────┼────────┤
  │ PA draw (50 W = 100 × 0.5)             │ 50.0  │ 22.5 % │
  ├────────────────────────────────────────┼───────┼────────┤
  │ Per-antenna circuit (80 W = 100 × 0.8) │ 80.0  │ 36.0 % │
  ├────────────────────────────────────────┼───────┼────────┤
  │ Fixed backhaul                         │ 82.5  │ 37.0 % │
  ├────────────────────────────────────────┼───────┼────────┤
  │ Traffic-dep. backhaul                  │ 10.0  │ 4.5 %  │
  ├────────────────────────────────────────┼───────┼────────┤
  │ Total                                  │ 222.5 │ 100 %  │
  └────────────────────────────────────────┴───────┴────────┘

  Observations:

  1. Circuit + fixed backhaul dominate. Only 22.5 % of power is the PA. The
  "cost of being switched on" (circuit + fixed BH) is 73 % of the total —
  classic small-cell regime.
  2. N trade-off. Going from N = 1 (so M = 400 to keep MN = 400) to N = 4 (so M
  = 100) leaves Σ_m N·P_{tc,m} = 80 W unchanged but slashes fixed backhaul from 
  330 W to 82.5 W (−247.5 W!) and traffic BH from 40 W to 10 W (−30 W). This is
  exactly the "fewer APs, more antennas per AP" trade-off the paper's Fig. 5
  visualizes.
  3. What if we ignored backhaul? P_total = 130 W ⇒ E_e ≈ 3.08 Mbit/J. An
  overestimate by 70 %. This is why an EE analysis without backhaul is
  misleading.

  Practice Problem

  Repeat the calculation for M = 200 APs, N = 2, K = 40, S_e = 40 bit/s/Hz,
  other parameters as before.

  (a) Σ_m P_m
  (b) Fixed backhaul Σ_m P_{0,m}
  (c) Traffic-dep. backhaul
  (d) P_total
  (e) E_e in Mbit/J
  (f) Backhaul fraction of P_total

  Expected answers:
  - (a) per-AP PA = 0.5 W, circuit = 2·0.2 = 0.4 W → per-AP = 0.9 W → 180 W
  - (b) 200 · 0.825 = 165 W
  - (c) 200 · 2.5×10⁻¹⁰ · (2×10⁷·40) = 200 · 0.2 = 40 W
  - (d) 180 + 165 + 40 = 385 W
  - (e) B·S_e = 8×10⁸ → E_e = 8×10⁸/385 ≈ 2.08 Mbit/J
  - (f) 205/385 ≈ 53 % — more than half of total power is backhaul!

  Note that doubling M (100→200) while halving N (4→2), keeping MN = 400,
  doubles fixed backhaul from 82.5 to 165 W. EE did not change much (1.80 vs
  2.08 Mbit/J) because B·S_e also doubled, but the backhaul share jumped from 37
   % to 53 %. That is the quantitative meaning of "backhaul kills EE at large M"
   the paper keeps emphasizing.

  6 · Summary of Section III

  - Component-level power model. Adopts the standard Björnson-2015 / Tombaz-2011
   / Zuo-2016 model and specializes it to CF-mMIMO. Total power = AP hardware +
  backhaul (Eq. 17).
  - AP hardware (Eq. 18): PA-draw + per-antenna circuit. PA-draw bounded by
  ρ_d·N₀/ν_m regardless of N thanks to the per-AP constraint; circuit scales
  linearly with N and is paid even when idle.
  - Backhaul (Eq. 19): fixed traffic-independent P_{0,m} + traffic-dependent
  B·S_e·P_{bt,m} linear in sum data rate. New term vs. colocated massive MIMO —
  the reason this paper exists.
  - All-in-one (Eq. 20): P_total = η-dependent PA + P̄_fix + η-dependent traffic 
  backhaul (∝ S_e).
  - Total EE (Eq. 21): E_e = B·S_e/P_total [bit/J] — the objective maximized in
  Sec IV.
  - Elephant in the room. Fixed backhaul Σ_m P_{0,m} scales linearly with M;
  traffic backhaul also scales with M (through Σ_m P_{bt,m}). At large M the
  backhaul dominates; naive "more APs = better" becomes counter-productive in
  bit/J terms. This motivates the AP selection schemes of Section V.
  - Divide-by-B·S_e trick. The critical analytic manoeuvre: dividing (21) by
  B·S_e removes the traffic-dep backhaul from the η-dependence and leaves a
  fractional objective whose denominator is affine in c_{m,k}² = η_{m,k}
  variables. This is what makes Section IV's SCA-SOCP possible.
  - Default Table I parameters:
  ν_m = 0.4,  P_{tc,m} = 0.2 W,  P_{0,m} = 0.825 W,
  P_{bt,m} = 0.25 W/(Gbit/s),  B = 20 MHz,  NF = 9 dB → N₀ ≈ 4×10⁻¹³ W
  τ_c = 200 samples

  ---
  1 · High-Level (Layman) Explanation

  We have E_e = B·S_e / P_total as the number we want to maximize, where both
  S_e and P_total depend on the power-control coefficients {η_{m,k}}. Section IV
   asks:

  ▎ "Which choice of {η_{m,k}} makes E_e as large as possible, while still 
  ▎ guaranteeing a minimum rate S_{o,k} for every user and respecting per-AP 
  ▎ power budgets?"

  Why is this hard?
  1. It is a ratio of two complicated functions of η — classical fractional 
  programming.
  2. The spectral-efficiency constraint SE_k ≥ S_{o,k} is itself a non-convex
  inequality (log of ratio of quadratics ≥ constant).
  3. The objective is non-convex.

  Standard solvers (Gurobi, MOSEK) cannot touch problems like this directly. The
   paper uses a three-step recipe from the convex-optimization toolbox:

  Step A — Transform the ratio. Use the "divide-by-B·S_e" trick from Section III
   to absorb the traffic-dependent backhaul into a constant. What's left has an
  affine denominator in the new variable c_{m,k} ≝ √η_{m,k}.

  Step B — Convex lower bounds for the remaining non-convexities. Two pieces
  remain:
  - a quadratic-in-c in the SE numerator — bounded below via a first-order 
  Taylor expansion of a quadratic-over-linear function,
  - a log₂(1 + SINR) — bounded below via the classical inequality ln(x) ≥ 1 − 
  1/x.

  Step C — Solve the resulting approximation as an SOCP (Second-Order Cone
  Program), which modern solvers handle in polynomial time. Iterate: each
  iteration produces a new, better point that is fed into the Taylor expansion
  of the next iteration.

  This loop over B–C is called Sequential Convex Approximation (SCA). The paper
  shows the sequence of objectives is monotonically non-decreasing,
  upper-bounded, and therefore converges — empirically in ~10 iterations.
  Section IV's deliverable is Algorithm 1.

  Intellectual bottom line:

  ▎ "Fractional, non-convex-looking EE maximization problems in wireless almost 
  ▎ always admit an SCA-SOCP reformulation. The cell-free case is no exception."

  2 · PhD-Level Detailed Explanation

  2.1 The original problem (P), Eq. 22

              E_e({η_{m,k}})
  (P):   max  ──────────────                                             (22)
         {η}

         s.t.  SE_k({η_{m,k}}) ≥ S_{o,k},   k = 1, …, K      [QoS]
               Σ_{k=1}^{K} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}  ≤  1/N,   m = 1, …, M   [per-AP]
               η_{m,k}  ≥  0,   ∀ m, k.

  - S_{o,k} = minimum spectral efficiency each user must receive (bit/s/Hz) —
  QoS floor.
  - MK decision variables (hundreds to tens of thousands in practice).

  Why is (P) non-convex?

  ┌─────────────┬───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Ingredient  │                       Non-convexity                       │
  ├─────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Objective   │ Ratio of two non-convex functions. Numerator is a sum of  │
  │ E_e         │ log-of-SINR terms (non-concave in η); denominator is      │
  │             │ non-trivial in η                                          │
  ├─────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ SE          │ log₂(1 + num(η)/den(η)) ≥ S_{o,k} τ_c/(τ_c − τ_p) ⇔ num ≥ │
  │ constraint  │  (2^{…}−1)·den — quadratic ≥ quadratic, non-convex in     │
  │             │ general                                                   │
  ├─────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Per-AP      │ Convex — linear inequality in η. This one is fine.        │
  │ power       │                                                           │
  └─────────────┴───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  2.2 First reformulation — absorb backhaul into the denominator (P1), Eq. 24

  Plug (20) into E_e:

                       B · S_e({η})
  E_e  =  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
          P̄_fix                                       ← constant
        + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ← linear in η
        + B · Σ_m P_{bt,m} · S_e({η})                 ← ∝ S_e

  Key observation: dividing numerator and denominator by B·S_e, the last term
  becomes Σ_m P_{bt,m} — a pure constant that doesn't depend on η. Since 1/E_e =
   A/(B·S_e) + const and B·S_e > 0, E_e is a strictly decreasing function of
  A/(B·S_e), so maximizing E_e is equivalent to maximizing (B·S_e)/A where

  A(η) = P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}   ← affine in η

  Equivalent problem:

                           B · S_e({η})
  (P1):  max  ────────────────────────────────────────────────            (24)
          η    P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
          s.t. SE_k ≥ S_{o,k},   Σ_k η_{m,k}γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,   η ≥ 0.

  This step is the only place the linear-in-S_e shape of backhaul power is used.
   If backhaul power were logarithmic in S_e, the trick fails.

  2.3 Substitution c_{m,k} = √η_{m,k} (Eq. 25–26)

  The SE formula (15) contains √η_{m,k} inside quadratic forms. For cleaner
  algebra:
  c_{m,k} ≝ √η_{m,k} ≥ 0,   c_k ≝ [c_{1,k},…,c_{M,k}]^T ∈ ℝ^M,   c ≝
  [c_1^T,…,c_K^T]^T ∈ ℝ^{MK}

  - SE numerator becomes |𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|² (purely quadratic in c).
  - Pilot contamination: |𝛄_{k,k'}^T c_{k'}|².
  - Per-AP power: Σ_{k=1}^{K} c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N — convex quadratic in c.
  - PA draw: Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k} — positive weighted sum of
  squares, convex in c.

  The SE constraint is still the awful "log of ratio of quadratics ≥ constant"
  creature — but we are one step closer.

  2.4 The SE constraint as a Second-Order Cone constraint (Eq. 27)

  Rewrite SE_k ≥ S_{o,k}:
  log₂(1 + num_k/den_k) ≥ S_{o,k} · τ_c/(τ_c − τ_p) ≝ Ŝ_{o,k}
  ⇔  num_k ≥ (2^{Ŝ_{o,k}} − 1) · den_k
  ⇔  ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|² ≥ (2^{Ŝ_{o,k}} − 1) · (ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T
  c_{k'}|²
                                                    + ρ_d N Σ_{k'} ‖𝐃_{k',k}
  c_{k'}‖² + 1)

  Critical observation: since c_{m,k} ≥ 0 and all entries of 𝛄_{k,k} are
  non-negative, 𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k ≥ 0 — a non-negative real scalar. Therefore
  |𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|² = (𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k)² and we can take square roots on both sides
   without losing information:

  𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k  ≥  √(2^{Ŝ_{o,k}} − 1) · ‖v_k(c)‖                        (27)
                                           ↑
                                   affine function of c

  This has the form linear_in_c ≥ constant · ‖affine_in_c‖ — precisely the
  definition of a second-order cone (Lorentz / ice-cream cone) constraint:
  Cone = { (t, v) ∈ ℝ × ℝ^n  :  t ≥ ‖v‖ }.
  SOC constraints are convex, and every modern solver (Gurobi, MOSEK, ECOS) has
  a dedicated SOCP backend. This is why the paper spends so much effort
  extracting SOC form — once it is SOC, it is essentially free.

  Subtlety: this trick uses c ≥ 0. If we had complex precoders, we lose
  non-negativity and the SOC trick fails — one would need SDP instead. Conjugate
   beamforming with real power-control coefficients is the sweet spot that makes
   the optimization tractable.

  2.5 Second reformulation — linear-fractional form (P̂1), Eq. 28

  The objective (25a)/(25b) is still a fraction. Convex solvers prefer linear
  objectives. Introduce slack variables:
  - t_k for each SE_k (one per user)
  - t_0 for the denominator

                   B · Σ_{k=1}^{K} t_k
                   ───────────────────
  (P̂1):  max                t_0                                          (28a)
        c,t,t_0

        s.t.  SE_k(c)  ≥  t_k,   k = 1, …, K                              (28b)
  ← non-convex
              P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k}  ≤  t_0  (28c)
   ← convex
              (25c), (25d), (27).                                         (28d)

  Charnes–Cooper intuition: for max (cᵀx + α)/(dᵀx + β) with dᵀx + β > 0 over a
  convex set, substituting y = x/(dᵀx+β), z = 1/(dᵀx+β) turns the problem into a
   linear program. The paper doesn't invoke Charnes–Cooper by name but the
  reasoning behind (P̂1) is identical. After the perspective transformation, the
  linear-fractional objective Σ t_k / t_0 becomes a linear objective over a
  slightly larger variable set.

  The remaining difficulty is (28b) — still a log-of-ratio-of-quadratics. The
  rest of Section IV attacks this with SCA.

  2.6 SCA approximation of (28b) via slack variables u_k (Eqs. 29–33)

  Decompose (28b) into two constraints using a new slack u_k:
  1 + num_k(c)/den_k(c)  ≥  u_k                                          (29a)
  log₂(u_k)              ≥  ε · t_k,   where ε ≝ (τ_c − τ_p)/τ_c         (29b)
  Both together are equivalent to (28b) at the optimum (u_k is free).

  Step 1 — Convex lower bound for (29a).

  Rewrite (29a) as num_k(c) + den_k(c) ≥ u_k · den_k(c), i.e.
                 num_k(c) + den_k(c)
  f(c, u_k) ≝  ──────────────────────  ≥  den_k(c)                       (30,
  31)
                         u_k

  f(c, u_k) is a quadratic-over-linear function — a classical convex function
  when the numerator is a PSD quadratic in c and u_k > 0 (see Boyd–Vandenberghe
  Ch. 3, or the Schur complement / perspective-function argument).

  Convex functions lie above any tangent hyperplane. Taylor-expanding f around
  the current iterate (cⁿ, uⁿ_k):
  f(c, u_k)  ≥  f(cⁿ, uⁿ_k)
              + ∇_c f(cⁿ, uⁿ_k)^T (c − cⁿ)
              + (∂f/∂u_k)(cⁿ, uⁿ_k) · (u_k − uⁿ_k)
            ≝  F(c, u_k; cⁿ, uⁿ_k)

  Replacing (30) with F(c, u_k; cⁿ, uⁿ_k) ≥ den_k(c) (Eq. 32) is a tighter
  constraint — any c feasible for (32) is feasible for (30) — but as SCA
  iterates, (cⁿ, uⁿ_k) is updated and the tangent improves.

  Explicit form of F (Eq. 33):
  F(c, u_k; cⁿ, uⁿ_k) = f(cⁿ, uⁿ_k)

    + (2 ρ_d / uⁿ_k) · Σ_{k'=1}^{K} (cⁿ_{k'})^T
                                   · [N² 𝛄_{k,k'} 𝛄_{k,k'}^T + N 𝐃_{k',k}²]
                                   · (c_{k'} − cⁿ_{k'})

    − (f(cⁿ, uⁿ_k) / uⁿ_k) · (u_k − uⁿ_k).

  The gradient uses ∇_x (x^T A x) = 2 A x for symmetric A. The k' = k term
  captures useful signal, k' ≠ k terms capture pilot contamination, and
  𝐃_{k',k}² captures non-coherent interference — all three ingredients of the 
  SINR structure appear inside one Taylor expansion.

  Right-hand side den_k(c) is a quadratic in c → the constraint (32) has the
  form (affine in c, u_k) ≥ (quadratic in c), which is SOC-representable
  (Boyd–Vandenberghe App. B).

  Step 2 — Convex lower bound for (29b).

  Now log₂(u_k) ≥ ε t_k. The function log₂(u_k) is concave → the constraint is
  already convex. In principle, we could leave it alone. But the authors want a
  pure SOCP (for speed and solver availability), and log constraints need
  "exponential cone" support — which was not yet efficient in 2017 solvers.

  They use the classical logarithm inequality:
  ln(x)  ≥  1 − 1/x,   ∀ x > 0   (equality at x = 1)   (‡)
  Substituting x = u_k/uⁿ_k and converting to log₂:
  log₂(u_k) ≥ log₂(uⁿ_k) + log₂(e) · (1 − uⁿ_k/u_k)                      (34)

  This bound is tight at u_k = uⁿ_k and has the same first derivative there —
  the standard "proper convex bound" condition for SCA (Scutari 2016, Beck
  2010).

  Replacing log₂(u_k) in (29b) yields:
  log₂(uⁿ_k) + log₂(e) · (1 − uⁿ_k/u_k)  ≥  ε · t_k                      (35)

  Rearranging: u_k · (log₂(uⁿ_k) + log₂(e) − ε t_k) ≥ log₂(e) · uⁿ_k — a
  hyperbolic x·y ≥ constant constraint, which is SOC-representable via the
  rotated-cone identity
  x · y ≥ z²   ⇔   (x + y)/2 ≥ ‖((x − y)/2, z)‖.

  2.7 The full SOCP at iteration n+1 (Eqs. 36–38)

  Substituting (32) and (35) into (28b):

                       B · Σ_{k=1}^{K} t_k
                       ───────────────────
  (P̂1, n+1):   max            t_0                                       (36a)
             c,t,u

              s.t.  (25c), (25d), (27), (28c), (32), (35)                (36b)

  Apply a perspective transformation (treating 1/t_0 as a new variable) to
  convert the linear-fractional objective into a linear one over a slightly
  larger variable set. The result is an honest SOCP in Eq. (37) — listed in the
  paper with constraints (37b)–(37g).

  Global-optimization benchmark. The authors note that (P̂1) could alternatively
  be solved to global optimality by a monotonic optimization method called
  Branch-and-Reduce-and-Bound (BRB) (ref [16] Tuy–Al-Khayyal–Thach 2005). They
  use BRB only for tiny problems (M = 8, K = 2) as a sanity check — BRB has
  exponential complexity and is hopeless for M = 100–200. SCA is polynomial per 
  iteration and empirically matches BRB's optimum in ~10 iterations.

  2.8 Algorithm 1 — the SCA loop

  Algorithm 1 (SCA for (28))

  1. Initialization:
         n ← 1
         choose feasible initial point (c¹, u¹) by solving a
           FEASIBILITY SOCP (find any c with SE_k ≥ S_{o,k}
           and Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N)
         set tolerance ε_tol, max iterations N_I.

  2. Iteration n:
         solve the SOCP (37) with tangent point (cⁿ, uⁿ)
         let (c⁺, t⁺, u⁺) be its solution.

  3. Convergence check:
         if  |Σ_k t⁺_k − Σ_k tⁿ_k|  <  ε_tol   or   n = N_I:
             STOP
         else go to Step 4.

  4. Update:
         (cⁿ⁺¹, uⁿ⁺¹) ← (c⁺, u⁺),   n ← n + 1,   go to Step 2.

  Paper defaults: ε_tol = 0.01, N_I = 10. Empirically converges in ≤ 10
  iterations per large-scale-fading realization.

  Remark on initialization. For a given {S_{o,k}}, the feasible set of (P1) — SE
   constraints + per-AP power + non-negativity — is convex (intersection of SOCs
   with ℝ₊). Finding an initial feasible point is itself an SOCP feasibility
  problem. If infeasible, set E_e = 0 and move on.

  2.9 Convergence analysis

  Standard SCA argument (Beck-2010, Scutari-2016):

  1. Monotone improvement. The convex bounds F(·) and (34) are tight at the
  tangent point (equal values + equal first derivatives). So (cⁿ, uⁿ) is
  feasible for the (n+1)-th approximation, and by optimization, the new iterate
  must be at least as good. Objective values are non-decreasing.
  2. Upper bound. E_e is bounded above because P_total ≥ P̄_fix > 0 and S_e is
  bounded by a constant for any finite feasible η.
  3. Convergence. A monotonically non-decreasing, bounded-above sequence
  converges. Algorithm 1 therefore converges.

  Note: SCA converges to a KKT point, not necessarily the global optimum.
  Empirically on small CF-mMIMO, SCA matches the BRB global optimum (Fig. 1 of
  the paper for M = 8, K = 2).

  2.10 Computational complexity

  Per iteration SOCP with:
  - Variables: c ∈ ℝ^{MK}_+, t ∈ ℝ^K, u ∈ ℝ^K, t_0 ∈ ℝ → dimension MK + 2K + 1.
  - Constraints: K SOC from (27) + K SOC from (32) + K from (35) + M quadratic
  per-AP + 1 linear (28c) → O(M + K) cones.

  Interior-point complexity is O((n + m)^{3.5}) — polynomial. For M = 100, K = 
  40 (typical CF-mMIMO), the solver handles it in seconds on a laptop.

       where P̄_fix = Σ_m (N P_{tc,m} + P_{0,m})

  Substitution: c_{m,k} ≝ √η_{m,k}, c_k ∈ ℝ^M, c ∈ ℝ^{MK}_+.

  Per-AP power (convex quadratic): Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,  ∀ m.

  SE in c:
  SE_k(c) = (1 − τ_p/τ_c)·log₂(1 +
            ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k|²
          / (ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k}|𝛄_{k,k'}^T c_{k'}|² + ρ_d N Σ_{k'}‖𝐃_{k',k}c_{k'}‖²
   + 1))

  SE as SOC (Eq. 27):
  𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k  ≥  √(2^{Ŝ_{o,k}} − 1) · ‖v_k(c)‖,   Ŝ_{o,k} = S_{o,k} τ_c/(τ_c
  − τ_p)

  Linear-fractional reformulation (P̂1):
  max  B · Σ_k t_k / t_0
  s.t. SE_k(c) ≥ t_k        (non-convex; to be SCA-approximated)
       P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k c_{m,k}² γ_{m,k}  ≤  t_0
       per-AP power, SE-SOC (27), c ≥ 0.

  Slack decomposition of SE ≥ t_k:
  1 + num_k(c)/den_k(c) ≥ u_k                                     (29a)
  log₂(u_k)             ≥ ε · t_k,   ε = (τ_c − τ_p)/τ_c          (29b)

  SCA bound for (29a) — Taylor of the convex quadratic-over-linear:
  F(c, u_k; cⁿ, uⁿ_k) = f(cⁿ, uⁿ_k)
    + (2 ρ_d / uⁿ_k) · Σ_{k'=1}^{K} (cⁿ_{k'})^T · [N² 𝛄_{k,k'} 𝛄_{k,k'}^T + N
  𝐃_{k',k}²] · (c_{k'} − cⁿ_{k'})
    − (f(cⁿ, uⁿ_k) / uⁿ_k) · (u_k − uⁿ_k)                        (33)

  F(c, u_k; cⁿ, uⁿ_k) ≥ den_k(c)                                  (32)

  SCA bound for (29b) — logarithm inequality:
  log₂(uⁿ_k) + log₂(e)·(1 − uⁿ_k/u_k) ≥ ε · t_k                   (35)
  (SOC-representable via rotated cone.)

  Iteration SOCP (P̂1,n+1): max B·Σt_k/t_0 subject to (25c), (25d), (27), (28c),
  (32), (35).

  Convergence: non-decreasing + upper-bounded → converges (typically ≤ 10
  iterations).

  5 · Numerical Mini-Example of One SCA Iteration

  Setup (toy — same as Sec II worked example):
  M = 2, K = 2, N = 1, τ_p = τ_c = 10, ρ_p = ρ_d = 1, orthogonal pilots.

  β matrix:
  β_{1,1} = 0.1,  β_{1,2} = 0.01
  β_{2,1} = 0.01, β_{2,2} = 0.1

  γ from Eq. 5:
  γ_{1,1} = γ_{2,2} = 10·(0.1)²/(10·0.1 + 1) = 0.1/2 = 0.05
  γ_{1,2} = γ_{2,1} = 10·(0.01)²/(10·0.01 + 1) = 0.001/1.1 ≈ 0.000909

  QoS target (relaxed for feasibility): S_{o,1} = S_{o,2} = 0.03 bit/s/Hz, so
  2^{0.03} − 1 ≈ 0.0210.

  Step 0 — feasible initial c⁰. Equal-power at each AP:
  c_{m,k}² = 1/(2·(γ_{m,1} + γ_{m,2})) ≈ 1/(2·0.0509) ≈ 9.82
  c⁰ ≈ [3.133, 3.133, 3.133, 3.133]^T
  Check (25d): 9.82·0.05 + 9.82·0.000909 ≈ 0.500 ≤ 1 ✓.

  Step 1 — compute num_1, den_1, u_1⁰:
  𝛄_{1,1} = [0.05, 0.000909]^T,   𝛄_{1,2} = 𝟎 (orthogonal pilots)
  𝐃_{1,1} = diag(√0.005, √0.00000909) ≈ diag(0.0707, 0.00302)
  𝐃_{2,1} = diag(√0.000909, √0.0005)  ≈ diag(0.0302, 0.02236)

  num_1(c⁰) = |0.05·3.133 + 0.000909·3.133|² = |0.1595|² ≈ 0.02545

  den_1(c⁰) = 0                                      (pilot-contamination)
            + ((0.0707·3.133)² + (0.00302·3.133)²)   (𝐃_{1,1} term)
            + ((0.0302·3.133)² + (0.02236·3.133)²)   (𝐃_{2,1} term)
            + 1                                      (noise)
            ≈ 0.04919 + 0.01385 + 1
            ≈ 1.063

  SINR_1(c⁰) ≈ 0.02545/1.063 ≈ 0.02394
  SE_1(c⁰)   ≈ log₂(1.02394) ≈ 0.0342 bit/s/Hz  ✓ (meets target 0.03)

  u_1⁰ = 1 + num_1/den_1 ≈ 1.02394

  Step 2 — compute f(c⁰, u_1⁰) and its Taylor expansion via (33). Mechanical;
  the SOCP solver handles it automatically once you hand it the closed form.

  Step 3 — build SOCP (37) with Taylor-coefficient matrices, pass to MOSEK, read
   off new iterate (c¹, u¹).

  Step 4 — check |Σ_k t_k| < ε_tol; if not, go to Step 2 with n ← 1.

  After roughly 3–5 iterations (for a toy problem with MK = 4 variables), the
  iterate converges to a KKT point and the corresponding E_e is the answer.

  Practice Problem

  (a) Prove f(c, u) = x^T A x / u with A ⪰ 0 and u > 0 is jointly convex in (x, 
  u). Hint: use Schur complement or the perspective-function theorem
  (Boyd–Vandenberghe Ch. 3).

  (b) Derive (34) from ln(x) ≥ 1 − 1/x by substituting x = u/uⁿ and noting
  log₂(u) = log₂(uⁿ) + log₂(e)·ln(u/uⁿ).

  (c) For the toy example with S_{o,k} = 0.03 bit/s/Hz, compute 2^{Ŝ_{o,k}} − 1 
  ≈ 0.0210. Write out (27) for user 1 with c_1 = [c_{1,1}, c_{2,1}]^T as the
  only variable (fix c_2 arbitrarily). Verify it is linear-LHS ≥ 
  constant·‖affine‖ — i.e., SOC.

  (d) Open https://github.com/tranlenam/cellfreeMIMOenergyefficiency and map
  MATLAB files to Eqs. (25)–(37): (i) feasibility initialization, (ii) SCA loop,

  6 · Summary of Section IV

  - Problem (P). Max E_e = B·S_e/P_total over η_{m,k} with per-user QoS SE_k ≥ 
  S_{o,k} and per-AP power Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N. Non-convex on two counts:
  fractional objective + log-of-ratio-of-quadratics constraint.
  - Reformulation (P1). Divide num/den by B·S_e. The traffic-dependent backhaul
  (being linear in S_e) becomes a constant that does not affect the argmax. New
  denominator is affine in c_{m,k}².
  - Substitution c = √η. Puts per-AP power into convex-quadratic form and makes
  SE numerator a perfect square — enables the SOC trick for SE constraint.
  - SOC representation of SE constraint (27). Because c ≥ 0 and all coefficients
   are non-negative real, 𝛄_{k,k}^T c_k ≥ 0 is a real non-negative scalar, so
  the SINR constraint has the form linear ≥ norm-of-affine. All the hard work in
   Sec IV boils down to extracting this form.
  - Linear-fractional reformulation (P̂1). Slacks t_k for SE and t_0 for
  denominator. Objective is Σ t_k / t_0 — linear-fractional, handled by
  Charnes–Cooper/perspective.
  - SCA of the non-convex SE constraint (28b) in two steps:
    - (a) decouple via slack u_k into (29a)+(29b);
    - (b) convex-bound (29a) by Taylor-expanding the quadratic-over-linear f(c, 
  u_k) around the current iterate → (32)–(33), SOC-representable;
    - (c) convex-bound log₂(u_k) below using ln(x) ≥ 1 − 1/x → (34)–(35), also
  SOC-representable.
  - Algorithm 1. Iteratively solve SOCP (37) with current tangent, update (cⁿ, 
  uⁿ), stop on |Σ t_k| convergence or N_I = 10 iterations.
  - Convergence. Non-decreasing (tight bounds ⇒ previous iterate feasible for
  next SOCP) + upper-bounded (finite E_e for finite power) ⇒ converges.
  Empirically matches BRB global optimum on small CF-mMIMO.
  - Computational complexity. Per-iter SOCP with MK + 2K + 1 variables and O(M +
   K) cones → polynomial interior-point. Scales to M ≈ 100–200, K ≈ 20–40.
  - Intellectual takeaway. The recipe — (i) absorb fractional objective into
  non-fractional form, (ii) substitute to get convex power constraints, (iii)
  decouple log-of-ratio via slacks, (iv) Taylor + log-inequality bounds — is a
  blueprint for optimizing any EE-like objective in wireless. Every post-2018
  CF-mMIMO EE paper uses a variant of this exact recipe.

  1 · High-Level (Layman) Explanation

  Section IV gave us an algorithm that squeezes maximum EE out of a fixed 
  topology where every AP serves every user. But that full-mesh assumption is
  exactly what Section III called the elephant in the room: it inflates backhaul
   power linearly with M, because every AP's backhaul must carry every user's
  data whether that AP is close enough to contribute or not.

  Section V attacks the topology. Two observations drive the whole section:

  1. Physical observation. For any given user k, there are many APs located very
   far away. Their large-scale fading β_{m,k} is essentially zero, so they
  contribute almost nothing to user k's desired signal. Forcing them to
  participate (allocate nonzero η, send data over backhaul) wastes power for no
  benefit.
  2. Backhaul observation. If AP m only serves a subset U_m of users, then its
  backhaul only needs to carry the data of those users. Since the
  traffic-dependent backhaul is proportional to the sum rate actually served,
  shrinking U_m shrinks the backhaul cost proportionally.

  Combining: for each user k, let a small group A_k ⊂ {1,…,M} of nearby APs
  serve it. Dually, U_m = {k : m ∈ A_k} is the set of users AP m actually
  serves. How small can A_k be without hurting performance? Numerically, only
  ~10–20 % of APs per user deliver 95 % of the received signal power.

  The paper proposes two grouping heuristics:

  - (A) Received-power-based (Algorithm 2): Rank APs by how much useful power
  they contribute to user k given the current optimal power-control solution,
  keep the top-few delivering ≥ ρ% (paper default ρ = 95). Requires running
  Algorithm 1 once first to get baseline η's — accurate but more expensive.
  - (B) Largest-large-scale-fading-based (Algorithm 3): Rank APs simply by
  β_{m,k} (the long-term-stationary large-scale fading) and keep the top-few
  whose β's sum to ≥ ρ% of the total. Does not require running Algorithm 1 first
   — much cheaper, almost as good.

  After A_k is chosen, the optimization is a modified version of (P) with
  η_{m,k} forced to zero for k ∉ U_m. The paper shows this can be solved by the 
  same Algorithm 1 from Section IV by simply zeroing out "inactive" coefficients
   via a modified γ.

  Practical headline:

  ▎ "For each user, you only need to listen to the closest ~15% of APs. The rest
  ▎  can be shut off, which slashes backhaul power and nearly doubles EE without
  ▎  appreciably hurting SE."

  This is the precursor to modern user-centric CF-mMIMO (Buzzi–D'Andrea 2017,
  Björnson–Sanguinetti 2020 scalable CF). Ngo-2018 is one of the very first
  papers to formalize the idea.

  2 · PhD-Level Detailed Explanation

  2.1 The motivation — re-reading the EE denominator (Eq. 39)

  Section V opens by re-writing E_e after the "divide by B·S_e" trick:

                                    1
  E_e = ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────         (39)
        [P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}] / (B·S_e)
      + Σ_m P_{bt,m}

  Look at the second term of the denominator of 1/E_e: Σ_m P_{bt,m}. This is the
   traffic-dependent backhaul power per unit throughput. It is a sum over all M 
  APs, whether the AP contributes useful signal or not.

  Critical observation: this elephant term scales linearly with M. Doubling M
  doubles Σ_m P_{bt,m}. For small M and low power, it's dwarfed by PA draw —
  nobody cares. But for large M, Σ_m P_{bt,m} becomes the dominant denominator 
  term, and Algorithm 1 is powerless to reduce it — because Algorithm 1 only
  tunes {η_{m,k}} and this term does not depend on η. Setting all η's to zero
  would still leave B·Σ_m P_{bt,m}·S_e unless you stop serving users entirely
  (which defeats the purpose).

  Conclusion: to reduce Σ_m P_{bt,m}, either physically remove APs, or more
  cleverly reduce the set of users each AP serves so that backhaul cost is
  proportional to actual active service relationships, not to the hypothetical
  M·K full mesh.

  2.2 The modified per-AP backhaul (Eq. 40)

  Let U_m ⊆ {1,…,K} be the set of users actually served by AP m. The backhaul
  between AP m and CPU only needs to carry the data of U_m users:

  P_{bh,m} = P_{0,m} + B · P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k ∈ U_m} SE_k                  (40)

  Compared to the original (19):
  - Fixed P_{0,m} does not shrink — backhaul transceiver is still powered on.
  - Traffic-dep part shrinks whenever U_m ⊊ {1,…,K}.
  - Reduces to (19) if U_m = {1,…,K} ∀m (full cell-free, no selection).

  2.3 The two guiding observations

  From the start of Section V (paper's bullets):

  (O1) Power argument: "If the mth AP serves only some users, then it needs to 
  send only the data corresponding to these users. As a result, the backhaul 
  power consumption depends only on the spectral efficiencies of these users."

  (O2) Signal-processing argument: "For a given user, there are many APs which 
  are located very far away. These APs will not add significantly to the overall
   spatial diversity gains. This implies that not all APs really participate in 
  serving this user."

  Together ⇒ each user k should be served by a small set A_k ⊂ {1,…,M} of nearby
   APs, and the dual set U_m = {k : m ∈ A_k} defines which users AP m serves.

  2.4 Scheme A — Received-Power-Based Selection (V-A)

  This scheme uses the optimal {η^*_{m,k}} from Algorithm 1 (Sec IV) to measure
  how much desired-signal power each AP actually delivers to each user.

  The expected total desired-signal power of user k can be written (see Eq. 48
  in the appendix) in the compact form:

  DS_k = ρ_d · N · Σ_{m=1}^{M} γ_{m,k} · η_{m,k}                        (41)

  (This is a high-level summary using η not √η — rigor lives in Eq. 15.)

  The fraction of desired-signal power contributed by AP m to user k:

                  γ_{m,k} · η_{m,k}
  p(m,k)  =  ───────────────────────────                                (42)
             Σ_{m'=1}^{M} γ_{m',k} · η_{m',k}

  This is a probability-like quantity: p(m,k) ≥ 0 and Σ_m p(m,k) = 1.

  Selection rule: A_k = smallest set whose cumulative p contribution reaches ρ%.

  1. Sort p(·, k) descending:  p(π_k(1), k) ≥ p(π_k(2), k) ≥ … ≥ p(π_k(M), k)
  2. Find smallest M_k such that   Σ_{i=1}^{M_k} p(π_k(i), k)  ≥  ρ/100
  3. Set  A_k = {π_k(1), π_k(2), …, π_k(M_k)}.

  With ρ = 95 (default), you keep the top-M_k APs that jointly deliver 95 % of
  the desired-signal power.

  After A_1,…,A_K are chosen, derive U_m = {k : m ∈ A_k}.

  2.5 The modified optimization problem (P2), Eq. 43

                            B · S_e({η})
  (P2):  max  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────     (43)
          η   P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
            + B · Σ_m P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k ∈ U_m} SE_k

         s.t.  SE_k ≥ S_{o,k}
               Σ_{k ∈ U_m} η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,   m = 1, …, M
               η_{m,k} ≥ 0  for k ∈ U_m,   η_{m,k} = 0  for k ∉ U_m.

  Changes from (P):
  - Per-AP power sums only over k ∈ U_m.
  - Equality η_{m,k} = 0 for k ∉ U_m is explicit.
  - Backhaul term in objective uses (40) instead of (19).

  Per-AP power budget is unchanged at 1/N — so an AP serving fewer users can
  concentrate all its transmit power on them, which helps SE.

  2.6 Clever trick — absorb zero-forcing into a modified γ̂ (Eq. 44)

  Equality constraints η_{m,k} = 0 for k ∉ U_m are ugly for an SOCP. Define:

            ⎧  γ_{m,k}   if k ∈ U_m
  γ̂_{m,k} = ⎨
            ⎩  0          if k ∉ U_m

  Since γ̂_{m,k} = 0 multiplies η_{m,k} everywhere it appears, the coefficients
  for k ∉ U_m are effectively projected out. Problem (43) becomes (44):

                             B · S_e
  max    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    η    P̄_fix + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ̂_{m,k}
       + B · Σ_m P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k ∈ U_m} SE_k                           (44)

  s.t.   SE_k ≥ S_{o,k}
         Σ_k η_{m,k} γ̂_{m,k}  ≤  1/N,  m = 1, …, M
         η_{m,k} ≥ 0.

  Only the backhaul term still involves the discrete U_m sets.

  2.7 The upper-bound approximation

  The backhaul term in (44) has Σ_{k ∈ U_m} SE_k, which makes the problem
  combinatorial. The paper side-steps this with a simple bound:

  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k ∈ U_m} SE_k  ≤  Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k=1}^{K}
   SE_k
                                            = Σ_{m=1}^{M} P_{bt,m} · S_e

  With this bound, the backhaul term becomes identical in form to the original
  (P)/(P1). The modified problem reduces to exactly the form solvable by 
  Algorithm 1, just with γ̂ substituted for γ. No new solver needed.

  Why is the bound OK?

  1. Direction of error. The bound is tight when U_m = {1,…,K} (no selection)
  and gets looser as more APs are pruned. But the original exact objective is
  larger than the bound suggests (because the approximation overestimates the
  backhaul penalty). So the approximate problem is pessimistic about EE → the
  true EE gain from selection is underestimated. That is the safe direction.
  2. Empirical verification. Section VI shows the approximate EE is very close
  to the exact EE (verifiable via BRB on small problems).

  2.8 Algorithm 2 — Received-Power-Based Selection

  Algorithm 2 (Received-power-based selection)

  1. Choose ρ% (default 95).
     Run Algorithm 1 on the FULL problem (P) → baseline {η^*_{m,k}}.
     Compute p(m, k) from (42).

  2. For each user k = 1, …, K:
       • Sort p(m, k) descending.
       • Initialize A_k ← {top AP}.
       • Walk down the sorted list, adding each next AP, until
            Σ_{m ∈ A_k} p(m, k)  ≥  ρ/100.

  3. Derive U_m = {k : m ∈ A_k}.
     Define γ̂_{m,k} = γ_{m,k} · 1_{k ∈ U_m}.

  4. Run Algorithm 1 AGAIN with γ̂ in place of γ → new {η^{**}_{m,k}}.

  Notes:
  - Requires two runs of Algorithm 1 — roughly double the compute.
  - {A_k}, {U_m} recomputed only when {β_{m,k}} changes (slowly).
  - ρ = 95 typically selects 10–20 % of M APs per user.

  2.9 Algorithm 3 — Largest-LSF-Based Selection (V-B)

  Avoids the first Algorithm-1 run by ranking APs directly on β_{m,k}.
  Intuition: if β_{m,k} is large, AP m is physically close to user k and will
  contribute strongly regardless of η. If β_{m,k} is tiny, no amount of power
  control can rescue it.

  Let β̃_{1,k} ≥ β̃_{2,k} ≥ … ≥ β̃_{M,k} be the sorted β values for user k. Pick
  the smallest M_{0,k} such that

  Σ_{m=1}^{M_{0,k}} β̃_{m,k}  /  Σ_{m=1}^{M} β_{m,k}  ≥  ρ/100           (45)

  i.e., the cumulative fraction of the largest M_{0,k} β's reaches at least ρ%.
  The rest of the algorithm is identical to Algorithm 2 from Step 3.

  Algorithm 3 (Largest-LSF-Based Selection)

  1. Use (45) to pick A_k for each user. Derive U_m.
  2. γ̂_{m,k} = γ_{m,k} · 1_{k ∈ U_m}.
  3. Run Algorithm 1 ONCE with γ̂.

  Advantages over Algorithm 2:
  - Only one run of Algorithm 1 (≈ half the compute).
  - Uses long-term-stationary β info already needed for γ_{m,k}.
  - Selection rule has a clear physical meaning: "choose the closest APs."

  Disadvantage: A_k is chosen without regard for power control or interference,
  so slightly sub-optimal. Empirically, the gap between Algorithms 2 and 3 is 
  small (Sec VI Fig. 3).

  │ Per-iter SOCP  │ MK → ~0.15 MK (same after │ ~0.15 MK                  │
  │ dim            │  sel.)                    │                           │
  ├────────────────┼───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────┤
  │ When re-run    │ On large-scale fading     │ On large-scale fading     │
  │                │ change (~s)               │ change (~s)               │

  - AP selection also speeds up the solver by a large factor (per-iteration SOCP
   cost is polynomial in dimension).
  - Frequency of re-running: only when β changes, so typically seconds-scale in

  4 · Mathematical Recap (Unicode)

  Notation:
  A_k  ⊆ {1,…,M}      set of APs serving user k
  U_m  ⊆ {1,…,K}      set of users served by AP m   (m ∈ A_k  ⇔  k ∈ U_m)
  ρ   ∈ (0, 100]       threshold percent (default 95)
  γ̂_{m,k} = γ_{m,k} · 1_{k ∈ U_m}    modified γ

  Modified per-AP backhaul (Eq. 40):
  P_{bh,m} = P_{0,m} + B · P_{bt,m} · Σ_{k ∈ U_m} SE_k

  Received-power fraction (Eq. 42):
                  γ_{m,k} · η_{m,k}
  p(m, k) = ───────────────────────────
            Σ_{m'=1}^{M} γ_{m',k} · η_{m',k}

  Received-power-based criterion:
  find smallest M_k such that
      Σ_{i=1}^{M_k} p(π_k(i), k)  ≥  ρ/100
  (π_k sorts p(·, k) descending)
  A_k = {π_k(1), …, π_k(M_k)}.

  Largest-LSF criterion (Eq. 45):
  find smallest M_{0,k} such that
      Σ_{i=1}^{M_{0,k}} β̃_{i,k}  /  Σ_{m=1}^{M} β_{m,k}  ≥  ρ/100

  Modified (P2) after γ̂ substitution (Eq. 44):
  max    B · S_e({η; γ̂})  /  [ P̄_fix
                                + ρ_d N₀ N · Σ_m (1/ν_m) Σ_k η_{m,k} γ̂_{m,k}
                                + B · Σ_m P_{bt,m} · S_e ]

  s.t.   SE_k({η; γ̂}) ≥ S_{o,k}
         Σ_k η_{m,k} γ̂_{m,k} ≤ 1/N,  m = 1,…,M
         η_{m,k} ≥ 0.

  After the backhaul upper-bound approximation Σ_m P_{bt,m} Σ_{k ∈ U_m} SE_k ≤ 
  Σ_m P_{bt,m} · S_e, this has the exact form of (P1) and Algorithm 1 solves it
  directly with γ̂ in place of γ.

  5 · Numerical Worked Example

  Walk-through of Algorithm 3 on a toy: M = 10 APs, K = 3 users, ρ = 95 %.

  β matrix (three "clusters" of strong APs per user):

  ┌─────┬─────────┬─────────┬─────────┐
  │  m  │ β_{m,1} │ β_{m,2} │ β_{m,3} │
  ├─────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ 1   │ 0.50    │ 0.003   │ 0.002   │
  ├─────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ 2   │ 0.30    │ 0.005   │ 0.001   │
  ├─────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ 3   │ 0.20    │ 0.010   │ 0.005   │
  ├─────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ 4   │ 0.050   │ 0.40    │ 0.003   │
  ├─────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ 5   │ 0.020   │ 0.30    │ 0.006   │
  ├─────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ 6   │ 0.010   │ 0.20    │ 0.010   │
  ├─────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ 7   │ 0.002   │ 0.05    │ 0.40    │
  ├─────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ 8   │ 0.003   │ 0.02    │ 0.30    │
  ├─────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ 9   │ 0.005   │ 0.01    │ 0.20    │
  ├─────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┤
  │ 10  │ 0.001   │ 0.003   │ 0.10    │
  └─────┴─────────┴─────────┴─────────┘

  Step 1 — Σ_m β_{m,k}:
  Σ_m β_{m,1} = 1.091
  Σ_m β_{m,2} = 1.001
  Σ_m β_{m,3} = 1.027

  Step 2 — Choose A_k with ρ = 95 %:

  User 1: sorted descending 0.50, 0.30, 0.20, 0.05, 0.02, …. Target = 0.95 × 
  1.091 = 1.036. Cumulative hits 1.05 at i = 4. → A_1 = {1, 2, 3, 4} (four APs).

  User 2: sorted descending 0.40, 0.30, 0.20, 0.05, 0.02, 0.01, …. Target = 0.95
   × 1.001 = 0.951. i = 4 gives 0.95 (marginally below), i = 5 gives 0.97
  (passes). → A_2 = {3, 4, 5, 6, 7} (five APs; these correspond to the five
  largest β_{m,2} values).

  User 3: sorted descending 0.40, 0.30, 0.20, 0.10, 0.010, 0.006, …. Target =
  0.95 × 1.027 = 0.976. i = 4 gives 1.00 (passes). → A_3 = {7, 8, 9, 10} (four
  APs).

  Step 3 — Build U_m:

  m=1:  U_1  = {1}        m=6:  U_6  = {2}
  m=2:  U_2  = {1}        m=7:  U_7  = {2, 3}
  m=3:  U_3  = {1, 2}     m=8:  U_8  = {3}
  m=4:  U_4  = {1, 2}     m=9:  U_9  = {3}
  m=5:  U_5  = {2}        m=10: U_10 = {3}

  Step 4 — Active association count:
  - Without selection: M × K = 10 × 3 = 30 pairs.
  - With selection: |A_1| + |A_2| + |A_3| = 4 + 5 + 4 = 13.
  - Reduction: 57 %.

  (For larger systems with M = 100 APs and ρ = 95 %, the paper reports that
  typically only 10–20 % of APs per user are active → 80–90 % reduction in
  active associations.)

  Step 5 — Traffic backhaul savings:

  Assume B = 20 MHz, SE_k = 1 bit/s/Hz per user (S_e = 3 bit/s/Hz), P_{bt,m} = 
  0.25 W/(Gbit/s), so Σ_m P_{bt,m} = 2.5 W/(Gbit/s).

  - Without selection: B · S_e · Σ_m P_{bt,m} = 0.06 Gbit/s · 2.5 W/(Gbit/s) = 
  0.15 W.
  - With selection (upper bound used in (44)): still 0.15 W (bound replaces U_m
  by full set).
  - Actual AP-by-AP traffic backhaul:
    - m=1,2: serve {1} → 0.25 × 0.02 × 1 = 0.005 W each
    - m=3,4: serve {1,2} → 0.25 × 0.02 × 2 = 0.010 W each
    - m=5,6: serve {2} → 0.005 W each
    - m=7: serves {2,3} → 0.010 W
    - m=8,9,10: serve {3} → 0.005 W each
    - Total: 0.065 W → 57 % reduction from actual BH.

  So the upper-bound approximation is conservative (underestimates the EE gain
  from selection), which is the safe direction.

  Practice Problem

  Use the same β matrix but with ρ = 80 % instead of 95 %.

  (a) Recompute |A_k| for each user.
  (b) Total number of active (m, k) pairs.
  (c) Actual traffic-dep backhaul, with the same assumptions SE_k = 1 bit/s/Hz, 
  B = 20 MHz, P_{bt,m} = 0.25 W/(Gbit/s).
  (d) Fraction of the no-selection backhaul that remains.

  Expected answers:
  - (a) |A_1| = |A_2| = |A_3| = 3 (the three strongest per user).
  - (b) 9 active pairs.
  - (c) Each active AP serves exactly one user → 9 × 0.005 = 0.045 W.
  - (d) 0.045 / 0.15 = 30 % — a 70 % BH saving, at the cost of some signal loss
  since ρ = 80 % < 95 %.

  6 · Summary of Section V

  - Motivation. Algorithm 1 cannot reduce Σ_m P_{bt,m} backhaul (it only tunes
  η). For large M, backhaul dominates and the only way to reduce it is to reduce
   the number of active (m, k) associations.
  - Guiding observations: (O1) if AP m serves only U_m, backhaul only carries
  those users; (O2) far-away APs contribute negligible signal. → each user needs
   only a small serving set.
  - Algorithm 2 (Received-Power-Based): (1) run Algorithm 1 once for baseline η;
   (2) compute p(m,k) from (42); (3) pick top-M_k APs cumulatively ≥ ρ%; (4)
  form U_m and re-run Algorithm 1 with γ̂. More accurate; two solver runs.
  - Algorithm 3 (Largest-LSF-Based): rank APs by β_{m,k}, pick top-M_{0,k}
  cumulatively ≥ ρ% of Σ_m β_{m,k}; single solver run. Uses slowly-varying info.
   Slightly sub-optimal but nearly as good.
  - Backhaul upper-bound approximation: Σ_m P_{bt,m} Σ_{k∈U_m} SE_k ≤ Σ_m 
  P_{bt,m} · S_e reduces (44) back to the original form so Algorithm 1 solves it
   with γ̂ in place of γ. Pessimistic about EE (true gains are even better).
  - Practical impact:
    - ρ = 95 % ⇒ 10–20 % of M APs per user → 80–90 % backhaul reduction.
    - Per-SOCP dimensionality shrinks by ~85 %, speeding up the solver.
    - Re-run only when β changes (~second-scale).
  - Historical significance. Together with Buzzi–D'Andrea 2017, Section V
  pioneered user-centric CF-mMIMO, which is now the dominant CF-mMIMO deployment
   philosophy. Every scalable CF-mMIMO paper since (incl. Björnson–Sanguinetti
  2020) is a descendant.
  - One-line takeaway:
  ▎ "Each user should be served by the ~15 % of nearest APs. Everybody wins — 
  lower backhaul power, smaller optimization, same spectral efficiency — and 
  cell-free massive MIMO finally becomes scalable."

  2 · PhD-Level Detailed Explanation

  Part A — Parameters and Setup

  2.1 Geometry: wrap-around square

  APs and users are placed uniformly at random inside a D × D km² area. The
  square is wrapped around at the edges (a 3×3 torus) so that every AP and every
   user is statistically equivalent. Without wrap-around, corner APs would have
  half as many neighbours as central ones and bias the averages. The trick was
  pioneered by Marzetta's massive-MIMO textbook [7]. Two sizes used:
  - D = 1 km — dense deployment (stadium, downtown)
  - D = 2 km — sparser deployment (residential)

  2.2 Random pilot assignment

  Each user independently picks one of τ_p orthogonal length-τ_p pilots
  uniformly at random. With K > τ_p, collisions occur and pilot contamination is
   present. Expected collision partners per user ≈ K/τ_p − 1. Random assignment
  is pessimistic (smart assignment would do better) — so numerical results here
  represent a lower bound on what CF-mMIMO can achieve.

  2.3 Large-scale fading: path loss × shadow fading (Eqs. 46–47)

  β_{m,k} = PL_{m,k} · z_{m,k}
  z_{m,k} = 10^(σ_sh · n_{m,k} / 10),   n_{m,k} ~ 𝒩(0,1)
  with σ_sh = 8 dB (cellular-industry default).

  The three-slope path-loss model (Tang-Sun-Gong 2001, ref [24]):

              ⎧ −L − 35 log₁₀(d_{m,k}),                            d_{m,k} > d_1
  PL_{m,k}  = ⎨ −L − 15 log₁₀(d_1) − 20 log₁₀(d_{m,k}),      d_0 < d_{m,k} ≤ d_1
              ⎩ −L − 15 log₁₀(d_1) − 20 log₁₀(d_0),                d_{m,k} ≤ d_0
  L = 140.7 dB,   d_0 = 10 m,   d_1 = 50 m

  Physical meaning:
  - Very close (d ≤ 10 m): PL frozen at a constant — prevents "infinite gain"
  artefacts when a user sits right next to an AP.
  - Near regime (10 m < d ≤ 50 m): slope −20 log₁₀(d) — free-space exponent 2.
  - Far regime (d > 50 m): slope −35 log₁₀(d) — exponent 3.5, aggressive decay
  for dense urban NLOS.

  L = 140.7 dB corresponds to ~1.9 GHz carrier with 15 m BS antenna height and
  1.65 m UE antenna — typical urban macro.

  2.4 Table I power parameters

  ┌────────────────────────────────┬─────────────────┐
  │           Parameter            │      Value      │
  ├────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ PA efficiency ν_m              │ 0.4             │
  ├────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ Per-antenna circuit P_{tc,m}   │ 0.2 W           │
  ├────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ Fixed backhaul P_{0,m}         │ 0.825 W         │
  ├────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ Traffic-dep. backhaul P_{bt,m} │ 0.25 W/(Gbit/s) │
  └────────────────────────────────┴─────────────────┘

  Calibrated to Björnson-2015 [11] and Zuo-2016 [14], which in turn track the
  EARTH project (Auer-2011) small-cell measurements. Newer PAs
  (envelope-tracking, GaN) hit ν_m = 0.5+ and would make CF-mMIMO even more
  efficient than shown.

  2.5 Other constants

  B     = 20 MHz              ρ_d   = 1    (normalized DL SNR)
  NF    = 9 dB                ρ_p   = 0.2  (normalized UL pilot SNR)
  τ_c   = 200 samples         N₀    ≈ 6.4 × 10⁻¹⁴ W  (≈ −132 dBm)

  SCA termination: N_I = 10, ε_tol = 0.01. AP-selection threshold ρ = 95 %.

  Part B — Results and Discussions

  3.1 Fig. 1 — Convergence of Algorithm 1

  Setup: N = 1, D = 1 km, τ_p = 20, various (M, K) including (M = 8, K = 2) for
  a BRB benchmark.

  What it shows: EE (Mbit/J) vs. SCA iteration index.

  Key observations:

  1. Monotone increase. Every curve rises without exception → SCA analysis of
  Sec IV-D verified (previous iterate always feasible for next SOCP).
  2. Fast convergence. All curves plateau by ≤ 10 iterations. Marginal
  improvement beyond that is < 1 %.
  3. BRB benchmark matches. On (M=8, K=2), Algorithm 1 converges exactly to the
  global optimum found by branch-and-reduce-and-bound. Strong empirical evidence
   that SCA is not just a KKT-point method but is globally optimal in practice 
  on CF-mMIMO.
  4. Larger networks converge equally fast. (M=200, K=40) needs ~10 iterations,
  same as (M=8, K=2). Convergence depends on problem shape, not size.

  Conclusion: it is safe to fix N_I = 10 for all subsequent figures.

  3.2 Fig. 2 — Power control vs. "no power control"

  Setup: N = 1, D = 1 km, τ_p = 20, M varies 20–100 on the x-axis, two K values
  (20, 40).

  "No power control": every AP uses full transmit power, equally split:
  η_{m,k} = 1 / (N · Σ_{k'=1}^{K} γ_{m,k'})     for all k
  so Σ_k η_{m,k} γ_{m,k} = 1/N exactly. The target S_{o,k} for Algorithm 1 is
  set to match what no-PC delivers, so the comparison is fair QoS-wise.

  Key observations:

  1. Power control wins by a huge margin. At M = 100:
    - K = 20: Algorithm 1 ≈ 11 Mbit/J, no-PC ≈ 3.8 Mbit/J → 2.9× improvement.
    - K = 40: Algorithm 1 ≈ 10 Mbit/J, no-PC ≈ 3.9 Mbit/J → 2.6× improvement.

  Algorithm 1 roughly triples EE at zero hardware cost. This is the single
  biggest "free win" of the paper.
  2. EE grows with M for Algorithm 1 — more APs mean more array gain and
  diversity, which raise S_e faster than backhaul drags it down (for M ≤ 100).
  3. EE drops slightly with K — more pilot contamination (random assignment + K 
  > τ_p) and more backhaul traffic.
  4. "No power control" curve is roughly flat in M — adding APs under
  equal-power just replicates the same waste of transmit power. Algorithm 1
  aggressively shuts down inefficient η_{m,k} entries.

  Conclusion: all later figures use power control.

  3.3 Fig. 3 — Impact of AP selection

  Setup: M = 100, K = 40, N = 1, D = 1 km, τ_p = 40 (no pilot contamination,
  since τ_p ≥ K), S_{o,k} = 1 bit/s/Hz, ρ = 95 %. x-axis: P_{bt} from 0.25 to 2
  W/(Gbit/s).

  Three curves: no selection (baseline), largest-LSF-based (Algorithm 3),
  received-power-based (Algorithm 2).

  Key observations:

  1. Baseline EE crashes with P_{bt} — from ~10 to ~3 Mbit/J as P_{bt} grows
  from 0.25 to 2. This is the "backhaul is the elephant" effect.
  2. Both AP-selection curves are nearly FLAT in P_{bt}. They shield EE from
  backhaul-cost increases by keeping only necessary APs active. This is the main
   argument for using AP selection.
  3. Received-power-based slightly > LSF-based, but gap is 5–10 %. Algorithm 3 
  is an excellent practical choice because it is cheaper (one Algorithm-1 run
  instead of two).
  4. Gains are largest at high P_{bt}. At P_{bt} = 2, selection doubles EE. At
  P_{bt} = 0.25, gain is ~20 % only. AP selection is essential in deployments 
  with expensive backhaul (wireless BH, distant APs); helpful but optional 
  elsewhere.

  3.4 Fig. 4 — How many APs per user are actually chosen?

  Setup: K = 40, N = 1, τ_p = 40, ρ = 95 %, D ∈ {1, 2} km, M from 80 to 200.

  What it shows: average |A_k| (number of serving APs per user) vs. M.

  Key observations:

  1. |A_k| is roughly CONSTANT in M for D = 1 km — ~10 APs at M = 80, ~15 APs at
   M = 200. Sub-linear scaling. As you densify the network, the backhaul load 
  per user stays roughly constant. Without selection it would scale linearly.
  This is scalability.
  2. D = 2 km requires more APs per user than D = 1 km — ~30 vs ~15 at M = 200.
  In sparser deployments, β decays more slowly across APs, so more are needed to
   reach 95 % cumulative.
  3. Received-power-based selects ~10 % fewer APs than LSF-based — it sees how
  Algorithm 1 is actually allocating η and correctly drops APs with strong β but
   small η (e.g. those serving many users simultaneously).

  The paper's conclusion:

  ▎ "Only about 10–20% of the total number of APs really participate in serving 
  ▎ a given user… it is enough for each user to choose a small number of APs 
  ▎ around it, which enables the PRACTICAL IMPLEMENTATION of cell-free massive 
  ▎ MIMO."

  This is the motto of user-centric CF-mMIMO that blossomed in 2018+. In the
  remaining figures the paper uses received-power-based with ρ = 95 %.

  3.5 Fig. 5 — Effect of N, fixed MN = 256

  Setup: K = 40, τ_p = 40, MN = 256, so M = 256/N. Received-power-based, ρ = 95 
  %. Three scenarios:

  ┌────────────┬──────┬──────────┬────────────┐
  │  Scenario  │  D   │ P_{bt,m} │  S_{o,k}   │
  ├────────────┼──────┼──────────┼────────────┤
  │ 1 (best)   │ 1 km │ 0.25     │ 1 bit/s/Hz │
  ├────────────┼──────┼──────────┼────────────┤
  │ 2 (medium) │ 2 km │ 0.5      │ 1 bit/s/Hz │
  ├────────────┼──────┼──────────┼────────────┤
  │ 3 (harder) │ 2 km │ 0.25     │ 2 bit/s/Hz │
  └────────────┴──────┴──────────┴────────────┘

  What it shows: EE is non-monotone in N — rises from N = 1, peaks at some N*,
  falls for large N. Inverted-U curves with distinct peaks.

  Intuition — the CF-mMIMO N trade-off:

  ┌───────────┬───────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Regime   │           Pros            │              Cons              │
  ├───────────┼───────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Small N   │ High macro-diversity,     │ High fixed backhaul Σ_m        │
  │ (many     │ high SE                   │ P_{0,m} = (256/N)·0.825 W;     │
  │ APs)      │                           │ high circuit                   │
  ├───────────┼───────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Large N   │ Low fixed backhaul, large │ Low macro-diversity; users far │
  │ (few APs) │  per-AP array gain (N²)   │  from nearest AP; SE drops     │
  └───────────┴───────────────────────────┴────────────────────────────────┘

  N* is the sweet spot.

  Optimal N per scenario:

  ┌────────────────────────────────┬─────┬────────────┐
  │            Scenario            │ N*  │  Peak EE   │
  ├────────────────────────────────┼─────┼────────────┤
  │ 1 (dense + cheap BH + low QoS) │ ~4  │ ~25 Mbit/J │
  ├────────────────────────────────┼─────┼────────────┤
  │ 2 (sparse + expensive BH)      │ ~16 │ ~15 Mbit/J │
  ├────────────────────────────────┼─────┼────────────┤
  │ 3 (sparse + harder QoS)        │ ~32 │ ~5 Mbit/J  │
  └────────────────────────────────┴─────┴────────────┘

  Design rule:
  - Low P_{bt}, low S_o, small D → small N* (many APs, few antennas per AP)
  - High P_{bt}, high S_o, large D → large N* (few APs, many antennas per AP)

  The paper does not derive a closed-form N* but Fig. 5 lets an engineer eyeball
   the right range.

  3.6 Fig. 6 — Cell-Free vs Colocated Massive MIMO (the headline)

  Setup: K = 20, τ_p = 40, D = 1 km. Two values of MN (128 and 256).

  - Colocated: M = 1, N ∈ {128, 256} (one huge BS).
  - Cell-free: received-power-based with N* from Fig. 5 (approximately N* ≈ 4
  here, so M = MN/4).

  x-axis: sum SE target Σ_k S_{o,k}. y-axis: average EE.

  Key observations:

  1. CF-mMIMO is uniformly better across the entire range of sum-rate targets
  for both MN values.
  2. Quantitative headline at Σ_k S_{o,k} = 20 bit/s/Hz (1 bit/s/Hz per user):

  | MN  | CF EE       | Colocated EE | CF factor |
  |-----|-------------|--------------|-----------|
  | 128 | ≈ 20 Mbit/J | ≈ 2.7 Mbit/J | ≈ 7.4×    |
  | 256 | ≈ 22 Mbit/J | ≈ 10 Mbit/J  | ≈ 2.2×    |

  3. The gap shrinks with MN. At very large antenna counts, the colocated BS has
   enough array gain to reach every user with little power. At moderate MN =
  128, the colocated BS struggles with cell-edge users (insufficient diversity),
   while distributed CF has an AP near every user and delivers QoS easily. CF's 
  macro-diversity gain is inversely proportional to how much array gain you 
  already have.
  4. CF curves are broader and more resilient — a diversity effect: even when
  the target becomes aggressive, CF has nearby APs that can still deliver.

  The paper's closing line of Section VI:

  ▎ "This shows the strong potential of cell-free massive MIMO to boost the 
  ▎ energy and spectral efficiency of 5G networks."

  Five years of subsequent literature have borne this out.

  Björnson-2015 and Zuo-2016. Became the standard "honeynut" numbers for
  CF-mMIMO EE papers 2017–2020.
  - BRB benchmark from Tervo-Tran-Juntti 2015 [25] — modified to check SCA
  global-optimality on small problems.
  - SOCP solver: MOSEK (via YALMIP/MATLAB) per the code repo.

  typical for CF-mMIMO papers of the era.

  Wait — E_e is monotonically increasing in N. This does NOT reproduce Fig. 5's 
  inverted-U. Why?

  Because this calculation assumed constant S_e = 40 bit/s/Hz. In reality, as N
  grows and M shrinks, macro-diversity drops, users get farther from the nearest
   AP, and S_e drops. For extreme large N / small M, some users may become
  infeasible and EE collapses.

  Lesson: the N* trade-off in Fig. 5 is not a power effect — it is a 
  spectral-efficiency effect. Large N / small M loses the "AP near every user"
  benefit and S_e crashes, driving EE down. Power savings from shrinking M
  cannot compensate for SE loss beyond N*.

  1 · High-Level (Layman) Explanation

  The paper is essentially done by Section VI. Section VII is a one-paragraph
  summary of the findings. Appendix A then backs up the central closed-form
  result (Proposition 1, Eq. 15) with a careful computation of the three
  statistical moments that define the "use-and-forget" capacity bound:

  - DS_k — expected desired-signal gain at user k
  - 𝔼[|BU_k|²] — variance of the beamforming-uncertainty term
  - 𝔼[|UI_{kk'}|²] — variance of the inter-user interference from user k' to
  user k

  Plugging these three into the Shannon-style SE expression (Eq. 11) lands you
  exactly at the closed-form Eq. 15 — the cornerstone of the entire paper.

  The tools used in Appendix A are elementary: (i) MMSE orthogonality principle,
   (ii) fourth moments of complex Gaussian vectors, (iii) independence of
  channels across APs. But the bookkeeping is tricky and several magical
  cancellations produce the simple final formula.

  In layman terms:
  - Appendix A answers "I claimed in Section II that per-user SE has a closed 
  form. How do I actually prove it?"
  - Section VII answers "What's the practical bottom line for building cell-free
   networks?"

  2 · Section VII — Conclusion (elaborated)

  2.1 The five claims

  The one-paragraph conclusion states:

  (a) Investigated CF-mMIMO EE with multi-antenna APs, including four realistic
  effects: channel-estimation errors, EE-optimized power control, pilot
  contamination, and backhaul power consumption.

  (b) Proposed two AP-selection schemes to reduce backhaul power (the dominant
  term at large M).

  (c) The combined power-allocation + AP-selection improves EE significantly.

  (d) For a given user, only ~10–20 % of APs really participate in serving it.

  (e) With 1 bit/s/Hz per-user QoS, CF-mMIMO improves EE by an order of 
  magnitude compared to colocated massive MIMO.

  2.2 What each claim really says (the subtext)

  (a) Multi-antenna APs are a first-class citizen. Before this paper, the
  CF-mMIMO literature treated APs as single-antenna. This paper delivers the
  first exact closed-form SE for N ≥ 1. Multi-antenna APs matter because they
  amortize fixed backhaul across more antennas, deliver coherent N² array gain
  within each AP, and give designers a knob (N) to trade macro-diversity against
   backhaul cost. A multi-antenna AP is the right abstraction layer between pure
   distributed antennas and colocated massive MIMO.

  (b) Backhaul is the elephant and AP selection is the cure. Without the
  AP-selection of Sec V, the backhaul term crushes EE at large M (Fig. 3: EE
  collapses at high P_{bt}; with selection it stays nearly flat). The paper
  proposes two schemes of different complexity, letting the deployer pick.

  (c) Power control is FREE. Algorithm 1 requires zero hardware change — only a
  software update that runs once per large-scale-fading realization (~1 s). Yet
  it boosts EE by 2.6–3×. The single most persuasive argument for turning it on
  by default.

  (d) "Only ~15 % of APs per user" = scalability. This is the claim that
  launched the user-centric CF-mMIMO paradigm. Subsequent papers (Buzzi-2017,
  Björnson-2020, Interdonato-2019) formalized it as a scalability property:
  per-user computational, backhaul, and CSI overhead stays constant as M grows.
  Ngo-2018 is one of the first papers to empirically document it via Fig. 4.

  (e) Order-of-magnitude EE improvement over colocated MIMO. The marketing
  headline: "CF-mMIMO is 10× greener than 4G+ massive MIMO at 1 bit/s/Hz QoS and
   MN = 128 service antennas." The gap shrinks at larger MN (2.2× at MN = 256)
  because colocated eventually acquires enough array gain, but at moderate
  antenna counts — the regime of interest for early 6G deployments — CF-mMIMO
  wins decisively.

  2.3 What Section VII does NOT discuss — limitations

  A PhD student should be aware of these gaps. They are exactly where new
  research lives:

  - Uplink not analyzed. Downlink-only paper. Bashar-Cumanan-Burr-Ngo 2017
  extends to uplink.
  - No CSI-sharing cost. Distributed conjugate beamforming only. If the CPU were
   to do centralized MMSE, backhaul would also carry CSI — an extra term
  Ngo-2018 does not model. The 2019 Björnson-Sanguinetti MMSE paper (which you 
  have studied) addresses centralized processing.
  - Fronthaul vs backhaul distinction ignored. The AP-CPU link in C-RAN
  terminology is more accurately a fronthaul carrying baseband samples, which is
   ~100× more expensive per bit/s than a user-data backhaul. Table I corresponds
   to backhaul assumptions.
  - Only conjugate beamforming. ZF and MMSE precoding (which would increase SE)
  are not considered. ZF CF: Nayebi 2015 [8]; MMSE CF: Björnson-Sanguinetti
  2019.
  - Random pilot assignment only. Smarter pilot schemes (graph-coloring,
  location-aware) would reduce contamination and boost EE.
  - No user mobility. Large-scale fading is static per run. Real users move →
  sets A_k must be re-computed over time → motivation for the ML-based AP
  selection papers of 2022–2024.

  3 · Appendix A — Proof of Proposition 1

  3.1 What we need to compute

  Recall the use-and-forget SE bound (Eq. 11):
                                             |DS_k|²
  SE_k = (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂(1 +  ────────────────────────────────── )
                                   𝔼{|BU_k|²} + Σ_{k'≠k} 𝔼{|UI_{kk'}|²} + 1
  with DS_k, BU_k, UI_{kk'} from (12)–(14). We compute |DS_k|², 𝔼[|BU_k|²], 
  𝔼[|UI_{kk'}|²] and plug into (11) to get (15).

  Six facts used throughout:
  - F1. MMSE orthogonality: ĝ_{m,k} ⊥ 𝐠̃_{m,k} statistically independent (jointly
   Gaussian + uncorrelated ⇒ independent).
  - F2. ĝ_{m,k} has IID entries 𝒞𝒩(0, γ_{m,k}), 𝐠̃_{m,k} has IID entries 𝒞𝒩(0, 
  β_{m,k} − γ_{m,k}).
  - F3. Channels across different APs are independent.
  - F4. Cross-AP-index terms with zero mean vanish in second-moment
  computations.
  - F5. Gaussian fourth-moment identity: for 𝐱 ~ 𝒞𝒩(𝟎, σ²𝐈_N), 𝔼‖𝐱‖² = Nσ² and
  𝔼‖𝐱‖⁴ = N(N+1)σ⁴.
  - F6. ĝ_{m,k} = b_{m,k} · y_{p,m,k} with b_{m,k} = √(τ_p ρ_p) β_{m,k} / (τ_p 
  ρ_p Σ_{k'} β_{m,k'} |φ_{k'}^H φ_k|² + 1).

  3.2 Computing DS_k (Eq. 48)

  Using 𝐠_{m,k} = ĝ_{m,k} + 𝐠̃_{m,k} and 𝔼[𝐠̃^T ĝ*] = 0 (F1):

  DS_k = √ρ_d · 𝔼{ Σ_m √η_{m,k} (ĝ_{m,k} + 𝐠̃_{m,k})^T ĝ_{m,k}^* }
       = √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k} · 𝔼{‖ĝ_{m,k}‖²}    (cross term vanishes)
       = √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k} · N γ_{m,k}
       = √ρ_d · N · Σ_m √η_{m,k} γ_{m,k}                                   (48)

  Therefore:
  |DS_k|² = ρ_d · N² · (Σ_m √η_{m,k} γ_{m,k})²  =  ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|²
  where 𝛈_k denotes the vector of √η_{m,k} (notational subtlety flagged in
  Section II).

  3.3 Computing 𝔼[|BU_k|²] (Eqs. 49–50)

  Let X_m ≝ 𝐠_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*. Then
  𝔼{|BU_k|²} = ρ_d · 𝔼{|Σ_m √η_{m,k}(X_m − 𝔼X_m)|²}
  The cross-AP terms (m ≠ m') vanish because channels across APs are independent
   and zero-mean (F3, F4), leaving:
  𝔼{|BU_k|²} = ρ_d · Σ_m η_{m,k} · Var(X_m)                               (49)

  Computing Var(X_m):

  Write X_m = ‖ĝ_{m,k}‖² + 𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*. So 𝔼X_m = N γ_{m,k} (cross term
  vanishes by F1).
  𝔼{|X_m|²} = 𝔼‖ĝ_{m,k}‖⁴ + 𝔼|𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*|²  (cross-term vanishes)

  Piece (a) — Gaussian fourth moment (F5): 𝔼‖ĝ_{m,k}‖⁴ = N(N+1) γ_{m,k}².

  Piece (b) — inner product of independent vectors:
  𝔼|𝐠̃_{m,k}^T ĝ_{m,k}^*|² = Σ_n 𝔼|[𝐠̃]_n|² · 𝔼|[ĝ]_n|²
                           = N · (β_{m,k} − γ_{m,k}) · γ_{m,k}
                           = N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k} − N γ_{m,k}²

  So:
  𝔼{|X_m|²} = N(N+1) γ_{m,k}² + N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k} − N γ_{m,k}²
            = N² γ_{m,k}² + N γ_{m,k}² + N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k} − N γ_{m,k}²
            = N² γ_{m,k}² + N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}

  The magic cancellation:
  Var(X_m) = 𝔼{|X_m|²} − |𝔼X_m|²
           = N² γ_{m,k}² + N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k} − N² γ_{m,k}²
           = N β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}.

  The N² γ² from the fourth moment cancels exactly with (𝔼X_m)² = N² γ², leaving
   only a linear-in-N residual. This is why channel hardening exists: variance ∝
   N, mean ∝ N² ⇒ relative uncertainty |BU|/|DS| ~ 1/√N → 0.

  Substituting back:
  𝔼{|BU_k|²} = ρ_d N · Σ_m η_{m,k} β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
             = ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k,k} 𝛈_k‖²                                    (50)
  where [𝐃_{k,k}]_{m,m} = √(β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}).

  3.4 Computing 𝔼[|UI_{kk'}|²] (Eqs. 51–55) — the pilot-contamination 
  calculation

  From (14) and F6 (ĝ_{m,k'} = b_{m,k'} · y_{p,m,k'}):
  UI_{kk'} = √ρ_d · Σ_m √η_{m,k'} · b_{m,k'} · 𝐠_{m,k}^T · y_{p,m,k'}^*

  Expanding y_{p,m,k'} using Eq. 3:
  y_{p,m,k'} = √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k'}
             + √(τ_p ρ_p) Σ_{k''≠k'} 𝐠_{m,k''}(φ_{k''}^H φ_{k'})
             + 𝐰̃_{p,m,k'}

  The middle sum has a critical k'' = k term:
  √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k}^T · 𝐠_{m,k}^* · (φ_k^H φ_{k'})^* = √(τ_p ρ_p) ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² ·
  (φ_k^H φ_{k'})^*
  This is pilot contamination. Proportional to ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖², so it does not 
  average out — it has nonzero mean and contributes a term scaling with N².

  All other terms (the √(τ_p ρ_p) 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐠_{m,k'}^* direct term with k ≠ k',
  the k'' ∉ {k, k'} cross products, and the noise piece 𝐠_{m,k}^T 𝐰̃^*) have zero
   mean and contribute N-scaling variance ("non-coherent interference").

  Computing 𝔼{|Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖²|²}, splitting into diagonal (m 
  = m') and off-diagonal (m ≠ m'):

  - Diagonal: uses 𝔼‖𝐠_{m,k}‖⁴ = N(N+1) β_{m,k}².
  - Off-diagonal: uses independence across APs → N² β_{m,k} β_{m',k}.

  Together:
  = N² · (Σ_m √η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} β_{m,k})²
    + N · Σ_m η_{m,k'} b_{m,k'}² β_{m,k}²

  Substituting γ_{m,k'} = √(τ_p ρ_p) β_{m,k'} b_{m,k'} (from Eq. 5) and
  simplifying:

  ρ_d · τ_p ρ_p · |φ_k^H φ_{k'}|² · N² · (Σ_m √η_{m,k'} γ_{m,k'}
  √(β_{m,k}/β_{m,k'}))²

  This is exactly the first term of the pilot-contamination quadratic in Eq. 15,
   with
  [𝛄_{k,k'}]_m = |φ_{k'}^H φ_k| · γ_{m,k'} · √(β_{m,k}/β_{m,k'})

  The non-contaminating pieces (zero-mean) plus the noise cross-term give the
  N-scaling part, which after some algebra becomes:
  ρ_d N · Σ_m η_{m,k'} γ_{m,k'} β_{m,k}  =  ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖²

  Final (Eq. 55):
  𝔼{|UI_{kk'}|²}
    = ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²       (pilot contamination — ∝ N²)
    + ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖²       (non-coherent — ∝ N)

  3.5 Assembling Proposition 1

  Substituting (48), (50), (55) into (11):
  - Numerator |DS_k|² = ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|²
  - BU term = ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k,k} 𝛈_k‖² — this is the k' = k instance of the
  non-coherent sum!
  - UI sum over k' ≠ k contributes ρ_d N² · Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|² + ρ_d 
  N · Σ_{k'≠k} ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖²

  Combining BU + UI into a unified sum from k' = 1 to K:
  SE_k = (1 − τ_p/τ_c) · log₂(1 +
          ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|² /
          (ρ_d N² Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²
         + ρ_d N Σ_{k'=1}^K ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖² + 1))                      (15) ∎

  3.6 Why this is a beautiful proof

  1. DS_k is a one-liner by MMSE orthogonality. Clean.
  2. BU_k variance has the magic cancellation N² γ² − N² γ² = 0, leaving only N 
  β γ. This is the mathematical reason channel hardening exists.
  3. UI_{kk'} exposes pilot contamination precisely: the N² term survives iff
  φ_k^H φ_{k'} ≠ 0, and its magnitude is the correlated part of 𝐠_{m,k}^T 
  𝐠_{m,k'}^* — i.e., ‖𝐠_{m,k}‖² scaled by the pilot inner product.
  4. All three moments share the same matrices 𝛄_{k,k'}, 𝐃_{k',k}, giving (15)
  its compact quadratic-form look.

  5 · Mathematical Recap (Unicode)

  Three central moments:
  |DS_k|²           = ρ_d N² (Σ_m √η_{m,k} γ_{m,k})²
                     = ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k}^T 𝛈_k|²

  𝔼{|BU_k|²}        = ρ_d N · Σ_m η_{m,k} β_{m,k} γ_{m,k}
                     = ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k,k} 𝛈_k‖²

  𝔼{|UI_{kk'}|²}    = ρ_d N² |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|²       (pilot contamination)
                    + ρ_d N · ‖𝐃_{k',k} 𝛈_{k'}‖²         (non-coherent)

  Definitions:
  𝛄_{k,k}    : [𝛄_{k,k}]_m = γ_{m,k}
  𝛄_{k,k'}   : [𝛄_{k,k'}]_m = |φ_{k'}^H φ_k| · γ_{m,k'} · √(β_{m,k}/β_{m,k'})
  𝐃_{k',k}   : [𝐃_{k',k}]_{m,m} = √(β_{m,k} γ_{m,k'})

  Key identities used:
  𝔼‖𝐱‖²  = Nσ²            for 𝐱 ~ 𝒞𝒩(𝟎, σ²𝐈_N)
  𝔼‖𝐱‖⁴  = N(N+1) σ⁴     (Gaussian/Wishart fourth moment)
  Var(‖𝐱‖²) = N σ⁴

  5.1 Sanity checks

  - N = 1 → (15) reduces to Eq. (24) of Ngo-2017 TWC [6] ✓
  - M = 1 (colocated, rename N → M') → Marzetta textbook Table 3.2 ✓
  - Orthogonal pilots → Σ_{k'≠k} |𝛄_{k,k'}^T 𝛈_{k'}|² vanishes
  - ρ_d → 0 → SE → 0 linearly ✓
  - ρ_d → ∞ with pilot contamination → SE → finite ceiling: the famous
  pilot-contamination limit of Marzetta 2010.

  Toy: N = 2, β = 1, γ = 0.5.

  1. 𝔼‖ĝ‖² = Nγ = 1

  7.2 The single most important equation

  Eq. (15) — the closed-form SE. Everything else is downstream:
  - Sec III's power model inherits S_e via the backhaul term
  - Sec IV plugs (15) into the SE constraint and objective
  - Sec V uses γ_{m,k} (the shape parameter of (15)) to rank APs
  - Sec VI evaluates (15) at every SCA iteration

  If you understand (15) — numerator + pilot contamination term + non-coherent 
  term + reduction to colocated/single-antenna limits — you understand the 
  paper.

  7.3 What a PhD student should take away

  1. Know the use-and-forget bound cold. Standard tool for every massive-MIMO
  capacity paper.
  2. The SCA-SOCP recipe of Section IV is a template. (i) Divide by numerator to
   linearize the denominator; (ii) substitute c = √η for quadratic constraints;
  (iii) slack-variable decompose log-of-ratio constraints; (iv) Taylor-expand
  quadratic-over-linear; (v) log-inequality bound. Practice it.
  3. User-centric CF-mMIMO is the dominant deployment philosophy. Section V is
  one of its two founding papers (Buzzi-2017 is the other). Björnson-Sanguinetti
   2020 scalable CF (which you studied) is the mature treatment.
  4. Backhaul always matters in distributed deployments. Any EE analysis that
  ignores it is optimistic. Sec III is the template for including it properly.
  5. Simulation methodology matters. Wrap-around torus + three-slope path loss +
   Björnson-calibrated Table I values are standard — use them so your numbers
  are comparable to the literature.
  6. Know what the paper does NOT do: uplink, CSI-sharing cost, fronthaul vs
  backhaul, ZF/MMSE precoding, user mobility. These open gaps are where PhD 
  contributions live.

  7.4 Notable influences on later CF-mMIMO literature

  After Ngo-2018, CF-mMIMO branched into:
  1. User-centric CF (Buzzi-2017, Björnson-2020, Interdonato-2019) — direct
  descendants of Section V.
  2. Centralized MMSE CF (Björnson-Sanguinetti 2019 — you have studied this) —
  upper-bound analysis with centralized signal processing.
  3. Scalable CF (Björnson-Sanguinetti 2020 — you have studied this) —
  formalizes Fig. 4's "15 % of APs" observation as a scalability property.
  4. EE + user-centric + MMSE (Bashar-Ngo-Duong-Cumanan 2018+) — combines Ngo's
  EE framework with Björnson's MMSE analysis.
  5. RIS-aided CF (2020+) — adds reconfigurable surfaces.
  6. Learning-based CF (2022+) — DRL for AP association and power control,
  replacing SCA with neural networks.

  In each, Ngo-2018-TGCN is a primary citation or methodological ancestor. Its
  citation count reflects this.
