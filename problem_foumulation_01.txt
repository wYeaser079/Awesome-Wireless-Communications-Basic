  Step 2: Write the Channel Model

  What it means: Describe HOW signals travel from transmitter to receiver.

  Common models from your papers:
  - Rayleigh fading (Papers 6, 7, 10): g_mk = sqrt(beta_mk) * h_mk where beta is
   large-scale, h is small-scale
  - Rician fading (Paper 1): Has both LoS and NLoS components with spatial
  correlation
  - Saleh-Valenzuela / geometric (Papers 2, 3): Sparse mmWave channels with L
  paths, array response vectors
  - With RIS (Papers 1, 2): u_lk = g_lk + H_l^H * Phi * z_k (direct + reflected
  path)

  Step 3: Write the Signal Model (Received Signal)

  What it means: Write the mathematical expression for what each user actually
  receives.

  The universal structure is always:

  received = desired_signal + interference + noise

  From Paper 1 (simplest form):
  y_k = u_k^H * w_k * s_k  +  sum_{k'!=k} u_k^H * w_{k'} * s_{k'}  +  n_k
          [desired]              [inter-user interference]          [noise]

  From Paper 2 (with RIS + HBF):
  y_k = sum_b w_k^H * H_{b,k} * F_{RF,b} * f_{BB,b,k} * s_k    [desired]
      + sum_b sum_{j!=k} w_k^H * H_{b,k} * F_{RF,b} * f_{BB,b,j} * s_j
  [interference]
      + w_k^H * n_k                                                [noise]

  Step 4: Derive the SINR and Performance Metric

  What it means: From the signal model, compute the SINR
  (Signal-to-Interference-plus-Noise Ratio) and then the achievable rate.

  SINR formula (universal):
  SINR_k = |desired_signal_power|^2 / (|interference_power|^2 + noise_power)

  Rate formula (always the same):
  R_k = log2(1 + SINR_k)

  From Paper 1:
  R_k = log2(1 + |u_k^H * w_k|^2 / (sum_{k'!=k} |u_k^H * w_{k'}|^2 + sigma^2))

  Step 5: Formulate the Optimization Problem

  What it means: State what you want to maximize/minimize, subject to what
  physical constraints.

  The general template:
  maximize/minimize   [Objective Function]
    over variables    [Design Variables]
    subject to        [Constraints]

  PRO TIPS From Reading Your 10 Papers

  1. Start simple, add complexity. Paper 10 (single-antenna APs, no RIS) is the
  simplest. Paper 2 (multi-antenna + HBF + RIS + combining) is the most complex.
   Master the simple case first.
  2. The signal model is 90% of the work. Once you have the correct received
  signal expression, the SINR writes itself, and the optimization problem is
  just choosing what to maximize.
  3. Every non-convex joint problem gets decomposed. Papers 1, 2, 3 all use
  alternating optimization. Fix passive BF, optimize active BF. Fix active BF,
  optimize passive BF. Iterate.
  4. Unit-modulus constraints are the hardest part. Analog beamformers and RIS
  elements all have |x| = 1 constraints. This is what makes problems NP-hard.
  Solutions include: SDR (relax to SDP), manifold optimization, ADMM, BFGS, or
  element-wise optimization.
  5. Power allocation and beamforming direction are often separable. Paper 1
  uses ZF for direction + geometric programming for power. Paper 7 uses ECB for
  direction + max-min for power. This separation simplifies the problem
  enormously.
  6. Read the "contribution" bullet points in the introduction of each paper.
  They tell you exactly what is NEW in their formulation compared to prior work.
   Your own paper needs similar novelty.
